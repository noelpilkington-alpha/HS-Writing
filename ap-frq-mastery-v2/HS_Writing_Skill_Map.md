# High School Writing — Skill Map

Every writing skill the course teaches, grouped, with the reason each belongs and
where it is practiced. Companion to the **Course Overview**. Skills are anchored to
the official standards and rubrics they come from: **Texas TEKS / STAAR English I &
II** and the **ACT Writing rubric** (the course maps to both STAAR and CCSS 9–10).

> **Design overview, not built content.** This describes the *designed* course
> (lesson sequence, standards crosswalk, and assessment specs are complete). It is a
> net-new course; lesson content is the next build step.

There are two kinds of skill:
- **Through-line skills** run through every lesson and grow across the course.
- **Unit skills** are the specific move each unit is built around.

---

## Through-line skills (built in every lesson, grown across the course)

| Skill | What the student does | Anchored to |
|-------|------------------------|-------------|
| Read for the idea | Read a source passage and determine what it says/argues before writing about it (comprehension precedes composition) | STAAR EI/EII (4)(F–I), (5)(B); the ECR is passage-based |
| Form a controlling idea | State a clear, defensible controlling idea (or position) about a passage's content, and hold consistent focus | STAAR (5)(C), (9)(B)(i); ECR Trait 1 (thesis development) |
| **Explain the evidence** | Cite specific text evidence and explain *how* it supports the idea — not drop the quote | STAAR (5)(C), (9)(B)(ii); **the ECR's 3-vs-2 lever** |
| Structure the response | Build a purposeful response with a real introduction and conclusion and logical transitions | STAAR (9)(B)(i); ECR Trait 1 (organization) |
| Self-assess against the rubric | Score one's own draft and find its weakest trait; grows from yes/no checks to self-diagnosis | research on formative self-assessment; surfaced, not gating |
| Control conventions in context | Write complete, controlled sentences (no run-ons/fragments); apply grammar, punctuation, spelling so errors don't impede clarity | STAAR (9)(D); ECR Trait 2 (Conventions) |
| Manage time | Plan, pace, and recover under the clock (enters late, once the moves are automatic) | ACT 40-min task; STAAR same-day window |

**Why these run throughout:** they are the moves used in *every* piece of writing,
so they are practiced in every lesson and deepened each time rather than taught once
and dropped. Reading-for-the-idea runs throughout because STAAR/ACT are
reading-dependent — a misread looks like a writing failure, so comprehension is
scored separately and a misread routes to re-read support, not to more writing reps.

**"Explain the evidence" is the load-bearing skill.** The verified STAAR ECR rubric
shows the single biggest lever between a top and a middling score is whether
commentary *explains how* the evidence supports the idea (rubric language: "clearly
explained" = 3 · "insufficiently explained" = 2 · "insufficient" = 1). The most
common down-move in the released scoring guides is *"instead of explaining the
evidence…"* — dropping a quote without the link. The whole course is built to fix
this.

## Where to put the practice weight (by mode)

The skills above are shared, but the verified evidence shows where the points
actually live. Same skill list, weighted by what the test rewards:

| Target | Practice the most | Keep light (already a strength) |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------------------|
| STAAR English I/II (the ECR) | **explaining evidence**; controlling-idea development; sentence-boundary control | the thesis (present in most responses; development, not presence, is the lever) |
| STAAR English II (synthesis) | synthesizing across **paired/multiple** sources (evidence from *all* texts) | — |
| ACT Writing | **engaging and evaluating multiple perspectives** (not restating); qualifying the argument; conveying significance | grammar (it only gates the bottom of the ACT rubric) |

**Why:** across both the STAAR ECR and the ACT rubric, the top-vs-middle gap is
*analysis and explanation*, not grammar or thesis presence. Weighting practice
toward explaining evidence (STAAR) and evaluating perspectives (ACT) targets where
students actually lose points.

**Mode weighting (from 6 released STAAR forms).** Every released English I/II ECR
has been **informational or analytical-response-to-text** (the primary target).
**Argumentative has not appeared on a released ECR** but is an eligible mode and is
ACT-aligned, so the course teaches it as the **secondary** mode — primary for ACT
readiness and a hedge against the eligible STAAR mode.

## Unit skills (the specific move each unit installs)

### English I (G9) — passage-based composition floor

**Unit 1 — Reading-to-write foundations (the bridge)**
- Reason, not restate: write the sentence that explains *how* evidence supports a
  point — taught first, before the controlling idea.
- Read a passage for its idea (comprehension precedes composition).
- Refine a response into a clear controlling idea about the passage's content.

*Why first:* everything else is a variation on these moves; they must be secure
before anything is layered on. *Why reasoning before the controlling idea:* a
serviceable thesis is already a student strength, so the unit leads with the move
students actually struggle with — explaining evidence — and treats the controlling
idea as the refinement step.

**Unit 2 — Expository response-to-text** *(the primary STAAR mode)*
- Build a full informational/analytical response: controlling idea → explained
  text evidence → unified structure, on a source passage.

*Why here:* this is the mode the released STAAR ECR actually sets most often, built
on the Unit 1 moves.

**Unit 3 — Argumentative response-to-text** *(secondary STAAR mode; primary for ACT)*
- Take a defensible position on a passage's issue; build a line of reasoning.
- Acknowledge and **respond to** a counterargument (acknowledge / concede / respond).
- Use evidence accurately and integrate it (no dropped quotes).

*Why counterargument here:* a real position has to answer the other side — and on
the STAAR argumentative rubric, *refuting* the counterargument is what separates a
top score from a middling one.

**Unit 4 — Conventions, short responses, and the gate**
- Control sentence boundaries (eliminate run-ons and fragments — the most-cited
  conventions fault).
- Sentence-combining (the STAAR writing short response).
- Reading short response: answer accurately **and** cite text evidence.

*Why here:* conventions are coached in context, and the short-response item types
are bounded skills that round out the STAAR English I task before the gate.

### English II (G10) — synthesis, depth, ACT readiness

**Unit 5 — Synthesis across sources** *(the STAAR English II top-band move)*
- Read paired/multiple sources for their stance; evaluate credibility and bias.
- Organize by theme/claim, not source-by-source (the synthesis matrix).
- Weave a position across the sources, with evidence drawn from *all* texts.

*Why it matters:* the STAAR ECR rubric awards the top band only when evidence is
drawn from *both* texts — synthesis, not serial summary, is the scored move.

**Unit 6 — Argument at depth**
- Move a flat position to a qualified one (nuance: scope, conditions, "holds except
  where…").
- Engage opposing views (agree-with-difference / disagree / complicate); concede and
  rebut genuinely (not strawman).

*Why here:* English II raises argument from "has a position" to "has a nuanced
position that answers real opposition" — the depth the rubric and the ACT reward.

**Unit 7 — Multi-perspective / ACT readiness**
- Decompose three given perspectives (claim / assumption / value / blind spot).
- Stake a position related to the perspectives; qualify and complicate it.
- Build an integrated line of reasoning that conveys significance (constructed
  support — no source passages).

*Why here:* this is the ACT Writing task, and its top-vs-middle lever is
*evaluating* multiple perspectives rather than restating them. Timing enters here.

**Unit 8 — Timed performance and the gate**
- Plan-to-essay pacing; self-diagnosis (find and fix your own weakest rubric row
  under the clock).

*Why last:* timing is added only once the moves are automatic, so the only new load
under the clock is pacing, not the writing itself.

## Style, conventions, and author's craft

- **Conventions** are checked in-context during the Polish stage of each essay and
  in a dedicated short-response strand, not drilled as isolated grammar exercises —
  because fixing them inside real writing transfers better. (English II adds
  parentheses to its punctuation repertoire — the one English I→II conventions step-up.)
- **Diction and tone for effect** (choosing word choice and syntax for mood/voice/
  tone) is a recurring **English II Polish** beat — the writing course owns
  author's-craft-for-effect (TEKS EII.8(F)), taught as a refinement after meaning is
  settled, not as rhetorical analysis.

## The thinking lenses behind the skills

The course teaches a small set of reusable "ways of seeing" that power the skills
above. They are *how* a skill is taught, not separately graded — consolidated to the
few that are genuinely load-bearing:

| Lens | The question it trains |
|------|------------------------|
| Structure → Function → Effect (S-F-E) | "What is it, what does it do, why does it matter?" — the engine of explaining evidence |
| Claim hierarchy | "Where does this fit in the argument's structure?" — the line of reasoning |
| The function question | "What is this doing here / why this move?" — read a text for what its parts do (introduced on the student's own writing, then on a passage) |

**Why so few:** a lens earns its place only if it *is* a move the rubric scores.
S-F-E is the reasoning/commentary the ECR rewards; claim hierarchy is the
organization/line-of-reasoning; the function question is how a student reads a
passage well enough to write about it. (Earlier designs tracked six named models;
the rest were either restatements of these or are now taught as plain procedures.)

## How the skills map to the gates (the verifiable outcomes)

| Gate | The skills it certifies | Aligns to |
|------|-------------------------|-----------|
| **English I** | read for the idea → controlling idea → **explained evidence** → structure → conventions; + short responses | STAAR English I (ECR Informational rubric primary; Argumentative eligible) + SCRs |
| **English II** | synthesis across sources (evidence from all texts) + multi-perspective analysis under time | STAAR English II (synthesis ECR) + ACT Writing readiness (4-domain) |

## Scope note for developers

This map and the Course Overview cover the **English I + II MVP** — the part that is
fully designed (lesson map, standards crosswalk, passage spec, and assessment specs
complete) and ready for content authoring. It is the foundation of a sliding ramp
that extends to **ACT readiness (G11+)** and **downstream AP FRQ preparation** — but
those are not promised outcomes of the MVP, and AP-specific modules are future work
not specified here. The course teaches the writing-movable skills; it does not teach
subject content (history, etc.), which the AP courses must supply.
