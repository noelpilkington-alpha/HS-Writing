## TOPIC 1 — Why Teenagers Are So Tired: Sleep and School Start Times

### 1A. Full Passage (~640 words)

Almost every teenager knows the feeling: the alarm goes off, the room is still dark, and getting out of bed feels nearly impossible. For a long time, many adults assumed that sleepy teenagers were simply lazy or staying up too late by choice. Researchers who study sleep, however, have found a different explanation. The science of sleep shows that being tired in the morning is partly built into the biology of growing up, and it helps explain a debate happening in schools across the country about when the school day should begin.

Sleep experts agree that teenagers need a large amount of sleep. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, teens aged 13 to 18 years should sleep 8 to 10 hours per 24 hours (CDC, 2024). That is more than most adults need. Sleep is not wasted time. While a person sleeps, the brain strengthens memories, the body repairs itself, and energy is restored for the next day. When teenagers do not get enough sleep night after night, they can have trouble concentrating, remembering what they learned, and managing their moods.

The problem is that most teenagers are not getting close to that recommended amount. In a national study of American students, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention reported that the share of high school students who slept fewer than 8 hours on school nights was 72.7% (CDC, report on 2015 survey data). In other words, roughly seven out of every ten high school students were not getting enough sleep. This was not a small or local finding; it described students across the entire United States.

Why is enough sleep so hard for teenagers to get? Part of the answer is the schedule of the school day. The same agency studied how early American schools open their doors. Using national data from the 2011–12 school year, it found that the average start time for public middle and high schools was 8:03 a.m. (CDC, report on 2011–12 survey data). To arrive on time, many students have to wake up well before that, sometimes in the dark, and often after going to bed late because of homework, activities, or jobs.

A group of doctors who specialize in children's health looked closely at this issue. The American Academy of Pediatrics, an organization of pediatricians, concluded that middle and high schools should aim for a starting time of no earlier than 8:30 a.m. (American Academy of Pediatrics, as reported by the CDC). The recommendation was based on the idea that a slightly later start would give teenagers a better chance to get the sleep their bodies need. Yet most schools at the time were starting earlier than that target. The national study covered an estimated 39,700 public schools, which shows how widespread early start times were (CDC, report on 2011–12 survey data).

These findings have led many communities to rethink the school clock. Some districts have pushed their start times later to better match what sleep scientists recommend. Others have kept earlier schedules because of bus routes, after-school sports, and the work schedules of families. The decision is complicated, and this passage does not argue that one choice is correct. What the research makes clear is the basic situation: teenagers need 8 to 10 hours of sleep, most high school students are not getting it, and the typical school day begins earlier than the time experts suggest.

Understanding this information does not make the morning alarm any quieter. But it does change how we explain it. A tired teenager is not necessarily a lazy one. The evidence shows a mismatch between the amount of sleep teenagers need and the schedules they actually live by, and that mismatch is at the center of an ongoing conversation about how schools should be run.

## TOPIC 2 — The Tiny Workers Behind Our Food: Bees and Other Pollinators

### 2A. Full Passage (~620 words)

When most people picture where their food comes from, they imagine farmers, tractors, and fields of crops. They rarely picture a bee. Yet bees and other pollinators do a quiet, essential job that makes much of our food possible. A pollinator is an animal — often an insect — that moves pollen from one flower to another, allowing plants to produce fruits, seeds, and the next generation of plants. Without that simple act, many of the foods we eat every day would become far harder to grow. This passage explains how pollinators support the food supply and why scientists pay close attention to them.

The reach of pollinators into our diet is larger than most people expect. According to the National Park Service, pollinators are responsible for 1 out of 3 bites of food we take each day (National Park Service). That includes many fruits, vegetables, and nuts. Apples, blueberries, almonds, melons, and squash all depend on the work of pollinators to develop properly. Foods like these would not disappear entirely without pollinators, but many would become scarcer, smaller, or more expensive.

The importance of pollinators is not limited to the food on our plates. It extends to plant life across the planet. The National Park Service reports that at least 75% of all flowering plants on Earth are pollinated by insects and animals (National Park Service). Those flowering plants do more than feed people; they also feed wildlife and form the base of many natural ecosystems.

The scale of this work becomes clearer when we count the plants involved. The National Park Service explains that pollinators support more than 1,200 food crops and 180,000 different types of plants (National Park Service). A single bee visiting flowers may seem unimportant, but multiplied across millions of bees and other pollinators, the effect on agriculture is enormous.

That effect can also be measured in money. According to the National Park Service, pollination by honey bees contributed to over $19 billion of crop production in 2010, and other insect pollinators contributed nearly $10 billion of crop production (National Park Service). These figures describe the value that pollinators add to crops grown for sale. They help explain why farmers sometimes rent beehives during the growing season and why the health of pollinators is treated as an agricultural issue, not only a nature issue.

Because pollinators matter so much, scientists watch their populations carefully. Many pollinator species, including some bees, face pressures such as habitat loss, disease, and pesticide exposure. This passage does not argue about which solution is best or who is to blame. Its purpose is to explain the role pollinators play. The evidence is consistent across sources: pollinators touch a large share of our food, the majority of the world's flowering plants, and billions of dollars in crops.

The next time you bite into an apple or a handful of almonds, it is worth remembering the path that food traveled. Long before it reached a store, a small animal moved pollen between flowers so that the plant could bear fruit. The bees and other pollinators that perform this task are easy to overlook, but the numbers show how much of our food depends on their work. Understanding that connection is the first step toward understanding why so many people are paying attention to the health of pollinators today.
