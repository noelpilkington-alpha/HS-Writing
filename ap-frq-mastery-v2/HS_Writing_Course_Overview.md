# High School Writing — Course Overview

A standalone writing course that picks up where AlphaWrite Read caps (grade 8) and
builds the writing floor students need before AP — gated to **STAAR English I/II**
and extending to **ACT Writing** readiness and **downstream AP FRQ** preparation.
Delivered on the existing AlphaWrite essay engine, AI-graded. This overview shows
the evidence behind the skills, the skills, the outline, the engine, and the full
40-lesson map.

> **Design overview — a net-new course.** No high-school writing course is in
> production today (students have only the truncated AP-textbook courses). The
> *design* is complete: lesson map, dual STAAR + CCSS standards crosswalk, passage
> spec, and verified assessment specs. Lesson content is the next build step. The
> curriculum team builds content + assessment specs; the dev team owns lesson
> delivery, the AI grader, and the tests.

## 1. The evidence behind the skills

The course targets exactly what the **official STAAR English I/II rubrics** and the
**ACT Writing rubric** reward — verified verbatim against TEA and ACT sources, and
cross-checked against six released STAAR forms. The targeting is rubric-driven, not
guesswork.

**What the STAAR English I/II writing task actually is.** A **passage-based**
Extended Constructed Response (ECR), scored on a 5-point rubric: **Organization &
Development of Ideas (0–3)** + **Conventions (0–2)**. Across six released forms
(2023–25), every ECR has been **informational or analytical-response-to-text** —
read a passage, form a controlling idea about its content, support it with explained
text evidence. (Argumentative is an eligible mode but hasn't appeared on a released
ECR; the course teaches it as a secondary mode and for ACT.)

**Where the points are won and lost (the rubric's own levers):**

| Rubric lever | Score 3 | Score 2 | Score 1 |
|--------------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Evidence explanation** (the dominant lever) | "clearly explained" | "insufficiently explained" | explanation "insufficient" |
| Controlling idea | "fully developed" | "partially developed" | "evident but not developed" |
| Paired-passage evidence | from **both** texts | from **at least one** | from **only one** |
| Organization (the floor) | effective intro + conclusion | intro + conclusion present | missing one or both |

The single most common down-move in the released scoring guides is *"instead of
explaining the evidence…"* — a student drops a quote without saying how it supports
the idea. **That is the skill this course is built to fix.**

**What the ACT Writing rubric rewards** (4 domains, 2–12): across all four, the
top-vs-middle gap is **analysis and integration** — *engaging and evaluating* the
three given perspectives (not restating them), *qualifying* the argument, and
conveying *significance* through an integrated line of reasoning. Grammar only gates
the bottom.

**The four evidence streams:**

| Source | What it told us | How the course responds |
|--------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| **STAAR English I/II rubrics** (verbatim, TEA) | The ECR is passage-based; the 3-vs-2 lever is *explaining evidence*; thesis is rarely the problem | Reasoning-first sequence; "explain the evidence" is the through-line; thesis taught light, as a refinement |
| **6 released STAAR forms** (TEA answer keys) | The ECR is informational/analytical-response, never correspondence; argumentative is eligible-but-unseen | Informational primary; argumentative secondary (kept for ACT + hedge); no correspondence thread |
| **ACT Writing rubric** (verbatim, ACT) | Top score = evaluate multiple perspectives + qualify + significance, not grammar | A dedicated multi-perspective unit aimed at those levers; timing added late |
| **Real AP FRQ failure data** (Timeback) | Students reach AP unable to explain evidence, synthesize, or analyze perspectives — the writing-movable rows | The bridge builds exactly those before AP; content-dependent AP rows are out of scope |

**Honest boundary.** This is a writing course, not a content course. It teaches *how*
to deploy and explain evidence, synthesize sources, and analyze perspectives — not
*which* historical or scientific facts are true. Content-dependent AP rows (sourcing,
complexity, outside evidence) are out of scope by design; the AP courses supply that.

## 2. The skills

Two kinds: **through-line skills** that run through every lesson and grow, and the
**unit skills** each unit installs. (Full detail in the companion **Skill Map**.)

**Through-line skills:** read for the idea · form a controlling idea · **explain the
evidence** · structure the response · self-assess against the rubric · control
conventions in context · manage time.

**The three thinking lenses** (how the skills are taught — consolidated to the few
that are load-bearing):

| Lens | The question it trains |
|------|------------------------|
| Structure → Function → Effect | "What is it, what does it do, why does it matter?" (the engine of explaining evidence) |
| Claim hierarchy | "Where does this fit in the argument's structure?" (the line of reasoning) |
| The function question | "What is this doing here / why this move?" (how to read a passage well enough to write about it) |

## 3. The course outline

A G8-to-AP sliding ramp. Each stage is a real grade level with its own gate; a
student progresses through writing proficiency, not a single one-shot target.

```
English I (G9)  →  English II (G10)  →  ACT Writing readiness  →  downstream AP FRQ prep
```

Every essay is a version of the same moves — read the passage, form a controlling
idea, explain the evidence, structure it — and only the passage, the mode, and the
support level change.

| Unit | Focus | By the end, the student can… | Builds toward |
|------|-------|------------------------------|---------------|
| **EI · 1** | Reading-to-write bridge | Reason from evidence, read a passage for its idea, form a controlling idea | the floor |
| **EI · 2** | Expository response-to-text | Write a full informational response to a passage with explained evidence | **STAAR EI (primary mode)** |
| **EI · 3** | Argumentative response-to-text | Take and defend a position; respond to a counterargument | STAAR EI (secondary), ACT |
| **EI · 4** | Conventions + short responses | Control sentence boundaries; combine sentences; answer + cite evidence | STAAR EI SCRs + gate |
| **EII · 5** | Synthesis across sources | Weave a position across paired/multiple passages | **STAAR EII (top band)** |
| **EII · 6** | Argument at depth | Qualify a position; engage and rebut opposing views | STAAR EII, ACT |
| **EII · 7** | Multi-perspective (ACT) | Analyze three perspectives and stake a qualified position (timed) | **ACT Writing readiness** |
| **EII · 8** | Timed performance + gate | Pace and self-diagnose under the clock | STAAR EII + ACT gate |

**Why this order:** reasoning before the controlling idea (thesis is already a
strength); expository before argumentative (the primary STAAR mode first);
single-passage before synthesis (sources are a layer on top); multi-perspective and
timing last (only once the moves are automatic). Reading is integrated throughout —
every passage-based rep starts with a comprehension pre-read scored separately, so a
misread routes to re-read support, not to a writing fix.

## 4. The AlphaWrite engine + the practice loop

Every lesson is **one piece of writing** run through the engine's stages — **Setup →
Outline → Draft → Revise → Polish → Submit** — on the paragraph-writing architecture
(early, faster reps) or the essay-writing architecture (the core). The ★ frontier
stage carries that unit's heaviest instruction.

| Stage | What happens |
|-------|--------------|
| **Setup** | Read the source passage; comprehension pre-read (scored separately) |
| **Outline** | Structured form — controlling-idea box, body cards with evidence + "explain how" sub-prompts |
| **Draft** | Compose: cite evidence and explain how it supports the idea |
| **Revise** | AI-validated checklist, **meaning before surface** (a broken argument can't be polished) |
| **Polish** | Conventions in context; (English II) diction/tone for effect |
| **Submit** | AI-graded on the real rubric → student rewrites the single weakest part and re-scores |

Instruction lives *inside* the cycle: the **AI Tutor** teaches the move and models it
with a deliberate false start → correction; **inline Tips** and **gated
discrimination** ("which is stronger?") precede production; the **rescore-the-weakest-
part loop** turns one essay into 2–3 graded reps. Scaffolds fade Heavy → Medium →
Light → None across each unit; timing enters only at the end. Advancement is by
**cold, single-attempt gates** on reserved passages; calibration (self-score vs
grader) is coached but does not gate.

## 5. The lesson map (~40 lessons)

English I = 20 lessons (frontier = Draft, the explain-the-evidence move). English II
= 20 lessons (frontier = Outline, the synthesis matrix / perspective map). Each
lesson introduces one new move; consolidation and gate lessons add none. Every gate
maps to a real STAAR/ACT rubric row.

### English I (G9)

**Unit 1 · Reading-to-Write Foundations**
- **1.1 Reason, not restate** — write the sentence that explains *how* evidence supports a point *(Draft ★ · Heavy · gated restate-vs-reason sort)*
- **1.2 Read for the idea** — comprehension pre-read: what is the passage saying? *(Setup · Heavy)*
- **1.3 Cite + explain** — pull text evidence and explain how it supports an idea *(Draft · Heavy→Med)*
- **1.4 Controlling idea from a passage** — refine a response into a clear controlling idea *(Outline · Medium)*
- **1.5 Bridge gate** — short expository response-to-text, cold *(Light · gate: Thesis + Evidence + Reasoning)*

**Unit 2 · Expository Response-to-Text** *(primary STAAR mode)*
- **2.1 Structure the response** — Outline form: controlling idea → body cards → conclusion *(Outline · Medium)*
- **2.2 Explain the evidence to standard** — every body reaches the "this shows / because" link *(Draft ★ · gated summary-vs-explanation sort)*
- **2.3 Revise meaning before surface** — altitude-ordered checklist *(Revise · Medium)*
- **2.4 Full expository ECR, cold** — assemble end to end *(Light · gate: full STAAR Informational ECR)*

**Unit 3 · Argumentative Response-to-Text**
- **3.1 Defensible position on the issue** *(Outline · Heavy · gated weak/strong-thesis sort)*
- **3.2 Line of reasoning** — sub-claims that build, not list *(Outline · gated build-vs-list sort)*
- **3.3 Counterargument** — acknowledge / concede / **respond** *(Outline→Draft · Medium)*
- **3.4 Evidence accuracy + integration** — name precisely; weave, don't drop *(Draft · Medium)*
- **3.5 Full argumentative ECR, cold** *(Light · gate: full STAAR Argumentative ECR)*

**Unit 4 · Conventions, Short Responses & Gate**
- **4.1 Sentence boundaries** — fix run-ons & fragments in context *(Polish · Medium)*
- **4.2 Sentence-combining** (writing SCR) *(gated weak/strong combine items)*
- **4.3 Reading SCR** — answer accurately **+** cite text evidence *(Medium)*
- **4.4 Revising/editing** — strengthen a draft's development + conventions *(Medium)*
- **4.5 Pre-gate full ECR + self-score** *(Light)*
- **4.6 ENGLISH I GATE** — cold passage-based ECR (informational/analytical primary) + writing SCR + reading SCR *(None · gate)*

### English II (G10)

**Unit 5 · Synthesis Across Sources** *(STAAR EII top band)*
- **5.1 Source stance** — read paired/multiple sources; evaluate credibility/bias *(Setup · Heavy)*
- **5.2 Serial vs synthesis + the synthesis matrix** *(Outline ★ · gated serial-vs-synthesis sort)*
- **5.3 Thesis across sources** — a position that answers across the set *(Outline · Medium)*
- **5.4 Weave sources** — idea + source → one sentence; evidence from *all* texts *(Draft · Heavy on move)*
- **5.5 Revise: synthesis not summary** *(Revise · Medium)*
- **5.6 Full synthesis essay, cold** *(Light · gate: STAAR EII synthesis ECR)*

**Unit 6 · Argument at Depth**
- **6.1 Nuance ladder** — flat position → qualified *(Outline · Heavy · gated binary-vs-nuanced sort)*
- **6.2 Engage opposing views** (They Say / I Say) *(Outline→Draft · Medium)*
- **6.3 Concede + rebut** — genuine, not strawman *(Draft · Medium)*
- **6.4 Full nuanced argument, cold** *(Light)*

**Unit 7 · Multi-Perspective / ACT Readiness** *(timing enters)*
- **7.1 Decompose 3 perspectives** — claim / assumption / value / blind spot *(Setup · Heavy)*
- **7.2 Stake your position relative to the perspectives** *(Outline ★ · gated engage-vs-restate sort)*
- **7.3 Qualify + integrated reasoning** — convey significance *(Draft · Medium)*
- **7.4 Timed ACT-style essay #1 + self-score** *(Light · Timed)*
- **7.5 Timed ACT-style essay #2 + calibration** *(None · Timed)*

**Unit 8 · Timed Performance & Gate**
- **8.1 Timing strategy** — 5-min plan → essay *(Medium · Timed)*
- **8.2 Self-diagnosis** — find and fix your weakest row under time *(Revise · Timed)*
- **8.3 Pre-gate synthesis (timed)** *(Light · Timed)*
- **8.4 Pre-gate ACT-style (timed)** *(Light · Timed)*
- **8.5 ENGLISH II GATE** — cold synthesis ECR (STAAR) + cold ACT-style essay (readiness) + conventions *(None · gate)*

## 6. The gates (the verifiable outcomes)

| Gate | Task | Pass bar | Aligns to |
|------|------|----------|-----------|
| **Bridge (1.5)** | short expository response-to-text | Thesis + Evidence + Reasoning earned | floor check |
| **English I (4.6)** | cold passage-based ECR + writing SCR + reading SCR | ECR Trait 1 ≥2 & Trait 2 ≥1; both SCRs earned | **STAAR English I** |
| **English II (8.5)** | cold synthesis ECR + cold ACT-style essay + conventions | synthesis ECR Trait 1 ≥2 & Trait 2 ≥1; ACT essay clears target on ≥3 of 4 domains | **STAAR English II** + **ACT readiness** |

All gates are single-attempt on reserved passages held out of instruction, so a pass
is genuine cold transfer. The rescore loop runs on practice reps only, never on a
gate.
