PART I: SHARED FRAMEWORK

This section defines principles, structures, and progressions shared across all four courses. Each course is a standalone Timeback course with its own OneRoster enrollment, but they share this common architecture.

1. Purpose

These four courses, organized into two tracks, take students who have completed AlphaWrite G8 (mastery through C.8.04, M.8.03, D.8.01) and develop them into writers capable of producing sophisticated analytical prose under timed, prompt-constrained conditions -- the conditions of AP exams, ACT Writing, college writing, and professional life.

Two Tracks

Track A: Core Writing (G9-G10) -- A complete, standalone writing program aligned to CCSS and ACT Writing standards. All students take Track A. Students who complete Track A are college-ready analytical writers prepared for the ACT Writing exam.

Track B: AP Writing (G11-G12) -- An advanced, optional track for students pursuing AP Language or AP Literature. Requires completion of Track A (or diagnostic entry). Students who complete Track B target AP scores of 5+.

Track Course Grade Standards
A (Core) A1: Analytical Writing Foundations G9 CCSS W.9-10
A (Core) A2: Argument, Synthesis & ACT Writing G10 CCSS W.9-10 + ACT Writing
B (AP) B1: AP Essay Writing G11 AP Rubric (target 4+)
B (AP) B2: AP Mastery & Sophistication G12 AP Rubric (target 5+)

Track A is NOT merely "preparation for AP." It is a complete writing education. Students who complete A2 and do not continue to Track B have mastered analytical argument, synthesis, and ACT Writing -- skills that transfer to college writing, professional communication, and standardized testing.

The courses are designed for Alpha School's 2 Hour Learning model: maximum learning in minimum time. Every minute has a purpose. No filler, no redundant practice, no instruction on skills students have already mastered.

3. Efficiency Principles (2 Hour Learning)

  • Minimum effective dose. Research shows 15 hours of focused strategy instruction produces massive effects (g = 4.86-6.98, Ray & Graham 2020). Each course is 8-12 hours because it targets one grade band of progression, not because any single skill needs extensive drilling.
  • Diagnostic-driven, not coverage-driven. Students skip what they already know. A strong writer may test into Course A2 directly.
  • Plans over essays. Essay planning exercises almost all the cognitive work of essay writing at 1/5 the time cost. The 4:2:1 ratio (plans : paragraphs : full essays) maximizes analytical reps per hour.
  • 5-item discrimination, not 10. If a student gets 5/5, they've demonstrated the skill. No padding.
  • 3-minute instruction, not 15. Teach the move concisely, show one coping model example, then practice. The practice IS the learning.
  • PowerPath handles spaced review. No explicit review hours -- advanced items implicitly practice earlier skills through encompassings.

4. The Analytical Thinking Layer: Mental Models

Why Mental Models Matter

The biggest gap in most writing instruction: students are asked to write analytically about texts they cannot read analytically. The Mental Models bridge this gap. They are reading/thinking lenses -- cognitive frameworks that teach students HOW to see what writers do, so they can write about it.

Mental Models are necessary but not sufficient. They answer "how should I think?" The Knowledge Components answer "how should I write?" Together, they produce analytical writers.

5. Instructional Framework

The Lesson Shell (SRSD-Aligned, Compressed for 2 Hour Learning)

Every lesson follows a 5-phase structure. Instruction is concise -- 3-5 minutes max for Teach and Model phases. The majority of time is spent on practice.

Phase Label Time What Happens
1. Teach "Here's the move" 2-3 min Brief instruction via stimulus. One rule, one example, one check question
2. Model "Watch the thinking" 2-3 min One worked example with coping modeling (false start -> correction). Not a perfect demo
3. Supported Practice "Try with support" 5-8 min Student practices with checklist/rubric visible. 3-5 items
4. Independent Practice "Now you" 5-10 min Same task type, no scaffold. Scored. Wise feedback
5. Transfer Bridge "Now use it" 3-5 min Apply skill in a more integrated context (sentence -> paragraph -> plan)

Total per lesson: ~15-25 minutes.

Force-Correct Mechanism

Wrong answer -> brief feedback explaining WHY -> "Try again" -> reset after ~1.2 seconds -> student must select correct answer. No passive consumption.

The 4:2:1 Practice Ratio

Writing practice follows this ratio throughout all courses:

  • 4 Essay Plans (5-10 min each): Sketch argument structure, identify evidence, note thesis and conclusion.
  • 2 Single Paragraphs to Exam Standard (5-7 min each): One claim, evidence, analysis, connection to thesis.
  • 1 Full Essay (35-45 min): Sustained argument under time pressure.

Transfer Bridges

After every skill cluster, a brief exercise connects the micro-skill to more integrated writing:

  • Analytical sentence drill -> "Now write a paragraph using 2 of those sentences"
  • Thesis crafting drill -> "Now find the thesis in this published essay" (reading-writing reciprocal)
  • Planning tool practice -> "Now convert this plan into one body paragraph"

Transfer bridges prevent the common failure mode where students master isolated drills but cannot deploy skills in full essays.

6. Motivation Architecture (HS-Adapted)

1. Competence as Primary Motivator (SPOV 8: Achievement Causes Motivation)

  • Students track progress against rubric criteria via grade-level gates
  • Each course begins and ends with a scored writing sample -- visible growth is the primary motivator
  • Mastery of challenging analytical tasks conveys evidence of enhanced competence (Bandura)

2. Status and Intellectual Respect (Yeager)

  • The mentor mindset governs all feedback: "I have high standards, and I believe you can meet them"
  • Passing each grade gate is a visible achievement with social weight
  • Analytical writing competitions and showcases (opt-in) create authentic stakes

3. Analytical Autonomy

  • Students cannot choose the passage (the exam doesn't let them)
  • Students CAN choose: their interpretive angle, which evidence to foreground, which complication to engage
  • The course makes this explicit: "You don't choose the passage. You choose what it means."

4. Wise Feedback

All feedback follows Yeager's formula: - "I have high standards, and I believe you can meet them" - Specific, actionable, using shared analytical vocabulary - Never: "This is wrong." Always: "Here's how to sharpen this analysis."

5. Coping Modeling (Critical)

  • All worked examples include false starts, weak drafts revised to strong ones
  • "Watch me struggle with this passage, then find a way in" -- not "Watch me produce a perfect essay"
  • Builds self-efficacy more effectively than flawless demonstrations (Garcia-Sanchez & Fidalgo-Redondo, 2006)

9. Assessment Philosophy

No MCQs

These courses assess writing through writing. All assessment items require production (extended-text, planning, paragraph sprints) or discrimination of writing quality (sorting, matching, hottext identification). No multiple-choice questions.

Discrimination Items (Not MCQs)

Discrimination exercises use QTI item types that require analytical judgment, not answer-guessing: - Hottext: Highlight the sentence that contains analysis (vs. summary) - Match: Match essay excerpts to structure types - Order: Arrange argument elements in logical sequence - Inline-choice: Select the stronger thesis from embedded options - Gap-match: Match evidence to the claims they support

Formative Assessment (Ongoing)

  • Discrimination checks (5 items per KC)
  • Planning assessments (most frequent -- per the 4:2:1 ratio)
  • Paragraph sprints (second most frequent)
  • Self-assessment calibration exercises (every course)

Summative Assessment (Per Course)

  • Gate assessment at the end of each course
  • Scored on the rubric appropriate to that course's calibration level
  • Must pass to advance to next course

10. What These Courses Must NOT Do

  1. No assigning essays without teaching the analytical moves. "Write a rhetorical analysis" without teaching how to connect strategy to purpose is malpractice at any level.
  2. No feature-spotting accepted as analysis. "The author uses a metaphor" is not analysis. The function/purpose connection is non-negotiable.
  3. No one-size-fits-all essay structure. The 5-paragraph essay is a scaffold, not a destination.
  4. No production before discrimination. Students must SEE weak vs. strong analysis before they produce it.
  5. No serial summarizing accepted as synthesis. "Source A says... Source B says..." is not synthesis.
  6. No full essays as the primary practice format. The 4:2:1 ratio maximizes analytical reps per hour.
  7. No mastery-only modeling. All worked examples include false starts and revision. Coping modeling builds self-efficacy.
  8. No feedback without shared vocabulary. All feedback uses specific terms: "thesis," "line of reasoning," "commentary," "evidence integration," "sophistication."
  9. No padding. If a student demonstrates mastery in 5 items, do not require 10. Every minute must earn its place.
  10. No wasted instruction. 3 minutes to teach a move, not 15. The practice IS the learning.

11. What These Courses Are NOT

  • NOT a literature survey course. Passages are tools for practicing analytical writing, not ends in themselves.
  • NOT "AP test prep" in the narrow sense. Students develop genuine analytical capability that transfers beyond any single exam.
  • NOT a creative writing course. The focus is analytical and argumentative prose.
  • NOT a reading comprehension course. Close reading is taught IN SERVICE of analytical writing, not as a standalone skill.

Mental Models Introduced: M1 + M2

M1: Everything Is a Choice

Core idea: Nothing in a published text is accidental. Word choice, sentence length, paragraph breaks, what's included, what's omitted -- all deliberate, all strategic, all chosen.

The shift: From "the author talks about X" to "the author CHOSE to do X for a reason."

How it's taught: Through the Choice Audit exercise. Students take a passage element and ask: What did the author choose? What could they have done instead? Why this choice?

Element What the Author Chose What They Could Have Done Instead Why This Choice?
Opening Personal anecdote Statistics, quotation, question Creates emotional investment before presenting data
Word: "ruts" Suggests wear, habit, erosion "tracks," "path," "footprints" Carries ambiguity -- dedication AND stuckness
Structure Triple parallel ("too far, too shaded, too small") Single inadequacy Rhythm of dismissal makes the reversal land harder

M2: Structure -> Function -> Effect (S-F-E)

Core idea: Every analytical observation should move through three levels: - Structure: What is it? (Name the technique or element) - Function: What does it do? (How does it serve the argument at this point?) - Effect: Why does it matter? (Impact on audience or argument's persuasive power)

The shift: From "The author uses a metaphor" (Structure only) to "The metaphor of the sinking ship (S) frames economic decline as an emergency requiring immediate collective action (F), positioning the audience as passengers who must act NOW rather than wait -- which serves the author's purpose of urging policy change before the next fiscal quarter (E)."

How it's taught: Through the S-F-E Chain drill. Every analytical sentence students write must be expressible as:

[Element] (Structure) serves to [role in the argument] (Function) which [impact on audience/persuasion] (Effect).

Why these two models come first: M1 teaches students to SEE choices. M2 gives them a PROCEDURE for analyzing those choices. Together, they produce the core analytical sentence -- the building block of all analytical writing.

Close Reading Foundation

Before students can write analytically, they must read analytically. This cluster teaches close reading as a systematic practice, not a vague instruction to "read carefully."

The Close Reading Protocol

  1. First read: Gist. What is the text about? What is the author's position? (2-3 minutes)
  2. Second read: Choices. Apply M1 -- mark 5+ deliberate choices with annotations. (3-4 minutes)
  3. Third read: The prompt. Re-read with the specific writing task in mind. Which choices are most relevant to YOUR argument? (2-3 minutes)

This protocol becomes automatic by Course B1 (AP level). In Course A1, it is explicitly taught and practiced.

Exercise Types

Argumentative Writing Foundations (~2.5 hrs)

  1. Expository vs. Argumentative Sort (Discrimination, 5 items): Sort paragraphs into expository or argumentative. Identify the signal: does it explain or persuade?
  2. Position Development (Production, 3 reps): Given broad topics, narrow each to a defensible position. Apply 3-test checklist (debatable? specific? supportable?). Write the strongest opposing position.
  3. Evidence + Reasoning Drill (Production, 2 reps): Given a claim, generate 3 pieces of evidence from knowledge. For each, write one sentence of reasoning explaining WHY it supports the claim.
  4. Counterargument Response (Production, 2 reps): Given a position + strongest counterargument, write a response paragraph using the 3-move structure: Acknowledge, Concede, Respond.
  5. Full Argument Essay (Timed, 1 rep): Choose from 3 debatable prompts. Plan (8 min) + write (20 min). Self-score on 4-point checklist (clear position? evidence + reasoning? counterargument addressed? organized?).

Bridge: From Own Choices to Author's Choices (~0.5 hrs)

  1. Analyze Your Own Choices (Production, 1 rep): Take your argument essay from Unit 1. Identify 3 deliberate choices you made (word choice, opening move, structure, evidence selected). For each: what did you choose, what could you have done instead, why this choice?
  2. Apply to a Short Text (Production, 1 rep): Read SP-010 (Chief Joseph, ~200 words). Apply the same "why this choice?" questions. Identify 3 choices the author made. Compare: what's the same about analyzing your own writing and analyzing someone else's?

Close Reading & Mental Models (~2 hrs)

  1. Three-Read Practice (Supported, 2 reps): Given a passage (~600 words), execute the close reading protocol. Annotate choices. Compare annotations to model.
  2. Choice Audit (Production, 3 reps): Complete a Choice Audit table for a passage. Identify element, what was chosen, alternative, why this choice. Compare to model.
  3. S-F-E Chain Drill (Production, 5 reps): Given a passage element, write a complete S-F-E chain. Each rep uses a different element type (diction, syntax, evidence type, structure, tone).
  4. The One-Level Test (Discrimination, 5 items): Read analytical statements. Identify whether each reaches Structure only, Structure + Function, or all three levels. Extend incomplete chains.

Essay Architecture (~1.5 hrs)

  1. Structure Sort (Discrimination, 5 items): Read essay excerpts, classify by structure type (classical, Rogerian, problem-solution, comparison, cause-effect).
  2. Purpose-Structure Match (5 items): Given prompts with different audiences/purposes, select and justify the best structure.
  3. Architecture Sketch (Production, 2 plans): Given a prompt, outline using a non-5-paragraph structure. Include thesis placement, paragraph functions, evidence plan.

Thesis Development (~2 hrs)

  1. Thesis Triage (Discrimination, 5 items): Sort thesis statements into Strong / Weak / Fixable. Explain why.
  2. The "So What?" Chain (Production, 3 reps): Start vague -> apply "So what?" three times -> arrive at strong thesis.
  3. Thesis from Passage (Timed production, 3 reps): Given a passage, generate a defensible thesis in 3 minutes. Uses Choice Audit and S-F-E as input.
  4. Counter-Thesis Test (Production, 2 reps): Write thesis + strongest opposing thesis. If you can't oppose it, it's not arguable.

Evidence & Commentary (~2 hrs)

  1. Analysis vs. Summary Sort (Discrimination, 5 items): Sort analytical sentences into "analysis" vs. "summary." Revise the summaries using S-F-E.
  2. The Analytical Sentence Drill (Production, 5 reps): Practice the S-F-E frame: "[Author]'s use of [strategy] (S) serves to [function] (F), thereby [effect on audience/argument] (E)."
  3. Body Paragraph Sprint (Timed production, 3 reps): Write one analytical body paragraph in 7 minutes. Must include: topic sentence (claim), evidence with attribution, S-F-E commentary, connection to thesis.
  4. Transfer Bridge -- Paragraph to Plan (Production, 1 rep): Convert 3 body paragraph topic sentences into a full essay plan with thesis, structure, and evidence map.

Self-Assessment & Revision (~1 hr)

  1. Score-Then-Compare (Discrimination, 3 reps): Read a sample paragraph. Score it as "analysis" or "summary." Then see the model answer and explanation. Track accuracy.
  2. Revise Summary into Analysis (Production, 3 reps): Given a summary sentence, rewrite it as an analytical sentence using S-F-E. Compare to model revision.
  3. Revise Own Work (Production, 1 rep): Take your most recent body paragraph sprint. Identify one sentence that is summary. Rewrite it as analysis.

Style Basics (~1 hr)

  1. Sentence Rhythm Analysis (Discrimination, 3 items): Map sentence lengths in a published analytical paragraph. Identify emphasis effects. Apply M1: "Why did the author choose this sentence length here?"
  2. Sentence Imitation (Production, 3 reps): Imitate syntactic structures from published criticism.
  3. Wordiness Detection + Revision (5 items + 3 revision reps): Cut 20%+ without losing meaning.

M3: Rhetorical Situation as Force Field

Core idea: The rhetorical situation is not a checklist (speaker, audience, purpose). It is a set of forces that CONSTRAIN and ENABLE the writer's choices. Every choice the author makes exists within a force field of pressures.

The shift: From "The audience is parents" (checklist) to "The audience is parents who already believe education matters but doubt THIS reform -- so the author can skip the 'education is important' argument and go directly to addressing skepticism about implementation" (force field).

The four forces: - Audience determines what evidence persuades, what tone is appropriate, what can be assumed - Exigence (the urgent gap that prompted the text) determines what the writer must accomplish - Writer's position determines what moves are available (a doctor asserts; a patient narrates) - Context determines what goes without saying and what must be made explicit

How it's taught: Through the Constraint Test: "What COULDN'T the author do?" This forces students out of checklist mode. Every constraint explains a choice.

Why here: Course A2 teaches sophisticated argument (They Say/I Say) and ACT Writing. Students need M3 to understand WHY different audiences require different argument strategies. M3 also feeds directly into source evaluation and ACT perspective analysis -- understanding whose perspective a source or position represents.

M4: The Claim Hierarchy

Core idea: Arguments are nested structures, not flat assertions.

THESIS (the overarching position)
  |
  +-- CLAIM 1 (a supporting assertion)
  |     +-- EVIDENCE (specific support)
  |     +-- COMMENTARY (interpretation connecting evidence to claim)
  |
  +-- CLAIM 2
  |     +-- EVIDENCE
  |     +-- COMMENTARY
  |
  +-- COUNTERCLAIM (opposition addressed)
  |     +-- CONCESSION (what the writer grants)
  |     +-- REBUTTAL (why the thesis still holds)
  |
  +-- CLAIM 3
        +-- EVIDENCE
        +-- COMMENTARY

The shift: From "The author thinks X" (flat) to "The author's thesis is X, supported by claims Y and Z, with evidence A and B, and a concession to counterargument C" (hierarchical).

How it's taught: Through Hierarchy Mapping. Students map published arguments into the tree structure, then build their own.

Why here: Course A2 teaches They Say/I Say moves, synthesis, and ACT Writing. All three require students to see arguments as nested structures -- to distinguish thesis from claim from evidence from commentary, and to position their own argument within an existing conversation (or within a set of given perspectives, as in the ACT).

M5 Preview: "If I Were Writing This"

Core idea (preview only): When stuck analyzing a passage, ask: "If I were writing this essay, why would I have made this move at this point?"

This technique is previewed in Course A2 as a problem-solving tool for argument analysis. It is fully developed in Course B1 as the primary AP reading strategy.

The Conversation Frame (Graff & Birkenstein)

Course A2 introduces the foundational insight from "They Say / I Say": all academic writing is entering a conversation. You must represent what others say before presenting your own view. This frame carries through Courses B1 and B2, and is directly applied in the ACT Writing exam where students must engage with given perspectives.

The three core moves: 1. They Say: Accurately represent the existing position ("Many people assume that...") 2. I Say: Present your own response ("However, I would argue that...") 3. The Naysayer: Anticipate and address objections ("Some might object that... but...")

Mental Model Introduced: M5 -- Reading as Reverse Engineering

Core idea: Reading AP passages is writing in reverse. The writer started with a purpose, chose a structure, selected evidence, crafted style, and produced a text. Your job as a reader is to start with the finished text and RECOVER the decisions that built it.

The shift: From reading for content/summary to reading as active investigation. Every paragraph is an answer to the question: "What problem was the writer solving at this point in the argument?"

The "If I Were Writing This" technique: When stuck on any analytical task, ask: "If I were writing this essay, why would I have made this move at this point?" This converts reading comprehension into writing empathy.

How it's taught: Through the Decision Reconstruction exercise. For each paragraph, students write: "The writer's problem at this point was , and they solved it by ."

Why here: M5 is the INTEGRATION model. It deploys M1 (choices), M2 (S-F-E), M3 (force field), and M4 (hierarchy) simultaneously on AP passages under time pressure. Students have been building these models across Track A (Courses A1 and A2). Now they integrate them into a single, fluid analytical process.

Mental Model Introduced: M6 -- Who Benefits?

Core idea: For any argument or rhetorical move, ask: Who benefits from the audience accepting this claim? Whose interests are served by framing the issue this way? What is made visible by this framing -- and what is obscured?

This is NOT cynicism. "Who Benefits?" doesn't mean "every writer is lying." It means every writer has a perspective, and that perspective shapes which evidence gets included, which counterarguments get addressed, which audiences get centered, and which framings feel natural. Recognizing perspective isn't an accusation -- it's an analytical move.

How M6 sharpens every previous model:

Previous Model Without M6 With M6
M1: Everything Is a Choice "The author chose to open with a statistic" "Opening with a statistic rather than a personal story foregrounds data over lived experience -- which benefits an argument that wants to appear objective"
M2: S-F-E "The formal tone creates authority" "The formal tone creates authority but distances the author from the affected community -- a trade-off that benefits credibility with policymakers at the cost of solidarity"
M3: Force Field "The audience is policymakers" "Writing for policymakers explains why human cost is translated into economic terms -- the framing this audience responds to, even though it reduces people to budget lines"
M4: Hierarchy "The author concedes implementation is difficult" "The concession frames difficulty as logistical, not structural -- which benefits the argument by making the obstacle seem solvable within existing systems"

How it's taught: Through the Framing Analysis exercise: (1) What does this framing make visible? (2) What does it obscure? (3) Who benefits? (4) What alternative framing exists?

Why here: M6 is what earns the AP Sophistication point. It produces the "broader context," "alternative interpretation," and "complexity/tension" moves that distinguish a 5 from a 6. Students who can ask "Who Benefits?" write with the critical depth the AP exam rewards.

Exercise Types

The Sophistication Point (~2.5 hrs)

The AP Sophistication point (Row C) is where most students plateau. It requires ONE of: - Situating in broader context - Acknowledging an alternative interpretation and explaining why yours is more persuasive - Identifying and exploring a complexity or tension - Writing with consistently vivid and persuasive style

  1. Framing Analysis (Production, 3 reps): For a passage, complete the M6 protocol: What does the framing make visible? Obscure? Who benefits? Alternative framing? How does this deepen your analysis?
  2. Sophistication Move Identification (Discrimination, 5 items): Read essays scored 5 vs. 6. Identify what the 6 does that the 5 doesn't. Name the specific move type.
  3. Broader Context Drill (Production, 2 reps): Take an existing analysis paragraph. Add a sentence situating it in broader historical, cultural, or literary context. Must feel organic, not tacked on.
  4. Alternative Reading Drill (Production, 2 reps): Write a paragraph acknowledging an alternative interpretation, then explain why yours is stronger. Use the frame: "An alternative reading might suggest... However, [your reading] is more persuasive because..."
  5. Tension/Complexity Drill (Production, 2 reps): Identify a tension in a passage that resists simple resolution. Write 3 sentences exploring it without resolving it. This IS the sophistication.
  6. Transfer Bridge -- Sophistication in Full Essay (Production, 1 rep): Write a full essay plan that includes a specific sophistication move. Indicate where in the essay it goes and which type it is.

Timed Performance (~3 hrs)

  1. Speed Runs (3 full essays at AP timing): Focus on maintaining quality under pressure. Each scored on AP rubric with specific feedback on WHERE quality dropped under time pressure (thesis? evidence? analysis? final paragraphs?).
  2. Plan-to-Essay Bridge (2 reps): Write a plan in 8 minutes, then the full essay in 32 minutes. Practice converting plans to prose efficiently. The plan must include a sophistication move.
  3. Recovery Drill (1 rep): Deliberately start an essay 10 minutes late. Practice the "minimum viable essay" -- what must be present for a passing score even when time is short.

Self-Diagnosis & Revision (~2 hrs)

  1. Expert Calibration (Discrimination, 4 reps): Score sample AP essays on all three rubric rows. By this course, calibration accuracy should be 80%+.
  2. Diagnose Your Own Work (Production, 2 reps): Score your own most recent essay. Identify the specific rubric row where improvement would have the biggest impact. Write a 2-sentence diagnosis.
  3. Surface vs. Substantive Sort (Discrimination, 5 items): Sort revision actions into "editing" (surface) vs. "revision" (substantive). Understand the difference.
  4. Multi-Pass Revision Protocol (Production, 2 reps): Apply structured revision to own essay:
  5. Pass 1: Thesis and argument structure (Does the thesis have a defensible line of reasoning?)
  6. Pass 2: Evidence and analysis quality (Does every piece of evidence have S-F-E commentary?)
  7. Pass 3: Sophistication (Add one M6-driven move: broader context, alternative reading, or tension)
  8. Pass 4: Style and concision (Voice, sentence variety, wordiness)
  9. Before/After Comparison (Production, 1 rep): Compare pre-revision and post-revision essays. Score both on AP rubric. Measure improvement.

Voice Development (~1 hr)

  1. Voice Analysis in Published Criticism (Discrimination, 3 items): Read 3 excerpts from different published critics. Identify the syntactic and diction choices that create each critic's distinctive voice. Apply M1: voice as authorial choice.
  2. Voice Experiment (Production, 2 reps): Write the same analytical paragraph in two different voices (e.g., spare and direct vs. elaborate and layered). Evaluate which is more effective for the task.
  3. Consistent Voice Under Pressure (Production, 1 rep): Write a timed body paragraph maintaining your chosen voice. Check: Does your voice hold, or does it collapse into generic academic prose under time pressure?

2. Sentence Frames for Analytical Writing

Students should memorize and internalize these frames. They are "templates as thinking tools" (Graff & Birkenstein) applied to AP-level analysis.

S-F-E Analysis Frames (All Courses)

  • "[Author]'s use of [strategy] (S) serves to [function] (F), which [effect on audience/argument] (E)."
  • "By [technique], [author] [function], thereby [effect]."
  • "The shift from [X] to [Y] signals [function], reinforcing [effect]."
  • "This choice is strategic because [force field reasoning -- M3]."

Rhetorical Analysis Frames (B1: AP Lang)

  • "[Author]'s strategic use of [device] functions to..."
  • "By juxtaposing [X] with [Y], [author] underscores..."
  • "The shift in tone from [X] to [Y] signals..."
  • "This appeal to [ethos/pathos/logos] is particularly effective because [audience reasoning]..."

Literary Analysis Frames (B1: AP Lit)

  • "The recurring image of [X] serves as a symbol for..."
  • "[Author]'s choice to narrate from [perspective] reveals..."
  • "The tension between [X] and [Y] reflects the larger thematic concern of..."
  • "What initially appears to be [surface reading] actually reveals [deeper interpretation]..."

Argument/Synthesis Frames (A2, B1, B2)

  • "While [source/author] argues that [X], a closer examination reveals..."
  • "Although [concession], this does not undermine the claim that [position] because..."
  • "[Source A]'s emphasis on [X] complements [Source B]'s finding that [Y], together suggesting..."
  • "In discussions of [topic], a common assumption is [they say]. However, [I say]..."

Sophistication Frames (B2)

  • "This analysis gains additional complexity when we consider [broader context]..."
  • "An alternative reading might suggest that... However, [your reading] is more persuasive because..."
  • "The tension between [X] and [Y] resists simple resolution, instead pointing to..."
  • "Situated within the broader context of [movement/period], [author]'s choice takes on added significance..."
