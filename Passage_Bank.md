### Counter-Perspective Sourcing Note

The Conversation (CC BY-ND) skews academic/progressive, making counter-perspective sources (pro-testing, pro-surveillance, anti-regulation) difficult to find there. Solution applied:
- **Government policy documents** (public domain) that implicitly present these perspectives (e.g., DOE accountability briefs for pro-testing, Senate surveillance bill summaries for pro-security, CBO cost analyses for regulation-skeptical perspective)
- **PLOS ONE** (CC BY) for balanced research that shows both sides (e.g., Valkenburg et al. on social media)
- **GAO audit reports** that provide fiscal scrutiny without ideological framing
- This approach actually strengthens the synthesis sets pedagogically: students learn that "counter-perspectives" in real policy debates often come from institutional/government sources rather than op-eds

## PHASE 2: SELECTION CRITERIA EVALUATION

### Evaluation Method

Each candidate evaluated against 5 criteria from the Passage Source Curation Spec:
1. **Choice Audit (CA)**: Can you identify 5+ deliberate authorial choices with non-obvious reasons? (Pass/Fail)
2. **Legal (L)**: Public domain, CC licensed, or fair use? (Verified/Edge Case/Reject)
3. **Complexity (CX)**: Matches target course level? (Match/Stretch/Too Easy/Too Hard)
4. **AP Cross-Check (AP)**: Not previously used as a passage in released AP exams? (Clear/Flagged)
5. **Rating**: Strong / Adequate / Reject

## PHASE 3: COURSE UNIT ASSIGNMENTS

### Assignment Principles

1. **Each passage used for its strongest analytical purpose** -- a text assigned to multiple exercises within a course is efficient (students build familiarity), but a text shouldn't be forced into an exercise where it's not the best fit.
2. **Progressive complexity** -- earlier exercises in each course use simpler/shorter texts; later exercises use more complex ones.
3. **No passage appears in both instruction AND gate assessment** -- gate passages must be unseen.
4. **Passages reused across courses deepen, not repeat** -- if a text appears in C1 for close reading and C3L for rhetorical analysis, the analytical task must be meaningfully different.
5. **Synthesis sets are locked to specific courses** -- each set used in only one course.

## PHASE 4: QUALITY CHECK

### Method

Each assigned passage evaluated by attempting the exercise it's assigned to. Four criteria per the Curation Spec:
1. **Does it actually work for the specified exercise?** (Try it)
2. **Is the complexity appropriate for the target course?**
3. **Does it meet all non-negotiable criteria?**
4. **Is it engaging enough for HS students?** (Motivation is 90%)

Rating: PASS / FLAG (fixable issue) / SWAP (replace passage)

- Engagement: Identity, belonging, race -- topics HS students connect with. Personal narrative form is accessible. **PASS**
- Non-negotiable: Public domain (1909). Real author, real choices. Not used in AP exams. **PASS**
- **VERDICT: PASS. Excellent gate passage.**

#### SP-030 Paine, "The Crisis, No. 1" -- S-F-E Chain Drill (5 reps, 5 element types)

**Exercise tryout:** Can a student produce 5 S-F-E chains from different element types using one passage?

1. **Diction:** Paine's use of "summer soldier" and "sunshine patriot" (S) creates a taxonomy of fair-weather commitment that distinguishes genuine from performative loyalty (F), pressuring the audience (Continental Army soldiers in winter) to prove they belong to the other category by continuing to fight (E).

2. **Syntax:** The opening sentence's symmetrical structure -- "These are the times that try men's souls" -- (S) inverts expected word order (normally: "Men's souls are tried by these times"), placing the abstract ("times") before the human ("souls") (F), which creates the sense that the historical moment is acting upon individuals rather than vice versa, establishing urgency that demands response (E).

3. **Evidence type:** Paine's analogy comparing Britain to a thief breaking into a house (S) translates the abstract political question of colonial taxation into a concrete, visceral domestic scenario (F), making the argument accessible to soldiers who may not follow constitutional theory but immediately understand property violation -- thereby democratizing the argument for independence (E).

4. **Structure:** Paine opens with the bleakest assessment of the war ("These are the times that try men's souls") before building toward optimism (S), structuring the essay as a journey FROM despair TO resolve (F), which mirrors the emotional arc he wants the audience to experience -- ensuring they don't dismiss his message as naive optimism because he first validates their suffering (E).

5. **Tone:** The shift from somber reflection to direct, almost conversational address ("I call not upon a few, but upon all") (S) moves from philosophical argument to personal appeal (F), collapsing the distance between pamphleteer and reader so that the abstract argument for independence becomes an intimate demand for each individual's commitment (E).

**5 distinct element types, each reaching all three S-F-E levels. This passage is analytically inexhaustible for G9 purposes.**

**VERDICT: PASS. Perfect S-F-E anchor text.**

#### Washington/Du Bois Pairing (SP-007 + ES-023 + ES-024) -- Course 2 They Say/I Say

**Exercise tryout:** Do these texts genuinely model They Say / I Say moves?

- Du Bois ("The Talented Tenth," 1903): Explicitly responds to Washington -- argues for higher education, intellectual leadership, political agitation. "The Negro race, like all races, is going to be saved by its exceptional men." This IS the "I Say" response.
- Washington ("Up from Slavery," 1901): Narrative version of the accommodation argument with personal testimony.

**This is a genuine historical They Say/I Say.** Students aren't practicing with artificial "some people think X, but others think Y." They're entering a real conversation between two towering intellectuals who genuinely disagreed about the most important question of their era.

**All three Graff & Birkenstein moves are possible:**
1. Agree with a difference: "While Washington is right that practical skills matter, his vision of education is too narrow to produce the leaders the race needs."
2. Disagree diplomatically: "Du Bois's argument that the Talented Tenth will save the race overlooks the economic realities that Washington addresses."
3. Complicate: "The Washington/Du Bois debate frames education as either vocational or intellectual, but this binary obscures the possibility that both are necessary at different scales."

**VERDICT: PASS. Pedagogically exceptional -- possibly the strongest assignment in the entire bank.**

### 4E. COURSE 4 ASSIGNMENTS -- SPOT CHECKS

#### ES-027 Macaulay, "Minute on Indian Education" -- Framing Analysis (M6)

**Exercise tryout:**
1. **What does the framing make visible?** The "backwardness" of Indian languages and literature relative to English. The urgency of "improving" Indian education.
2. **What does it obscure?** Thousands of years of Sanskrit literature, mathematical and scientific achievement. The political function of language imposition in maintaining colonial control. The fact that Macaulay admitted he had no knowledge of Sanskrit or Arabic.
3. **Who benefits?** The British colonial administration, which needs English-speaking Indian intermediaries to govern cheaply. English publishers who gain a new market. Macaulay's own career (he was a member of the Governor-General's Council).
4. **Alternative framing?** "Indian education already has a rich literary tradition; the question is how to create bilingual fluency that serves Indian interests" -- this framing centers Indian agency rather than British judgment.

**This is the single most M6-productive text in the entire bank.** The colonial framing is so transparent once students have the M6 lens that it practically teaches itself, but sophisticated enough that extending the analysis to "Whose interests does 'improvement' language serve?" transfers to countless modern contexts (development rhetoric, standardized testing, language policy).

**VERDICT: PASS. M6 anchor text. Outstanding.**

#### PO-009 Frost, "Road Not Taken" -- Alternative Reading Drill

**Exercise tryout:**
- **Common reading:** The speaker took the less-traveled road and it "made all the difference" -- a poem about nonconformity and brave individualism.
- **Alternative reading (textually supported):** The speaker admits both roads were "really about the same" and "equally lay." The "difference" is something the speaker will claim "ages and ages hence" -- a future fabrication. The poem is actually about the human tendency to create narratives of meaningful choice retroactively, not about the choice itself.
- **Why the alternative is stronger:** The text explicitly says both paths were "worn... really about the same." The sigh in "I shall be telling this with a sigh" signals self-aware storytelling, not genuine regret. The poem's irony is directed at the speaker's own future myth-making.

**This is a PERFECT alternative reading exercise because students almost certainly hold the common misreading. Discovering the textual evidence against it is a genuine intellectual thrill -- the M5 (reverse engineering) and M6 (who benefits from this misreading?) dimensions are rich.**

**VERDICT: PASS. Possibly the best single exercise-text pairing in Course 4.**

#### ES-005 Swift, "A Modest Proposal" -- Framing Analysis Rep 3

**Exercise tryout:**
1. **What does the framing make visible?** The "economic burden" of Irish poverty. Children as an economic variable.
2. **What does it obscure (by satire)?** The actual humanity of Irish families. England's role in creating the poverty it decries. The fact that "solutions" to poverty are always framed in terms convenient to the powerful.
3. **Who benefits from THIS framing?** The projectors and economists who reduce human suffering to balance sheets -- Swift's real target.
4. **Alternative framing?** The satirical framing IS the alternative framing -- Swift adopts the voice of a callous economist precisely to expose how that voice dehumanizes.

**FLAG: Students need to understand it's satire BEFORE doing the framing analysis, otherwise the exercise fails. The text must be introduced with a brief note on satirical context. This is a Rep 3 (third attempt at framing analysis), so students should have the M6 framework, but satire adds a layer of complexity: the framing is deliberately constructed to be exposed.**

**VERDICT: PASS with flag.** Ensure introductory note establishes satirical genre before analysis. The double-layer framing (Swift's satirical frame containing the economist's sincere frame) is actually a sophistication upgrade from the first two reps -- appropriate for progression.
