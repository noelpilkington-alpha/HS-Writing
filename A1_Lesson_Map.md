# Course A1: G9 Analytical Writing Foundations -- Lesson Map (v2)

## Course Summary
- **Track:** A (Core)
- **Grade:** 9
- **Calibration:** CCSS W.9-10
- **Hours:** ~10-12
- **Mental Models Introduced:** M1 (Everything Is a Choice), M2 (Structure -> Function -> Effect)
- **Gate:** Analytical essay (40 min) + conventions (20 min), pass = 4/6

## Design Rationale (v2 restructure)

G8 completers know: sentences (TWR), paragraphs, expository essays (interest-based topics), and basic academic argument (C.8.04: claim + evidence + reasoning from knowledge). They have NEVER analyzed an assigned text, done close reading, or written about another author's choices.

**The old v1 lesson map jumped straight to close reading and text analysis.** This is too large a leap. Students need to solidify argumentative writing skills FIRST -- using topics they can argue from knowledge -- before pivoting to text-based analytical writing.

**New sequence:**
1. Unit 1: Argumentative Essay Foundations (argue from knowledge, no assigned texts)
2. Unit 2: Bridge -- From Your Choices to the Author's Choices (M1 + M2 introduced)
3. Unit 3: Analytical Reading & Writing (Choice Audit, S-F-E, Analysis vs. Summary)
4. Unit 4: Essay Architecture & Thesis (structures, planning, thesis development)
5. Unit 5: Evidence, Commentary & Calibration (core analytical sentence, paragraphs, self-assessment)
6. Unit 6: Integration & Style (revision, voice, pre-gate essay)
7. Gate Assessment

## Unit 1: Argumentative Essay Foundations (~2.5 hrs, 5 lessons)

Students argue from knowledge on debatable topics. No assigned texts. This unit bridges G8 expository writing to G9 argument.

### Lesson 01 | From Expository to Argumentative
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.9.B01 (Distinguish Expository from Argumentative Writing) [NEW bridge KC]
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Quick review of paragraph anatomy (topic sentence, supporting sentences, concluding sentence) -- you know this from G8. Then: Expository = explains. Argumentative = takes a position and defends it. You already do both -- now we name the difference. (3 min)
  - Model: Show two student paragraphs on the same topic (school lunch). One explains how school lunch programs work (expository). One argues school lunches should be free (argumentative). Point out: both have topic sentences, but the argumentative one's topic sentence is a CLAIM. (3 min)
  - Supported: Sort 5 short paragraphs into Expository or Argumentative. For each, underline the topic sentence and identify: does it STATE A FACT or MAKE A CLAIM? (7 min)
  - Independent: Given 2 topics, write one expository paragraph and one argumentative paragraph for each. Each must have: topic sentence, 2-3 supporting sentences, concluding sentence. (8 min)
  - Transfer: "Think of the last essay you wrote in G8. Was it expository or argumentative? How would you turn it into the other type?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** None (student-generated content, model paragraphs)
- **Content Files:** To be generated (5 sort items + 2 model paragraphs with paragraph anatomy labeled)
- **Notes:** Accessible entry point. Students realize they already know more than they think. The key insight: argument requires a POSITION someone could disagree with. Paragraph anatomy is reviewed (not taught from scratch) -- students know this from G8, but naming the parts explicitly ensures they can build on them. The sort task doubles as paragraph mechanics reinforcement: underlining topic sentences makes structure visible.

### Lesson 02 | Taking a Position
- **Time:** ~20 min
- **KCs:** C.9.B02 (Take a Defensible Position on a Debatable Issue) [NEW bridge KC]
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: A strong position is: (1) debatable -- someone reasonable could disagree, (2) specific -- not "X is good/bad," and (3) supportable -- you can give reasons. (2 min)
  - Model: Coping model -- start with "Social media is bad" (weak). Revise: "Social media platforms should be required to disable algorithmic feeds for users under 16" (strong). Show the thinking process. (3 min)
  - Supported: Given 3 broad topics, narrow each to a defensible position. Use the 3-test checklist (debatable? specific? supportable?). (7 min)
  - Independent: Choose a topic you care about. Write a defensible position statement, then expand it into a full paragraph (topic sentence = your position, 2-3 supporting sentences, concluding sentence). Apply the 3 tests to your position. Revise if needed. (7 min)
  - Transfer: For your position, write the strongest OPPOSING position. If you can't, your position isn't debatable -- revise. (3 min)
- **Passages:** None
- **Content Files:** To be generated (3 topic prompts + model + weak/strong position pairs from WfS Ch 9 taxonomy)
- **Notes:** The "write the opposing position" move is introduced here and reinforced in the Thesis Development lesson later. This is also a preview of They Say/I Say from A2.

### Lesson 03 | Supporting with Evidence and Reasoning
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.9.B03 (Support Claims with Evidence and Reasoning from Knowledge) [NEW bridge KC]
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Two types of support -- evidence (facts, examples, data you know) and reasoning (logical connections: "This matters because..."). Both are required. Evidence without reasoning = list. Reasoning without evidence = opinion. (3 min)
  - Model: Coping model -- write a claim, add evidence only ("Studies show screen time affects sleep"), then add reasoning ("This matters because adolescent brain development depends on consistent sleep patterns, meaning the harm is not just immediate discomfort but long-term cognitive impact"). (3 min)
  - Supported: Given a claim, generate 3 pieces of evidence from knowledge. For each, write one sentence of reasoning explaining WHY it supports the claim. (8 min)
  - Independent: Take your position from L02. Write 2 supporting paragraphs -- each with a sub-claim, evidence, and reasoning. (8 min)
  - Transfer: Look at your 2 paragraphs. Which evidence is strongest? Which reasoning is weakest? Mark each. (3 min)
- **Passages:** None
- **Content Files:** To be generated
- **Notes:** "Evidence from knowledge" is the skill. Students draw on what they know -- personal experience, general knowledge, things they've read. This is the same skill ACT Writing will demand in A2 (C.10.18), so we're building it early.

### Lesson 04 | Addressing Counterarguments
- **Time:** ~20 min
- **KCs:** C.9.B04 (Acknowledge and Respond to Counterarguments) [NEW bridge KC]
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Strong arguments don't ignore the other side -- they address it. Three moves: (1) Acknowledge ("Some argue that..."), (2) Concede what's valid ("While it's true that..."), (3) Respond ("However, this overlooks..."). (3 min)
  - Model: Take the social media position from L02. Write a counterargument paragraph using all 3 moves. Show the false start: first attempt ignores the opposition ("My opponents are wrong because..."). Self-correct to genuine engagement. (3 min)
  - Supported: Given 2 positions + their strongest counterarguments, write a response paragraph for each using the 3-move structure. Scaffold visible. (8 min)
  - Independent: Take your L02 position. Write the strongest counterargument you can think of. Then write a response paragraph using the 3 moves. No scaffold. (6 min)
- **Passages:** None
- **Content Files:** To be generated (2 position/counterargument pairs)
- **Notes:** This is a preview of They Say/I Say (A2, C.10.03-C.10.05) at a basic level. Students learn the MOVES here; A2 refines them. The key insight: acknowledging the other side makes YOUR argument stronger, not weaker.

### Lesson 05 | Planning and Writing an Argument Essay
- **Time:** ~35 min
- **KCs:** C.9.B05 (Plan and Write a Complete Argument Essay from Knowledge) [NEW bridge KC]
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The Argument Essay Plan: Position -> 2-3 Supporting Claims (each with evidence + reasoning) -> Counterargument + Response -> Conclusion. (2 min)
  - Model: Show a complete plan for an argument essay on a familiar topic. Walk through how the plan becomes paragraphs. (3 min)
  - Supported: Choose from 3 debatable prompts. Spend 8 minutes planning using the template. (8 min)
  - Independent: Write the full essay from your plan. 20 minutes. (20 min)
  - Self-score: Check your essay against the 4-point argument checklist: (1) clear position? (2) evidence + reasoning for each claim? (3) counterargument addressed? (4) organized logically? (2 min)
- **Passages:** None
- **Content Files:** Argument Essay Plan template (new), 3 debatable prompts (to be generated)
- **Notes:** First full essay in Course A1. Written from knowledge, not from a text. This proves to students they CAN write a structured argument -- the foundation everything else builds on. The 20-minute essay is shorter than the 40-minute gate essay because this is argument-from-knowledge (faster than text analysis).

## Unit 2: Bridge -- From Your Choices to the Author's Choices (~1.5 hrs, 3 lessons)

The pivot from "writing your own arguments" to "analyzing someone else's writing." M1 is introduced through the student's OWN writing first, then applied to published texts.

### Lesson 06 | You Already Make Choices (M1 Introduction)
- **Time:** ~20 min
- **KCs:** C.9.01 (Close Reading Protocol -- preview), C.9.02 (Choice Audit -- preview)
- **Mental Model:** M1 introduced through own writing
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: M1 -- "Every writing choice is deliberate." But we start with YOUR choices, not someone else's. (2 min)
  - Model: Take the model argument essay from L05. "Why did the writer open with a question instead of a statistic? Why 'devastating' instead of 'bad'? Why address the counterargument in paragraph 3, not paragraph 2?" Every answer reveals a CHOICE. (3 min)
  - Supported: Take your own L05 essay. Identify 3 choices you made (word choice, opening move, structure, evidence selected). For each: "What did I choose? What could I have done instead? Why did I choose this?" (8 min)
  - Independent: Now read SP-010 (Chief Joseph, ~200 words). Apply the same questions. Identify 3 choices Chief Joseph made. (5 min)
  - Transfer: "What's the difference between analyzing your own choices and analyzing Chief Joseph's? What's the same?" (2 min)
- **Passages:** SP-010 (Chief Joseph, "I Will Fight No More Forever")
- **Content Files:** CM-01 (Coping_Models.md -- to be adapted for this lesson)
- **Notes:** The critical bridge moment. M1 starts with the student as writer, then pivots to the student as reader. The questions are IDENTICAL -- only the text changes. This makes analytical reading feel like a natural extension of what they already do, not a foreign skill.

### Lesson 07 | Close Reading Protocol
- **Time:** ~20 min
- **KCs:** C.9.01 (Close Reading Protocol), C.9.02 (Perform Choice Audit)
- **Mental Model:** M1 applied to published texts
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The 3-read protocol: (1) Gist -- what's it about? (2) Choices -- mark 5+ deliberate choices. (3) Prompt -- which choices matter for YOUR argument? (2 min)
  - Model: Coping model CM-01 -- demonstrate the 3-read protocol on SP-030 (Paine, "The Crisis"). Show the false start: first read misses the key choice, second read catches it. (4 min)
  - Supported: Choice Audit on SP-030 with scaffold table visible. Complete the table for 5 choices. (8 min)
  - Independent: Choice Audit on SP-011 (Lincoln, Gettysburg Address) -- no scaffold, 5 choices. (5 min)
  - Transfer: Pick your strongest Choice Audit entry. "Why is this the most interesting choice?" (1 min)
- **Passages:** SP-030 (Paine), SP-011 (Lincoln, Gettysburg)
- **Content Files:** CM-01 (Coping_Models.md)
- **Notes:** Now students have context. They know what "authorial choices" means from analyzing their OWN writing in L06. Applying it to published texts is the next step, not the first.

### Lesson 08 | Structure -> Function -> Effect (M2)
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.9.03 (Produce S-F-E Chains)
- **Mental Model:** M2 introduced
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: M2 -- three levels of analytical observation. S = name it. F = what does it do? E = why does it matter? Most students stop at S. Your job is to reach E every time. Also: transition words signal the MOVE between S, F, and E (e.g., "by doing so" = S→F, "which suggests" = F→E, "ultimately" = E→thesis). Introduce the Transition Reference by function (sequence, cause-effect, contrast, emphasis). (3 min)
  - Model: Coping model CM-02 (S-F-E Chain with false start -- stops at Function, self-corrects to reach Effect). Highlight the transition words that signal each move. (3 min)
  - Supported: One-Level Test -- identify whether 5 analytical statements reach S only, S+F, or S+F+E. Extend the incomplete ones, using at least one transition word per extension. (7 min)
  - Independent: Write 3 complete S-F-E chains from SP-030 (Paine) passage elements. Each chain must use at least one transition from the reference. (8 min)
  - Transfer: Take your best Choice Audit entry from L07. Convert it into a full S-F-E chain. "Is this better than what you wrote before?" (2 min)
- **Passages:** SP-030
- **Content Files:** CM-02 (Coping_Models.md), Transition_Reference.md (new -- transitions categorized by rhetorical function: sequence, cause-effect, contrast, emphasis, example, conclusion; adapted from WfS Ch 8 transition tables)
- **Notes:** S-F-E is the analytical engine. From this point forward, every analytical sentence uses it. Transitions are introduced HERE rather than as a standalone lesson because they are most meaningful when students need them to connect S→F→E moves. The Transition Reference becomes a reusable scaffold for all subsequent writing.

## Unit 3: Analytical Reading & Writing (~1.5 hrs, 3 lessons)

### Lesson 09 | Analysis vs. Summary (Gateway)
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.9.09 (Spot Analysis vs. Summary) -- GATEWAY KC
- **Mental Model:** M2 applied
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: "Retelling what happens" vs. "explaining why the author did it." The S-F-E test: if your sentence doesn't reach Function or Effect, it's summary. (2 min)
  - Model: Side-by-side comparison -- summary sentence vs. analytical sentence on the same passage moment. (2 min)
  - Supported: Sort 5 sentences into Analysis or Summary (C1_Analysis_vs_Summary_Items.md, Pairs 1-5). (7 min)
  - Independent: Sort 5 more sentences (Pairs 6-10) -- no scaffold, scored. (5 min)
  - Transfer: Write one analytical sentence and one summary sentence about the same passage moment. Label each. (5 min)
  - Gateway check: Must score 4/5 on Independent to proceed. (4 min)
- **Passages:** SP-030, ES-006 (Bacon), SP-002 (Sojourner Truth)
- **Content Files:** C1_Analysis_vs_Summary_Items.md (B2)
- **Notes:** Gateway KC. Now students have the context to understand this distinction -- they've been writing arguments (Unit 1) and learning to see choices (Unit 2). The question "is this analysis or summary?" makes sense because they know what analysis looks like.

### Lesson 10 | S-F-E Fluency Drill
- **Time:** ~20 min
- **KCs:** C.9.03 (S-F-E Chains -- fluency building)
- **Mental Model:** M2 applied to varied element types
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Brief review -- 5 element types to analyze: diction, syntax, evidence type, structure, tone. (1 min)
  - Model: Coping model CM-03 (S-F-E on an unfamiliar passage -- demonstrates productive struggle). (3 min)
  - Supported: Write S-F-E chains for 3 different element types from ES-002 (Emerson, "Self-Reliance"). (8 min)
  - Independent: Write 2 S-F-E chains from SP-001 (Douglass, "Fourth of July") in 5 min -- timed for fluency. (5 min)
  - Transfer: Pick your strongest chain. Expand it into a 2-sentence analytical observation. (3 min)
- **Passages:** ES-002, SP-001
- **Content Files:** CM-03 (Coping_Models.md)
- **Notes:** Fluency lesson. Speed and range across element types.

### Lesson 11 | Thesis Development (Gateway)
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.9.07 (Spot Weak vs. Strong Thesis), C.9.08 (Craft Arguable, Specific, Significant Thesis) -- GATEWAY KC
- **Mental Model:** --
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The 3 tests (from L02, now applied to TEXT-BASED thesis): Arguable? Specific? Significant ("So what?")? A thesis about a TEXT must say something about the AUTHOR'S CHOICES, not just the topic. (2 min)
  - Model: Coping model -- start with "Douglass's speech is powerful" (weak). Apply "So what?" three times: -> "Douglass's speech is powerful because of his word choices" -> "Douglass's strategic use of irony forces his audience to confront their own complicity" (strong). (3 min)
  - Supported: Thesis Triage -- sort 5 thesis statements into Strong/Weak/Fixable (C1_Thesis_Triage_Items.md, TH-01 to TH-05). (5 min)
  - Independent: Thesis Triage -- 5 more (TH-06 to TH-10), scored. (5 min)
  - Production: Given SP-022 (Tecumseh), generate a defensible thesis in 3 min. (3 min)
  - Transfer: Counter-Thesis Test -- write the strongest opposing thesis. (4 min)
  - Gateway check: Must score 4/5 on Triage + produce thesis rated "Strong." (3 min)
- **Passages:** SP-022 (Tecumseh)
- **Content Files:** C1_Thesis_Triage_Items.md (B1)
- **Notes:** Students already practiced position-taking in L02 (from knowledge). Now they apply the SAME skill to text-based claims. The continuity is explicit: "You already know how to take a position. Now your position is about what the AUTHOR is doing."

### Lesson 13 | Architecture Sketch + Evidence Planning

- **Mental Model:** M2, M4 preview
- **Phases:**

  - Model: Coping model -- plan an analytical essay from SP-003 (Lincoln, Second Inaugural). Show the false start: evidence dropped without commentary. Self-correct by adding S-F-E. (4 min)

## Unit 5: Paragraph Sprints & Calibration (~1.5 hrs, 3 lessons)

### Lesson 14 | Body Paragraph Sprint
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.9.10 (applied), C.9.08 (applied)
- **Mental Model:** M2
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Anatomy of a body paragraph: topic sentence (claim) -> evidence with attribution -> S-F-E commentary -> connection to thesis. (2 min)
  - Model: Show a complete model body paragraph. Then show the same paragraph with commentary removed -- "see the difference?" (2 min)
  - Supported: Write one body paragraph from SP-028 (Garrison, first Liberator editorial) in 10 min with checklist visible. (10 min)
  - Independent: Body Paragraph Sprint -- write one body paragraph from ES-022 (Zitkala-Sa) in 7 min, no scaffold, scored. (7 min)
  - Transfer: Convert your 3 strongest body paragraph claims into a full essay plan (thesis + 3 body claims + structure choice). (4 min)
- **Passages:** SP-028, ES-022
- **Content Files:** --
- **Notes:** First timed analytical production under pressure. The 7-minute sprint becomes a recurring format.

### Lesson 15 | Analysis vs. Summary -- Calibration
- **Time:** ~20 min
- **KCs:** C.9.11 (Self-Assess: Analysis vs. Summary), C.9.12 (Revise Summary into Analysis)
- **Mental Model:** M2
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Self-assessment is a skill -- you must learn to SEE quality in your own work. (1 min)
  - Model: Score a sample paragraph as "mostly analysis" or "mostly summary" -- model the reasoning. (2 min)
  - Supported: Score-Then-Compare -- read 3 sample paragraphs from C1_Binary_Sample_Paragraphs.md (Sets 1-3), score each, compare to model answer. (7 min)
  - Independent: Score 3 more paragraphs (Sets 4-6), scored on calibration accuracy. (5 min)
  - Production: Revise Summary into Analysis -- take 2 summary sentences and rewrite as analytical sentences using S-F-E. (5 min)
- **Passages:** Sample paragraphs (from content bank)
- **Content Files:** C1_Binary_Sample_Paragraphs.md (B3)
- **Notes:** Students learn to see their own work objectively. Critical for self-regulation.

### Lesson 16 | Revise Your Own Work (Three-Pass Protocol)
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.9.11, C.9.12
- **Mental Model:** M2
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Revision vs. editing -- revision changes IDEAS, editing fixes surface errors. Introduce the **Three-Pass Revision Protocol** -- a named, separable strategy: **Pass 1: Unity** (does every sentence serve the claim?), **Pass 2: Coherence** (are transitions logical? does each sentence connect to the next?), **Pass 3: Concision** (cut wordiness -- there is/are, unnecessary modifiers, passive voice, deadwood). Do ONE pass at a time -- never all three at once. (3 min)
  - Model: Take a student paragraph (from L14 sprint). Demonstrate all three passes in sequence: Pass 1 finds a sentence that is summary (cut it). Pass 2 finds a missing transition between evidence and commentary (add it). Pass 3 finds a 22-word sentence that says what 14 words can say (trim it). Show before/after for each pass. (4 min)
  - Supported: Run Pass 1 (Unity) on your own L14 body paragraph sprint. Mark each sentence: serves the claim? or drifts? Cut or revise drifting sentences. (5 min)
  - Independent: Run Passes 2 and 3 on the same paragraph. Add transitions where missing. Cut your wordiest sentence by 20%+. Compare your before/after. (8 min)
  - Transfer: Write a 2-sentence self-diagnosis: "My biggest analytical writing weakness right now is ___, and I plan to fix it by ___." (3 min)
- **Passages:** Student's own work from L14
- **Content Files:** Three-Pass Revision Checklist (new -- one-page reference card: Pass 1 Unity questions, Pass 2 Coherence questions + transition reference, Pass 3 Concision patterns to cut; adapted from WfS Ch 8 revision model)
- **Notes:** The Three-Pass Protocol is a named SRSD self-regulation strategy. Students use it from this point forward on every piece of writing. The checklist becomes a permanent scaffold, fading only when passes become automatic. The concision patterns (there is/are, deadwood, passive voice, unnecessary modifiers) are borrowed from WfS Ch 8 and converted into concrete cut-targets.

## Unit 6: Integration & Style (~1.5 hrs, 3 lessons)

### Lesson 17 | Style -- Rhythm, Parallelism & Sentence Combining
- **Time:** ~30 min
- **KCs:** M.9.01 (Spot Stylistic Strengths), M.9.02 (Sentence Imitation), M.9.03 (Spot Wordiness), M.9.04 (Revise for Concision), M.9.05 (Parallelism) [NEW], M.9.06 (Sentence Combining) [NEW]
- **Mental Model:** M1 (style as choice)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Three style tools: (1) Sentence rhythm (short = emphasis, long = elaboration, variation = flow). (2) Parallelism -- matching grammatical structure in lists and paired ideas (faulty: "She likes reading, writing, and to swim" → correct: "She likes reading, writing, and swimming"). (3) Sentence combining via coordination (FANBOYS, semicolons for equal ideas) and subordination (because, although, while for unequal ideas). (4 min)
  - Model: Map sentence lengths in a paragraph from SP-001 (Douglass). Then fix a faulty parallelism example. Then combine two choppy sentences using subordination. (4 min)
  - Supported: Three quick drills --
    - Parallelism Sort: classify 4 sentence pairs as correct or faulty parallelism, fix the faulty ones (from Style_Discrimination_Items.md).
    - Sentence Combining: given 3 pairs of short sentences, combine each using coordination OR subordination -- student chooses which and justifies.
    - Wordiness Detection: identify unnecessary words in 3 sentences (from Supplementary_Items.md). (10 min)
  - Independent: Revise 2 wordy sentences (cut 20%+). Fix 2 faulty parallelism items. Combine 2 choppy sentence pairs. Then write one analytical sentence imitating a mentor sentence pattern from Mentor_Sentences.md. (8 min)
  - Transfer: Take your L14 body paragraph. Apply all three tools: vary sentence lengths, fix any faulty parallelism, combine choppy sentences. Run Pass 3 (Concision) from the Three-Pass Protocol. (4 min)
- **Passages:** SP-001
- **Content Files:** Mentor_Sentences.md (B9), Supplementary_Items.md (B9), Style_Discrimination_Items.md (new -- 8 faulty/correct parallelism pairs + 6 sentence-combining pairs; parallelism items adapted from WfS Ch 7 §7.3, sentence-combining from WfS Ch 7 §7.2)
- **Notes:** Style expanded from 20 to 30 min to include parallelism and sentence combining -- both have strong evidence bases in writing research (Graham & Perin meta-analysis). Parallelism items are discrimination-before-production: sort first, then fix, then produce. Sentence combining teaches subordination explicitly, which is the syntactic foundation of analytical writing (because, although, while are the words that CREATE analysis). Style continues to develop across A2 and Track B.

- **Content Files:** A1 Gate Rubric (from blueprint)
- **Notes:** Full rehearsal before the gate. Uses a fresh passage. Self-scoring builds calibration.

### Lesson 19 | Voice + Final Revision
- **Time:** ~20 min

- **Mental Model:** M1
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Voice in academic writing -- scholars maintain personality within formal constraints. Voice = consistent choices in diction, syntax, tone. (2 min)
  - Model: Read 2 short analytical excerpts analyzing the same passage. Identify the choices that create each voice. (3 min)
  - Supported: Write the same analytical claim in 2 different voices (spare/direct vs. elaborate/layered). (6 min)
  - Independent: Revise one paragraph from your L18 practice essay -- strengthen your voice while maintaining analytical rigor. (6 min)
  - Transfer: "Which voice feels more natural to you? This IS your developing analytical voice." (3 min)

## Gate Assessment

### Lesson 20 | G9 Gate Assessment
- **Time:** ~60 min
- **KCs:** All A1 KCs assessed
- **Format:**
  - Part 1 -- Analytical Essay (40 min): Given a nonfiction passage (~800 words), write an analytical essay examining how the author's choices contribute to the text's purpose.
  - Part 2 -- Conventions (20 min): 15 QTI items (inline-choice, hottext, match, order) testing sentence structure, punctuation, agreement, verb tense, concision.
- **Passage:** Fresh passage not used in any lesson (SP-031, Abigail Adams, or ES-028, Equiano)
- **Scoring:** 6-point rubric (Thesis 0-1, Evidence 0-2, Commentary 0-2, Conventions 0-1)
- **Pass:** 4/6, must earn >=1 on Commentary
