## 1. AP RUBRIC CALIBRATION BANK

These 3 sample essays are designed for students to practice scoring using the AP Language rubric (Rows A, B, C). Each essay models a different FRQ type and a different score profile to help students discriminate performance levels.

### Essay 1: Rhetorical Analysis (Ida B. Wells's "Lynch Law in All Its Phases")

**Prompt Context:** Write an essay that analyzes the rhetorical choices Wells makes to convey her argument about racial violence in America.

**Student Essay:**

In her speech "Lynch Law in All Its Phases," Ida B. Wells argues against the practice of lynching in America and exposes the false justifications used to defend it. Wells uses several rhetorical strategies to make her case, including appeals to emotion, factual evidence, and powerful language.

One strategy Wells uses is emotional appeal. She describes the brutal violence of lynching in graphic detail, which makes the audience feel shocked and horrified. For example, she talks about how victims were burned alive and tortured. This pathos makes readers sympathize with the victims and feel angry about the injustice. Wells also uses statistics and facts to support her claims. She provides specific numbers about lynching cases and shows that the reasons given for these murders were often false. This logos helps establish her credibility because she is not just speaking from emotion but from real evidence.

Another strategy is her use of direct, accusatory language. Wells doesn't soften her message or try to be diplomatic. She directly calls out white Americans and challenges their lies about why lynching happens. This bold approach shows her courage and forces the audience to confront uncomfortable truths. The tone is serious and urgent, which matches the gravity of her subject. By using these different strategies together, Wells creates a powerful argument that is both emotionally moving and logically sound, making it difficult for her audience to dismiss her claims.

---

**Scoring Breakdown:**

**Row A (Thesis): 1 point**
The essay responds to the prompt with a defensible thesis in the first paragraph: "Wells uses several rhetorical strategies to make her case, including appeals to emotion, factual evidence, and powerful language." This establishes a line of reasoning about Wells's rhetorical choices.

**Row B (Evidence & Commentary): 2 points**
The essay provides specific evidence from Wells's text (graphic descriptions, statistics, direct language) and explains how these choices function rhetorically. However, the analysis is inconsistent. The second body paragraph reaches S-F-E analysis by explaining how "bold approach shows her courage and forces the audience to confront uncomfortable truths," but the first paragraph largely feature-spots by naming devices (pathos, logos) and stating what they do without deeply analyzing how or why Wells's specific choices achieve their effect.

**Row C (Sophistication): 0 points**
The essay does not demonstrate sophistication. It lacks complexity in the line of reasoning, doesn't situate Wells's argument in broader context, and doesn't explore tensions or alternative interpretations.

**Total Score: 3/6**

**Weakest Row:** Row C (Sophistication) — The essay needs to move beyond feature-spotting and develop more complex insights about Wells's rhetorical situation or the implications of her choices.

**Revision Suggestion:** To reach Row B = 3, consistently analyze the effect and significance of each choice rather than just naming devices. To earn Row C, consider adding analysis of Wells's rhetorical situation (speaking to white Northern audiences in 1893) or exploring tensions in her approach (balancing moral outrage with factual evidence).

### Essay 2: AP Argument (The Value of Failure)

**Prompt Context:** Many successful people claim that failure was essential to their success. Others argue that society's emphasis on learning from failure minimizes the real harm and discouragement that failure causes. Write an essay that argues your position on the value of failure in personal and professional development.

**Student Essay:**

While society often romanticizes failure as a necessary stepping stone to success, this perspective oversimplifies a more complex reality: failure's value depends entirely on whether individuals have the resources and support systems to learn from it rather than be crushed by it.

For many successful people, failure indeed served as a powerful teacher. J.K. Rowling, before publishing Harry Potter, faced rejection from twelve publishers and struggled as a single mother on welfare. However, she had several crucial advantages: she had completed her education at Exeter, she lived in a country with social safety nets, and she had family support for childcare. Her failure taught her resilience, but only because she had resources to survive it. Similarly, Steve Jobs's ousting from Apple in 1985 is often celebrated as the failure that humbled him and made him a better leader when he returned. Yet Jobs had wealth, connections, and a track record that allowed him to start NeXT and Pixar. His failure came with a safety net that most people don't have.

In contrast, failure without resources often compounds disadvantage rather than building character. A student who fails out of college due to working full-time to support their family doesn't gain wisdom from that experience—they gain debt and lost opportunity. A small business owner whose restaurant fails during an economic downturn doesn't emerge stronger; they emerge bankrupt, possibly losing their home and retirement savings. The difference isn't in the individual's character or willingness to learn—it's in their circumstances.

This means we need a more nuanced view of failure. It can be valuable, but only when failure is survivable and when systems exist to help people recover and learn. Celebrating failure without acknowledging this reality is simply a way to justify inequality.

---

**Scoring Breakdown:**

**Row A (Thesis): 1 point**
The essay presents a clear, defensible thesis with a sophisticated position: "failure's value depends entirely on whether individuals have the resources and support systems to learn from it rather than be crushed by it." This goes beyond agree/disagree to complicate the prompt.

**Row B (Evidence & Commentary): 3 points**
The essay provides specific, relevant evidence (Rowling, Jobs, hypothetical struggling student and business owner) and develops commentary that consistently explains how these examples support the argument. The analysis is strong throughout—explaining not just what happened but why it matters to the argument about resources. Each example is paired with explicit reasoning about safety nets, privilege, and circumstances.

**Row C (Sophistication): 0 points**
While the essay takes a sophisticated position (complicating the prompt), it doesn't demonstrate sophistication as defined by the rubric. It lacks a broader context move (historical/philosophical/social implications beyond the immediate argument) or exploration of tensions within the reasoning itself.

**Total Score: 4/6**

**Weakest Row:** Row C (Sophistication) — The essay is strong on Row A and Row B but needs an additional layer of insight to reach the highest level.

**Revision Suggestion:** To earn Row C, the essay could add analysis of whose interests are served by the "failure is valuable" narrative (M6), explore historical context about American myths of self-reliance, or examine a tension within the argument itself (perhaps acknowledging that some individuals do overcome failure without resources, but this shouldn't be the expected norm).

### Essay 3: AP Synthesis (Space Exploration Funding)

**Prompt Context:** As nations debate how to allocate limited resources, the question of space exploration funding becomes increasingly contentious. Some argue that space exploration represents humanity's future and deserves increased investment; others contend that urgent problems on Earth should take priority.

**Prompt:** Read the following six sources carefully, including the introductory information for each source. Write an essay that develops your position on whether governments should increase funding for space exploration. Use at least three of the sources to support your argument.

**Hypothetical Sources Summary:**
- Source A: NASA research showing technological innovations from space programs (GPS, medical devices)
- Source B: Economic analysis showing space program's limited ROI compared to other investments
- Source C: Philosophical essay on human curiosity and exploration as intrinsic values
- Source D: Data on climate change, poverty, and healthcare funding gaps
- Source E: Article on private space companies reducing need for government funding
- Source F: Interview with astronaut discussing inspirational value of space programs

**Student Essay:**

Governments should maintain moderate funding for space exploration, not because space represents an escape from Earth's problems, but because the capacity for long-term thinking required by space programs is the same capacity we need to solve terrestrial crises. However, this funding must be justified by demonstrable returns to addressing Earth-based challenges.

The practical innovations from space programs support this measured investment approach. Source A documents how NASA research produced technologies we now consider essential, from GPS navigation to water purification systems used in developing nations to medical devices that monitor heart function. These weren't incidental byproducts—they emerged because space exploration demands solutions to extreme problems (communication across vast distances, survival in hostile environments), and these solutions often translate to Earth applications. This suggests that space funding can be justified when it's explicitly directed toward research with terrestrial applications, not merely for exploration's own sake.

However, Source B's economic analysis provides necessary perspective: space programs' return on investment is modest compared to direct funding of medical research, education, or infrastructure. A dollar spent on cancer research yields more immediate health benefits than a dollar spent on Mars exploration. This doesn't invalidate space funding, but it demands that we be honest about trade-offs. Source D's data on underfunded climate research and healthcare gaps makes these trade-offs concrete—we're not choosing between space and nothing; we're choosing between space and addressing urgent crises.

The key is recognizing what Source E's discussion of private space companies reveals: we're asking the wrong question. The debate shouldn't be whether to fund space exploration, but who should fund it and for what purpose. Billionaire-funded space tourism is vanity; government-funded research with climate monitoring applications is investment. Perhaps the most honest justification comes from Source C's philosophical argument about human curiosity, but with a caveat: curiosity is a luxury that requires we first ensure human survival.

Ultimately, the space exploration debate reveals our society's priorities. As Source F's astronaut eloquently argues, space programs inspire young people toward science—but one must ask which young people have access to that inspiration, and whether the same investment in their schools might inspire them more directly. Moderate space funding is defensible, but only when it serves Earth's needs and doesn't become an excuse to avoid urgent problems by dreaming of distant planets.

---

**Scoring Breakdown:**

**Row A (Thesis): 1 point**
The essay presents a clear, defensible thesis: "Governments should maintain moderate funding for space exploration, not because space represents an escape from Earth's problems, but because the capacity for long-term thinking required by space programs is the same capacity we need to solve terrestrial crises." This responds to the prompt with a specific position.

**Row B (Evidence & Commentary): 3 points**
The essay effectively uses four sources (A, B, D, E, C, F) with citations, integrating them smoothly into the argument rather than just listing them. Each source is paired with commentary that explains its relevance. For example, Source A isn't just mentioned—the essay explains how NASA innovations support "measured investment" and then qualifies this with "when it's explicitly directed toward research with terrestrial applications." The synthesis is genuine, with sources in conversation (Source B provides "necessary perspective" to Source A; Source D makes Source B's "trade-offs concrete").

**Row C (Sophistication): 1 point**
The essay demonstrates sophistication through multiple moves:
1. **Broader context:** The final paragraph situates the debate within questions about societal priorities and whose interests are served ("one must ask which young people have access to that inspiration")
2. **Complexity:** The essay doesn't simply take a side but navigates tensions throughout (practical benefits vs. opportunity costs, curiosity as value vs. survival as priority)
3. **Alternative perspectives:** Consistently acknowledges counterarguments (space as inspiration, philosophical value) while maintaining the line of reasoning

The M6 lens (Who Benefits?) appears explicitly in the final analysis, adding a layer of critical thinking beyond the immediate debate.

**Total Score: 5/6**

**Weakest Row:** None significant. This is a strong essay across all rows.

**Revision Suggestion:** To reach 6/6, the essay could further develop Row B by engaging with one more source in depth or providing an additional example. The sophistication is already present, but slightly more evidence would make Row B unquestionable for the highest score.

## 2. PROMPT DECONSTRUCTION ITEMS

These 3 AP-style argument prompts are designed for L08 practice in deconstructing arguments and mapping claim hierarchies (M4). Students will practice identifying the core claim, understanding complexity, and generating three positions (agree, disagree, complicate).

---

### Prompt 1: Technology in Education

**The Prompt:**

In his 1928 book *Disenchantment*, the social critic Charles Delisle wrote: "Every new technology promises to revolutionize education, yet the essential relationship between teacher and student remains unchanged. We mistake novelty for progress and gadgets for wisdom."

Nearly a century later, schools invest billions in educational technology, from interactive whiteboards to artificial intelligence tutors. Advocates argue that technology personalizes learning and prepares students for a digital world. Critics worry that screens replace human connection and that educational technology primarily serves corporate interests rather than student needs.

Write an essay that argues your position on the role of technology in education. Use appropriate evidence from your reading, experience, or observations to support your argument.

---

**Core Claim (from Delisle):**
New educational technologies are superficial improvements that don't change the fundamental nature of learning, which depends on human relationships between teachers and students. We confuse the novelty of new tools with actual educational progress.

**Complexity (What Makes This Debatable):**
This is debatable because both sides have compelling evidence:
- Technology clearly enables things impossible before (access to information, personalized pacing, remote learning, adaptive practice)
- Yet student outcomes haven't dramatically improved despite massive technology investments
- The teacher-student relationship *does* seem essential across cultures and centuries
- But what counts as "education" and "progress" is itself contested—perhaps we're measuring the wrong things
- There's also the complication of *which* technologies and *how* they're used (a smart phone used for research vs. distraction)

**Three Positions (M4 Claim Hierarchy):**

**1. AGREE: Technology is overrated in education; human relationships are what matter**

*Supporting reasoning:*
- Research shows teacher quality is the single strongest predictor of student outcomes—more than technology, class size, or resources
- Students in low-tech schools with excellent teachers outperform students in high-tech schools with mediocre teachers
- The "personalization" technology promises often means students staring at screens alone, which reduces the modeling, feedback, and motivation that human teachers provide
- Educational technology is often driven by profit motives (selling subscriptions) rather than pedagogical research
- Historical pattern: radio, TV, computers, tablets all promised revolution; none delivered dramatic improvements
- The Socratic method, writing instruction, mathematical reasoning—all existed before technology and remain central

*Why this is limited for AP:*
This position is defensible but somewhat simplistic. It risks setting up a false binary (technology vs. teachers) when the reality is more complex. It also doesn't account for legitimate ways technology expands access (rural students taking AP courses online, students with disabilities using assistive tech).

**2. DISAGREE: Technology is transforming education in fundamental ways**

*Supporting reasoning:*
- Technology enables personalized pacing impossible for a teacher with 30 students (adaptive learning systems can diagnose exactly where a student struggles)
- Access: Students in remote areas can learn from world-class teachers via video; entire libraries fit on a device
- Engagement: Interactive simulations allow students to conduct chemistry experiments or explore historical sites virtually—experiences impossible otherwise
- Preparation: Students need digital literacy for modern careers; using technology *is* part of what they need to learn
- Data: Technology can identify struggling students early and track progress more precisely than paper records
- Delisle was wrong because he couldn't foresee genuinely transformative technologies (AI tutors that respond to individual questions, VR that creates immersive learning environments)

*Why this is limited for AP:*
This position has evidence but risks being uncritical. It doesn't account for the disconnect between technology investment and outcomes, the loss of certain cognitive skills (deep reading, sustained attention), or the equity issues (not all students have equal access or support for using technology effectively). It also assumes technology's current form is its final form.

**3. COMPLICATE: Technology changes education, but whether this is progress depends on what we value and who controls it**

*Supporting reasoning:*
- Delisle is right that technology alone doesn't improve education, but wrong that the teacher-student relationship is unchanged—technology shifts what that relationship looks like and what it's for
- The question isn't "technology or not" but "which technologies, used how, for what purpose, and in whose interest?"
- Technology does enable new forms of learning (global collaboration, instant access to diverse perspectives) but also creates new problems (distraction, surveillance, algorithmic bias in adaptive systems)
- The "revolution" in education isn't technology itself—it's whether we use technology to reinforce traditional models (digital worksheets) or reimagine education entirely (student-driven inquiry, global projects)
- Crucial distinction: technology as tool (teacher-controlled, supplementing human judgment) vs. technology as replacement (algorithm-driven, reducing teacher autonomy)
- The real issue is power and profit: when corporations design educational technology for profit, "personalization" often means data extraction and teaching to standardized tests

*Why this is strongest for AP:*
This position has the best hierarchy potential because it:
- Acknowledges validity in both agree and disagree positions
- Introduces a higher-order framework (purpose, control, values) that encompasses both
- Applies M6 (Who Benefits?) to show how the debate itself may miss the real issue
- Allows for nuanced evidence (technology succeeds in some contexts, fails in others—what's the difference?)
- Enables sophistication moves about societal priorities, historical context, and power dynamics

---

**Strongest Position for AP:** **Complicate (#3)**

**Why:** This position allows the writer to demonstrate sophistication from the start. Instead of defending one side of a binary, the writer can show that the binary itself is the problem. This opens space for:
- Using evidence from both sides (technology's benefits AND limitations)
- Applying M4 claim hierarchy (moving from "Does technology help?" to "What do we mean by 'help' and who decides?")
- Employing M6 (examining whose interests are served by different approaches to educational technology)
- Situating the argument in broader context (historical patterns of technology adoption, debates about education's purpose, issues of equity and access)

A "complicate" thesis might sound like: "Delisle's skepticism about educational technology remains relevant nearly a century later, not because technology can't improve education, but because we keep asking the wrong question—debating whether technology helps learning rather than interrogating what kind of learning we value and whose interests are served by different technological approaches."

### Prompt 2: Competition vs. Cooperation

**The Prompt:**

The economist Adam Smith famously argued that competition drives innovation and prosperity: "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own self-interest." This view sees competition as the engine of human progress.

However, the anthropologist Margaret Mead countered that cooperation, not competition, defines humanity's greatest achievements. She pointed to the discovery of a 15,000-year-old healed femur—a broken thighbone that had mended—as the first sign of civilization. In the animal kingdom, a broken femur means death; an animal can't escape danger or reach food and water. A healed femur shows that someone cared for the injured person, brought them food, and protected them until they recovered. "Helping someone else through difficulty is where civilization starts," Mead argued.

Write an essay that argues your position on whether competition or cooperation better drives human progress. Use appropriate evidence from your reading, experience, or observations to support your argument.

---

**Core Claim (The Debate):**
Competition (Smith) drives progress through self-interest and the pursuit of advantage, while cooperation (Mead) drives progress through collective care and shared benefit. The question is which force is more fundamental to human achievement.

**Complexity (What Makes This Debatable):**
This is debatable because:
- Both forces clearly operate in human societies—markets are competitive AND depend on cooperative infrastructure (laws, trust, shared currency)
- Defining "progress" is contested—economic growth? Scientific discovery? Human wellbeing? Different goals might favor different approaches
- Scale matters: families/small groups often cooperate; larger systems (nations, markets) often compete
- History provides examples of both: competitive space race led to moon landing, but cooperative international science solved the ozone crisis
- The two forces aren't necessarily opposed—competition can drive cooperation (teams competing against other teams) and cooperation can enable competition (rules that make fair competition possible)
- Cultural variation: some societies emphasize individual competition, others emphasize group harmony

**Three Positions (M4 Claim Hierarchy):**

**1. AGREE with Smith: Competition drives progress**

*Supporting reasoning:*
- Economic evidence: competitive markets produce innovation (tech companies competing created smartphones), while monopolies stagnate
- Scientific discovery: researchers compete for grants, recognition, and breakthrough findings—this urgency accelerates progress
- Evolutionary biology: natural selection is competition; competition "works" at the most fundamental level
- Historical examples: Cold War competition led to space technology, military competition led to internet, corporate competition improved quality while lowering prices
- Human motivation: competition provides clear goals, measurable success, and incentives to excel
- Without competition, systems become complacent (Soviet planned economy vs. dynamic market economies)

*Why this is limited for AP:*
This position has evidence but risks ignoring the cooperative infrastructure that enables competition (legal systems, public education, basic research) and the costs of competition (inequality, environmental damage, winner-take-all dynamics). It also conflates correlation with causation—progress happened during competitive periods, but was competition the cause?

**2. AGREE with Mead: Cooperation drives progress**

*Supporting reasoning:*
- Human survival depends on cooperation: language, agriculture, cities, science—all require collective effort across generations
- Biggest achievements are cooperative: eradicating smallpox (global health cooperation), mapping human genome (international research consortium), building democracies (collective governance)
- Competition often creates waste: duplicate research, planned obsolescence, fighting over finite resources rather than sharing
- Mead's example is powerful: civilization begins when we care for vulnerable others, not when we outcompete them
- Modern challenges (climate change, pandemics, AI safety) require cooperation—competition exacerbates them
- Happiness research shows cooperative communities have higher wellbeing than competitive, unequal societies

*Why this is limited for AP:*
This position has merit but may underestimate competition's role in driving innovation and effort. It risks romanticizing cooperation—history shows cooperative systems can also be oppressive (enforced conformity) or stagnant (no incentive to improve). It also doesn't address why competitive systems have been so widespread across cultures.

**3. COMPLICATE: Progress requires both competition and cooperation, but our era demands rethinking which we prioritize**

*Supporting reasoning:*
- False binary: Smith and Mead are both right because they're describing different aspects of progress
  - Competition drives efficiency and innovation within existing systems
  - Cooperation builds the infrastructure and addresses the challenges that transcend individual gain
- Historical context matters: in Smith's era (scarcity, local markets), competition efficiently allocated resources; in our era (abundance, global systems, existential risks), cooperation is increasingly necessary
- Scale distinction: individuals/companies compete, but within cooperative frameworks (laws, norms, public goods like roads and education)
- The question isn't which force is better, but when and how to apply each
- Current problems reveal competition's limits: climate change results from competing nations prioritizing their own growth; solution requires cooperation
- But cooperation's limits too: international agreements are hard to enforce without competitive pressure (sanctions, incentives)
- The real insight: we've over-indexed on competition in recent decades, creating inequality and collective action problems; progress now requires rebalancing toward cooperation on shared challenges

*Why this is strongest for AP:*
This position allows for:
- Claim hierarchy: moving from "which is better?" to "when does each apply?" to "what does our current moment require?"
- Using evidence from both sides productively
- Historical/contextual thinking (what worked in Smith's era vs. what we need now)
- Sophistication through examining tensions (competition's benefits and costs, cooperation's necessity and difficulty)
- M6 lens: questioning who benefits from narratives that prioritize competition (wealthy elites) vs. cooperation (collective wellbeing)

---

**Strongest Position for AP:** **Complicate (#3)**

**Why:** This position enables the strongest essay because it:
- Avoids the weakness of choosing one side in a false binary
- Demonstrates complex thinking by showing how both forces operate simultaneously
- Allows for nuanced evidence and acknowledgment of counterarguments
- Creates opportunity for sophistication through historical context (what moment in history requires) or M6 analysis (whose interests are served by prioritizing competition)
- Applies M4 by moving up the hierarchy from "which force?" to "when and how to apply each force?" to "what does this reveal about our current values and challenges?"

A "complicate" thesis might sound like: "The debate between Smith's competitive self-interest and Mead's cooperative care presents a false choice—human progress has always required both forces operating at different scales and for different purposes. However, our era's defining challenges, from climate change to pandemics to technological risks, increasingly reveal the limits of competition-first thinking and demand that we rebuild institutions prioritizing cooperation without abandoning competition's motivational power."

### Prompt 3: Individual vs. Institutional Responsibility for Social Change

**The Prompt:**

In a 1963 letter from Birmingham Jail, Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. wrote: "Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere. We are caught in an inescapable network of mutuality, tied in a single garment of destiny. Whatever affects one directly, affects all indirectly." King argued that individuals have a moral obligation to confront injustice directly, even when doing so means breaking unjust laws.

However, the sociologist C. Wright Mills argued that social problems require institutional solutions: "Neither the life of an individual nor the history of a society can be understood without understanding both." Mills contended that personal troubles become public issues only when we address them through systemic change—reforming laws, institutions, and power structures rather than relying on individual moral action.

Write an essay that argues your position on the responsibility of individuals versus institutions for creating social change. Use appropriate evidence from your reading, experience, or observations to support your argument.

---

**Core Claim (The Debate):**
Should social change come primarily through individual moral action and personal responsibility (King's emphasis on individual conscience and direct action), or through institutional reform and systemic change (Mills's emphasis on structural solutions)?

**Complexity (What Makes This Debatable):**
This is debatable because:
- Both individuals and institutions clearly matter—civil rights required both individual activists (Rosa Parks, King) and institutional changes (Civil Rights Act, court decisions)
- Chicken-and-egg problem: individuals can't change institutions without power, but institutions are made of individuals making choices
- Defining "responsibility" is complex—moral responsibility vs. practical effectiveness vs. legal obligation
- Scale and timeframe: individual action is immediate but limited; institutional change is powerful but slow
- Risk of false solutions: emphasizing individual responsibility can let institutions off the hook ("just recycle more" instead of regulating corporations); emphasizing institutions can enable individual inaction ("I can't change the system")
- Different problems may require different approaches: some injustices require immediate individual intervention, others require policy change
- Power dynamics: those with institutional power have different responsibilities than those without it

**Three Positions (M4 Claim Hierarchy):**

**1. AGREE with King: Individuals have primary responsibility for social change**

*Supporting reasoning:*
- Institutions are made of individuals making choices—institutional change requires individuals willing to act first
- Historical examples: King, Gandhi, Malala—individuals who catalyzed movements through personal courage
- Waiting for institutions to change is an excuse for inaction; injustice demands immediate response
- Individual action creates moral pressure that forces institutional change (boycotts, protests, civil disobedience)
- Institutions are inherently conservative (designed for stability); change requires individual disruption
- Personal responsibility for our complicity: if we benefit from unjust systems, we have obligation to resist
- "Be the change" principle: modeling the values we want to see influences culture, which eventually influences institutions
- Institutional change without individual moral transformation is hollow (laws change but hearts don't)

*Why this is limited for AP:*
This position has moral force but risks ignoring structural constraints. Individual action is necessary but not sufficient—Rosa Parks's courage required the institutional apparatus of NAACP legal strategy, media attention, and political pressure. Overemphasizing individual responsibility can also blame victims ("just work harder," "make better choices") while ignoring systemic barriers. It doesn't account for how institutions shape what individual actions are possible or effective.

**2. AGREE with Mills: Institutions must change for meaningful social progress**

*Supporting reasoning:*
- Individual charity doesn't solve poverty; we need living wage laws, affordable housing policy, progressive taxation
- Individual recycling won't solve climate crisis; we need regulation of corporate emissions and fossil fuel subsidies
- Civil rights weren't won by individual kindness but by legal and political victories (Brown v. Board, Voting Rights Act)
- Power is structural: individuals can change their own behavior, but systems determine opportunities and outcomes
- Mills's insight: "personal troubles" (one person can't afford healthcare) become "public issues" (healthcare system is broken) when we analyze root causes
- Focusing on individual responsibility is often a way to avoid institutional accountability (corporations promoting "personal carbon footprint" to avoid regulation)
- Scale: individual actions affect dozens of people; institutional changes affect millions
- Sustainability: individual heroism is exhausting; institutional change embeds values in systems that persist

*Why this is limited for AP:*
This position is structurally sophisticated but risks several problems: it can enable individual passivity ("nothing I do matters until the system changes"), it doesn't explain how institutions change without individual action, and it may underestimate the role of cultural/moral shifts that precede legal change. It also risks technocratic thinking—assuming expertise and policy can solve problems that require moral transformation and relationship-building.

**3. COMPLICATE: Individual and institutional responsibility are inseparable; effective change requires both, strategically deployed**

*Supporting reasoning:*
- False binary: King himself engaged in both individual moral witness AND strategic institutional pressure (organizing, litigation, legislation)
- Mills didn't dismiss individual agency—he argued for understanding how individual troubles connect to public issues, which requires individuals to see and act on these connections
- The relationship is dialectical: individual action creates pressure for institutional change; institutional change empowers and protects individual action
  - Example: Civil rights activists' individual courage → media attention and moral pressure → Civil Rights Act → legal protection for more activism
- Different problems, different strategies:
  - Immediate harm (someone being attacked): individual intervention required
  - Systemic patterns (discriminatory hiring): institutional solution (policy change) required
  - Cultural norms (sexism, racism): both individual modeling and institutional accountability
- Position in power matters: those with institutional power (legislators, CEOs, judges) have different responsibilities than those without
- The real question: not either/or, but how do we build movements that combine individual moral action with strategic institutional pressure?
- Sophistication: both positions can be co-opted—individual responsibility rhetoric can distract from institutional accountability; institutional focus can enable elite-driven change without grassroots power

*Why this is strongest for AP:*
This position enables the strongest essay because it:
- Resolves the apparent contradiction between King and Mills by showing they address different aspects of the same process
- Demonstrates claim hierarchy by moving from "who is responsible?" to "how do individual and institutional change interact?" to "what does this reveal about how change actually happens?"
- Allows for diverse evidence from different domains (civil rights, environmental justice, labor movements, etc.)
- Enables sophistication through historical analysis (how successful movements combined both) or M6 analysis (who benefits when we focus only on individual responsibility? who benefits when we focus only on institutional change?)
- Acknowledges tensions within the position itself (risk of both approaches being co-opted)

---

**Strongest Position for AP:** **Complicate (#3)**

**Why:** This position allows the writer to:
- Show that King and Mills aren't actually opposed—both understood the need for individual and institutional change
- Demonstrate sophisticated thinking about how change happens (not just what should happen)
- Use M4 to build claim hierarchy from basic positions to strategic thinking about power and change
- Apply M6 to examine whose interests are served by either/or framing (elites benefit when individuals are blamed for systemic problems; elites also benefit when "institutional change" means top-down reform without grassroots power)
- Develop nuanced analysis that acknowledges different contexts require different approaches
- Avoid the weakness of one-sided arguments while maintaining a clear, defendable position

A "complicate" thesis might sound like: "The debate between individual and institutional responsibility for social change presents a false dichotomy that obscures how change actually happens. As the civil rights movement demonstrates, meaningful progress requires individuals willing to take moral action AND strategic efforts to transform institutions—neither sufficient alone, but powerful in combination. The real question is not who bears responsibility, but how we build movements that hold both individuals and institutions accountable while recognizing that those with institutional power bear correspondingly greater responsibility for structural change."
