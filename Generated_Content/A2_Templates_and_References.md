## 2. Claim Hierarchy Outline Template

Use this structure when planning an argument essay.

```
THESIS (Your overall position on the issue)
├─ State your nuanced position in one sentence
└─ Preview your reasoning (optional)

INTRODUCTION FRAMEWORK
├─ THEY SAY (What others argue about this issue)
│   └─ Establish the conversation you're entering
└─ I SAY (Your position in response)
    └─ Your thesis with context

CLAIM 1 (First reason supporting your thesis)
├─ Topic sentence stating the claim
├─ EVIDENCE (data, example, quotation)
├─ COMMENTARY (explain how evidence supports claim)
└─ Link back to thesis

CLAIM 2 (Second reason supporting your thesis)
├─ Topic sentence stating the claim
├─ EVIDENCE (data, example, quotation)
├─ COMMENTARY (explain how evidence supports claim)
└─ Link back to thesis

NAYSAYER (Address counterarguments)
├─ CONCESSION (acknowledge opposing view fairly)
│   └─ "Some argue that..."
└─ REBUTTAL (explain why your position still holds)
    └─ "However, this overlooks..."

CLAIM 3 (Third reason, often your strongest)
├─ Topic sentence stating the claim
├─ EVIDENCE (data, example, quotation)
├─ COMMENTARY (explain how evidence supports claim)
└─ Link back to thesis

CONCLUSION
├─ Restate thesis in fresh language
├─ Emphasize significance (so what?)
└─ Optional: implication, call to action, or broader context
```

**Instructions**: Not every essay needs exactly 3 claims—adjust as needed. The NAYSAYER can appear anywhere after your first claim. Keep the hierarchy clear: thesis governs claims, claims govern evidence, evidence requires commentary.

## 3. Combined Analysis Reference (M1-M4)

Use all four mental models together when analyzing a text.

### The Four-Step Analysis Process

**M1: Everything Is a Choice**
- What specific choice did the author make? (word, structure, appeal, example)
- Name it precisely: "The author uses a personal anecdote" or "The author chooses formal diction"

**M2: Structure → Function → Effect (S-F-E)**
- STRUCTURE: What is it? (Describe the choice objectively)
- FUNCTION: What does it DO? (Define, illustrate, emphasize, concede, contrast)
- EFFECT: What impact does it have? (On audience's understanding, emotion, or belief)

**M3: Force Field**
- What FORCES shaped this choice?
  - **Audience**: What does this audience need or expect?
  - **Exigence**: How does this choice respond to the problem?
  - **Writer's Position**: How does the author's role enable this choice?
  - **Context**: What cultural or situational factors make this choice effective?

**M4: Claim Hierarchy**
- Where does this choice fit in the argument's HIERARCHY?
  - Does it support the thesis?
  - Does it develop a specific claim?
  - Is it evidence, or commentary on evidence?
  - Does it address counterarguments?

### Example in Action

**Text**: "According to the CDC, teen vaping rates have doubled since 2017."

- **M1**: Author chose statistical evidence from an authority
- **M2**: Structure (CDC statistic) → Function (establishes urgency) → Effect (audience sees problem as serious and recent)
- **M3**: Forces — Audience needs credible data; exigence is rising health crisis; writer's position as journalist enables citing official sources; context is skepticism about health claims
- **M4**: This is EVIDENCE supporting the claim that vaping is a growing problem, which supports the thesis that schools need stricter policies

## 4. They Say / I Say Move Reference

Three core moves for entering academic conversations. Use these to position your argument.

### Move 1: Agree with a Difference
Add a new dimension while accepting the basic argument.

- "While I agree that ___, I want to add that ___."
- "X is right that ___, but she overlooks the equally important point that ___."
- "I agree that ___, but I would argue that this is true only when ___."
- "X's argument about ___ is compelling, yet we should also consider ___."

### Move 2: Disagree Diplomatically
Challenge an argument while acknowledging its appeal or partial validity.

- "X's argument that ___ overlooks the fact that ___."
- "While X insists that ___, the evidence suggests instead that ___."
- "Though X claims ___, a closer examination reveals ___."
- "X is mistaken when she argues that ___ because ___."

### Move 3: Complicate the Debate
Reframe the conversation by showing it's more complex than either/or.

- "The debate typically focuses on ___, but we should also consider ___."
- "Rather than viewing this as a choice between ___ and ___, we should recognize that ___."
- "Both sides of the ___ debate fail to account for ___."
- "While proponents argue ___ and critics counter that ___, both overlook the underlying issue of ___."

## 5. Attribution Reference

Use varied signal phrases to integrate source material. Choose phrases that match your stance toward the source.

### Neutral Attribution
Use when reporting information objectively.

- **notes, observes, states, reports, describes, explains, illustrates, discusses**

Examples:
- Chen **describes** three phases of urban development in the 20th century.
- The study **reports** that reading scores declined by 12% over five years.

### Supportive Attribution
Use when the source strengthens your argument.

- **argues convincingly, demonstrates, confirms, establishes, proves, reveals, shows**

Examples:
- Rodriguez **demonstrates** that early intervention programs significantly improve outcomes.
- The research **confirms** what teachers have long suspected about homework effectiveness.

### Critical Attribution
Use when questioning or challenging the source.

- **claims, insists, asserts, contends, overlooks, fails to account for, underestimates**

Examples:
- While the author **claims** that technology improves learning, she **overlooks** the equity issues.
- The report **fails to account for** regional variations in implementation.

### Tentative Attribution
Use when the source is suggestive rather than definitive.

- **suggests, implies, hints at, raises the possibility, appears to indicate, seems to show**

Examples:
- The data **suggests** a correlation between funding levels and student achievement.
- This pattern **raises the possibility** that other factors may be at work.

## 8. Source Weaving Patterns Reference

Three ways to integrate multiple sources in a single paragraph.

### Pattern 1: Agreement Amplification
Show how multiple sources converge on the same point.

**Structure**: Source A + Source B → Reinforced claim

**Example**:
"Both Martinez and the WHO study confirm that early childhood nutrition has lasting cognitive effects. Martinez's longitudinal research demonstrates that nutritional interventions before age 3 correlate with higher academic achievement through high school, while the WHO data reveals similar patterns across 23 countries, suggesting this relationship transcends cultural context."

### Pattern 2: Tension/Contrast
Show how sources disagree or complicate each other.

**Structure**: Source A argues X, but Source B suggests Y → Your synthesis

**Example**:
"While Thompson argues that social media increases teen anxiety, Chen's data suggests the relationship is more complex. Thompson's survey captures teens' self-reported stress levels, but Chen's neurological study reveals that active engagement (posting, creating) correlates with decreased anxiety, whereas passive scrolling correlates with increased anxiety. The issue, then, is not social media itself but how teens use it."

### Pattern 3: Building Synthesis
Layer sources to construct a more complete understanding.

**Structure**: Source A provides X, Source B adds Y, which together reveal Z

**Example**:
"Understanding the achievement gap requires synthesizing economic and pedagogical perspectives. Rothstein's economic analysis establishes that income inequality accounts for roughly 60% of variance in test scores. However, Ladson-Billings' classroom research demonstrates that culturally responsive teaching can narrow gaps even in under-resourced schools. Together, these findings reveal that while structural inequality is the dominant factor, instructional practice matters more than resource levels alone would predict."

**Key Principle**: Never just list what sources say. Show how they RELATE—through agreement, tension, or synthesis.

## 10. Constructed Support Types Reference

When building an argument without assigned sources, use these four types of support. Ranked by persuasive strength.

| **Type** | **Description** | **Example** | **Strength** | **When to Use** |
|----------|----------------|-------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Real-World Example** | Specific, verifiable case or event | "When Seattle raised minimum wage to $15/hour in 2014, small business employment grew 1.3% annually over the next three years, according to UW research." | **Strongest** — Concrete, specific, verifiable | When you know specific facts, data, or events. Most persuasive for skeptical audiences. |
| **Logical Reasoning Chain** | Step-by-step cause-effect logic | "If students work 20+ hours weekly, they have less time for homework. Less homework time leads to lower grades. Lower grades reduce college options. Therefore, excessive work hours limit educational opportunity." | **Strong** — Shows clear thinking, reveals implications | When explaining why something matters or predicting consequences. Essential for "so what?" questions. |
| **Hypothetical Scenario** | Plausible illustration of possibility | "Imagine a high school senior who must choose between attending evening classes and working to help pay family rent. Even with strong academic potential, financial pressure forces them toward immediate income rather than long-term education." | **Moderate** — Illustrates but doesn't prove | When making abstract issues concrete or showing how a principle works in practice. Always explain after presenting. |
| **Analogy** | Comparison to more familiar situation | "Banning phones in schools is like banning pencils because students might doodle—we'd eliminate a valuable tool to prevent a minor distraction." | **Useful but weakest alone** — Highlights similarity but can oversimplify | When explaining unfamiliar concepts or revealing hidden assumptions. Must acknowledge where analogy breaks down. |

### Combining Support Types

**Strong paragraph structure**: Real-world example (what happened) → Logical reasoning (why it matters) → Anticipate objection with hypothetical (what if...)

**Example**:
"When Finland reduced standardized testing in the 1990s, student performance improved across all metrics [real-world]. This suggests that excessive testing creates anxiety that interferes with learning, and that teachers make better instructional decisions when freed from test-prep constraints [logical reasoning]. Critics worry that without tests, standards would decline, but consider countries like Norway and Denmark, which use sparse testing yet maintain high performance through strong teacher training [hypothetical defense]."

## 11. Time Allocation Reference

Use these time profiles to manage your writing process effectively.

| **Task** | **40-Min Argument Essay** (ACT Writing) | **55-Min Synthesis Essay** (AP-style) | **40-Min Argument Essay** (from knowledge) |
|----------|---------------------------|------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Reading/Analyzing** | 5-6 min (read prompt + 3 perspectives) | 15 min (read prompt + 6-7 sources, annotate) | 2-3 min (read prompt, understand issue) |
| **Planning** | 5-6 min (map perspectives, choose position, outline) | 8 min (matrix, choose 3-4 sources, outline synthesis structure) | 6-7 min (brainstorm support, outline claims + counterargument) |
| **Writing** | 25 min (intro + 3 body ¶s + conclusion) | 28 min (intro + 3-4 body ¶s + conclusion) | 27 min (intro + 3 body ¶s + conclusion) |
| **Reviewing** | 3-4 min (clarity, citation format, thesis alignment) | 4 min (attribution accuracy, citation format, synthesis quality) | 4-5 min (evidence clarity, argument flow, grammar) |

### Key Principles

1. **Never skip planning**: 5-8 minutes of outlining saves 10-15 minutes of stalled writing
2. **For synthesis**: Spend MORE time reading/planning, LESS time writing—your outline should be so clear that writing is fast
3. **For ACT**: Spend equal time on perspectives analysis and on planning your own position
4. **Reviewing is non-negotiable**: Always save 3-5 minutes, even if you must trim writing time
5. **If you finish early**: Use extra time to strengthen your weakest paragraph, not to add a new one

## 12. 3-Point Rubric Reference

Use this to self-assess your drafts. Most essays fall in the "Adequate" category—your goal is to revise toward "Strong."

| **Criterion** | **Weak (1)** | **Adequate (2)** | **Strong (3)** |
|---------------|-------------|------------------|---------------|
| **Position** | Binary (yes/no, good/bad) with no qualification | Clear position with some context or qualification | Nuanced position that acknowledges complexity while maintaining clear stance |
| **Evidence** | Minimal or vague ("studies show," "people say") | Adequate specific evidence, but may rely too heavily on one type | Sophisticated integration of multiple evidence types (data, examples, reasoning) |
| **Counterargument** | Ignores opposition entirely, or dismisses it without engagement | Acknowledges other views but treats them superficially | Engages opposition thoughtfully, conceding valid points before rebutting |
| **Organization** | Serial structure (first this, then that) with weak connections | Organized by argument with clear topic sentences and transitions | Hierarchical claim structure with claims building toward thesis; sophisticated transitions show relationships |
| **Source Use** (synthesis only) | Lists what sources say without showing relationships | Shows some source relationships but mostly within single paragraphs | Weaves sources throughout essay to build synthesis; sources complicate each other |
| **Analysis Depth** (analytical writing) | Identifies choices but doesn't explain effect or purpose | Explains what choices do (function) and some effect | Uses full S-F-E chain and connects to rhetorical situation (Force Field) |
| **Style** | Informal or unclear phrasing; grammar issues interfere with meaning | Generally clear with few errors; some awkward phrasing | Precise language, varied sentence structure, fluent integration of sources |

### How to Use This Rubric

1. **During drafting**: Aim for "Adequate" across all criteria—don't let perfectionism slow you down
2. **During revision**: Identify your weakest criterion and revise specifically to move from Weak → Adequate or Adequate → Strong
3. **Final check**: If any criterion is still "Weak," prioritize fixing that before polishing "Adequate" areas

### Most Common Weak → Strong Moves

- **Position**: Add "When..." or "In contexts where..." to show your position has boundaries
- **Evidence**: Add one specific real-world example to support abstract reasoning
- **Counterargument**: Move from "Some say X, but they're wrong" to "X is valid when..., but in this context Y matters more because..."
- **Organization**: Revise topic sentences to show how each claim supports your thesis (not just what the paragraph will discuss)

## 13. Nuance Ladder Reference

Use this ladder to revise flat positions into nuanced arguments. Each level adds sophistication.

### The Three Levels

| **Level** | **Structure** | **Example: "Social media is harmful to teens"** |
|-----------|---------------|------------------------------------------------|
| **Level 1: Flat** | X is good/bad | "Social media is harmful to teens because it causes anxiety and reduces face-to-face interaction." |
| **Level 2: Qualified** | X is good/bad in context Y because... | "Social media is harmful to teens when used passively for comparison, because scrolling feeds anxiety while creating the illusion of connection." |
| **Level 3: Nuanced** | While X has merit in context Y, it fails in context Z because..., suggesting we need... | "While social media can harm teens who use it passively for social comparison, it enables creative expression and community-building for marginalized teens who lack local peer groups. Rather than debating whether social media is good or bad, we should teach teens to recognize and resist comparison-driven scrolling while leveraging platforms for active creation and authentic connection." |

### How to Climb the Ladder

**From Level 1 to Level 2**: Add CONTEXT
- Ask: "When is this true? Under what conditions?"
- Add: "when...", "in contexts where...", "for people who..."

**From Level 2 to Level 3**: Add TENSION and SYNTHESIS
- Ask: "What's valid about the opposing view? When does my position NOT hold?"
- Structure: "While X [opposing view] in context A, Y [your view] in context B, suggesting Z [synthesis]"

## Worked Example 1: Technology in Schools

**Level 1 (Flat)**:
"Schools should ban smartphones because they distract students from learning."

**Level 2 (Qualified)**:
"Schools should restrict smartphone use during instructional time because constant notifications fragment attention, making it difficult for students to engage deeply with complex material."

**Level 3 (Nuanced)**:
"While unrestricted smartphone access during class does fragment attention and reduce engagement with complex material, outright bans ignore smartphones' legitimate educational uses—research, collaboration tools, and accessibility features for students with disabilities. Rather than choosing between unfettered access and complete prohibition, schools should teach students to recognize contexts requiring sustained attention (lectures, reading, problem-solving) versus contexts where phones enhance learning (research, documentation, communication with absent peers). The goal is not to eliminate distraction but to develop metacognitive awareness about when devices serve learning and when they undermine it."

**What Changed**: The Level 3 version acknowledges both the problem (distraction) and the opposing view (educational value), then synthesizes them into a more sophisticated position (context-dependent use + metacognitive awareness).

---

## Worked Example 2: Paying College Athletes

**Level 1 (Flat)**:
"College athletes should be paid because they generate revenue for their schools."

**Level 2 (Qualified)**:
"College athletes in revenue-generating sports like football and basketball should be paid because they dedicate 40+ hours weekly to their sport while universities profit from ticket sales, television contracts, and merchandise."

**Level 3 (Nuanced)**:
"While athletes in major revenue sports like football and basketball clearly deserve compensation given the time demands and commercial value of their labor, direct payment creates significant equity problems—would swimmers and track athletes also be paid? how much? funded how? A more equitable approach allows all athletes to profit from their name, image, and likeness (NIL) while preserving scholarship funding across all sports. This shifts the question from whether athletes deserve compensation (they do) to how we compensate them without dismantling opportunities in non-revenue sports."

**What Changed**: The Level 3 version accepts the premise (athletes deserve compensation), anticipates complications (equity across sports), and proposes a synthesis that addresses both concerns (NIL rights rather than direct payment).

### Practice Prompt

Take this flat position and revise it through all three levels:

**Flat**: "Schools should require students to take a personal finance course because most adults don't understand money management."

Try it yourself, then check your revision:
- Did you add context at Level 2? (When? For whom? Under what conditions?)
- Did you acknowledge tensions at Level 3? (What's valid about opposing views? What complicates your position?)
- Did you synthesize at Level 3? (What more sophisticated position emerges from the tension?)
