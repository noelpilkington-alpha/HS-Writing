# A1 L08: Structure -> Function -> Effect (M2) -- Content Items

---

## M2 Introduction: The Three Levels of Analytical Observation

**S = Structure:** Name the device, technique, or choice. ("Paine uses the metaphor 'summer soldier.'")
- This is IDENTIFICATION. Anyone can do this. It's the starting line, not the finish.

**F = Function:** What does it DO? How does it work in the text? ("The metaphor creates a category of fake commitment -- soldiers who only show up when it's easy.")
- This is INTERPRETATION. You're explaining the mechanism -- how the device operates.

**E = Effect:** Why does it MATTER? What is the impact on the audience, argument, or meaning? ("This shames wavering colonists by making inaction feel like a character flaw, not a practical decision.")
- This is SIGNIFICANCE. You're connecting the device to the text's larger purpose.

**The chain:** S -> F -> E. Each link connects to the next. If you stop at S, you've identified. If you stop at F, you've interpreted. Only when you reach E have you analyzed.

**Common trap:** Most students stop at S and think they're done. Naming a metaphor is not analyzing a metaphor. S is necessary but insufficient.

---

## Transition Signals for S-F-E Moves

See your Transition Reference sheet (Transition_Reference.md) for signal phrases that connect S->F and F->E. The S-F-E Move Signals section gives you the exact language for each analytical move.

Quick reminder:
- S->F signals: "This serves to...", "By doing so...", "The function of this is..."
- F->E signals: "Which suggests...", "The effect is...", "This ultimately...", "As a result, the reader..."
- Direct to E: "This matters because...", "The significance is...", "This reveals..."

## One-Level Test (Supported Practice): 5 Analytical Statements

**Directions:** Classify each statement as reaching **S only**, **S+F**, or **S+F+E**. Then EXTEND the incomplete ones to reach the full S+F+E chain, using at least one transition signal from your Transition Reference.

---

### OLT-01
**Statement:** "Paine uses a rhetorical question in the opening of his argument."

**Level:** S only

**What's missing:** The statement identifies the device (rhetorical question) but does not explain what it does in the text (Function) or why it matters (Effect). It is pure identification.

**Extended version:** Paine uses a rhetorical question in the opening of his argument. **This serves to** position the reader as someone who must actively decide rather than passively observe -- the question demands an internal answer. **As a result, the reader** feels personally implicated in the political crisis, making neutrality feel impossible before Paine has even presented his case.

---

### OLT-02
**Statement:** "Chief Joseph repeats the phrase 'from where the sun now stands' at the end of his speech."

**Level:** S only

**What's missing:** The statement names the repetition and its location but says nothing about what the repetition accomplishes (Function) or its impact on the audience (Effect).

**Extended version:** Chief Joseph repeats the phrase "from where the sun now stands" at the end of his speech. **By doing so,** he anchors his surrender in a specific, physical moment in time, making the decision feel permanent and ceremonial rather than casual. **This ultimately** transforms a military defeat into a solemn vow, forcing his audience to witness not weakness but dignity -- which makes the injustice he has endured harder to dismiss.

---

### OLT-03
**Statement:** "Paine compares tyranny to hell, which creates a sense of moral urgency by framing the conflict as a battle between good and evil rather than a political dispute."

**Level:** S+F

**What's missing:** The statement identifies the device (comparison of tyranny to hell) and explains what it does (creates moral urgency, reframes the conflict). But it does not reach Effect -- it doesn't explain why this reframing matters for Paine's PURPOSE or what it does to the AUDIENCE.

**Extended version:** Paine compares tyranny to hell, which creates a sense of moral urgency by framing the conflict as a battle between good and evil rather than a political dispute. **The effect is** that colonists who might have viewed resistance as optional now feel they are choosing between righteousness and damnation -- making inaction a moral failure, not a reasonable preference.

---

### OLT-04
**Statement:** "Chief Joseph uses short, simple sentences throughout his surrender speech, which strips his language down to raw declaration and removes any rhetorical flourish that could be dismissed as performance."

**Level:** S+F

**What's missing:** The statement identifies the device (short, simple sentences) and explains the mechanism (strips language to raw declaration, removes rhetorical flourish). But it stops before reaching Effect -- it doesn't explain what this plainness DOES to the audience or how it serves Chief Joseph's larger purpose.

**Extended version:** Chief Joseph uses short, simple sentences throughout his surrender speech, which strips his language down to raw declaration and removes any rhetorical flourish that could be dismissed as performance. **This matters because** the plainness makes each statement land like a fact rather than an appeal, giving the audience no emotional distance from the reality of what has been done to his people -- and making the speech impossible to argue against.

---

### OLT-05
**Statement:** "Paine opens with 'These are the times that try men's souls,' using the abstract noun 'souls' instead of a concrete word like 'patience' or 'resolve.' **This serves to** elevate the stakes from a practical inconvenience to a spiritual test. **The effect is** that readers understand the revolution not as a matter of taxes or territory but as a trial of their fundamental character -- meaning that refusing to act is not just politically unwise but a failure of who they are."

**Level:** S+F+E

**What's missing:** Nothing. This is a complete S-F-E chain. The Structure is identified (word choice: "souls" over "patience" or "resolve"), the Function is explained (elevates stakes from practical to spiritual), and the Effect reaches significance (makes refusal a character failure, not just a political miscalculation). Notice how the transition signals ("This serves to" and "The effect is") mark each move clearly.

## Independent Practice: Write 3 S-F-E Chains from SP-030 (Paine)

Using the same Paine excerpt from L07 ("The Crisis"), write 3 complete S-F-E chains. Each chain must:
- Start from a **different** passage element (not the same device three times)
- Use at least one transition signal from the Transition Reference
- Reach E (significance/effect on audience or argument)

**Suggested elements to analyze** (choose 3):
1. The opening sentence: "These are the times that try men's souls."
2. The contrast between "summer soldier" and the committed patriot
3. The direct address to the reader/audience
4. The analogy comparing tyranny to hell ("Tyranny, like hell, is not easily conquered")
5. The shift from philosophical opening to concrete argument
6. The emotional appeal through personal voice

---

### Model Answer 1: The contrast between "summer soldier" and the committed patriot

Paine creates a contrast between the "summer soldier and the sunshine patriot" and those who remain committed through crisis. **By doing so,** he divides the colonial population into two clear categories -- those whose loyalty depends on comfort and those whose loyalty is principled -- eliminating any middle ground where a colonist could feel neutral without feeling judged. **This ultimately** pressures wavering readers to self-identify: if they do nothing, they have already placed themselves in the "summer soldier" category, which means Paine's metaphor does not just describe cowardice but actively produces the shame that makes cowardice harder to choose.

---

### Model Answer 2: The analogy comparing tyranny to hell

Paine draws an analogy between tyranny and hell, writing "Tyranny, like hell, is not easily conquered." **The function of this** comparison is to recast the political struggle as a cosmic one -- tyranny is not an inconvenient policy but a force of evil, and resisting it is not optional but morally necessary. **As a result, the reader** is positioned not as a citizen weighing costs and benefits but as a soul facing damnation or salvation, which makes the decision to fight feel less like a choice and more like an obligation built into the fabric of morality itself.

---

### Model Answer 3: The direct address to the reader/audience

Paine addresses his audience directly, speaking in the second person and using inclusive pronouns like "we" alongside pointed references to "you." **This works by** collapsing the distance between writer and reader -- Paine is not lecturing from outside the crisis but standing inside it, making the reader a participant rather than a spectator. **This reveals** that Paine's rhetorical strategy depends on eliminating the possibility of detachment: if the reader is already inside the argument, they cannot observe it neutrally, and the only remaining question is whether they will act or be exposed as the "sunshine patriot" Paine has already defined.

## Transfer Prompt

**Prompt:** "Take your best Choice Audit entry from L07 (Gettysburg Address). Convert it into a full S-F-E chain. Is this better than what you wrote before? Why?"

### Model Response

**Original Choice Audit entry (sample):** "Lincoln chooses to open with 'Four score and seven years ago' instead of saying 'eighty-seven years ago.' This is a word choice that sounds more formal and biblical."

**Converted S-F-E chain:** Lincoln opens the Gettysburg Address with "Four score and seven years ago" rather than the plain "eighty-seven years ago." **This serves to** invoke the cadence of scripture and founding documents, making the speech feel like a continuation of sacred American tradition rather than a wartime update. **The significance is** that by anchoring his argument in this biblical register, Lincoln frames the soldiers' deaths not as casualties of a political conflict but as sacrifices in a covenant -- which means the audience must honor them by completing the work, or else break a promise that predates their own lives.

**Reflection:** The S-F-E version is stronger because the Choice Audit entry stopped at identification and a surface-level interpretation ("sounds more formal and biblical"). It named the choice but never explained the mechanism or connected it to Lincoln's purpose. The S-F-E chain pushes past "what" into "how it works" and "why it matters," which is the difference between observation and analysis.
