### ARG-01 | Course: 2

**Topic:** The value of failure in education

---

In his 1910 lecture "The Moral Equivalent of War," William James argued that struggle and hardship are essential to building character, suggesting that ease and comfort alone cannot produce resilient, capable individuals. Many educators today echo this sentiment, claiming that students learn more from failure than from success and that schools should create more opportunities for students to experience productive struggle rather than shielding them from difficulty.

Write an essay that argues your position on the extent to which failure is a valuable part of the educational experience.

In your response you should do the following:

- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position.
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning.
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning.
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument.

---

**Viable Positions (for scoring guidance):**
1. Failure is essential to learning; schools should deliberately build in opportunities for productive struggle and revision.
2. Failure can be harmful when students lack support systems; schools should prioritize scaffolded success to build confidence.
3. Failure is valuable only under specific conditions -- when students have the resources, mentorship, and emotional support to learn from it; otherwise it reinforces inequality.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might explore how the value of failure depends on systemic factors (socioeconomic background, school resources), distinguish between types of failure (low-stakes revision vs. high-stakes consequences), or examine how cultural attitudes toward failure vary and shape educational outcomes.

### ARG-02 | Course: 2

**Topic:** Mandatory community service for high school students

---

Many school districts across the country now require students to complete a set number of community service hours in order to graduate. Proponents argue that mandatory service cultivates civic responsibility and empathy, while critics contend that compelled volunteerism is a contradiction in terms -- that service loses its moral value when it is required rather than freely chosen.

Write an essay that argues your position on whether high schools should require community service for graduation.

In your response you should do the following:

- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position.
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning.
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning.
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument.

---

**Viable Positions (for scoring guidance):**
1. Mandatory service is justified because exposure to community needs builds habits of civic engagement that persist beyond high school.
2. Requiring service undermines its purpose; schools should encourage but not mandate volunteerism.
3. A modified approach -- such as offering service-learning courses rather than hour requirements -- preserves the educational value while respecting student autonomy.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might interrogate the definition of "volunteerism" when compelled, examine whether mandatory service disproportionately burdens students who already work or have caregiving responsibilities, or consider how the structure of the requirement (rigid hours vs. integrated learning) changes its impact.

### ARG-03 | Course: 2

**Topic:** The role of arts education in public schools

---

In his 1916 work *Democracy and Education*, John Dewey argued that aesthetic experience is not a luxury but a fundamental part of how human beings make meaning, and that education which neglects the arts produces incomplete thinkers. Today, many public schools facing budget pressures have reduced or eliminated arts programs, prioritizing subjects measured by standardized assessments. Some educators and policymakers defend these cuts as practical, while others insist that arts education is essential to a well-rounded curriculum.

Write an essay that argues your position on the role that arts education should play in public schools.

In your response you should do the following:

- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position.
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning.
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning.
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument.

---

**Viable Positions (for scoring guidance):**
1. Arts education is essential and should be protected from budget cuts because it develops creativity, emotional intelligence, and critical thinking that transfer across disciplines.
2. In a world of limited resources, schools must prioritize literacy and numeracy; arts can be pursued through extracurricular or community programs.
3. The debate is not whether arts matter but how they are integrated; arts should be woven into existing subjects rather than treated as standalone courses vulnerable to cuts.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might consider whose definition of "the arts" is being used and what gets excluded, examine the tension between instrumental justifications for art (it boosts test scores) and intrinsic ones (it has value in itself), or analyze how socioeconomic disparities shape access to arts education outside of school.

### ARG-04 | Course: 3L

**Topic:** The ethics of separating an artist's work from their personal conduct

---

Many literary scholars and cultural critics have long debated whether an artist's personal behavior should affect how audiences engage with their creative work. In his 1849 essay "The Poetic Principle," Edgar Allan Poe argued that a poem's value lies in the beauty and pleasure it produces, independent of any moral or didactic purpose -- a position that many have extended to mean art should be judged on aesthetic merit alone, apart from its creator's character. Yet others argue that art is inseparable from the conditions and character of its creation -- that to ignore an artist's harmful actions is to perpetuate the systems that enabled them.

Write an essay that argues your position on the extent to which an audience should separate an artist's creative work from the artist's personal conduct.

In your response you should do the following:

- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position.
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning.
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning.
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument.

---

**Viable Positions (for scoring guidance):**
1. Art must be evaluated on its own terms; conflating aesthetic merit with moral biography impoverishes our engagement with important works.
2. Consuming and celebrating the work of harmful individuals provides them with cultural capital and financial reward, making separation irresponsible.
3. The answer depends on context -- the severity of the conduct, whether the artist is living or dead, and whether the harmful views are embedded in the work itself all matter.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might distinguish between private consumption and public celebration, examine how power dynamics determine which artists face consequences and which do not, explore the tension between historical works (whose creators cannot benefit) and contemporary ones, or consider whether "separating art from artist" is itself a position that reflects privilege.

### ARG-05 | Course: 3L

**Topic:** The value of a college degree versus alternative pathways

---

Many educators and economists argue that a four-year college degree remains the most reliable pathway to economic stability and informed citizenship. However, a growing number of voices -- including employers, trade organizations, and workforce development experts -- contend that the emphasis on college has devalued alternative pathways such as apprenticeships, trade certifications, and entrepreneurship, leaving many capable young people burdened with debt and without practical skills. As college costs continue to rise, the question of what constitutes a worthwhile education has become increasingly urgent.

Write an essay that argues your position on whether a four-year college degree should be considered the expected pathway for most high school graduates.

In your response you should do the following:

- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position.
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning.
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning.
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument.

---

**Viable Positions (for scoring guidance):**
1. A college degree provides irreplaceable intellectual and social development; society should focus on making it accessible rather than devaluing it.
2. The cultural expectation of college has created a crisis of debt and credential inflation; alternative pathways deserve equal respect and investment.
3. The real issue is not college vs. alternatives but the false binary itself; a flexible system that allows people to move between education and work throughout their lives would serve more people.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might analyze how the college debate intersects with class and race, examine the difference between the economic argument for college and the civic or intellectual one, consider how the "value" of education changes depending on what we measure, or explore how other countries structure post-secondary pathways differently.

### ARG-06 | Course: 3L

**Topic:** The obligation to preserve versus modernize historical monuments and public spaces

---

Many historians argue that historical monuments and public spaces serve as essential records of collective memory, preserving the values and narratives of past generations for the education of future ones. Others contend that public spaces should reflect the values of the present community, and that preserving monuments uncritically can entrench narratives that marginalize or harm certain groups. The tension between preservation and modernization raises fundamental questions about who controls public memory and for what purpose.

Write an essay that argues your position on how communities should approach the preservation or alteration of historical monuments and public spaces.

In your response you should do the following:

- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position.
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning.
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning.
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument.

---

**Viable Positions (for scoring guidance):**
1. Historical monuments should be preserved because altering the physical record distorts collective memory and sets a dangerous precedent for erasing history.
2. Public spaces belong to the present community and should be updated to reflect evolving values; preservation of harmful symbols inflicts ongoing harm.
3. Context rather than removal is the best approach -- monuments can be reframed with additional signage, relocation to museums, or the addition of counter-monuments that complicate the original narrative.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might examine who has historically controlled monument-building and what narratives were excluded, analyze the difference between history and commemoration (preserving a record vs. honoring a figure), consider how the debate varies across different types of monuments, or explore the tension between democratic decision-making and expert historical judgment.

### ARG-07 | Course: 4

**Topic:** The tension between individual meritocracy and systemic structural advantage

---

In his 1841 essay "Self-Reliance," Ralph Waldo Emerson declared, "Trust thyself: every heart vibrates to that iron string," articulating a vision of individual potential unconstrained by external circumstance. This belief in self-determination remains deeply embedded in many cultural narratives, where success is attributed primarily to individual effort, talent, and perseverance. Yet sociologists and economists increasingly demonstrate that structural factors -- including wealth inheritance, geographic location, and institutional access -- profoundly shape individual outcomes, often more than personal effort alone.

Write an essay that argues your position on the extent to which individual success is a product of personal merit versus structural advantage.

In your response you should do the following:

- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position.
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning.
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning.
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument.

---

**Viable Positions (for scoring guidance):**
1. Individual merit remains the primary driver of success; structural explanations risk undermining personal agency and accountability.
2. Structural factors are so determinative that meritocracy is largely a myth; policy should focus on equalizing conditions rather than rewarding individual achievement.
3. Both forces operate simultaneously, but the cultural narrative of meritocracy is itself a structural force -- it justifies inequality by attributing systemic outcomes to individual choices, making it harder to address root causes.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might analyze how the meritocracy narrative functions ideologically (who benefits from the belief), examine how merit itself is socially constructed (which talents and efforts "count"), explore how different societies define and distribute opportunity, or consider the psychological and political consequences of either narrative taken to its extreme.

### ARG-08 | Course: 4

**Topic:** The responsibility of the present generation to future generations

---

In his 1789 letter to James Madison, Thomas Jefferson wrote, "The earth belongs in usufruct to the living," arguing that no generation has the right to bind future ones with its debts or decisions. Yet many philosophers, ethicists, and policymakers argue the reverse -- that the present generation bears a profound moral obligation to those who will come after, and that short-term thinking in policy, economics, and culture represents a fundamental ethical failure. This tension between the sovereignty of the present and the rights of the future shapes debates on issues from fiscal policy to infrastructure investment to cultural preservation.

Write an essay that argues your position on the extent to which the present generation has a moral obligation to prioritize the interests of future generations.

In your response you should do the following:

- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position.
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning.
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning.
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument.

---

**Viable Positions (for scoring guidance):**
1. The present generation's primary obligation is to itself; future generations will have their own resources, technologies, and values, and imposing current priorities on them is presumptuous.
2. The present generation has a binding moral obligation to future generations; sacrificing long-term well-being for short-term gain is ethically indefensible.
3. The obligation exists but is limited by epistemic humility -- we cannot predict what future generations will need, so our duty is to preserve optionality and flexibility rather than to prescribe specific outcomes.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might grapple with the philosophical problem of obligations to people who do not yet exist, examine how discount rates in economics reflect (and distort) intergenerational values, analyze how different cultural and Indigenous traditions conceptualize obligations to future generations, or explore the tension between democratic governance (which responds to present voters) and long-term stewardship.

### LIT-01 | Course: 3T

**Theme:** The moral cost of ambition

---

Many works of literature explore the idea that the pursuit of greatness -- whether in power, knowledge, wealth, or status -- exacts a moral cost from those who pursue it and from those around them. The ambitious character may achieve remarkable things, yet the narrative often reveals what has been sacrificed, compromised, or destroyed in the process.

Either from your own reading or from the list below, choose a work of fiction in which a character's ambition leads to significant moral compromise or loss. Then, in a well-written essay, analyze how the author uses that character's ambition to explore a broader commentary on the relationship between aspiration and moral integrity.

*Suggested works:*
- *Macbeth* by William Shakespeare
- *Frankenstein* by Mary Shelley
- *Great Expectations* by Charles Dickens
- *The Great Gatsby* by F. Scott Fitzgerald
- *A Raisin in the Sun* by Lorraine Hansberry
- *Americanah* by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie
- *The Kite Runner* by Khaled Hosseini
- *Julius Caesar* by William Shakespeare
- *An American Tragedy* by Theodore Dreiser

You may choose another work of comparable literary merit.

Do not merely summarize the plot.

---

**Viable Analytical Approaches:**
1. Analyze how the author structures the character's arc so that ambition initially appears noble or justified before its costs become visible, and what this structure reveals about the nature of moral compromise.
2. Examine how the character's ambition affects not only themselves but those around them, using the collateral damage as the key lens for the author's commentary.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might explore how the text complicates a simple moral reading (ambition as purely destructive) by showing its generative or liberating dimensions, analyze how the social context determines whether ambition is punished or rewarded, or examine how the author's narrative choices (point of view, symbolism, structure) shape the reader's moral judgment of the ambitious character.

### LIT-02 | Course: 3T

**Theme:** The function of secrets and concealment in shaping identity

---

In many works of literature, characters conceal significant truths -- about their past, their desires, their identity, or their actions. These secrets often function not merely as plot devices but as revelations of deeper truths about the characters who keep them and the societies that make concealment necessary. The act of hiding can be an assertion of power, a survival strategy, or a source of profound self-alienation.

Either from your own reading or from the list below, choose a work of fiction in which a character's secret or act of concealment plays a central role. Then, in a well-written essay, analyze how the author uses that concealment to develop a larger meaning about identity, power, or social expectation.

*Suggested works:*
- *Hamlet* by William Shakespeare
- *Jane Eyre* by Charlotte Bronte
- *The Scarlet Letter* by Nathaniel Hawthorne
- *Passing* by Nella Larsen
- *Atonement* by Ian McEwan
- *The Remains of the Day* by Kazuo Ishiguro
- *Invisible Man* by Ralph Ellison
- *Persepolis* by Marjane Satrapi
- *The House of the Spirits* by Isabel Allende

You may choose another work of comparable literary merit.

Do not merely summarize the plot.

---

**Viable Analytical Approaches:**
1. Analyze how the secret reflects the social structures that make concealment necessary, treating the character's hiding as an indictment of the world rather than of the individual.
2. Examine how the act of concealment transforms the character internally -- how maintaining a secret reshapes their relationships, self-perception, and capacity for authentic connection.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might explore how the text distinguishes between secrets kept for survival and secrets kept for advantage, analyze moments where concealment and revelation are both shown to be destructive, or examine how the author's use of narrative perspective (who knows, when, and how) implicates the reader in the dynamics of secrecy.

Many significant works of literature dramatize moments in which a character must choose between obedience to an institution -- a government, a church, a family, a military, a social code -- and fidelity to their own moral convictions. These conflicts rarely resolve cleanly; the works that explore them most powerfully tend to reveal the costs of both compliance and resistance, exposing the complex relationship between individual moral agency and collective structures of power.

- *All the Light We Cannot See* by Anthony Doerr

---

**Viable Analytical Approaches:**

2. Examine how the text complicates the heroism of individual resistance by showing its costs, limitations, or unintended consequences -- treating conscience not as automatically triumphant but as a force with its own dangers.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might explore how the work questions whether individual resistance is sufficient to change unjust systems or whether it serves primarily as a moral gesture, analyze how the author implicates characters who are neither fully compliant nor fully resistant, or examine how narrative form (tragedy, allegory, realism) shapes the reader's understanding of whether resistance is portrayed as noble, futile, or something more complex.

### LIT-05 | Course: 4

**Theme:** The role of storytelling and narrative within the narrative

---

In many works of literature, characters tell stories within the larger story -- they narrate their pasts, construct myths, offer competing accounts of events, or use storytelling as a means of survival, persuasion, or self-invention. These embedded narratives often call attention to the power and limitations of storytelling itself, raising questions about truth, memory, and whose version of events prevails.

Either from your own reading or from the list below, choose a work of fiction in which storytelling or narrative-making within the work plays a significant role. Then, in a well-written essay, analyze how the author uses the act of storytelling within the text to develop a larger commentary on truth, power, memory, or identity.

*Suggested works:*
- *The Canterbury Tales* by Geoffrey Chaucer
- *Heart of Darkness* by Joseph Conrad
- *One Thousand and One Nights* (traditional)
- *Beloved* by Toni Morrison
- *The Things They Carried* by Tim O'Brien
- *Life of Pi* by Yann Martel
- *The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao* by Junot Diaz
- *Homegoing* by Yaa Gyasi
- *The Metamorphosis* (*Die Verwandlung*) -- Franz Kafka
- *An Artist of the Floating World* by Kazuo Ishiguro

You may choose another work of comparable literary merit.

Do not merely summarize the plot.

---

**Viable Analytical Approaches:**
1. Analyze how the embedded narratives reveal what characters need to believe about themselves or their world, treating storytelling as a form of self-construction rather than neutral communication.
2. Examine how competing or unreliable narratives within the text expose the relationship between storytelling and power -- who gets to narrate, whose story is believed, and what is at stake in controlling the narrative.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might explore how the author uses metanarrative elements to implicate the reader in the act of interpretation, analyze the tension between storytelling as healing and storytelling as evasion or distortion, or examine how the work ultimately positions itself on the question of whether narrative can access truth or only construct it.

### LIT-06 | Course: 4

**Theme:** The weight of inherited obligation and the past's claim on the present

---

Many works of literature present characters who find themselves bound -- by family legacy, cultural expectation, historical trauma, or inherited debt -- to a past they did not choose. The tension between honoring what has been passed down and forging an independent path often drives these characters into profound internal conflict. The most complex treatments of this theme resist simple resolution, suggesting that the past's hold on the present is neither purely oppressive nor purely enriching but something more ambiguous.

Either from your own reading or from the list below, choose a work of fiction in which a character grapples with an inherited obligation or the weight of the past. Then, in a well-written essay, analyze how the author uses the character's relationship to what they have inherited to develop a theme about duty, freedom, identity, or the passage of time.

*Suggested works:*
- *Hamlet* by William Shakespeare
- *The House of Mirth* by Edith Wharton
- *Fences* by August Wilson
- *Song of Solomon* by Toni Morrison
- *The Namesake* by Jhumpa Lahiri
- *Purple Hibiscus* by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie
- *Homegoing* by Yaa Gyasi
- *Oedipus Rex* by Sophocles
- *The God of Small Things* by Arundhati Roy
- *Pachinko* by Min Jin Lee

You may choose another work of comparable literary merit.

Do not merely summarize the plot.

---

**Viable Analytical Approaches:**
1. Analyze how the author uses the inherited obligation as a lens for exploring the tension between individual identity and collective or familial identity -- how the character's struggle to define themselves is inseparable from what they have been given.
2. Examine how the text treats the past not as a static burden but as a living force that shifts in meaning as the character's understanding evolves, analyzing key moments where the character's relationship to their inheritance changes.

**Sophistication Opportunities:** Students might explore how the work refuses to resolve the tension between past and present, leaving the character (and reader) in productive ambiguity about whether inherited obligations should be embraced, rejected, or transformed. They might also analyze how the author uses structural choices (chronology, multiple perspectives, recurring motifs) to embody the theme of the past's persistence, or examine how the text distinguishes between different kinds of inheritance (cultural, material, psychological) and the different demands each makes.
