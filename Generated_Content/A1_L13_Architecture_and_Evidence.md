## The Analytical Essay Plan

**The formula:** Thesis → Structure choice → Body paragraphs (each: Claim → Evidence from text → S-F-E Commentary → Connection to thesis)

**The core analytical sentence:** [Claim] + [Evidence from text] + [S-F-E Commentary] + [Connection to thesis]

This is the sentence pattern that powers every analytical paragraph:

1. **Claim:** What are you arguing about this part of the text?
2. **Evidence:** A specific quote or detail from the text
3. **S-F-E Commentary:** Structure → Function → Effect analysis of that evidence
4. **Connection:** How does this support your thesis?

## Passage Excerpt: SP-003 -- Abraham Lincoln, Second Inaugural Address (1865)

> Fellow countrymen: At this second appearing to take the oath of the presidential office, there is less occasion for an extended address than there was at the first. Then a statement, somewhat in detail, of a course to be pursued, seemed fitting and proper. Now, at the expiration of four years, during which public declarations have been constantly called forth on every point and phase of the great contest which still absorbs the attention and engrosses the energies of the nation, little that is new could be presented. The progress of our arms, upon which all else chiefly depends, is as well known to the public as to myself; and it is, I trust, reasonably satisfactory and encouraging to all. With high hope for the future, no prediction in regard to it is ventured.
>
> On the occasion corresponding to this four years ago, all thoughts were anxiously directed to an impending civil war. All dreaded it -- all sought to avert it. While the inaugural address was being delivered from this place, devoted altogether to saving the Union without war, insurgent agents were in the city seeking to destroy it without war -- seeking to dissolve the Union, and divide effects, by negotiation. Both parties deprecated war; but one of them would make war rather than let the nation survive; and the other would accept war rather than let it perish. And the war came.
>
> One eighth of the whole population were colored slaves, not distributed generally over the Union, but localized in the Southern part of it. These slaves constituted a peculiar and powerful interest. All knew that this interest was, somehow, the cause of the war. To strengthen, perpetuate, and extend this interest was the object for which the insurgents would rend the Union, even by war; while the government claimed no right to do more than to restrict the territorial enlargement of it. Neither party expected for the war the magnitude or the duration which it has already attained. Neither anticipated that the cause of the conflict might cease with, or even before, the conflict itself should cease. Each looked for an easier triumph, and a result less fundamental and astounding. Both read the same Bible, and pray to the same God; and each invokes His aid against the other. It may seem strange that any men should dare to ask a just God's assistance in wringing their bread from the sweat of other men's faces; but let us judge not that we be not judged. The prayers of both could not be answered; that of neither has been answered fully. The Almighty has His own purposes.

*(Public domain. Full text available via National Archives and Library of Congress.)*

## Coping Model: Planning an Analytical Essay from SP-003 (Lincoln, Second Inaugural)

### Student Think-Aloud

Okay, so I need to plan an analytical essay about Lincoln's Second Inaugural Address. Let me start by figuring out my thesis.

*[First attempt -- WRONG approach]*

So Lincoln's speech is about the Civil War and how it's almost over, and he talks about slavery being the cause of the war and how both sides prayed to God. So my thesis could be: "In his Second Inaugural Address, Lincoln discusses the Civil War, slavery, and religion."

Wait. That's just... a summary. That's just listing topics. That's not analytical at all. I'm describing WHAT he talks about, not what he's DOING as a writer. Let me back up.

> **[Teaching note 1: The student catches the most common G9 error -- summarizing content instead of analyzing choices. The self-correction question below is the key pivot.]**

*[Self-correction]*

Okay, what is Lincoln CHOOSING to do here? He could have just said "we won, the South lost, let's move on." But instead he keeps using "both" -- "both read the same Bible," "both parties deprecated war," "the prayers of both." He's choosing to put the North and South together, like they're the same. Why does that choice matter? Because he's trying to reunify the country. He can't do that if he's pointing fingers.

> **[Teaching note 2: Asking "What is the author CHOOSING to do?" and "Why does this choice matter?" transforms summary into analysis. This is the Mental Model 1 pivot -- Everything Is a Choice.]**

*[Drafting thesis]*

New thesis: "In his Second Inaugural Address, Lincoln strategically uses parallel structure and shared language to collapse the moral distance between North and South, positioning reunification as a shared responsibility rather than a Northern victory."

That's about his CHOICES (parallel structure, shared language) and their PURPOSE (collapse moral distance, position reunification). Way better.

*[Choosing structure]*

Structure: Classical. Why? Because I'm making a clear, direct argument about Lincoln's rhetorical strategy. I'm not comparing two texts or solving a problem. I just need to prove my thesis with evidence, step by step.

> **[Teaching note 3: The student connects structure choice to the nature of the argument -- this is the L12 skill in action.]**

*[Planning Body 1]*

Body 1 claim: Lincoln uses parallel phrasing to place North and South in identical grammatical positions, forcing the audience to see both sides as equal participants.

Evidence: "Both read the same Bible, and pray to the same God; and each invokes His aid against the other."

Commentary -- S: parallel structure with "both" and "same"...

Hmm. I just identified the device. That's only Structure. I haven't said what it DOES or how it LANDS. Let me push to Function and Effect.

> **[Teaching note 4: This is the critical S-F-E error -- stopping at device identification. Students must be trained to push past "I see parallelism" into "what does parallelism accomplish here?" and "what does the audience experience?"]**

S-F-E Commentary:
- **S:** Parallel structure -- "Both read the same Bible, and pray to the same God" places North and South in identical grammatical slots.
- **F:** This structural mirroring functions to erase the distinction between the two sides, making it grammatically impossible to assign blame to one without assigning it to both.
- **E:** The effect on the audience is disarming -- listeners from the North who might expect a victory speech instead find themselves yoked to the South in shared culpability, which prepares them to accept Lincoln's call for reconciliation rather than punishment.

Connection to thesis: This parallel construction is Lincoln's primary tool for collapsing moral distance -- by making the two sides grammatically identical, he makes them morally identical, which is the foundation for his reunification argument.

> **[Teaching note 5: Now the commentary reaches Effect -- the audience's experience. This is the gateway standard for C.9.10.]**

Done with Body 1. That's a complete plan for one paragraph. Now I'd repeat this for Body 2 with a different piece of evidence.

## Supported Practice: Complete Plan from ES-014 (Douglass, Learning to Read)

**Passage:** See Passage Bank, ES-014 -- Frederick Douglass, excerpt from *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave* (1845).

**Summary:** In this excerpt, Douglass recounts how Mrs. Auld begins teaching him to read until Mr. Auld discovers and forbids it, declaring that education would make Douglass unfit for slavery. Douglass describes this moment as a pivotal revelation: he now understands that literacy is the "pathway from slavery to freedom," and he resolves to learn to read by any means necessary. The passage traces Douglass's transformation from passive recipient of instruction to active, strategic pursuer of knowledge.

### Model Completed Plan

**Passage:** ES-014, Frederick Douglass, *Narrative of the Life* -- Learning to Read excerpt
**Thesis:** Douglass uses a structure of reversal -- transforming Mr. Auld's prohibition into motivation -- to recast the master-slave power dynamic, positioning literacy not as a gift granted by white benefactors but as a weapon seized by the enslaved.

*(Test: About the author's choices? Yes -- "uses a structure of reversal," "recast," "positioning." Arguable? Yes -- one could argue Douglass frames literacy as liberation rather than weapon. Specific? Yes -- focuses on the reversal pattern and its effect on power dynamics.)*

**Structure choice:** ☐ Classical  ☐ Rogerian  ☐ Problem-Solution  ☐ Comparison  ☒ Cause-Effect
**Why this structure?** The thesis is fundamentally about how one event (Auld's prohibition) causes a transformation (Douglass's resolve and reframing of power). A cause-effect structure lets each body paragraph trace a stage of that transformation.

**Body Paragraph 1:**
- **Claim:** Douglass presents Mr. Auld's words not as a setback but as an inadvertent education, using direct quotation to let the oppressor reveal the mechanics of oppression.
- **Evidence:** "From that moment, I understood the pathway from slavery to freedom" -- immediately following Douglass's account of Auld's declaration that learning would make a slave unmanageable.
- **S-F-E Commentary:**
  - S: Douglass places his moment of revelation in a single, short declarative sentence directly after a long passage of Auld's indirect speech.
  - F: The structural contrast -- long, winding reported speech followed by one crisp sentence -- functions to dramatize the speed and force of Douglass's insight, as if understanding strikes like lightning.
  - E: The effect is that the reader experiences the revelation alongside Douglass; the brevity makes the sentence feel inevitable and powerful, positioning Douglass as intellectually superior to the master who has just unwittingly handed him the key to freedom.
- **Connection to thesis:** This moment is the pivot of Douglass's reversal structure -- the prohibition becomes the catalyst, and the master's attempt to maintain power is the very thing that destroys it.

**Body Paragraph 2:**
- **Claim:** Douglass reframes the language of slavery -- "unfit," "unmanageable," "no benefit" -- as evidence of literacy's liberating power, turning the slaveholder's vocabulary against the institution itself.
- **Evidence:** Douglass writes that Auld's words "sank deep into my heart" and that he now saw reading as "the pathway from slavery to freedom," adopting the very framework Auld intended as a warning and converting it into a declaration of purpose.
- **S-F-E Commentary:**
  - S: Douglass employs appropriation -- he takes Auld's reasoning (that literacy makes slaves "unfit") and repurposes the same logic with reversed value judgments.
  - F: This rhetorical appropriation functions to dismantle the master's authority from within his own language; Auld's logic, intended to justify ignorance, now justifies Douglass's pursuit of knowledge.
  - E: The effect on the reader is a sense of intellectual triumph -- we watch Douglass outthink his oppressor using the oppressor's own words, which makes the injustice of slavery feel not only cruel but intellectually absurd.
- **Connection to thesis:** This appropriation of the master's language is the core mechanism of Douglass's reversal. By seizing the slaveholder's vocabulary and inverting its purpose, Douglass enacts at the level of rhetoric exactly what his thesis promises: literacy is not given but taken, and power flows to the one who controls language.

**Body Paragraph 3 (optional):**
- Note: A third body paragraph could analyze Douglass's shift in tone from the passage's opening (grateful, passive -- describing Mrs. Auld's kindness) to its close (determined, strategic -- describing his own resolve), tracing how the cause-effect structure culminates in a complete transformation of agency. This paragraph is optional and would extend the essay but is not required to support the thesis.

## Independent Practice: Evidence + Commentary Sentences

Students write 2 evidence + commentary sentences for their own plan (Bodies 2-3) without scaffold.

Below are 2 model evidence + commentary sentences drawn from the Douglass plan above. Each demonstrates the full pattern: claim, embedded quote, S-F-E commentary reaching Effect, and connection to thesis.

### Model Sentence 1

Douglass reframes the master's language as a tool of liberation when he writes that Auld's words "sank deep into my heart," using the metaphor of depth and permanence -- a structural choice (S) that functions to show that the prohibition does not silence Douglass but instead embeds itself as unshakable resolve (F), leaving the reader with the striking impression that the slaveholder has inadvertently armed the very person he sought to disarm (E), which reinforces Douglass's larger reversal of the power dynamic central to his narrative.

### Model Sentence 2

By placing the short, declarative revelation -- "I now understood the pathway from slavery to freedom" -- immediately after a long, controlling passage of Mr. Auld's reported speech, Douglass creates a structural contrast (S) that functions to dramatize the moment of insight as sudden and irreversible, as if the entire weight of Auld's prohibition collapses into a single line of clarity (F), an effect that makes the reader feel the intellectual triumph alongside Douglass and recognize that literacy is not a gentle gift but a seized weapon (E), directly supporting the thesis that Douglass recasts the master-slave power dynamic through the act of understanding itself.

### Gateway Check

**Gateway KC C.9.10:** At least 1 of 2 independent sentences must demonstrate complete S-F-E (reaches Effect, not just Structure or Function).

- **Meets standard:** The sentence includes a specific textual detail, identifies a structural choice (S), explains what that choice accomplishes in context (F), AND describes the impact on the reader or the shift in meaning (E).
- **Does not yet meet standard:** The sentence identifies a device (S) and may explain its purpose (F) but stops short of describing the audience's experience or the deeper significance (E). *Feedback: "You've named the choice and explained what it does -- now push one step further. What does the reader FEEL or UNDERSTAND because of this choice?"*
