# A1 L06: You Already Make Choices -- Content Items

---

## M1 Introduction: Everything Is a Choice

**Core idea:** Every piece of writing is the result of deliberate choices. When you wrote your L05 essay, you made dozens of choices -- word choice, structure, opening move, evidence selected, counterargument placement. Most of those choices were instinctive. Now we are going to make them VISIBLE.

**Why this matters:** If YOUR writing is made of choices, then so is every other writer's. Analyzing a text means asking three questions:

1. What did the writer choose?
2. What could they have chosen instead?
3. Why does this choice matter?

These three questions are the engine of everything we do from this point forward. They work on YOUR writing. They work on a speech from 1877. They work on anything anyone has ever written, because writing is choosing.

### Model: Choice Audit of the L05 Model Essay (Grades vs. Pass/Fail)

The model essay from L05 argues that schools should keep traditional letter grades. Here are 3 choices the writer made, analyzed using the three questions.

**Choice 1: Opening Strategy**

| # | Question | Answer |
|---|----------|--------|
| 1a | **What did I choose?** | I opened with an analogy -- "Imagine training for a race but never being told your time" -- before stating my position. |
| 1b | **What could I have done instead?** | I could have opened with a direct statement of my position: "Schools should keep traditional letter grades." I could also have opened with a statistic about student performance under different grading systems, or with a question like "How do you know if you are improving in school?" |
| 1c | **Why did I make this choice?** | The race analogy makes the reader FEEL what it is like to lose information before I explain the argument logically. A direct thesis statement would be clear but would not create that gut-level "wait, that would be frustrating" reaction. I wanted the reader to already agree with my point emotionally before I made it intellectually. |

**Choice 2: Metaphor in Body 1**

| # | Question | Answer |
|---|----------|--------|
| 2a | **What did I choose?** | I compared grades to a GPS: "Grades function like a GPS for learning -- they do not just confirm 'you arrived' but show exactly where you are on the route." |
| 2b | **What could I have done instead?** | I could have explained the same idea without a metaphor: "Grades give students detailed information about their progress, not just whether they passed." I could also have used a different comparison, like a medical checkup (which gives you specific numbers, not just "healthy" or "sick"). |
| 2c | **Why did I make this choice?** | A GPS is something every reader has used. It makes an abstract idea -- "grades provide specific feedback" -- feel concrete and familiar. The metaphor also does double duty: it implies grades are a TOOL that helps you navigate, not a judgment that punishes you. That framing quietly counters the argument that grades cause stress. |

**Choice 3: Counterargument Concession Strategy**

| # | Question | Answer |
|---|----------|--------|
| 3a | **What did I choose?** | I conceded that pass/fail grading works well "in certain contexts, like elective courses" before arguing against it as a system-wide policy. |
| 3b | **What could I have done instead?** | I could have dismissed the stress argument entirely: "While some say grades cause stress, this is an exaggeration." I could also have conceded more broadly: "It is true that our grading system has serious flaws." |
| 3c | **Why did I make this choice?** | Conceding a narrow, specific case ("elective courses") makes me look reasonable without giving away my main argument. If I dismissed the other side entirely, I would sound defensive. If I conceded too broadly, I would undermine my own position. The narrow concession says: "I hear you, and you have a point -- but only in a limited situation, not as a general policy." |

## Part 2: Choices in a Published Text (Independent Practice)

### Passage: SP-010 -- Chief Joseph, "I Will Fight No More Forever" (1877)

Tell General Howard I know his heart. What he told me before, I have it in my heart. I am tired of fighting. Our chiefs are killed; Looking Glass is dead, Toohoolhoolzote is dead. The old men are all dead. It is the young men who say, "Yes" or "No." He who led the young men [Olikut] is dead. It is cold, and we have no blankets; the little children are freezing to death. My people, some of them, have run away to the hills, and have no blankets, no food. No one knows where they are -- perhaps freezing to death. I want to have time to look for my children, and see how many of them I can find. Maybe I shall find them among the dead. Hear me, my chiefs! I am tired. My heart is sick and sad. From where the sun now stands I will fight no more forever.

### Model Answers (reveal after student writes)

**Choice 1: Naming the Dead**

| # | Question | Answer |
|---|----------|--------|
| 1a | **What did the author choose?** | Chief Joseph names specific people who have died -- Looking Glass, Toohoolhoolzote, Olikut -- rather than speaking in generalities about losses. |
| 1b | **What could he have done instead?** | He could have said "Many of our warriors and leaders have been killed" or "We have suffered terrible losses." Either statement communicates the same basic fact without naming individuals. |
| 1c | **Why does this choice matter?** | Naming the dead forces the listener to recognize them as individual human beings, not anonymous casualties. Each name is a person General Howard's army killed. The names also accumulate -- one after another -- creating a weight that a general statement could never carry. By the time Chief Joseph reaches "The old men are all dead," the listener has already felt the losses one by one. |

**Choice 2: Short, Accumulating Sentences**

| # | Question | Answer |
|---|----------|--------|
| 2a | **What did the author choose?** | Chief Joseph uses short, blunt sentences stacked one after another: "Our chiefs are killed; Looking Glass is dead, Toohoolhoolzote is dead. The old men are all dead." Most sentences are under fifteen words. |
| 2b | **What could he have done instead?** | He could have combined these into one longer, more flowing sentence: "Our chiefs -- Looking Glass, Toohoolhoolzote, and the other old men -- have all been killed in this war." That sentence communicates the same information in a more conventional structure. |
| 2c | **Why does this choice matter?** | The short sentences sound like someone who is exhausted. There is no energy left for elaborate phrasing. Each sentence lands like a separate blow -- dead, dead, dead, all dead. The rhythm mirrors the emotional state Chief Joseph is describing: he is too tired and grief-stricken for complex speech. A long, polished sentence would sound like someone with composure. These sentences sound like someone at the end of endurance. |

**Choice 3: Addressing His Own Chiefs, Not the U.S. Army**

| # | Question | Answer |
|---|----------|--------|
| 3a | **What did the author choose?** | Near the end of the speech, Chief Joseph says "Hear me, my chiefs!" -- he addresses his own Nez Perce leaders directly, even though the speech is being delivered as a surrender to General Howard and the U.S. Army. |
| 3b | **What could he have done instead?** | He could have addressed General Howard directly: "General Howard, I surrender" or "I ask the United States for peace." He could have framed the speech entirely as a message to his enemy, which is what a typical surrender statement does. |
| 3c | **Why does this choice matter?** | By turning to his own chiefs, Chief Joseph makes this speech about his PEOPLE, not about his enemy. He is not begging for mercy from the Army -- he is explaining to his own leaders why he can no longer fight. This reframes the surrender: it is not defeat imposed from outside but a decision made from within, driven by concern for the freezing children and the missing families. The audience shift preserves Chief Joseph's dignity and authority even in the act of giving up. |

## Part 3: Transfer Bridge

**Prompt:** "What's the difference between analyzing your own choices and analyzing Chief Joseph's? What's the same?"

### Model Response

The three questions are identical in both cases: What was chosen? What was the alternative? Why does this choice matter? The process of analysis does not change when the text changes. The only real difference is that when you analyze your own writing, you KNOW why you made a choice -- you can remember your thinking. When you analyze someone else's writing, you have to INFER the reasoning from the evidence in the text. That inference is the new skill, but the framework you are using to make it is exactly the same one you just practiced on your own essay.
