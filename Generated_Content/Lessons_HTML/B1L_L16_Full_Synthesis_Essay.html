Phase 1: Teach (2 min)

55-Minute Timing Breakdown

The AP Synthesis essay is the longest FRQ (55 minutes vs. 40 for Rhetorical Analysis and Argument) because you must READ sources before writing. Here's the timing strategy:

Phase Time Task
1. Read Prompt 1 min Understand the question before reading sources
2. Rapid Source Analysis 14 min Skim all sources, build Rapid Synthesis Matrix (L13 strategy)
3. Thesis + Outline 5 min Write thesis, outline 3 body paragraphs with source assignments
4. Introduction 5 min Write intro paragraph + thesis
5. Body Paragraphs 22 min 3 body paragraphs (7-8 min each) with source integration
6. Conclusion 5 min Closing paragraph (opportunity for Row C sophistication)
7. Review 3 min Check: 3+ sources cited? Transitions? Obvious errors?

Phase 2: Model (3 min)

Annotated Model Synthesis Essay

Prompt: Some argue that environmental protection and economic growth are fundamentally in conflict, while others contend they can coexist. Develop a position on this relationship, using at least three sources.

[INTRODUCTION] For decades, policymakers have framed environmental protection and economic growth as competing priorities, as if choosing clean air meant accepting unemployment. This zero-sum framing is both inaccurate and dangerous. [THESIS — Row A] Environmental sustainability and economic prosperity are not only compatible but mutually reinforcing when policies prioritize long-term investment, account for the true costs of inaction, and ensure equitable transitions for affected workers. The evidence demonstrates that green economies create jobs, climate inaction imposes catastrophic costs, and smart policy design can address legitimate concerns about worker displacement.

[BODY ¶1: Economic Opportunity] [Row B: Sources + Commentary] Environmental investment generates substantial economic returns. According to a 2021 UN report, the global transition to renewable energy could create 24 million jobs by 2030, far exceeding the 6 million jobs lost in fossil fuel industries (Source A). This 4-to-1 job creation ratio challenges the assumption that environmental policy destroys employment. Moreover, Costa Rica's experience demonstrates that green economies can thrive: the country generates 99% of its electricity from renewables while maintaining 5% annual GDP growth and sustaining a robust ecotourism sector worth 10% of GDP (Source D). These examples reveal that the conflict between environment and economy is not inherent but results from short-term thinking that ignores long-term gains.

[BODY ¶2: Cost of Inaction] Beyond job creation, the economic case for climate action rests on a stark reality: inaction costs more than action. A 2022 McKinsey analysis projects that unmitigated climate change could reduce global GDP by 11-14% by 2050 through agricultural collapse, infrastructure damage, and forced migration, while achieving net-zero emissions requires $9.2 trillion annually but yields $43 trillion in benefits over 30 years (Source F). [Row C: Sophistication via Alternative Interpretation] Business interests like the Chamber of Commerce frame environmental regulations as imposing $14 billion in annual compliance costs (Source B), but this accounting omits the externalities—pollution-related healthcare costs, climate-driven agricultural losses, disaster recovery expenses—that dwarf regulatory costs. As one economist notes, markets already favor clean energy because solar and wind are now cheaper than coal; what businesses need is policy certainty, not lax regulation (Source C). The question is not whether environmental action is expensive, but whether we can afford the alternative.

[BODY ¶3: Just Transitions] Yet dismissing concerns about worker displacement would be both callous and politically unwise. Labor unions rightly argue that fossil fuel workers face real economic harm unless policymakers provide retraining, relocation support, and bridge income (Source E). The environment-economy debate too often ignores these human costs borne disproportionately by working-class communities. A credible climate policy must embed a "just transition" framework that protects livelihoods while advancing sustainability. Costa Rica's payment-for-ecosystem-services model demonstrates one approach: compensating affected parties creates buy-in rather than resistance (Source D). Environmental and economic goals align when policy includes rather than excludes vulnerable workers.

[CONCLUSION] [Row C: Broader Context] The environment-economy dichotomy persists because short-term electoral cycles reward politicians who defer costs, while the benefits of sustainability accrue over decades. But this political convenience is economically irrational. Green investment creates jobs, inaction guarantees catastrophe, and just transitions address worker concerns. The real conflict is not environment versus economy—it is the present versus the future. And the future, whether we like it or not, has already begun to arrive.

Scoring Annotations:

  • Row A = 1: Thesis states position ("compatible but mutually reinforcing") + line of reasoning (long-term investment, true costs, equitable transitions)
  • Row B = 4: Uses 6 sources (A, D, F, B, C, E) with S-F-E commentary throughout; sources serve the argument
  • Row C = 1: Sophistication via alternative interpretation (¶2: reframes Chamber of Commerce costs) + broader context (conclusion: short-term politics vs. long-term rationality)
  • Total: 6/6

Phase 4: Independent Writing (40 min)

Write Your Full AP Synthesis Essay

Your Task: Write a complete synthesis essay in 40 minutes. Use your finalized plan from Phase 3. Follow this timing:

  • Introduction + thesis: 5 min
  • Body paragraph 1: 7-8 min
  • Body paragraph 2: 7-8 min
  • Body paragraph 3: 7-8 min
  • Conclusion: 5 min
  • Review: 3 min (check sources cited, add transitions, fix errors)

Essay Requirements:

  • Use at least 3 sources (4-5 is strategic)
  • Cite sources parenthetically: (Source A), (Source B), etc.
  • Sources must SERVE your argument, not drive it
  • Aim for 500-700 words total

[Write your full synthesis essay here. Set a timer for 40 minutes. Begin when ready.]

As You Write, Remember:

  • Start each body paragraph with YOUR claim, not "Source X says..."
  • Use varied attribution patterns (embedded credentials, cross-source synthesis, etc.)
  • Add S-F-E commentary after each source—explain the "So what?"
  • Consider a Row C sophistication move in paragraph 3 or conclusion
