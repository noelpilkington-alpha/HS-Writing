Phase 2: Independent — Synthesis Essay (55 min)

AP Synthesis FRQ: Universal Basic Income

Directions: The following prompt is based on the accompanying seven sources. This question requires you to synthesize a variety of sources into a coherent, well-written essay. Refer to the sources to support your position; avoid mere paraphrase or summary. Your argument should be central; the sources should support this argument.

Remember to attribute both direct and indirect citations.

Essay Prompt

As automation and artificial intelligence transform labor markets, policymakers increasingly debate Universal Basic Income (UBI)—a system in which all citizens receive regular, unconditional cash payments from the government regardless of employment status. Advocates argue that UBI addresses technological unemployment, reduces poverty, and provides economic security. Critics contend that it discourages work, costs too much, and fails to address systemic inequities.

Assignment: Read the following sources carefully. Then, write an essay that develops your position on whether governments should implement Universal Basic Income. Use at least three of the sources to support your argument. Indicate clearly which sources you are drawing from, whether through direct quotation, paraphrase, or summary. You may cite sources as Source A, Source B, etc., or by the descriptions in parentheses.

Source Materials (Read for 15 minutes)

Source A (Economic Policy Institute Report)

Excerpt from "Labor Market Impacts of Automation" (2024), Economic Policy Institute

Summary: This report analyzes job displacement due to automation across sectors. Key findings: Manufacturing, retail, and transportation sectors face 35-40% job loss by 2035. However, new jobs emerge in healthcare, education, and technology maintenance. The report argues that UBI alone won't retrain displaced workers—we need targeted education and job transition programs. Cash payments without skills development leave workers dependent on government support rather than reintegrated into the economy.

Key Data: 47% of current U.S. jobs are at high risk of automation within 20 years. But historical precedent shows technology creates as many jobs as it eliminates—just different ones requiring different skills.

Source B (Alaska Permanent Fund Case Study)

Excerpt from "Alaska's Dividend: A UBI Model?" (2023), Journal of Public Economics

Summary: Alaska has distributed annual payments (averaging $1,000-2,000) from oil revenues to all residents since 1982. This study finds NO decrease in work participation rates. Recipients use payments for necessities, savings, and education. Child poverty rates in Alaska are lower than national average. However, the Alaska dividend is small—not enough to live on—and funded by natural resource extraction, not taxation. Scaling this to full UBI ($12,000-15,000 annually) remains untested.

Key Finding: Small unconditional payments don't discourage work. People want to work for income beyond basic survival, for social connection, and for purpose. But would larger UBI payments (enough to live on) have different effects? We don't know.

Source C (Fiscal Analysis)

Excerpt from "The Price Tag of Universal Basic Income" (2025), Congressional Budget Office

Summary: A UBI of $12,000/year for every U.S. adult would cost $3 trillion annually—nearly equal to current total federal spending. Even consolidating existing welfare programs (food stamps, housing assistance, etc.) saves only $700 billion. This leaves a $2.3 trillion funding gap requiring massive tax increases or deficit spending. A 25% value-added tax (VAT) could fund UBI, but regressive taxation (VAT) funding progressive policy (UBI) creates equity concerns. Wealthier nations (lower population, higher GDP per capita) may afford UBI; the U.S. scale is challenging.

Key Data: UBI cost = $3T/year. Current welfare spending = $700B/year. Funding gap = $2.3T. For comparison, Medicare = $800B, Social Security = $1.3T, Defense = $750B.

Source D (Feminist Economics Perspective)

Excerpt from "UBI and Unpaid Labor" by Dr. Sarah Martinez, Institute for Women's Policy Research (2024)

Summary: UBI would, for the first time, compensate unpaid care work—disproportionately performed by women—that sustains society but is economically invisible. Childcare, elder care, household labor: these total 10 billion hours annually in the U.S., yet GDP ignores them. UBI acknowledges that this work has value, enabling caregivers to exit exploitative low-wage jobs and choose care work with dignity. Critics say UBI might trap women in traditional roles, but evidence suggests choice is empowering: Finland's UBI pilot showed women used payments to start businesses and pursue education while continuing care work.

Key Argument: Current welfare systems penalize caregivers (benefits lost if income rises); UBI empowers them. This reframes work beyond market employment.

Source E (Philosophical/Ethical Essay)

Excerpt from "The Right to Economic Security" by philosopher James Chen (2024)

Summary: In affluent societies, poverty is a policy choice, not an inevitability. We have sufficient wealth; the question is distribution. UBI isn't charity—it's recognition that economic participation should guarantee dignity. The ethical case: (1) No one chooses the economic conditions they're born into; (2) Automation profits belong to society, not just capital owners; (3) Economic security is prerequisite for genuine freedom—no one is free when choosing between homelessness and exploitation. UBI's cost is irrelevant if the alternative is tolerating preventable suffering in an abundant society. The question isn't "Can we afford UBI?" but "Can we morally justify NOT implementing it?"

Source F (Behavioral Economics Critique)

Excerpt from "UBI and Incentive Structures" (2025), Behavioral Economics Review

Summary: Small UBI pilots show positive effects, but behavioral economics warns against generalizing. "Hawthorne effect": participants in studies alter behavior because they're observed. Also, pilot participants know payments are temporary—this creates different incentives than permanent UBI. Larger concern: meaning and structure. Work provides identity, routine, social connection—not just income. UBI proponents romanticize "freed" time for creativity, but research on unemployment shows deterioration in mental health, relationships, and purpose. Humans need structure and meaning; removing work obligation without replacing it with meaningful alternatives risks anomie and depression.

Key Concern: We're testing UBI's income effects, not its social-psychological effects. Those may be larger and more concerning.

Source G (Tech Industry Perspective)

Excerpt from interview with venture capitalist Maria Kowalski, TechFuture podcast (2024)

Summary: "Silicon Valley supports UBI because it solves the problem we're creating. AI will displace millions of workers—that's not debatable. UBI is cheaper than social unrest. But here's what UBI critics miss: entrepreneurship. How many brilliant ideas die because people can't afford to quit their job and take a risk? UBI is venture capital for everyone—it funds the next generation of startups, artists, and inventors. The ROI on UBI isn't just poverty reduction; it's innovation unleashed. Of course, we should tax AI profits to fund it—the companies benefiting from automation should pay for the transition."

Perspective: Pro-UBI from self-interest (reduces social friction from automation) AND idealism (enables risk-taking and creativity).
