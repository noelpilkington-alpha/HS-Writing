Full Rhetorical Analysis Essay

First Timed AP Essay: 40 Minutes

⏱ ~60 min 📍 KCs: C.AP.L.06 (GATEWAY) 🧠 Mental Model: M5

⚠️ Gateway Lesson

Gateway Requirements: You must earn Row A = 1 (thesis with line of reasoning) AND Row B ≥ 2 (some analysis with evidence) to proceed to L08.

This is your first complete AP essay. The gateway threshold ensures you can write a defensible thesis and provide analytical commentary before moving to argument writing.

Essay Structure: Rhetorical situation map (5 min) → Select strategies (2 min) → Write thesis (3 min) → Write 2-3 body paragraphs with S-F-E (25 min) → Conclusion (3 min) → Review (2 min) = 40 minutes total.

Phase 1: Teach (2 min)

Putting It All Together

You've learned thesis writing (L05) and body paragraph writing (L06). Now you'll combine them under timed conditions. Here's the AP rhetorical analysis essay sequence:

40-Minute AP Rhetorical Analysis Timeline

Time Task
0-5 min Read passage + map rhetorical situation (quick Force Field: audience, exigence, writer, context)
5-7 min Select 2-3 key strategies that matter most (L03 criteria: serve purpose, address resistance, appear at structural moments)
7-10 min Write thesis with line of reasoning + outline body paragraphs
10-35 min Write 2-3 body paragraphs with S-F-E analysis (~8 min per paragraph)
35-38 min Write brief conclusion (connect to larger significance, don't just summarize)
38-40 min Review: check for thesis clarity, add transitions if needed

Key insight: You don't have time to analyze every strategy in the passage. Choose 2-3 that are most important and analyze them deeply. Quality over quantity.

Phase 2: Model (3 min)

Annotated Model Essay

Below is a model rhetorical analysis essay scored at Row A = 1, Row B = 3, Row C = 0 (total: 4/6). Notice how the thesis establishes line of reasoning, body paragraphs use S-F-E, and the conclusion avoids mere summary.

Show Model Essay (Frederick Douglass, "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?")

[THESIS - Row A] In his 1852 Fourth of July address, Frederick Douglass uses ironic juxtaposition, accusatory direct address, and biblical allusion to expose the hypocrisy of American independence celebrations, building from a shared religious foundation to an indictment his white abolitionist audience cannot dismiss without rejecting their own principles.

[BODY PARAGRAPH 1 - Row B] Douglass's ironic use of the second-person pronoun "your" throughout the speech—"your celebration," "your boasted liberty," "your national greatness"—transforms what should be a unifying national holiday into an exclusionary ritual that exposes moral hypocrisy. By refusing to claim ownership of Independence Day, Douglass positions himself as an outsider looking in, forcing his audience to see the celebration through the eyes of the enslaved. Each repetition of "your" followed by a damning descriptor ("sham," "unholy license," "hollow mockery") creates a rhetorical accumulation that makes the hypocrisy impossible to ignore. This strategy works because Douglass's audience—white abolitionists who pride themselves on supporting freedom—cannot comfortably own a celebration that excludes four million people. The accusatory "your" implicates them not just as witnesses to injustice but as participants in a national delusion.

[BODY PARAGRAPH 2 - Row B] Douglass deploys biblical allusion strategically, invoking shared religious values to create a moral standard his audience cannot reject. When he references scriptural principles of justice and human dignity, he leverages the very foundation his Christian abolitionist audience claims as their moral authority. This move is rhetorically powerful because it traps the audience: to dismiss Douglass's argument, they would have to reject the biblical principles they publicly espouse. The allusions function not as decorative references but as an inescapable moral framework that transforms the slavery debate from a political disagreement into a test of religious integrity. By building his indictment on their own professed values, Douglass makes hypocrisy the only alternative to action.

[CONCLUSION - avoids mere summary] Douglass's rhetorical choices reveal a sophisticated understanding of his audience: he knows they already oppose slavery intellectually, but he forces them to confront their complicity emotionally. By using their own language, their own values, and their own celebrations against them, he creates a cognitive dissonance that transforms passive sympathy into moral crisis. The speech works not by providing new information but by making the familiar unbearable.

Scoring Annotation:

  • Row A = 1: Thesis names strategies (ironic juxtaposition, accusatory address, biblical allusion), states purpose (expose hypocrisy), and includes line of reasoning ("building from shared religious foundation to indictment they cannot dismiss").
  • Row B = 3: Both body paragraphs provide evidence and reach S-F-E analysis. They explain FUNCTION (what the strategy does) and EFFECT (how it impacts audience). Consistent analysis throughout.
  • Row C = 0: The essay is competent but doesn't demonstrate sophistication. It doesn't situate Douglass in broader historical context, doesn't explore tensions within the argument, and doesn't use vivid/persuasive style beyond functional prose.

What to notice: The essay is only ~400 words but earns Row A and Row B scores because it completes the required moves. You don't need a long essay—you need one that meets the rubric requirements.

Phase 3: Planning (10 min)

Plan Your Essay: Frances Harper

Frances E.W. Harper, "We Are All Bound Up Together" (1866)

We are all bound up together in one great bundle of humanity, and society cannot trample on the weakest and feeblest of its members without receiving the curse in its own soul. You tried that in the case of the Negro. You pressed him down for two centuries; and in so doing you crippled the moral strength and paralyzed the spiritual energies of the white men of the country. When the hands of the black were fettered, white men were deprived of the liberty of speech and the freedom of the press. While the Negroes of the South are plundered of their rights, poor white men, North and South, are also deprived of theirs. What in this country shall have the benefit of democratic institutions, it must be the democracy which is willing that all men shall have justice.

You white women speak here of rights. I speak of wrongs. I, as a colored woman, have had in this country an education which has made me feel as if I were in the situation of Ishmael, my hand against every man, and every man's hand against me. Let me go tomorrow morning and take my seat in one of your street cars—I do not know that they will do it in New York, but they will in Philadelphia—and the conductor will put up his hand and stop the car rather than let me ride. Going from Washington to Baltimore this Spring, they put me in the smoking car. Aye, in the capital of the nation, where the black man consecrated himself to the nation's defense, faithful when the white man was faithless, they put me in the smoking car!

We are all bound up together in one great bundle of humanity. The white man's happiness cannot be purchased by the black man's misery. I do not believe the freedom of women can be purchased by the degradation of the black race.

SP-024: Frances E.W. Harper, Eleventh National Woman's Rights Convention, New York, May 1866

⏱ 10 MINUTES: PLANNING ONLY

Complete the planning sequence before writing.

Step 1: Map Rhetorical Situation (3 min)

Quick Force Field: Who is Harper's audience? What is the exigence (occasion/problem)? What is her position as a speaker? What constraints does this create?

Rhetorical Situation Notes:

  • Audience: White suffragists at women's rights convention (mostly focused on gender, not race)
  • Exigence: Post-Civil War debate about prioritizing Black male suffrage vs. women's suffrage
  • Writer: Black woman (faces both racism and sexism; has authority from lived experience)
  • Constraints: Must address her white audience's concerns without alienating them, while insisting on the intersection of race and gender

Step 2: Select Strategies (2 min)

Identify 2-3 strategies that matter most. Consider: What can't Harper ignore given her rhetorical situation? What appears at key structural moments? What most clearly serves her purpose?

Step 3: Write Thesis + Outline (5 min)

Write your thesis: Name strategies, state purpose, include line of reasoning.

Outline body paragraphs: For each strategy, note what evidence you'll use and what S-F-E point you'll make.

Phase 3B: Plan-to-Prose Bridge (5 min)

Turning Your Outline into Draft Prose

You have a thesis and an outline. Now what? Many students stare at a blank page because they don't know how to turn planning notes into actual sentences. Let's walk through that translation process using the Harper passage you just planned.

Coping Model: From Outline Notes to Opening Body Paragraph

[Looking at outline notes] "OK, my first body paragraph is about Harper's use of second-person address — the repeated 'you' and 'your.' I need to turn this claim into a topic sentence..."

First attempt: "Frances Harper uses many rhetorical strategies in her speech, including the word 'you' which she repeats several times."

[Pausing] "Wait — that's just summarizing what she does. It doesn't say WHY it matters or what it accomplishes. I need an analytical claim, not a summary statement. Let me think about FUNCTION — what does the 'you' actually DO to the audience?"

Revised topic sentence: "Harper's accusatory second-person address — 'You pressed him down,' 'You white women' — forces her audience of white suffragists to confront their own complicity in racial oppression rather than positioning themselves as innocent allies."

[Building from outline notes into sentences] "Good. Now I need evidence. My outline says 'quote: You pressed him down for two centuries.' Let me embed that and then do the S-F-E move..."

Continuing the paragraph: "When Harper declares, 'You pressed him down for two centuries; and in so doing you crippled the moral strength and paralyzed the spiritual energies of the white men of the country,' the pronoun 'you' transforms her audience from sympathetic onlookers into active participants in oppression. This direct accusation functions as a rhetorical mirror: rather than letting white suffragists see themselves as separate from the system that enslaved Black Americans, Harper implicates them in its consequences."

"See the pattern? Outline note → analytical topic sentence → embedded quote → explain function → explain effect on audience. That's the translation process for every body paragraph."

Pre-Writing Checkpoint

Checkpoint: Thesis Verification

Before you start your timed writing, verify your thesis has all three required elements:

  • Names at least 2 specific rhetorical strategies (not vague terms like "rhetorical devices")
  • States Harper's purpose (what she's trying to accomplish)
  • Includes a line of reasoning (shows HOW the strategies work together or build on each other)

If any element is missing, fix your thesis now — before the clock starts.

Checkpoint: Topic Sentence Test

Look at your first body paragraph plan. Can you turn your claim into an analytical topic sentence — one that states what the strategy accomplishes, not just that it exists?

Try it here: Write your first body paragraph's topic sentence before starting the timed essay.

Test: Does your sentence answer "what does this strategy DO?" If it only answers "what strategy does Harper use?" — revise it.

You're ready. You have a verified thesis, an outline, and you've practiced the hardest move — turning a plan into prose. Now write.

Phase 4: Independent Writing (30 min)

Write Your Full Rhetorical Analysis Essay

⏱ 30 MINUTES: TIMED WRITING

Write introduction + thesis, 2-3 body paragraphs, conclusion. Full AP conditions.

Your Task: Write the complete essay analyzing Frances Harper's rhetorical choices. Use your planning notes, but write under timed conditions (30 minutes).

Essay Structure Reminder

  • Introduction + Thesis (5 min): Context + thesis with line of reasoning
  • Body Paragraph 1 (8 min): Claim → Evidence → S-F-E Analysis → Connection to purpose
  • Body Paragraph 2 (8 min): Claim → Evidence → S-F-E Analysis → Connection to purpose
  • Body Paragraph 3 (optional, 8 min): Third strategy OR deeper analysis
  • Conclusion (3 min): Larger significance, don't just summarize
  • Review (3 min): Read through, check thesis clarity, add transitions

Start your timer now. Write for 30 minutes.

Phase 6: Gateway Check (2 min)

⚠️ Gateway Assessment

Did you meet the gateway threshold?

  • Row A = 1: Thesis with defensible claim and line of reasoning
  • Row B ≥ 2: At least some evidence with analysis (even if feature-spotting)

If YES (Row A = 1 AND Row B ≥ 2): You've passed the gateway. Proceed to L08.

If NO: Revise your essay before moving on. Focus on the missing element:

  • If Row A = 0: Rewrite your thesis to include line of reasoning
  • If Row B = 0-1: Revise one body paragraph to include evidence + commentary
