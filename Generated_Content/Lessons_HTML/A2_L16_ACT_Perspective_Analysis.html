ACT Perspective Analysis

Decompose ACT perspectives before forming your own position

~25 min C.10.15, C.10.16 M3 (Force Field), M4 (Claim Hierarchy)

Phase 1: Teach (3 min)

Teach

Decomposing ACT Perspectives

ACT Writing gives you an issue and three perspectives. Each perspective is a mini-argument with layers you need to unpack. Most students rush past the perspectives to start "their" essay. The highest scorers slow down and decompose each perspective BEFORE forming a position.

Every ACT perspective contains four layers:

  • A core claim -- the main position being taken
  • An underlying assumption -- what must be true for the claim to work
  • A value it prioritizes -- what it cares about most
  • A gap it overlooks -- complications it ignores

Your job: decompose each perspective BEFORE forming your own position. Use M3: Force Field Analysis -- "Under what conditions is this perspective strongest? Weakest?"

ACT Perspective Map Template

Layer Question to Ask
Core Claim What specific position or action does this perspective advocate?
Underlying Assumption What must be true for this claim to make sense?
Value Prioritized What does this perspective care about above all else?
Gap / Blind Spot What complications or counterarguments does it ignore?
Strongest When... Under what conditions does this perspective hold up best?
Weakest When... Under what conditions does it break down?

Phase 2: Model (4 min)

Model

Mapping All Three Perspectives: Social Media Age Restrictions

Issue: Many social media platforms officially require users to be at least 13 years old, but enforcement is minimal and millions of younger children use these platforms daily. Some lawmakers have proposed raising the minimum age to 16 and requiring platforms to verify users' ages.

Perspective One: Social media companies should be required to enforce strict age verification and raise the minimum age to 16. Young people's brains are still developing, and exposure to social media during critical years has been linked to anxiety, depression, and shortened attention spans.

Perspective Two: Age restrictions are the wrong approach because they treat all young people the same regardless of maturity, and they are nearly impossible to enforce without invasive surveillance. Rather than banning access, we should invest in digital literacy education.

Perspective Three: The responsibility for children's social media use belongs primarily to parents and families, not to corporations or the government. Families understand their own children's maturity levels far better than any blanket policy can.

ACT-style prompt: Social Media Age Restrictions (ACT-P01)

Here is the completed Perspective Map for all three:

Perspective 1: Age Verification

LayerAnalysis
Core ClaimCompanies should verify ages and enforce a minimum age of 16
AssumptionAge is a reliable proxy for developmental readiness
ValuesProtection of youth, corporate responsibility
OverlooksPrivacy concerns of age verification; assumes sharp maturity cutoff at 16
Strongest WhenDiscussing young children (ages 8-12) exposed to harmful content
Weakest WhenDiscussing mature 14-15 year olds who use platforms responsibly

Perspective 2: Education Over Restriction

LayerAnalysis
Core ClaimDigital literacy education, not age bans, is the solution
AssumptionEducation can counteract psychologically addictive platform design
ValuesEmpowerment, agency, avoiding surveillance
OverlooksPower asymmetry between billion-dollar algorithms and a teenager's willpower
Strongest WhenConsidering long-term strategy and skill-building
Weakest WhenImmediate harm is occurring and education is too slow to prevent it

Perspective 3: Parental Responsibility

LayerAnalysis
Core ClaimParents, not companies or government, should decide for their children
AssumptionFamilies can effectively monitor and guide their children's online activity
ValuesFamily autonomy, limited government
OverlooksCollective action problem -- when all peers are online, opting out has social costs; many parents lack tech knowledge
Strongest WhenFamilies are engaged, informed, and have resources to guide their children
Weakest WhenParents lack technical knowledge or time, or peer pressure overrides family rules

Notice the pattern: Every perspective is partially right and partially blind. The conditional language ("strongest when... weakest when...") is how you move from summary to genuine analysis.

Phase 3: Supported Practice (10 min)

Supported Practice

Perspective Decomposition: Homework in Public Schools

Issue: The role of homework in education has become increasingly controversial. Some schools have dramatically reduced or eliminated homework, while others have doubled down on it as essential practice. The debate touches on questions about how students learn best, what schools owe to families' time, and whether homework deepens or merely reinforces inequality.

Perspective One: Homework is essential -- it builds discipline, reinforces classroom instruction, and prepares students for the independent work expectations of college and careers. Students who practice skills outside of class consistently outperform those who do not. Reducing homework may feel compassionate, but it ultimately lowers academic standards and leaves students unprepared.

Perspective Two: Homework should be significantly reduced because it disproportionately harms students who lack quiet study spaces, internet access, or parental support at home. What looks like a "study habit" gap is often a resource gap. Schools should focus on making the school day itself more effective rather than outsourcing learning to environments they cannot control.

Perspective Three: The real problem is not the quantity of homework but its quality. Most homework consists of repetitive busywork that does little to deepen understanding. Schools should assign less homework overall but ensure that what they do assign involves meaningful, creative application of ideas -- projects, reflections, and real-world problem-solving rather than worksheets and drills.

ACT-style prompt: Homework in Public Schools (ACT-P02)

Use the Perspective Map to decompose each perspective. For each one, identify all four layers, then write a Strength/Limitation sentence using "strongest when... weakest when..." language.

Perspective One: Homework Is Essential

Draft your decomposition of Perspective One. Identify the core claim, assumption, value, and gap. Then write when it is strongest and weakest.

Perspective Two: Reduce Homework

Draft your decomposition of Perspective Two.

Perspective Three: Improve Quality

Draft your decomposition of Perspective Three.

Success Criteria

  • Identifies core claim for each perspective
  • Names underlying assumption (not just restating the claim)
  • Identifies what each perspective overlooks
  • Writes strength/limitation using "strongest when... weakest when..." conditional language

Phase 4: Independent Practice (7 min)

Independent Practice

Full Perspective Analysis: Renewable Energy Transition

No scaffold this time. Decompose all three perspectives completely and evaluate strengths and limitations for each.

Issue: Many governments have set ambitious targets to transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy sources like solar and wind within the next two decades. While most people agree that reducing carbon emissions is important, there is significant disagreement about how quickly this transition should happen and who should bear the costs. Communities that depend on fossil fuel industries for jobs and economic stability are especially affected by these decisions.

Perspective One: The transition to renewable energy must happen as quickly as possible, even if it causes short-term economic disruption. Climate change is an existential threat, and every year of delay makes the eventual consequences more severe and more costly. The jobs lost in fossil fuel industries will be replaced by new jobs in the growing clean energy sector, and governments should accelerate this shift with aggressive policy and investment.

Perspective Two: A rapid transition to renewable energy will devastate working-class communities that depend on fossil fuel industries, and the people making these policies rarely live in those communities. Any transition plan must prioritize the economic security of affected workers and towns first, moving at a pace that allows communities to adapt rather than collapse. Justice for the planet cannot come at the expense of justice for people.

Perspective Three: The best path forward is not choosing between fossil fuels and renewables but investing heavily in new technologies -- like advanced nuclear power, carbon capture, and next-generation batteries -- that could allow us to reduce emissions without forcing a disruptive either-or transition. Technological innovation has solved seemingly impossible problems before, and it is more realistic than asking entire economies to transform overnight.

ACT-style prompt: Renewable Energy Transition (ACT-P03)

Compose a complete perspective analysis for all three perspectives. For each, identify the core claim, underlying assumption, what it values, what it overlooks, and when it is strongest and weakest.

Success Criteria

  • Decomposes all 3 perspectives completely (all four layers for each)
  • Identifies strengths AND limitations for each perspective
  • Analysis goes beyond summary -- probes assumptions, not just restates claims

Phase 5: Transfer (3 min)

Transfer

Connecting ACT Analysis to Source Evaluation

Self-Check

How is analyzing ACT perspectives similar to evaluating source perspectives (L08)?

Both require you to see THROUGH the argument to its assumptions. In L08, you evaluated sources by asking: "What does this source value? What does it overlook? When is it most credible?" The four-layer Perspective Map you used today is the same critical thinking applied to pre-packaged arguments.

The Force Field (M3) applies to perspectives, not just texts. Whether you are analyzing a published article or an ACT perspective, the analytical move is identical: identify the claim, probe the assumption, find the blind spot, test the conditions.

The only difference: In synthesis essays, you read 6-7 sources and decide which perspectives to engage. In ACT essays, you are given exactly 3 perspectives and must engage all of them. But the analytical skill is identical. If you can do one, you can do the other.
