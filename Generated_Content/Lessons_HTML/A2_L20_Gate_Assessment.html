Part 1: Synthesis Essay (40 min)

Gate — Part 1

Source Set D: School Start Times

Source D1

The American Academy of Pediatrics has recommended since 2014 that middle and high schools start no earlier than 8:30 AM, yet as of 2023, fewer than 20 percent of U.S. high schools meet this standard. The science behind the recommendation is unambiguous: during puberty, the circadian rhythm shifts forward by approximately two hours, making it biologically difficult for most teenagers to fall asleep before 11 PM. When a student's alarm goes off at 6:00 AM for a 7:15 start, they are operating on five to six hours of sleep—well below the eight to ten hours the National Sleep Foundation identifies as necessary for adolescent health and cognitive function.

The consequences of chronic sleep deprivation in teenagers are not trivial. A 2019 study published in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine tracked over 9,000 students across 29 high schools and found that schools shifting start times to 8:30 or later saw measurable improvements: average grades rose by half a letter grade, standardized test scores increased by 4.5 percentile points, and chronic absenteeism dropped by 15 percent. Perhaps most strikingly, a separate study by the Children's National Medical Center found that car crash rates among teen drivers fell by 16.5 percent in districts that delayed start times, consistent with research linking sleep deprivation to impaired reaction time comparable to moderate alcohol intoxication.

Critics often argue that later start times are logistically impractical, but the growing number of districts that have successfully implemented them suggests otherwise. Seattle Public Schools shifted to an 8:45 start in 2016 and reported not only academic gains but also a 5 percent increase in daily attendance. The Cherry Creek School District in Colorado phased in later starts over two years, using a bus-route optimization study to contain transportation costs. These districts demonstrate that the logistical challenges, while real, are solvable—and that the academic and health returns on the investment are substantial.

Dr. Rachel Nguyen, pediatric sleep researcher, University of Minnesota Medical School — "The Case for Later School Start Times," Sleep Health Policy Brief, 2023

Source D2

As a superintendent who has spent eighteen months studying the feasibility of later high school start times in our district, I can say with confidence that the sleep science is compelling but the implementation realities are formidable. Our district serves 14,000 students across 22 schools using a three-tier bus system. Currently, high school buses run first (6:45 AM pickup), followed by middle school, then elementary. Flipping this order would mean elementary students boarding buses in pre-dawn darkness during winter months—a safety concern that parents in our community have vocally opposed.

The financial dimension cannot be ignored. An independent transportation study estimated that adding a fourth bus tier to avoid the elementary darkness problem would cost our district $2.3 million annually—roughly the equivalent of 30 teaching positions. In an era when we are already struggling to retain qualified teachers and fund special education mandates, this trade-off is not abstract. Every dollar spent on bus routes is a dollar not spent in classrooms.

There are also downstream effects that sleep researchers rarely address. Pushing dismissal to 3:45 or 4:00 PM compresses the window for after-school activities, athletics, and part-time employment. In our community, 38 percent of high school students hold jobs that begin at 4:00 or 4:30 PM—jobs many of them need to contribute to family income. Athletic practices that currently end at 5:30 would end at 6:30 or later, creating conflicts with evening family time and homework. And for families with younger children, a later high school dismissal eliminates the older-sibling childcare that many working parents depend on.

I am not arguing that sleep does not matter. I am arguing that school districts operate within real constraints—budgets, bus routes, community expectations, and the cascading effects of schedule changes on thousands of families. The right answer is rarely as simple as the research makes it appear.

Michael Torres, Superintendent, Riverside Unified School District — Testimony to the State Board of Education, 2023

Source D3

The debate over school start times tends to be framed as a binary: shift to 8:30 or later, or maintain the status quo. But this framing obscures a more important question: why are American teenagers so sleep-deprived in the first place, and can start times alone fix the problem?

Research from the RAND Corporation and the National Academies of Sciences suggests that school start time is one factor among several. Excessive homework loads, evening extracurricular commitments, and above all, late-night screen exposure all contribute to adolescent sleep loss. A 2022 Stanford study found that teenagers who used screens within 30 minutes of bedtime slept an average of 47 minutes less per night than those who did not—an effect roughly equivalent to the sleep gained by a 45-minute start-time delay. Addressing start times without addressing screen habits and homework volume is, at best, a partial solution.

That said, the districts that have moved to later start times most successfully did so as part of a broader wellness initiative. The California Healthy Start Act (2019) mandated 8:30 starts for high schools statewide beginning in 2022, but the most effective districts paired the change with sleep education curricula, homework policy reforms, and partnerships with pediatric health providers. These comprehensive approaches produced larger gains in student well-being than schedule changes alone.

The path forward is phased implementation that accounts for regional variation. Rural districts with long bus routes face different constraints than urban districts with public transit options. One-size-fits-all mandates risk creating as many problems as they solve. The goal should be a systems-level approach: later start times where feasible, combined with homework limits, screen-time education, and community dialogue about what a healthy adolescent schedule actually looks like.

Dr. Amara Okafor, education policy researcher, Brookings Institution — "Beyond the Bell: A Comprehensive Approach to Adolescent Sleep," 2023

Source D4

I am writing this at 6:14 AM, sitting in my school's parking lot waiting for the building to open at 6:30 so I can get to my locker before first period at 7:10. I have been awake since 5:40. Last night I finished my AP History reading at 11:30, scrolled through my phone for twenty minutes trying to wind down (I know, I know), and fell asleep around midnight. That is five hours and forty minutes of sleep. This is a normal Tuesday for me.

Last spring, our student government conducted a school-wide survey on sleep and school performance. Out of 1,247 respondents, 73 percent reported feeling tired or very tired during first and second period. Forty-one percent said they had fallen asleep in class at least once in the past month. And 68 percent said they would support a later start time even if it meant a later dismissal.

The adults in this debate talk about bus routes and budgets and after-school activities. I understand those are real concerns. But from where I am sitting—literally, in a dark parking lot before sunrise—it feels like the system is asking me to perform at my best during the hours when my brain is at its worst. My first two classes are AP Calculus and AP English. These are the hardest courses I take, and I am taking them while functionally sleep-deprived.

I am not asking for a perfect solution. I am asking for acknowledgment that the current system is designed around adult work schedules, not around how teenage brains actually work. If the science says our brains do not wake up until 8:00 or 9:00 AM, then starting school at 7:10 is not just inconvenient—it is working against us. We deserve a schedule that gives us a fighting chance.

Sofia Medina, junior, Lincoln High School — Student editorial, The Lincoln Lion, 2023

Part 2: ACT Writing Essay (40 min)

Gate — Part 2

ACT-P07: Arts Education in Public Schools

Issue: As public school budgets face increasing pressure, many districts have reduced or eliminated funding for arts programs—including visual arts, music, theater, and dance. Supporters of arts education argue that these programs develop creativity, critical thinking, and emotional intelligence that benefit students across all subjects. Critics counter that schools must prioritize subjects with clearer academic and career outcomes. The debate raises fundamental questions about the purpose of public education and how limited resources should be allocated.

Perspective One: Arts education is not a luxury but a necessity. Research consistently shows that students who participate in arts programs demonstrate stronger problem-solving skills, higher rates of school engagement, and better social-emotional development. Cutting arts programs does not just remove a subject—it removes a way of thinking that makes students more creative, more empathetic, and more adaptable in a world that increasingly values those qualities. Schools that eliminate the arts are sacrificing long-term student development for short-term budget relief.

Perspective Two: Schools have limited resources and must make difficult choices. In an economy that increasingly demands STEM skills, literacy, and quantitative reasoning, schools have an obligation to prioritize subjects with the clearest pathways to college readiness and career success. Arts programs, while enriching, do not provide the foundational skills that most students need to compete in the modern workforce. Students who are passionate about the arts can pursue them through community programs, private lessons, or extracurricular activities outside the school day.

Perspective Three: The debate over arts funding is a false choice created by inadequate school funding overall. Pitting arts against STEM or literacy against music forces educators into zero-sum thinking that harms students. The real issue is that public schools are chronically underfunded, and the solution is not to decide which programs to cut but to advocate for funding levels that allow schools to offer a comprehensive education. Accepting the premise that schools must choose between arts and academics concedes a point that should never have been conceded.

ACT Writing Prompt — ACT-P07 (Gate Assessment)

Prompt: Write a unified, coherent essay evaluating multiple perspectives on the role of arts education in public schools. State your own perspective, analyze the relationship between your perspective and at least one other, and develop your ideas with reasoning and examples.

Part 3: Conventions (20 min)

Gate — Part 3

Editing in Context (6 Items)

Each question presents a sentence with an underlined or highlighted portion. Choose the best revision, or select "NO CHANGE" if the original is correct.

Question 1 — Subject-Verb Agreement

Read the following sentence:

The collection of essays written by students from across the district were published in the school's annual literary magazine.

Which choice best replaces the underlined portion?

  1. NO CHANGE
  2. was
  3. are
  4. have been
Show Answer

Answer: B

The subject is "collection" (singular), not "essays" or "students." A singular subject requires a singular verb: "was published."

Question 2 — Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

Read the following sentence:

Each of the researchers presented their findings at the annual conference, hoping to receive feedback from peers in the field.

Which choice best replaces the underlined portion?

  1. NO CHANGE
  2. his or her findings
  3. its findings
  4. the findings
Show Answer

Answer: A

In contemporary standard English, singular "their" is widely accepted with indefinite pronouns like "each." The original is correct. While "his or her" (B) is also grammatically defensible, "their" is the more natural and increasingly preferred usage.

Question 3 — Modifier Placement

Read the following sentence:

Racing to finish before the deadline, the essay was submitted with several uncorrected errors.

Which choice best corrects the sentence?

  1. NO CHANGE
  2. Racing to finish before the deadline, the student submitted the essay with several uncorrected errors.
  3. The essay, racing to finish before the deadline, was submitted with several uncorrected errors.
  4. Racing to finish before the deadline, several uncorrected errors appeared in the submitted essay.
Show Answer

Answer: B

The original is a dangling modifier: "Racing to finish before the deadline" modifies "the essay," but essays do not race. The modifier must be followed by the person doing the racing—the student.

Question 4 — Parallel Structure

Read the following sentence:

The new curriculum emphasizes critical thinking, analyzing primary sources, and to write persuasive arguments based on evidence.

Which choice best replaces the underlined portion?

  1. NO CHANGE
  2. analyzing primary sources, and writing persuasive arguments
  3. the analysis of primary sources, and to write persuasive arguments
  4. analyzing primary sources, and persuasive argument writing
Show Answer

Answer: B

The sentence lists three things the curriculum emphasizes. All three must be in the same grammatical form. "Critical thinking" and "analyzing" use gerund/noun forms, so the third item must match: "writing persuasive arguments" (not "to write").

Question 5 — Comma Usage

Read the following sentence:

The students who completed all three drafts, received the highest scores on the final portfolio assessment.

Which choice best handles the underlined punctuation?

  1. NO CHANGE
  2. Remove the comma (no punctuation)
  3. Replace the comma with a semicolon
  4. Replace the comma with a dash
Show Answer

Answer: B

The clause "who completed all three drafts" is a restrictive (essential) relative clause—it identifies WHICH students received the highest scores. Restrictive clauses are not set off by commas. Removing the comma is correct.

Question 6 — Semicolon Usage

Read the following sentence:

The researcher's hypothesis was not supported by the data; however, the unexpected results opened several promising avenues for future investigation.

Which choice best handles the underlined punctuation?

  1. NO CHANGE
  2. Replace the semicolon with a comma: data, however,
  3. Replace the semicolon with a period: data. However,
  4. Remove the semicolon and "however": data, the unexpected results
Show Answer

Answer: A

A semicolon before a conjunctive adverb ("however") connecting two independent clauses is correct standard usage. Option B would create a comma splice. Option C is grammatically acceptable but less stylistically smooth. The original is the best choice.
