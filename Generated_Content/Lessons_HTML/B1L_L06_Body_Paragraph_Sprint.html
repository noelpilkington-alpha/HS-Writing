Body Paragraph Sprint

Writing AP Rhetorical Analysis Paragraphs Under Time Pressure

⏱ ~30 min 📍 KCs: C.AP.L.05 🧠 Mental Model: M2
The 7-Minute Paragraph is your building block. A full rhetorical analysis essay = thesis + 3 of these + conclusion. If you can write one strong analytical paragraph in 7 minutes, you can write the essay in 40.

Phase 1: Teach (2 min)

AP Body Paragraph Structure and Row B Scoring

The AP rhetorical analysis body paragraph follows a structure you already know from M2 (S-F-E), but with one addition: connection to purpose. Here's the pattern:

Body Paragraph Structure

  1. Claim: What does the strategy accomplish? (topic sentence)
  2. Evidence: Specific quote or paraphrase from the passage
  3. S-F-E Analysis:
    • Strategy: Name the rhetorical technique specifically
    • Function: What rhetorical work does it do?
    • Effect: How does it impact THIS audience in THIS rhetorical situation?
  4. Connection to Purpose: Link back to thesis (what the writer is trying to accomplish overall)

Row B scores 0-4 based on how many paragraphs reach genuine S-F-E analysis. The critical distinction:

Row B Scoring (0-4 points)

Score What This Looks Like
Row B 2 Feature-spotting with evidence: identifies strategies and quotes examples, but stops at naming ("The author uses irony here"). Does not reach Effect.
Row B 3 S-F-E analysis: explains HOW the strategy works (function) and WHAT effect it has on the audience. Most paragraphs reach Effect.
Row B 4 Consistently sophisticated S-F-E analysis: connects strategies to rhetorical situation, explores nuance, shows how strategies work together.

Your goal today: write paragraphs that reach Row B 3 by completing the S-F-E chain in 7 minutes.

Phase 2: Model (4 min)

Coping Model: Writing a Body Paragraph on Douglass

Passage: Frederick Douglass, "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" (1852)

What, to the American slave, is your 4th of July? I answer: a day that reveals to him, more than all other days in the year, the gross injustice and cruelty to which he is the constant victim. To him, your celebration is a sham; your boasted liberty, an unholy license; your national greatness, swelling vanity; your sounds of rejoicing are empty and heartless; your denunciations of tyrants, brass fronted impudence; your shouts of liberty and equality, hollow mockery.

SP-001: Frederick Douglass, July 5, 1852

Strategy to analyze: Douglass's use of irony (specifically, addressing the audience as "your celebration" while describing it from the slave's perspective)

Writer's thinking process:

Douglass uses irony in this passage. He calls the Fourth of July celebration a "sham" and says liberty is "hollow mockery." This shows irony because he's saying the opposite of what the audience believes. The irony makes his point strong.

Wait—I named the device (irony) and gave evidence, but I didn't explain HOW the irony works or what EFFECT it has. I'm feature-spotting. That's Row B 2, not 3. Let me reach Effect.

Douglass's strategic use of the second person pronoun "your" throughout this passage—"your celebration," "your boasted liberty," "your national greatness"—creates a biting irony that positions him as an outsider judging the audience's hypocrisy. By shifting from the expected first-person plural ("our celebration") to the accusatory "your," he makes explicit what his white abolitionist audience might have preferred to ignore: the Fourth of July belongs to them, not to the four million enslaved people in America. The repeated use of "your" combined with negative descriptors ("sham," "unholy license," "hollow mockery") transforms each element of the celebration into evidence of moral failure. This ironic distance forces the audience to see their Independence Day through the eyes of those excluded from its promises, creating a cognitive dissonance they cannot dismiss—to reject Douglass's characterization, they would have to defend celebrating freedom while enslaving millions, a position that would expose their own hypocrisy. The irony thus serves not merely to criticize but to trap the audience in an inescapable recognition of the contradiction between their professed values and their nation's reality.

What Changed: The strong version doesn't just name irony—it explains the FUNCTION (positions him as outsider, creates cognitive dissonance) and the EFFECT (forces audience to see through enslaved person's eyes, traps them in recognition). That's S-F-E analysis that reaches Row B 3.

Phase 3: Supported Practice (9 min)

Body Paragraph Sprint #1: Ida B. Wells (7 minutes)

Passage: Ida B. Wells, "Lynch Law in All Its Phases" (1893)

The Negro has been too long associated with the idea of inferiority to be believed when he protests against it. His protest is called insolence; his self-defense is called conspiracy; his protection of his family and home, a violation of the white man's law; and his efforts to elevate himself are called "uppity." The very existence of three hundred thousand educated Negroes, two hundred and fifty of whom are graduates of colleges like Harvard, Yale, and the University of Pennsylvania, and more than twenty-five hundred college students, is a refutation of the charge that the Negro is intellectually inferior. Yet the South persistently circulates the lie that the Negro race is inferior, in order to justify its oppression.

SP-013: Ida B. Wells, Washington, D.C., 1893

⏱ 7 MINUTES

Write one body paragraph analyzing Wells's use of evidence accumulation and refutation.

Your Task: Write one analytical paragraph about Wells's rhetorical strategy. Focus on how she uses documented evidence (specific numbers, statistics about educated Black Americans) to refute the justification for racial violence.

Structure Reminder (scaffold for Sprint #1):

  1. Claim: What does Wells's evidence accomplish?
  2. Evidence: Quote or paraphrase the specific data she provides
  3. S-F-E: What FUNCTION does this evidence serve? What EFFECT does it create for her Northern white audience?
  4. Connection: How does this support Wells's overall purpose in the speech?

Start your timer now. Write for 7 minutes.

Phase 4: Independent Practice (9 min)

Body Paragraph Sprint #2: Carrie Chapman Catt (7 minutes)

Passage: Carrie Chapman Catt, "The Crisis" (1916)

Do you realize that in no other country in the world with democratic tendencies is suffrage so completely denied as in a considerable number of our own states? There are thirteen black states where no suffrage for women exists. There are fourteen others where suffrage provisions are so limited that they are little more than a mockery of the word. Do you realize that when you ask women to be patient you are asking them to be patient with a condition of servitude which no man in any country would endure for a single year?

SP-015: Carrie Chapman Catt, Emergency Convention of the National American Woman Suffrage Association, 1916

⏱ 7 MINUTES

Write one body paragraph. No scaffold visible this time.

Your Task: Write one analytical paragraph about Catt's use of rhetorical questions and comparative evidence. How do these strategies work together to challenge her audience's complacency?

This time, no structure scaffold is provided. You should know the pattern: Claim → Evidence → S-F-E Analysis → Connection to Purpose.

Start your timer now. Write for 7 minutes.

Phase 5: Self-Score (3 min)

Score Both Paragraphs on Row B

Self-Assessment

Score each of your two paragraphs on Row B. Be honest with yourself:

Paragraph 1 (Wells):

Did this paragraph reach S-F-E analysis, or did it stop at feature-spotting?

  • Row B 2 (feature-spotting): You identified the strategy and gave evidence, but your commentary just named what Wells does without explaining HOW it works or WHAT effect it creates.
  • Row B 3 (S-F-E analysis): You explained the FUNCTION (what rhetorical work the evidence does) AND the EFFECT (how it impacts the audience's understanding or beliefs).

Paragraph 2 (Catt):

Same question: Did you reach Effect, or stop at naming strategies?

The Critical Question for Row B 3:

Did your commentary answer these questions?

  • Function: What rhetorical work does this strategy do? (e.g., "reframes the issue," "creates urgency," "establishes credibility," "dismantles counterargument")
  • Effect: How does it change what THIS AUDIENCE thinks, feels, or believes?

If you answered both questions, you reached Row B 3. If you only named the strategy and gave evidence, you stopped at Row B 2.

Phase 6: Transfer (3 min)

The 7-Minute Paragraph Is Your Building Block

You just wrote two body paragraphs in 7 minutes each. A full rhetorical analysis essay on the AP exam = thesis (3 min) + 3 body paragraphs (21 min) + conclusion (3 min) + review (2 min) = 29 minutes of writing within the 40-minute limit.

If you can write one strong paragraph in 7 minutes, you can write the essay.

The 7-minute constraint forces you to prioritize depth over breadth. You can't list 5 devices in 7 minutes, but you CAN deeply analyze 1 strategy with S-F-E. This is exactly what the AP rubric rewards: fewer strategies analyzed deeply beats many strategies listed superficially.

Reflection

Which took you longer: writing the analysis or finding the right evidence?

If finding evidence was slow, you need more practice identifying key moments in passages during the reading phase. If writing analysis was slow, you need to internalize the S-F-E pattern so it becomes automatic.

Your answer tells you what to practice before L07's full essay.
