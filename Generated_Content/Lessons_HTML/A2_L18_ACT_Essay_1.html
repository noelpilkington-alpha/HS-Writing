Phase 1: Teach

The 5-Minute ACT Planning Protocol

You have 40 minutes for an ACT essay. The first 5 minutes determine whether the next 35 are productive or chaotic. Here's the protocol:

5-Minute Plan Template

Time Task
0:00–1:30 Map Perspectives: Read the three perspectives. For each, identify the core assumption. Which perspective is closest to your view? Which is furthest?
1:30–2:30 Choose Position: State your position in one sentence. Must relate to at least one given perspective ("I agree with P1 that... but want to add..." OR "I complicate the P2 vs. P3 debate by...")
2:30–4:00 Select Examples: Identify 2 specific examples or reasons from your knowledge. Can be personal, historical, current events, hypotheticals. Quick notes, not full sentences.
4:00–5:00 Sketch Structure: Decide paragraph sequence. Typical: Intro (position + perspectives engaged) → Body 1 (perspective you agree with + why) → Body 2 (perspective you complicate/disagree with + why) → Body 3 (your support/examples) → Conclusion.

Key insight: A messy-but-sufficient 5-minute plan beats a blank page. You don't need elegant prose in your plan — you need direction.

Phase 2: Model

Watch the 5-Minute Protocol in Action

ACT-P01: Social Media Age Restrictions

Issue: Many social media platforms officially require users to be at least 13 years old, but enforcement is minimal and millions of younger children use these platforms daily. Some lawmakers have proposed raising the minimum age to 16 and requiring platforms to verify users' ages. The debate raises questions about who is responsible for protecting young people online—parents, technology companies, or the government.

Perspective One: Social media companies should be required to enforce strict age verification and raise the minimum age to 16. Young people's brains are still developing, and exposure to social media during critical years has been linked to anxiety, depression, and shortened attention spans. Companies that profit from young users' attention have a moral obligation to limit access until users are mature enough to handle these platforms.

Perspective Two: Age restrictions are the wrong approach because they treat all young people the same regardless of maturity, and they are nearly impossible to enforce without invasive surveillance. Rather than banning access, we should invest in digital literacy education that teaches young people how to navigate social media critically and responsibly. Equipping people with skills is always more effective than shielding them from reality.

Perspective Three: The responsibility for children's social media use belongs primarily to parents and families, not to corporations or the government. Families understand their own children's maturity levels far better than any blanket policy can. When the government steps in to regulate what young people can access, it sets a dangerous precedent for controlling information and communication more broadly.

ACT Writing Prompt (Practice)

Model 5-Minute Plan (messy but sufficient)

0:00–1:30 — Map Perspectives:

P1 = corporate responsibility, age 16, brain development. Assumes corps CAN enforce. P2 = education over restriction, digital literacy. Assumes education works. P3 = family responsibility, government overreach risk. Assumes all families equipped.

Closest to me: P2. Furthest: P3 (families can't handle this alone).

1:30–2:30 — Choose Position:

My position: I agree with P2 that education is key, but P1 is right that platforms have responsibility too. Both/and, not either/or. Companies should redesign (not ban), and schools should teach literacy.

2:30–4:00 — Select Examples:

Ex 1: Instagram's algorithm actively promotes harmful content (eating disorder accounts) to teens — that's not just a user problem, it's a design problem. Ex 2: Schools that teach media literacy see students make better choices (Finland example?).

4:00–5:00 — Sketch Structure:

Intro: state position (P2+P1 combined). Para 2: agree with P2 on education. Para 3: complicate P2 by adding P1's point — education can't work if design is predatory. Para 4: examples (Instagram algorithm, media literacy programs). Conclusion: both needed.

Notice: The plan is rough. That's fine. It gives direction without wasting time on polish.

Phase 3: Supported Practice

Your Turn: 5-Minute Planning Sprint

ACT-P04: Standardized School Dress Codes

Issue: Many public schools have implemented strict dress codes or uniform policies, arguing that they reduce distractions, minimize bullying over clothing, and create a more focused learning environment. Critics, however, argue that dress codes suppress individuality and are often enforced in ways that disproportionately target certain students. The debate reflects broader tensions between institutional order and personal expression.

Perspective One: School dress codes and uniform policies benefit students by removing a major source of social comparison and distraction. When students are not competing over brands and fashion, they can focus on learning, and lower-income students are spared the pressure of keeping up with wealthier peers. Schools exist for education, and dress codes help maintain that focus.

Perspective Two: Dress codes are more about control than education, and their enforcement consistently falls hardest on girls, students of color, and students who do not conform to traditional gender norms. What schools call "appropriate" dress often reflects narrow cultural standards rather than genuine educational needs. A policy that claims to treat everyone equally but punishes some groups more than others is not truly fair.

Perspective Three: Rather than imposing dress codes from above, schools should involve students in creating community standards for dress that balance self-expression with mutual respect. When students participate in making the rules, they develop ownership over their school culture and practice democratic decision-making. Top-down policies miss an opportunity to teach students how to negotiate shared norms.

Phase 4: Independent Practice

Full ACT Essay #1 (40 Minutes Total)

ACT-P05: Paying College Athletes

Issue: College athletics in the United States generates billions of dollars in revenue through television deals, merchandise, and ticket sales. While recent rule changes have allowed college athletes to profit from their name, image, and likeness, the question of whether universities should directly pay athletes as employees remains hotly debated. The issue raises questions about fairness, the purpose of college, and the nature of amateur sport.

Perspective One: College athletes whose sports generate millions in revenue deserve to be paid as employees of their universities. These athletes dedicate 40 or more hours per week to their sport, generate enormous profits for their schools, and risk serious injury—all while being told their scholarship is compensation enough. Refusing to pay them is exploitative, especially when coaches and administrators earn massive salaries from the same labor.

Perspective Two: Paying college athletes as employees would fundamentally undermine the purpose of higher education. College is meant to be a place of learning, and athletic scholarships already provide extraordinary value—free tuition, room, board, coaching, and career networking. Turning students into employees blurs the line between universities and professional sports leagues and would further widen the gap between a handful of wealthy athletic programs and everyone else.

Perspective Three: The real problem is not whether individual athletes get paid but the entire structure of college athletics, which funnels enormous resources into a few high-profile sports while most college athletes and academic programs receive comparatively little. Rather than paying football and basketball stars, universities should redistribute athletic revenue more equitably—supporting all sports, investing in academics, and ensuring that every student-athlete receives a meaningful education.

ACT Writing Prompt (Practice)

⏱ Time Management:
  • Minutes 0-5: Plan using the protocol (map, position, examples, structure)
  • Minutes 5-35: Write your essay (300+ words)
  • Minutes 35-40: Quick revision pass (fix obvious errors, strengthen transitions)

Set your timer and go. Stop writing at 40 minutes.

Quick Reminders

  • Introduction: State your position clearly and name the perspectives you'll engage with
  • Body paragraphs: Engage perspectives using They Say/I Say moves + support with reasoning and examples
  • Your examples: Can be personal experience, historical events, current issues, or thoughtful hypotheticals
  • Conclusion: Briefly summarize your position and why it matters

ACT Essay Rubric (What Graders Look For)

  • Clear position that relates to at least one given perspective
  • Engages at least 2 of 3 perspectives (not just restating your own view)
  • Support includes reasoning AND specific examples (not just assertions)
  • Organized with clear paragraph transitions
  • Counterargument from opposing perspective addressed

Write your essay below. Aim for 300+ words. You'll self-score in the next phase.

Phase 5: Self-Score

ACT Self-Assessment (4 Domains)

ACT essays are scored on 4 domains, each rated 1-6. Use this simplified self-check to identify your strengths and weaknesses.

Self-Check: Rate Your Essay

Domain 1: Ideas & Analysis

Did you state a clear position and analyze how it relates to multiple perspectives?

  • Low (1-2): Position unclear or only restates one perspective
  • Mid (3-4): Clear position, engages 1-2 perspectives
  • High (5-6): Nuanced position, analyzes relationships among multiple perspectives

Your self-rating: _____ (1-6)

Domain 2: Development & Support

Did you support your position with specific reasoning and examples?

  • Low (1-2): Mostly assertions, few examples
  • Mid (3-4): Some reasoning and examples, but underdeveloped
  • High (5-6): Detailed reasoning and specific, relevant examples throughout

Your self-rating: _____ (1-6)

Domain 3: Organization

Is your essay clearly organized with logical transitions?

  • Low (1-2): Hard to follow, no clear structure
  • Mid (3-4): Basic organization, some transitions
  • High (5-6): Smooth flow, clear paragraph logic, strong transitions

Your self-rating: _____ (1-6)

Domain 4: Language Use

Is your writing clear and mostly error-free?

  • Low (1-2): Frequent errors impede understanding
  • Mid (3-4): Mostly clear, some errors
  • High (5-6): Clear and precise, minimal errors

Your self-rating: _____ (1-6)

Identify your weakest domain. That's your focus for L19 Essay #2. Write it down:

My weakest domain: _____________________

What I'll focus on improving in Essay #2: _____________________

Reflection: Most writers score unevenly across domains. That's normal. The goal is to diagnose where you lost points so you can adjust your strategy for Essay #2. If Development was weak, you need more specific examples. If Organization was weak, you need clearer paragraph structure. Use this feedback.
