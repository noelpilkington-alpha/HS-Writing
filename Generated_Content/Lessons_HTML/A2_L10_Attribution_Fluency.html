Lesson 10: Attribution Fluency Drill

Course A2 (Grade 10) | Time: ~20 minutes | KC: C.10.09 (Attribution and Citation Fluency)
1
Teach: Signal Phrases Carry Attitude

You already know how to cite a source. But how you bring that source into your writing is where the analysis happens. The signal phrase you choose—"argues," "suggests," "acknowledges," "overlooks"—tells your reader what you think about the evidence before they even read it.

Most students use the same 2-3 signal phrases over and over: "says," "states," "shows." But skilled writers have 10+ ways to integrate sources, and each one frames the evidence differently.

Key Concept: Signal Phrase Categories

Your signal phrase IS analysis. It tells the reader your stance on the source's credibility, strength, and relevance.

Neutral

  • notes
  • observes
  • states
  • reports
  • describes

Supportive

  • argues convincingly
  • demonstrates
  • confirms
  • establishes
  • reveals

Critical

  • claims
  • insists
  • overlooks
  • fails to account for
  • assumes without evidence

Tentative

  • suggests
  • implies
  • hints at
  • raises the possibility
  • indicates

The Bottom Line: Before you write "According to Smith," ask yourself: Do I fully trust this source? Am I skeptical? Is this a strong claim or a weak possibility? Then choose your signal phrase accordingly.

2
Model: Five Ways to Frame the Same Evidence
🔒

Let's take one piece of evidence and integrate it five different ways. Watch how the signal phrase changes the meaning—even though the data stays the same.

Original Evidence:

"A 2023 Gallup poll found that 67% of teachers reported spending more time on test preparation than on creative projects."

Five Different Integrations:

1. Neutral (just the facts)

According to a 2023 Gallup poll, 67% of teachers reported spending more time on test preparation than on creative projects.

2. Supportive (this evidence is strong)

A 2023 Gallup poll demonstrates the scope of the problem: 67% of teachers reported spending more time on test preparation than on creative projects.

3. Critical (I'm skeptical of this framing)

While a 2023 Gallup poll claims that 67% of teachers spend more time on test prep than creative projects, this data fails to account for how teachers define "creative" work.

4. Tentative (this suggests a pattern but isn't definitive)

A 2023 Gallup poll suggests a troubling trend: 67% of teachers reported spending more time on test preparation than on creative projects.

5. Revealing (this evidence uncovers something hidden)

A 2023 Gallup poll reveals the extent to which standardized testing has reshaped classroom practice: 67% of teachers now spend more time on test preparation than on creative projects.

Notice: Same data, five different arguments. The signal phrase is doing the analytical work.

Check Your Understanding:

A writer wants to use the Gallup poll data to argue that standardized testing has had a negative impact on education. Which signal phrase would be MOST effective?

  1. states
  2. suggests
  3. reveals
  4. claims
Show Answer

Answer: C. "Reveals" is the strongest supportive signal phrase—it suggests the evidence uncovers something important and troubling. "States" is too neutral, "suggests" is too tentative, and "claims" is critical (implying doubt).

Supported Practice: Attribution Pattern Drill

Now it's your turn. You'll practice integrating the same piece of evidence five different ways, using five different signal phrases.

4
Independent Practice: Match Signal Phrase to Source Stance
🔒

This time, you're choosing the most appropriate signal phrase for each piece of evidence based on how you want to use it in your argument.

Evidence A (from a pro-testing advocacy group): "Standardized tests provide the only consistent measure of student achievement across diverse school districts, ensuring accountability and equity."
Evidence B (from a peer-reviewed education study): "A longitudinal study of 12,000 students found that test scores in grades 3-5 had no correlation with college graduation rates, creative problem-solving abilities, or career satisfaction."
Evidence C (from a teacher interview): "I love teaching, but some days I feel like a test-prep coach rather than an educator. We've lost something important."

Your Task:

For each piece of evidence (A, B, and C):

  1. Write an integration using the most appropriate signal phrase
  2. Briefly explain (1-2 sentences) WHY you chose that signal phrase

Assume you are arguing AGAINST standardized testing. Choose signal phrases that support your position.

Word count: 0

Success Criteria:

  • Uses at least 3 different signal phrases
  • Each signal phrase matches the source's stance and your argument's needs
  • Integration is grammatically smooth
  • Brief explanation of why each signal phrase was chosen
5
Transfer: Apply to Your Own Writing
🔒

Attribution fluency is one of the fastest ways to elevate your writing from "competent" to "sophisticated." Readers notice when you vary your signal phrases—it shows you're thinking critically about your sources, not just plugging them in.

Self-Check:

Look back at your L07 argument essay (or any recent essay where you used sources). How many different attribution patterns did you use?

Most students use 1-2 signal phrases throughout an entire essay (usually "states" and "shows"). Strong writers use 4-6 different patterns, choosing each one strategically based on the source's credibility and their own argument's needs.

Your goal for the next essay: Use at least 4 different signal phrases. Bonus points if you use one from each category (neutral, supportive, critical, tentative).

Why This Matters:

On the ACT Writing test and in college essays, graders are looking for source integration sophistication. Using varied, precise signal phrases is one of the clearest markers of advanced writing.

Two students cite the same evidence. One writes: "The study shows that..." The other writes: "The study reveals that..." Same data, but the second student sounds like they're in control of their sources, not just summarizing them.

That's the difference between a 4 and a 5 on the AP scale.

Lesson Complete! You've learned how to vary signal phrases strategically to strengthen your analysis.
