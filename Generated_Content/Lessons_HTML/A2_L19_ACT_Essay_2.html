Essay A

The question of whether social media should have stricter age restrictions is an important topic today. There are three perspectives on this issue, and I will discuss them in this essay.

Perspective One says that social media companies should verify ages and raise the minimum to 16. I agree with this because social media really does cause problems for young people. Many teenagers spend too much time on their phones and it affects their mental health. Studies have shown that social media can lead to depression and anxiety in teens.

Perspective Two says we should use education instead of restrictions. This is a good point because education is always important. However, I think restrictions are still needed because not all education programs work and some kids will still use social media irresponsibly.

Perspective Three says parents should be responsible. While parents do play a role, they cannot monitor their children all the time, especially when kids have their own phones and accounts.

In conclusion, I believe that social media companies should have stricter age requirements because young people need to be protected from the harmful effects of these platforms.

Essay B

The impulse to protect children from social media is understandable—and the evidence of harm is mounting. But the way we frame the problem determines the solutions we consider. Perspective One frames it as a corporate responsibility issue, Perspective Two as an education issue, and Perspective Three as a family issue. Each framing captures something real but misses something crucial.

Perspective One is right that platforms bear significant responsibility. Instagram's own internal research, leaked in 2021, showed the company knew its algorithm was promoting eating disorder content to teenage girls—and chose profit over protection. When the product itself is engineered to exploit developmental vulnerabilities, blaming users or parents is like blaming pedestrians for unsafe roads. Age verification is a reasonable first step, though Perspective Two correctly notes that enforcement without surveillance is technically challenging.

Where Perspective Two offers the strongest insight is in its long-term vision. Finland integrated media literacy into its national curriculum in 2016, and Finnish students now consistently outperform peers in identifying misinformation. Education builds the kind of resilience that outlasts any single policy. But education alone cannot neutralize platforms designed by teams of engineers to maximize engagement—that is an unfair fight between a classroom lesson and a billion-dollar algorithm.

Perspective Three's emphasis on family autonomy reflects an important value, but it rests on the assumption that all families have equal capacity to monitor and guide their children's digital lives. A single parent working two jobs cannot match the resources of a technology company. Families need structural support, not just moral responsibility.

The most effective approach synthesizes all three: regulate platform design to reduce algorithmic harm (Perspective One), invest in media literacy education (Perspective Two), and support families with tools and information rather than blame (Perspective Three). No single stakeholder can solve this alone because the problem itself is systemic.

Planning Sprint

5-Minute Plan on ACT Prompt 6

ACT-P06: Public Libraries in the Digital Age

Issue: As more information, entertainment, and services move online, some communities have questioned the role and funding of public libraries. Library supporters point to their expanding services—computer access, community programs, social services—while critics argue that limited public funds could be better spent elsewhere. The debate reflects larger questions about what communities owe their members and how public spaces should evolve.

Perspective One: Public libraries are more essential than ever because they serve as equalizers in an increasingly digital world. Not everyone can afford internet access, computers, or e-books at home, and libraries provide these resources for free alongside job-search assistance, literacy programs, and safe community spaces. Reducing library funding would widen the gap between those who can pay for access to information and those who cannot.

Perspective Two: While libraries once served a vital purpose, the digital age has made their core function—providing access to information—largely obsolete for most people. Public funds are limited, and communities should redirect library budgets toward services with broader impact, such as expanding broadband internet access to underserved areas. Investing in infrastructure that reaches people where they already are is smarter than maintaining buildings fewer people visit each year.

Perspective Three: The future of libraries lies not in their traditional role as book repositories but in their transformation into multi-purpose community hubs—spaces for collaboration, maker labs, adult education, small business support, and civic gathering. Libraries should be reimagined and reinvested in, not as relics of the past but as adaptable institutions that serve whatever needs a community identifies.
