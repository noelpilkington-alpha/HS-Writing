Phase 1: Teach

ACT Rubric Calibration

The ACT essay rubric uses 4 domains scored 1-6. Before you can accurately self-assess, you need to calibrate your judgment by scoring real examples. This is the same principle from A1 L13 (thesis triage, self-assessment calibration) applied to ACT writing.

Calibration Principle: You can't hit a target you can't see. By scoring sample essays, you internalize what "strong" and "weak" look like in each domain. Then you can diagnose your own work.

Sample Essay B (excerpt)

The debate over homework often pits those who see it as essential practice against those who argue it deepens inequality. Perspective Two makes a compelling point: homework punishes students who lack resources at home. I've watched my younger brother struggle with math homework in our noisy apartment while classmates with private tutors and quiet study rooms sail through the same assignments. The issue isn't whether practice matters—it's whether schools can fairly assign work that depends on resources they don't control.

However, Perspective Three's emphasis on quality over quantity offers a path forward. The problem isn't all homework; it's busywork. When my AP History teacher assigned us to research a local historical site and present our findings, I learned more in that one project than in a semester of textbook chapter questions. That kind of homework—creative, meaningful, connected to the real world—doesn't require expensive resources, just thoughtful design. Schools should assign less homework overall, but ensure that what they do assign is worth students' time regardless of what advantages they bring from home.

Perspective One argues that reducing homework lowers standards, but this assumes that quantity equals rigor. Finland, frequently cited as having one of the world's strongest education systems, assigns far less homework than the U.S. yet consistently outperforms us on international assessments. This suggests that what happens during the school day matters more than what happens after. The goal should be effectiveness, not exhaustion.

Phase 3: Planning Sprint

5-Minute Plan for Essay #2

ACT-P06: Public Libraries in the Digital Age

Issue: As more information, entertainment, and services move online, some communities have questioned the role and funding of public libraries. Library supporters point to their expanding services—computer access, community programs, social services—while critics argue that limited public funds could be better spent elsewhere. The debate reflects larger questions about what communities owe their members and how public spaces should evolve.

Perspective One: Public libraries are more essential than ever because they serve as equalizers in an increasingly digital world. Not everyone can afford internet access, computers, or e-books at home, and libraries provide these resources for free alongside job-search assistance, literacy programs, and safe community spaces. Reducing library funding would widen the gap between those who can pay for access to information and those who cannot.

Perspective Two: While libraries once served a vital purpose, the digital age has made their core function—providing access to information—largely obsolete for most people. Public funds are limited, and communities should redirect library budgets toward services with broader impact, such as expanding broadband internet access to underserved areas. Investing in infrastructure that reaches people where they already are is smarter than maintaining buildings fewer people visit each year.

Perspective Three: The future of libraries lies not in their traditional role as book repositories but in their transformation into multi-purpose community hubs—spaces for collaboration, maker labs, adult education, small business support, and civic gathering. Libraries should be reimagined and reinvested in, not as relics of the past but as adaptable institutions that serve whatever needs a community identifies. The building and the mission are assets, even if the specific services change entirely.
