Rhetorical Analysis Thesis

Writing AP-Level Thesis Statements with Line of Reasoning

⏱ ~25 min 📍 KCs: C.AP.L.04 🧠 Mental Model: M4
The AP Rhetorical Analysis Thesis must do three things in one sentence: (1) name specific rhetorical strategies, (2) state the writer's purpose or argument, and (3) establish a line of reasoning that connects strategies to purpose. This is the Row A bar.

Phase 1: Teach (3 min)

What Makes a Thesis Earn Row A?

In A1 and A2, you learned to write thesis statements for argumentative and analytical essays. The AP rhetorical analysis thesis builds on those skills but adds a critical requirement: line of reasoning.

The AP rubric's Row A awards 0 or 1 point for the thesis. To earn that point, your thesis must:

Row A Requirements (0 or 1 point)

  • Be defensible: Take a position about HOW the writer achieves their purpose
  • Establish a line of reasoning: Preview the analytical path your essay will follow
  • Connect strategies to purpose: Don't just list strategies—name what they accomplish

The line of reasoning is what distinguishes a Row A score of 1 from a score of 0. It's not enough to say "The author uses strategies"—you must preview how those strategies work together to achieve a specific effect.

Thesis Formula (to fade):

[Author] uses [strategy 1] and [strategy 2] to [purpose] by [how—the line of reasoning].

Note: This formula is training wheels. By the end of today's lesson, you'll write theses without needing the template.

Check Question

Which of the following would earn Row A = 0?

  1. A thesis that names strategies but doesn't state what the writer accomplishes
  2. A thesis that states the writer's purpose but doesn't name strategies
  3. A thesis that lists strategies and purpose but provides no line of reasoning
  4. All of the above
Show Answer

Answer: D — All of the above

Why: Row A requires ALL THREE elements: (1) named strategies, (2) stated purpose, and (3) line of reasoning connecting them. Missing any one element results in a score of 0.

Phase 2: Model (4 min)

Scoring Three Thesis Attempts: Douglass's "Fourth of July" Speech

Passage Context: Frederick Douglass, "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" (1852)

Douglass delivered this speech to a white abolitionist audience in Rochester, New York, the day after Independence Day. Speaking as a formerly enslaved person, he used the occasion to expose the hypocrisy of celebrating American freedom while millions remained enslaved.

SP-001: Frederick Douglass, July 5, 1852

Watch how three different thesis statements score on Row A:

Row A: 0 points

Thesis Attempt 1: "Douglass uses many rhetorical strategies in his speech."

Why 0? No strategies are named ("many" is vague), no purpose is stated (what does Douglass accomplish?), and there's no line of reasoning. This thesis could apply to literally any speech.

Row A: 0 points

Thesis Attempt 2: "Douglass uses irony and biblical allusion to criticize slavery."

Why 0? Strategies are named (irony, biblical allusion) and the topic is mentioned (criticize slavery), but there's no line of reasoning. How do these strategies accomplish that criticism? What's the analytical path? The thesis doesn't tell us.

Row A: 1 point

Thesis Attempt 3: "Douglass deploys irony and biblical allusion to expose the hypocrisy of celebrating freedom while maintaining slavery, building from shared religious values to an indictment his audience cannot dismiss without rejecting their own principles."

Why 1? This thesis has all three elements: (1) Strategies named (irony, biblical allusion), (2) Purpose stated (expose hypocrisy), and (3) Line of reasoning ("building from shared religious values to an indictment his audience cannot dismiss without rejecting their own principles"). The last phrase is the line of reasoning—it tells us how the strategies work together.

The Line of Reasoning Is the Key: Notice that Thesis 2 and Thesis 3 name the same strategies and similar purposes, but only Thesis 3 earns the point because it explains how the strategies build toward the effect. That "building from... to..." phrase is what transforms identification into analysis.

Phase 3: Supported Practice (8 min)

Write Two Thesis Statements

You'll write thesis statements for two different passages. Use the formula if you need it, but try to internalize the three requirements: strategies, purpose, and line of reasoning.

Passage 1: Ida B. Wells

Passage Context: Ida B. Wells, "Lynch Law in All Its Phases" (1893)

That the Southern white man by educating himself — by dispelling his own ignorance, by learning the history of this country and its laws, and by acquainting himself with what civilization means, may possibly equip himself with the power to deal with this question rationally. And when the day shall come that he does that, he will discover that what he calls 'the race problem' is nothing but an invention to cover the real question at issue. The real question is: will the white man of America maintain his supremacy by violence and subjugation, or will he grant to the Negro what is the Negro's right — the right to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness?

SP-013: Ida B. Wells, Washington, D.C., 1893

Your Task: Write a thesis statement that analyzes Wells's rhetorical choices. Consider: What strategies does she use? What is her purpose? How do those strategies work together?

Formula Reminder (if you need it):

[Author] uses [strategy 1] and [strategy 2] to [purpose] by [line of reasoning showing how strategies work together].

Self-Check

After writing your thesis, check it against Row A criteria:

  • Did you name at least 2 specific strategies?
  • Did you state Wells's purpose or argument?
  • Did you include a line of reasoning that explains how the strategies achieve that purpose?

If all three = yes, you've earned Row A = 1.

Passage 2: Frances Harper

Passage Context: Frances E.W. Harper, "We Are All Bound Up Together" (1866)

We are all bound up together in one great bundle of humanity, and society cannot trample on the weakest and feeblest of its members without receiving the curse in its own soul. You tried that in the case of the Negro. You pressed him down for two centuries; and in so doing you crippled the moral strength and paralyzed the spiritual energies of the white men of the country. When the hands of the black were fettered, white men were deprived of the liberty of speech and the freedom of the press.

SP-024: Frances E.W. Harper, Eleventh National Woman's Rights Convention, 1866

Your Task: Write a thesis statement analyzing Harper's rhetorical choices. This time, try writing without looking at the formula.

Self-Score

Score your own thesis on Row A (0 or 1). Be honest: Did you include all three elements, or did you stop at naming strategies and purpose without the line of reasoning?

Phase 4: Independent Practice (7 min)

Write a Thesis for a Fresh Passage

Passage Context: William Lloyd Garrison, "No Compromise with Slavery" (1854)

I am a believer in that portion of the Declaration of American Independence in which it is set forth, as among self-evident truths, 'that all men are created equal; that they are endowed by their Creator with certain inalienable rights; that among these are life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.' Hence, I am an abolitionist. Hence, I cannot but regard oppression in every form — and most of all, that which turns a man into a thing — with indignation and abhorrence. Not to cherish these feelings would be recreancy to principle. They who desire me to be dumb on the subject of slavery, unless I will open my mouth in its defense, ask me to give the lie to my professions, to degrade my manhood, and to stain my soul.

SP-028: William Lloyd Garrison, Boston, July 4, 1854

Your Task: Write a thesis statement analyzing Garrison's rhetorical choices. No formula visible. After writing, score your thesis on Row A (0 or 1), then revise if needed.

Row A Self-Assessment (0 or 1)

Give yourself 1 point only if your thesis has ALL THREE:

  • Named at least 2 specific rhetorical strategies
  • Stated Garrison's purpose or argument
  • Established a line of reasoning (showed how the strategies work together to achieve the purpose)

If you scored 0, revise your thesis before moving on. The most common error: naming strategies and purpose but forgetting the line of reasoning.

Show Model Thesis (after you've written yours)

Model Thesis: "Garrison uses direct quotation from the Declaration of Independence, moral absolutes, and personal testimony to reframe abolitionism not as a radical position but as loyalty to America's founding principles, thereby positioning his opponents as the true traitors who demand he 'give the lie' to those self-evident truths."

Why this earns Row A = 1:

  • Strategies named: direct quotation from Declaration, moral absolutes, personal testimony
  • Purpose stated: reframe abolitionism as loyalty to founding principles
  • Line of reasoning: "thereby positioning his opponents as the true traitors who demand he 'give the lie' to those self-evident truths" — this shows how the strategies work together to accomplish the reframing

Phase 5: Transfer (3 min)

Compare Your AP Thesis to Earlier Work

Think back to the thesis statements you wrote in A1 and A2. What's different about the AP thesis?

The AP thesis must do three things in one sentence: name strategies, state purpose, and preview reasoning. That's the Row A bar. In your A1 essays, your thesis stated your position. In your A2 synthesis essays, your thesis stated your claim and acknowledged complexity. The AP rhetorical analysis thesis adds a new layer: you must preview how the strategies work together, not just what they do.

Reflection

Which of the three Row A requirements is hardest for you?

  • Naming specific strategies (not just "ethos" or "pathos")
  • Stating the writer's precise purpose (not just "to persuade")
  • Establishing line of reasoning (the how, not just the what)

Your answer tells you what to focus on as you practice. Most students find the line of reasoning hardest because it requires showing the connection between strategies and effect.

Looking Ahead: In L06, you'll write full body paragraphs that deliver on what your thesis promises. Each body paragraph will analyze one strategy using the S-F-E framework (Strategy-Function-Effect) you already know from A2. The thesis is your roadmap; the body paragraphs are where you develop the analysis.
