Alternative Reading Drill

Writing Sophistication Move 2: Alternative Interpretation

⏱ ~25 min 📍 KCs: C.HS.03 (Alternative Reading) 🧠 Mental Model: M6
Alternative Reading: A sophistication move that acknowledges a different possible interpretation of the same evidence, then argues why your reading is more persuasive. This is NOT counterargument (which addresses opposing positions). Alternative reading shows you can hold two interpretations simultaneously and defend your choice. Format: "An alternative reading might suggest that... However, [your reading] is more persuasive because..."

Phase 1: Teach (2 min)

Alternative Reading vs. Counterargument

You've practiced counterargument since A1: acknowledging opposing POSITIONS and refuting them. Counterargument says: "Some believe X, but I argue Y."

Alternative Reading is different. It acknowledges a different INTERPRETATION of the same text or evidence. Alternative reading says: "This passage could be read as X, but a stronger reading sees it as Y."

The distinction:

  • Counterargument: "Some argue Douglass is too angry to persuade moderates." (About the argument's effectiveness)
  • Alternative Reading: "Douglass's anger could be read as alienating, but a closer reading reveals it as strategically controlled to shame rather than alienate." (About how to interpret the rhetorical choice)

Alternative reading is a sophistication move because it shows you understand that texts support multiple interpretations. The AP reader sees you thinking PAST your first interpretation.

Check Yourself

Which of these is an alternative reading (not a counterargument)?

  1. "Critics say Wells exaggerates lynching statistics, but the evidence supports her claims."
  2. "Wells's statistical evidence could be read as emotionally distant, but it actually establishes rational authority in a context where emotional appeals might be dismissed."
  3. "Some believe Wells should have focused on legal reform rather than public opinion."
Show Answer

Answer: B

Why: B offers an alternative INTERPRETATION of Wells's rhetorical choice (statistics) and explains why one reading is stronger. A and C are counterarguments about her claims and strategy, not alternative readings of textual evidence. Alternative reading stays within the text's rhetorical choices and explores different ways to understand them.

Phase 2: Model (4 min)

Modeling Alternative Reading: Frances Harper

Excerpt: Frances Harper, "We Are All Bound Up Together" (1866)

We are all bound up together in one great bundle of humanity, and society cannot trample on the weakest and feeblest of its members without receiving the curse in its own soul. You tried that in the case of the negro. You pressed him down for two centuries; and in so doing you crippled the moral strength and paralyzed the spiritual energies of the white men of the country. When the hands of the black were fettered, white men were deprived of the liberty of speech and the freedom of the press. Society cannot afford to neglect the enlightenment of any class of its members.

I do not believe that giving the woman the ballot is immediately going to cure all the ills of life. I do not believe that white women are dew-drops just exhaled from the skies. I think that like men they may be divided into three classes, the good, the bad, and the indifferent. The good would vote according to their convictions and principles; the bad, as dictated by prejudice or malice; and the indifferent will vote on the strongest side of the question, with the winning party.

Frances Ellen Watkins Harper, "We Are All Bound Up Together," speech at the Eleventh National Women's Rights Convention, New York, May 1866 (public domain excerpt)

Watch a writer build an alternative reading, including the false start.

Writer's thinking process:

Harper uses personal testimony about being bound together to establish authority. Her lived experience as a Black woman gives her credibility to speak about both racial and gender oppression.

Wait — I'm analyzing her strategy, but I haven't acknowledged that this passage could support a DIFFERENT interpretation. Let me try the alternative reading frame.

Harper's metaphor of "one great bundle of humanity" uses personal testimony to establish authority. An alternative reading might suggest that Harper's personal testimony risks reducing a systemic argument to individual experience—that by framing oppression as interpersonal rather than structural, she makes injustice seem like a failure of empathy rather than law. However, this reading is less persuasive than recognizing that Harper's personal language ("we are all bound up together") functions strategically to make systemic oppression VISIBLE through relational metaphor. The "bundle" imagery doesn't sentimentalize injustice—it demonstrates that white supremacy damages the oppressor as surely as the oppressed ("you crippled the moral strength... of white men"). Harper's personal frame thus becomes the vehicle for a structural argument: society cannot separate itself from the consequences of its own violence. The testimony doesn't replace analysis; it embodies it.

Notice: The alternative reading ("risks reducing systemic to individual") is plausible—it's a reading someone COULD make. But the writer argues why their original reading is stronger. This is sophistication: holding two interpretations and defending one.

Phase 3: Supported Practice (10 min)

Build Alternative Readings

Your Task: For each of the 2 analysis claims below, write an alternative reading using the frame: "An alternative reading might suggest that... However, [your original reading] is more persuasive because..."

Original Analysis Claim Your Alternative Reading
Claim 1: Harper's acknowledgment that women "may be divided into three classes, the good, the bad, and the indifferent" uses realism to strengthen her credibility. By refusing to idealize women voters, she disarms the objection that women's suffrage would purify politics. Write: "An alternative reading might suggest that... However, [the realism reading] is more persuasive because..."
Claim 2: Harper's claim that white men "were deprived of the liberty of speech and the freedom of the press" when Black hands were fettered reframes abolition as serving white interests. This rhetorical move transforms racial justice from charity into self-interest. Write: "An alternative reading might suggest that... However, [the self-interest reading] is more persuasive because..."
Self-Check

After writing your alternative readings, ask:

  • Is my alternative reading PLAUSIBLE? (Could a reasonable reader see the text this way?)
  • Does my rebuttal explain WHY the original reading is STRONGER? (Not just "I prefer it," but "it accounts for X that the alternative misses")
  • Did I stay focused on INTERPRETATION of the text, not on whether Harper's argument is correct?

Phase 4: Independent Practice (7 min)

Write a Full Body Paragraph with Alternative Reading

Excerpt: William Apess, "An Indian's Looking-Glass for the White Man" (1833)

By what you read, you must judge how good or bad they are. Can you charge the Indians with robbing a nation almost of their whole continent, and murdering their women and children, and then depriving the remainder of their lawful rights, that nature and God require them to have? And to cap the climax, rob another nation to till their grounds and welter out their days under the lash with hunger and fatigue under the scorching rays of a burning sun? I should look at all the skins, and I know that when I cast my eye upon that white skin, and if I saw those crimes written upon it, I should enter my protest against it immediately and cleave to that which is more honorable.

William Apess, "An Indian's Looking-Glass for the White Man," essay, 1833 (public domain excerpt)

Your Task: Write a body paragraph (7-10 sentences) that:

  1. Makes an analytical claim about Apess's rhetorical strategy in this passage
  2. Provides S-F-E analysis (Strategy-Function-Effect)
  3. Includes an alternative reading move using the frame: "An alternative reading might suggest... However..."

Success Criteria:

  • Clear topic sentence identifying a rhetorical strategy
  • S-F-E analysis that explains how the strategy works
  • Alternative reading that is plausible (not a straw man)
  • Rebuttal that explains why the original reading is stronger
  • Organic integration (doesn't feel tacked on)
Click for a model paragraph

Apess's inversion strategy—his use of "I should look at all the skins" to judge moral worth rather than racial identity—employs irony to expose the hypocrisy of white Christian judgment. By adopting the rhetoric of moral evaluation ("judge how good or bad they are") and systematically cataloging white crimes (continental theft, genocide, enslavement), Apess demonstrates that any honest application of Christian moral standards would condemn whiteness itself. An alternative reading might suggest that Apess's reversal risks reinforcing racial essentialism—that by speaking of "crimes written upon" white skin, he reproduces the very logic of racial judgment he critiques. However, the ironic frame ("if I saw those crimes written upon it") makes clear that Apess is performing white logic to reveal its absurdity, not endorsing skin-based judgment. The conditional "if I saw" signals that Apess doesn't actually believe moral worth inheres in skin color; he's demonstrating that IF one applied white America's own standards consistently, whiteness would be the condemned category. The irony doesn't perpetuate essentialism—it weaponizes it against itself.

Phase 5: Transfer (2 min)

Alternative Reading in Your Planning: When you plan an essay, you naturally form a first interpretation of the text's strategies. Before you write, ask: "What's a different way someone could read this same evidence?" That question generates your alternative reading. The sophistication comes from acknowledging the alternative and defending your interpretation.

Test: If you removed the alternative reading sentence from your paragraph, would your analysis lose depth? If yes, the alternative reading is organic. If no, it's decorative.

Alternative reading shows the AP reader that you thought PAST your first interpretation. Most students write their first reading and stop. The sophistication point rewards the second thought—the moment where you ask, "But what if someone read this differently?" and then argue back.

Reflection Prompt

Compare alternative reading to the counterargument strategy you learned in A1. When would you use counterargument (opposing positions) vs. alternative reading (different interpretations)? How does alternative reading change the level of analytical depth in your writing?
