Phase 1: Teach (2 min)

AP Attribution Patterns: Beyond "Source A Says"

You learned attribution patterns in A2. AP Synthesis demands attribution fluency—the ability to integrate sources smoothly and vary your language so the essay reads as sophisticated prose, not a list of source summaries.

Three New AP-Level Attribution Patterns:

Pattern Example
Embedded Credentials "According to a 2022 Pew study (Source D), 68% of Americans prioritize infrastructure over space exploration." — The credential (Pew study, 2022) builds ethos.
Attributing While Disagreeing "While Source B claims that space spending diverts resources from urgent needs, this framing assumes a zero-sum budget when history shows investment in exploration generates economic returns." — You cite and rebut simultaneously.
Cross-Source Synthesis "Both Source A and Source C acknowledge the economic benefits of space research, yet they diverge on whether private industry or government should lead investment." — You synthesize agreement and disagreement across sources.

Why this matters: Varied attribution = sophisticated writing. Essays that use 5+ different attribution patterns score higher on Row B and often earn Row C (sophistication) because they demonstrate rhetorical control.

Check Yourself

Which sentence demonstrates attribution fluency?

  1. "Source C says that space exploration is expensive."
  2. "According to Source C, space exploration is expensive."
  3. "NASA's $25 billion budget, while substantial in absolute terms, represents less than 0.5% of federal spending (Source C), challenging the notion that space investment crowds out other priorities."
Show Answer

Answer: C

Why: Sentence C integrates the source smoothly into an analytical claim. It uses embedded data, parenthetical citation, and commentary ("challenging the notion...") rather than just reporting what the source says. Sentences A and B are mechanical attribution—they report but don't integrate or analyze.

Phase 3: Supported Practice — Full Synthesis Planning (13 min)

15-Minute Full Synthesis Planning: SYN-06 Environment & Economic Growth

Context: This is the full planning sequence you'll use tomorrow in L16 when you write the complete synthesis essay. You have 15 minutes to: (1) Read 6 sources using Rapid Source Analysis (L13 strategy), (2) Build matrix, (3) Select sources, (4) Write thesis, (5) Outline 3 body paragraphs with source assignments.

Prompt: Some argue that environmental protection and economic growth are fundamentally in conflict, while others contend they can coexist. Develop a position on the relationship between environmental sustainability and economic prosperity, using at least three of the provided sources.

Source A: UN Report on Green Economy (2021)

The transition to renewable energy could create 24 million jobs globally by 2030, offsetting the 6 million jobs lost in fossil fuel industries. Green infrastructure investment yields long-term economic returns: every $1 invested in climate resilience saves $4-6 in disaster recovery costs. The notion that environmental protection stifles growth rests on outdated models that ignore externalities like pollution-related healthcare costs and climate-driven agricultural losses.

Source B: Chamber of Commerce Position Paper (2022)

Aggressive environmental regulations impose compliance costs that reduce business competitiveness, particularly for small manufacturers. The 2021 EPA emissions standards cost U.S. businesses an estimated $14 billion annually in retrofitting expenses. While sustainability is important, policymakers must balance environmental goals with economic realities. Overly rapid transitions risk job losses in coal, oil, and gas sectors where workers lack retraining pathways.

Source C: Economist Interview, *The Atlantic* (2023)

The environment-economy dichotomy is false. Markets already favor clean energy because solar and wind are now cheaper than coal in most regions. What businesses need is policy certainty—not lax regulation or aggressive mandates, but consistent long-term frameworks. The real conflict isn't environment versus economy; it's short-term thinking versus long-term prosperity. Companies that invest in sustainability outperform competitors over 10-year periods.

Source D: Case Study — Costa Rica's Green Economy Model (2020)

Costa Rica generates 99% of its electricity from renewable sources while maintaining 5% annual GDP growth over the past decade. The country's "payment for ecosystem services" program pays landowners to preserve forests, which sustains biodiversity, ecotourism (10% of GDP), and carbon sequestration. This model demonstrates that environmental investment can drive economic growth when designed strategically, challenging the zero-sum framing common in U.S. debates.

Source E: Labor Union Statement on Energy Transition (2023)

Workers in fossil fuel industries face real economic harm from rapid energy transitions unless policymakers provide retraining, relocation support, and bridge income. The environment-economy debate ignores the human costs borne disproportionately by working-class communities. Any credible climate policy must include a "just transition" that protects livelihoods, not abstract arguments about long-term GDP growth that mean little to laid-off coal miners.

Source F: McKinsey Global Institute Report (2022)

Climate inaction costs more than climate action. By 2050, unmitigated climate change could reduce global GDP by 11-14% through agricultural collapse, infrastructure damage, and forced migration. In contrast, achieving net-zero emissions by 2050 requires $9.2 trillion in annual investment but yields $43 trillion in economic benefits over 30 years. The question isn't whether we can afford climate action, but whether we can afford inaction.
