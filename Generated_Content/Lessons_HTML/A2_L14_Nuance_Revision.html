Course A2 • Lesson 14

Nuance Revision + Self-Score

Revising flat arguments into nuanced positions using three deliberate moves

⏱ ~25 min 🎯 C.10.13, C.10.12 📖 Revision practice + self-assessment

Overview

Now that you can identify Weak, Adequate, and Strong writing (L13), you'll practice the revision moves that transform flat, binary arguments into nuanced positions. This lesson focuses on three specific techniques you learned in L06: qualifying, acknowledging complexity, and reframing.

Three Nuance Moves (from L06):

  1. Qualify: Add conditions or limits ("In most cases..." "When X is true..." "For certain populations...")
  2. Acknowledge Complexity: Concede valid counterpoints before responding ("While X is true in situation Y, it fails to account for Z...")
  3. Reframe: Shift the question ("The real question isn't X vs. Y, but Z...")

All three moves are drawn from They Say/I Say templates. The key is applying them deliberately to revision—not just recognizing them in others' writing.

Teach

The Revision Mindset

Time: 2 minutes

Most students think revision means "fixing errors." That's editing. Revision means re-seeing your argument—identifying where you've taken a binary position and replacing it with a nuanced one.

Binary thinking sounds like this: "X is good." "Y is bad." "We should do Z."

Nuanced thinking sounds like this: "While X has benefits in context A, it creates problems in context B." "The debate between Y and Z overlooks the real issue, which is W."

The three moves give you concrete tools for this transformation. You're not inventing nuance from scratch—you're applying specific templates to your existing argument.

Model

Revising a Flat Paragraph into a Nuanced One

Time: 3 minutes

Before (Flat/Binary):

"Standardized testing should be eliminated from college admissions. These tests are biased against low-income students and don't measure real intelligence or potential. Students who can afford expensive test prep courses score higher, which is unfair. Colleges should focus on grades, essays, and extracurriculars instead. Standardized tests do more harm than good and have no place in a fair admissions process."

Binary argument—no nuance, dismisses counterarguments

After (Nuanced—using all three moves):

"While standardized testing was designed to create a common measure across different high schools [Move 2: Acknowledge Complexity], the reality is that access to test preparation is not evenly distributed. Students whose families can afford expensive prep courses score an average of 100 points higher on the SAT than peers with similar grades but no test prep, according to a 2019 study by the National Association for College Admission Counseling. [Move 1: Qualify] For students from well-resourced schools with built-in test prep, standardized tests may indeed provide useful data; for students without those resources, the tests measure opportunity, not aptitude. [Move 3: Reframe] The question is not whether standardized tests should exist, but whether colleges should rely on a metric that reflects family income as much as it reflects academic potential."

Nuanced revision—acknowledges complexity, qualifies claims, reframes the debate

Revision process (thinking aloud):

The original says "should be eliminated"—that's too absolute. Let me just say "should be optional."

Wait, that's still binary thinking. I need to use the three moves deliberately. What's the strongest counterargument? That standardized tests were designed to be fair—to give all students the same measure. Let me acknowledge that first.

Then I'll qualify: tests might work for SOME students (those with resources) but not others. That's Move 1.

Finally, reframe: The issue isn't whether tests should exist, but whether colleges should rely on a measure that's influenced by family income. That shifts from yes/no to "what does this metric actually measure?"

Supported Practice

Revise 2 Flat Paragraphs

Time: 8 minutes

Instructions: Read each flat paragraph below. Revise it into a nuanced paragraph using at least ONE of the three moves. Write your revision in the text box provided.

Flat Paragraph 1: Screen Time for Kids

"Parents should strictly limit their children's screen time. Too much time on phones and tablets is bad for kids' development and makes them less social. Studies show that screen time is linked to attention problems and poor sleep. Children need to play outside and interact with other people face-to-face, not stare at screens all day. Unlimited screen time is harmful and parents need to set clear boundaries."

Binary position—no acknowledgment of complexity or counterarguments

Your Task: Revise this into a nuanced paragraph (60+ words). Use at least one of the three moves: qualify, acknowledge complexity, or reframe.

Hint: What's the strongest counterargument? (Screen time isn't all bad—it enables learning, connection.) How can you acknowledge that before explaining why unlimited access is still problematic?

Flat Paragraph 2: Fast Fashion

"Fast fashion companies like Shein and H&M are destroying the environment and exploiting workers. These companies produce cheap clothing that falls apart quickly, which fills landfills. They also pay garment workers unfair wages in unsafe conditions. People should stop buying from fast fashion brands and support sustainable fashion instead. If everyone boycotted these companies, the industry would have to change."

Binary position—no qualification or engagement with complexity

Your Task: Revise this into a nuanced paragraph (60+ words). Use at least one of the three moves: qualify, acknowledge complexity, or reframe.

Hint: What does the binary position overlook? (Many people rely on fast fashion because they can't afford sustainable alternatives.) How can you reframe from "boycott vs. don't boycott" to a more complex question?

Independent Practice

Self-Score + Revise Your L07 Essay

Time: 8 minutes

Task: Return to your Lesson 7 argument essay (Sophisticated Argument Essay). Read it carefully. Score it using the 3-point rubric (Weak, Adequate, Strong). Identify ONE paragraph or claim that could be strengthened through nuance. Write a revised version of that paragraph using the three moves.

Success Criteria

  • You've identified a specific paragraph or claim from your L07 essay that reads as flat or binary
  • Your revision uses at least ONE of the three nuance moves (qualify, acknowledge complexity, reframe)
  • The revised version is more nuanced than the original—it doesn't just restate the same position in different words
  • Revised paragraph is 80+ words
Before You Revise

Self-score your L07 essay: Based on the 3-point rubric from L13, would you score your essay as Weak, Adequate, or Strong? Why?

Identify one weakness: Which paragraph or claim in your essay is the flattest (most binary, least nuanced)?

Write your revised paragraph below:

Include a brief note at the top: "Original claim: [state your original flat claim]. Revision goal: [which nuance move you're using]."

Transfer

Reflection: Before and After

Time: 3 minutes

Self-Check

Compare your original paragraph to your revision:

  • Is the revised version more nuanced? How?
  • Which move did you use (qualify, acknowledge complexity, reframe)?
  • Does the revision feel stronger, or does it feel like you're "backing down" from your original position?

Key insight: Many students worry that adding nuance makes their argument weaker—like they're "admitting they're wrong." In reality, nuance strengthens arguments by demonstrating sophisticated thinking. Readers trust writers who acknowledge complexity more than writers who pretend issues are simple.

Looking Ahead: In Lesson 15, you'll practice timed writing with a focus on "good enough" planning. The nuance moves you've practiced here—qualify, acknowledge complexity, reframe—can be applied quickly under time pressure once you've internalized them. You don't need to write perfectly nuanced first drafts; you need to know how to revise toward nuance efficiently.
