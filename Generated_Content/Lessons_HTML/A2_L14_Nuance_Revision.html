Course A2 • Lesson 14

Nuance Revision + Self-Score

Revising flat arguments into nuanced positions using three deliberate moves

~25 min C.10.13, C.10.12 Revision practice + self-assessment

Overview

Now that you can identify Weak, Adequate, and Strong writing (L13), you will practice the revision moves that transform flat, binary arguments into nuanced positions. Nuance Revision means taking a binary argument and making it sophisticated—using the same They Say/I Say moves you learned earlier, applied to REVISION.

Phase 1: Teach (2 min)

Teach

Three Nuance Moves for Revision

Nuance Revision = taking a binary argument and making it sophisticated. Three moves:

  1. Qualify the claim — "In most cases..." not "Always..." Add conditions, limits, or contexts that make the claim more precise.
  2. Acknowledge complexity — "While X is true in context Y, it fails in context Z." Concede valid opposing points before responding.
  3. Reframe the debate — "The real question isn't X vs. Y, but Z." Shift from the obvious binary to a deeper issue.

These are the same They Say/I Say templates from L04-L06, applied to revision. You already know how to recognize these moves. Now you will use them to upgrade your own writing from Adequate toward Strong.

Phase 2: Model (3 min)

Model

Before and After: Smartphone Ban Revision

Watch a flat student paragraph get revised into a nuanced one. Pay attention to which moves are used and where.

Before (Flat)

"Schools should ban smartphones because they distract students from learning. Students are always on their phones during class and it makes them not pay attention. Teachers can't teach when everyone is looking at their screens. Phones should not be allowed in school."

Flat/binary argument — no nuance, no counterargument engagement

After (Nuanced)

"While unrestricted smartphone access during class does fragment attention and reduce engagement with complex material [Move 2: Acknowledge Complexity], outright bans ignore smartphones' legitimate educational uses—research, collaboration tools, and accessibility features for students with disabilities [Move 1: Qualify]. Rather than choosing between unfettered access and complete prohibition, schools should establish context-dependent policies that teach students to recognize when devices serve learning and when they undermine it [Move 3: Reframe]."

Nuanced revision — all three moves applied

Revision process (thinking aloud):

The original says "should ban"—that's absolute. I could just soften it to "should limit"...

Wait, just softening the claim isn't real nuance. I need to use the moves deliberately. What's the strongest counterargument to a phone ban? Some students NEED phones—accessibility features, research tools, collaboration apps. Let me acknowledge that complexity first.

Now I can qualify: unrestricted access IS a problem (I'm not backing down from that), but the solution isn't binary. Context matters—phones during a lecture are different from phones during a research project.

Finally, reframe: The real question isn't "ban or allow" but "how do we teach students to manage their own device use?" That shifts from a rule-enforcement question to an educational question.

Notice: The revised version is actually shorter than the original but says far more. Nuance is not about adding words—it is about replacing binary thinking with sophisticated thinking.

Phase 3: Supported Practice (8 min)

Supported Practice

Revise 2 Flat Paragraphs

Read each flat, binary argument paragraph. Revise it into a nuanced version using at least ONE of the three moves. Write your revision in the text box.

Flat Paragraph 1: Social Media and Teenagers

"Social media is bad for teenagers and should be banned for anyone under 18. It causes depression, anxiety, and cyberbullying. Kids spend too much time on their phones instead of doing productive things. Parents should delete all social media apps from their children's devices."

Binary argument — no qualification, no counterargument engagement

Your Task: Draft a nuanced revision of this paragraph (60+ words). Use at least one of the three moves: qualify, acknowledge complexity, or reframe.

Hint: What's the strongest counterargument? (Social media also connects teens, provides community for isolated youth, enables creative expression.) How can you acknowledge that before explaining why unregulated access is still problematic?

Success Criteria

  • Qualifies the claim (adds conditions/contexts)
  • Acknowledges complexity (concedes valid opposing points)
  • Maintains a clear position despite nuance

Flat Paragraph 2: Free College

"College should be free for everyone because education is a right. Students are drowning in debt and it's not fair. Other countries offer free college and their citizens are better off. The government should pay for all college tuition."

Binary argument — no qualification, generic evidence

Your Task: Draft a nuanced revision of this paragraph (60+ words). Use at least one of the three moves: qualify, acknowledge complexity, or reframe.

Hint: What does the binary position overlook? (Who pays for "free" college? Does free tuition address the real barriers to access? Are all degrees equally valuable?) How can you reframe from "free vs. not free" to a deeper question about access and value?

Success Criteria

  • Qualifies the claim (adds conditions/contexts)
  • Acknowledges complexity (concedes valid opposing points)
  • Maintains a clear position despite nuance

Phase 4: Independent Practice (8 min)

Independent Practice

Self-Score + Revise Your L07 Essay

Task: Return to your Lesson 7 argument essay (Sophisticated Argument Essay). Score it using the 3-point rubric from L13. Then identify one specific paragraph that could be improved, and revise it.

Before You Revise

Self-score your L07 essay: Based on the 3-point rubric (Weak, Adequate, Strong), how would you score your L07 essay overall? What specific evidence supports that score?

Identify one weakness: Which paragraph or claim in your essay is the flattest (most binary, least nuanced)? What makes it flat?

Write your revised paragraph below (80+ words). Include a brief note at the top: "Original claim: [state your original flat claim]. Revision goal: [which nuance move you are using]."

Success Criteria

  • Identifies specific weakness from L07
  • Revision demonstrates at least one nuance move
  • Score improves from original

Phase 5: Transfer (3 min)

Transfer

Reflection: Before and After

Self-Check

Compare your original L07 paragraph to your revision:

  • What changed? Be specific about which moves you applied.
  • Score the revision using the 3-point rubric. Did the score improve?
  • Does the revision feel stronger, or does it feel like you are "backing down" from your original position?

Key insight: Many students worry that adding nuance makes their argument weaker—like they are admitting they are wrong. In reality, nuance strengthens arguments by demonstrating sophisticated thinking. Readers trust writers who acknowledge complexity more than writers who pretend issues are simple.

Looking Ahead: In Lesson 15, you will practice timed writing with a focus on "good enough" planning. The nuance moves you practiced here—qualify, acknowledge complexity, reframe—can be applied quickly under time pressure once you have internalized them. You do not need to write perfectly nuanced first drafts; you need to know how to revise toward nuance efficiently.
