ACT Argument Development

Develop your own position and construct support without source texts

~25 min C.10.17, C.10.18 M4 (Claim Hierarchy)

Phase 1: Teach (3 min)

Teach

Two New Skills for ACT Writing

In L16, you learned to decompose and evaluate ACT perspectives. Now you need to develop your own position and support it without any source texts. Two distinct skills:

Skill 1: Position Development

Your position must RELATE to the given perspectives. You cannot ignore them. Three strategies:

Strategy How It Works Example Move
Agree/Extend Choose one perspective as your starting point and push it further "Perspective 2 is right about X, but it doesn't go far enough. We also need..."
Synthesize Combine insights from two or more perspectives into a hybrid position "The best approach uses P1's concern for X with P2's method of Y."
Reframe Argue all perspectives share a flawed assumption or ask the wrong question "All three assume X, but the real issue is Y."

Skill 2: Constructed Support

ACT has NO source texts. You must build evidence from your own knowledge and reasoning. Four types, ranked by persuasive strength:

Type Description Strength When to Use
Real-World Example Specific events, policies, historical precedents Strongest -- verifiable and concrete Lead with this whenever possible
Logical Reasoning Step-by-step causal chains, if-then arguments Strong -- builds airtight case When you need to explain WHY something works
Hypothetical Scenario "Imagine if..." thought experiments Moderate -- illustrative but debatable When testing a principle or making abstract ideas concrete
Analogy Comparisons to parallel situations Useful but weakest alone -- can be challenged When clarifying, not as primary evidence

Phase 2: Model (4 min)

Model

Developing Positions and Generating Support: Social Media

Returning to the Social Media Age Restrictions prompt from L16, I will develop three possible positions using the three strategies, then choose the strongest and generate all four types of support.

Prompt Reminder: Social media platforms require users to be at least 13, but enforcement is minimal. Lawmakers propose raising the minimum to 16 with age verification.

P1: Companies should enforce age 16+ with verification (protection).

P2: Education over restriction (empowerment).

P3: Parents should decide (family autonomy).

ACT-style prompt: Social Media Age Restrictions (ACT-P01)

Three Possible Positions

Position 1 -- Agree/Extend P1: Companies should verify ages, AND design platforms that are less addictive for young users. Age gates plus safer design.

Position 2 -- Synthesize P1 + P2: Age restrictions combined with mandatory digital literacy education. Corporate regulation to fund the education that P2 calls for.

Position 3 -- Reframe: The real issue is not access but design. All three perspectives debate who controls access, but none questions whether the product itself should be allowed to exploit developing brains. Platforms should be required to eliminate features that exploit cognitive vulnerabilities.

Choosing the strongest: Position 3 (Reframe) is the most intellectually interesting because it challenges the shared assumption of all three perspectives. It also creates the richest claim hierarchy (M4) -- a main claim with multiple supporting sub-claims.

Four Types of Support for Position 3

Claim: "Platforms should be required to eliminate design features that exploit developing brains."

1. Real-World Example

The UK's Age Appropriate Design Code required YouTube to disable autoplay for minors and Instagram to default under-18 accounts to private. These design changes reduced harmful exposure without requiring invasive age verification -- proving that regulating the product works better than restricting the user.

2. Logical Reasoning

If platform features are specifically designed to exploit cognitive vulnerabilities (infinite scroll, variable-ratio reinforcement, social comparison metrics), then removing those features targets the cause of harm rather than restricting the victims. Education cannot fully counteract design that teams of behavioral psychologists have optimized for addiction.

3. Hypothetical Scenario

Imagine if food companies were allowed to add chemicals specifically designed to be addictive, targeting children's developing taste buds. We would not debate whether parents should monitor meals or whether children need nutrition education -- we would regulate the product. Social media design deserves the same scrutiny.

4. Analogy

Requiring safer platform design is like requiring seat belts in cars rather than banning young drivers from the road. The fix targets the product, not the user -- and it protects everyone, not just one age group.

Ranking: Real-World Example (most persuasive -- concrete, verifiable) > Logical Reasoning (strong causal chain) > Hypothetical (illustrative but debatable) > Analogy (clarifying but not proof). Lead with the strongest; use the others to reinforce.

Phase 3: Supported Practice (8 min)

Supported Practice

Position Development: Homework Debate

Prompt Reminder -- Homework in Public Schools:

P1: Homework is essential -- builds discipline, reinforces instruction, prepares for college.

P2: Homework should be reduced -- disproportionately harms disadvantaged students.

P3: Real problem is quality, not quantity -- replace busywork with meaningful work.

ACT-style prompt: Homework in Public Schools (ACT-P02)

Develop two different positions using two different strategies (Agree/Extend, Synthesize, or Reframe). For each, explain the position and how it relates to the perspectives.

Position A

Label your strategy (Agree/Extend Perspective ___, Synthesize P___ + P___, or Reframe).

Position B

Use a DIFFERENT strategy from Position A.

Self-Check

Which position has better claim hierarchy potential (M4)? Why?

Consider: Which position generates more sub-claims? Which addresses more of the complexity? Which would produce a richer, more nuanced essay? Choose the stronger one and be ready to use it.

Phase 4: Independent Practice (8 min)

Independent Practice

Constructed Support Drill + Opposition Paragraph

Given the following claim from the homework debate, generate all four types of support and then write an opposition paragraph.

Your Claim: "Schools should replace traditional homework with in-class practice time and optional take-home enrichment projects."

Part 1: Generate All Four Types of Support

Draft a real-world example, logical reasoning chain, hypothetical scenario, and analogy to support the claim above. Then rank them by persuasiveness.

Write all four types of constructed support for the claim. Label each type clearly.

Success Criteria -- Part 1

  • Includes real-world example (specific, not vague)
  • Includes logical reasoning chain (if-then or causal)
  • Includes hypothetical scenario (tests the principle)
  • Includes analogy (parallel situation)
  • Ranks by persuasiveness with brief justification

Part 2: Opposition Paragraph

Write an opposition paragraph: acknowledge the strongest point of an opposing perspective, explain why your position still holds. Use They Say / I Say moves from L04-L05.

Compose an opposition paragraph. Identify the strongest challenge to the claim, acknowledge its validity, then explain why the position holds despite this challenge.

Success Criteria -- Part 2

  • Identifies strongest opposing point (not a straw man)
  • Acknowledges its validity genuinely
  • Explains why the position still holds despite the objection
  • Uses They Say / I Say structure

Phase 5: Transfer (3 min)

Transfer

Constructed Support vs. Textual Evidence

How is constructed support different from textual evidence (A1)?

In synthesis essays, you are a curator -- selecting, integrating, and attributing evidence from given sources. In ACT Writing, you are a builder -- generating reasoning and examples from your own knowledge. Both are essential college-level skills, but they exercise different muscles.

In ACT Writing, you are the evidence source. This means your general knowledge matters. The wider you read -- news, history, science, current events -- the richer your constructed support will be. Read widely.
