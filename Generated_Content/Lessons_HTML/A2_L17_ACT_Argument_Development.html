Lesson 17: ACT Argument Development

Course A2 (Grade 10) | Time: ~25 minutes | KCs: C.10.17 (Develop Own Perspective), C.10.18 (Generate Reasoning & Examples) | Mental Model: M4 (Claim Hierarchy)
1
Teach: Two Skills for ACT Argument Development

In Lesson 16, you learned how to analyze the three given perspectives. Now you need to develop your own position. But here's the challenge: you don't have any source texts. No articles, no data sets, no expert quotes. Just the issue and the three perspectives.

This means you need two distinct skills:

Skill 1: Position Development Strategies

Your position must relate to the given perspectives. You have three strategic options:

Strategy A: Agree and Extend

Choose one perspective as your starting point and push it further. "Perspective 2 is right about X, but it doesn't go far enough. We also need to consider Y."

Strategy B: Synthesize

Combine insights from two (or all three) perspectives to create a hybrid position. "The best approach uses Perspective 1's concern for X with Perspective 2's method of Y."

Strategy C: Reframe

Argue that all three perspectives share a flawed assumption or are asking the wrong question. "All three perspectives assume X, but the real issue is Y."

Key Point: You can't just ignore the perspectives and write your own opinion. The ACT specifically asks you to "analyze the relationship between your perspective and at least one other perspective." Your position must be in conversation with the given perspectives.

Skill 2: Constructed Support (No Sources!)

Since you have no source texts, you must build evidence from scratch. Four types of support, ranked by persuasiveness:

Ranked from Most to Least Persuasive:

  1. Real-World Examples: Specific events, historical precedents, current policies (e.g., "When Finland eliminated standardized homework in 2016, student test scores remained stable while reported stress declined.")
  2. Logical Reasoning: Step-by-step causal chains, if-then arguments (e.g., "If we increase age restrictions without enforcement mechanisms, we create a law that signals concern without changing behavior.")
  3. Hypothetical Scenarios: "Imagine if..." thought experiments that test a principle (e.g., "Consider a rural student whose only internet access is the library. Under Perspective 2's approach, she would be...")
  4. Analogies: Comparisons to parallel situations (e.g., "Banning social media for teens is like Prohibition—well-intentioned but unenforceable, breeding disrespect for the rule itself.")

Note: Real-world examples are strongest because they're verifiable and specific. Analogies are weakest because they can be challenged as imperfect comparisons. Use a mix, but lead with real-world evidence when possible.

The Bottom Line: ACT Writing tests whether you can construct a persuasive argument without the crutch of sources. This is pure reasoning and synthesis.

Model: Developing Position + Generating Support
🔒

Let's return to the Social Media Age Restrictions prompt and develop three possible positions using the three strategies. Then I'll choose the strongest and generate all four types of support.

Prompt Reminder: Social Media Age Restrictions

Perspective 1: Companies should enforce strict age verification and raise minimum age to 16 (protection approach)

Perspective 2: Education, not restriction, is the solution (empowerment approach)

Perspective 3: Parents, not companies/government, should control access (family authority approach)

Possible Position A: Agree and Extend (from Perspective 1)

Claim: Perspective 1 is right that companies bear responsibility, but age verification is the wrong tool. Instead, platforms should be required to disable algorithmic recommendation feeds for all users under 18, allowing access but removing the addictive design.

Relationship to Perspectives: Agrees with P1's corporate responsibility but addresses P2's concern about surveillance and P3's concern about blanket age bans.

Possible Position B: Synthesize (P1 + P2)

Claim: The strongest approach combines Perspective 1's insistence on corporate responsibility with Perspective 2's emphasis on education. Platforms should be required to fund digital literacy programs in schools as a condition of operating—making companies pay for the education that helps users navigate their products critically.

Relationship to Perspectives: Uses P1's mechanism (corporate regulation) to achieve P2's goal (education). Sidesteps P3's family-centered approach by focusing on school-based education.

Possible Position C: Reframe

Claim: All three perspectives assume the problem is who controls access, but the real problem is the design of the platforms themselves. We don't need age restrictions, education programs, or parental controls—we need platforms engineered for well-being rather than engagement maximization. Regulate the algorithm, not the user.

Relationship to Perspectives: Challenges the shared assumption of all three perspectives that access control is the solution. Offers a fourth option they all overlook.

I'm choosing Position C (Reframe) because it offers the most intellectually interesting position—it challenges the terms of the debate itself. Now I'll generate support using all four types.

Real-World Example:

In 2021, Frances Haugen's leaked documents revealed that Facebook's own research showed Instagram's algorithm amplified content that made teen girls feel worse about their bodies—and the company chose not to change the algorithm because it would reduce engagement. This proves the problem isn't whether teens have access, but what they're being shown when they do.

Logical Reasoning:

If platforms are designed to maximize time-on-site through dopamine-triggering feedback loops, then no amount of user education or age restrictions will solve the problem. A 17-year-old with a digital literacy class is still up against algorithms designed by teams of psychologists. The only durable solution is to change the algorithms themselves—require platforms to offer chronological feeds, disable auto-play, and remove infinite scroll for young users.

Hypothetical Scenario:

Imagine two social media platforms: Platform A has no age restrictions but uses a chronological feed with no algorithmic amplification. Platform B requires age verification but uses an engagement-maximizing algorithm. Which would be healthier for a 15-year-old? Most experts would choose Platform A—proving that design matters more than access control.

Analogy:

Debating who should control teens' social media access is like debating who should supervise kids eating candy—when the real problem is that we've engineered candy to be as addictive as possible. The question isn't "who supervises?" but "why are we allowing this product design in the first place?"

Notice: I used all four types, but I led with the real-world example (Frances Haugen) because it's the most concrete and verifiable. The logical reasoning builds the core argument. The hypothetical and analogy reinforce it.

Supported Practice: Position Development + Opposition Paragraph
🔒

Now you'll practice position development and constructed support. You'll also write an opposition paragraph—a key ACT skill where you acknowledge the strongest challenge to your position and explain why your position still holds.

ACT Prompt: Homework in Public Schools

Issue:

The role of homework in education has become increasingly controversial. Some schools have dramatically reduced or eliminated homework, while others have doubled down on it as essential practice. The debate touches on questions about how students learn best, what schools owe to families' time, and whether homework deepens or merely reinforces inequality.

Perspective One:

Homework is an essential part of learning because it builds discipline, reinforces classroom instruction, and prepares students for the independent work expectations of college and careers. Students who practice skills outside of class consistently outperform those who do not. Reducing homework may feel compassionate, but it ultimately lowers academic standards and leaves students unprepared.

Perspective Two:

Homework should be significantly reduced because it disproportionately harms students who lack quiet study spaces, internet access, or parental support at home. What looks like a "study habit" gap is often a resource gap. Schools should focus on making the school day itself more effective rather than outsourcing learning to environments they cannot control.

Perspective Three:

The real problem is not the quantity of homework but its quality. Most homework consists of repetitive busywork that does little to deepen understanding. Schools should assign less homework overall but ensure that what they do assign involves meaningful, creative application of ideas—projects, reflections, and real-world problem-solving rather than worksheets and drills.

Write a unified, coherent essay in which you evaluate multiple perspectives on the role of homework in public schools.

Your Task (Two Parts):

Part 1: Position Development (Choose Your Strategy)

Develop TWO different positions on the homework issue using two different strategies (Agree/Extend, Synthesize, or Reframe). For each, write 2-3 sentences explaining the position and its relationship to the perspectives.

Part 2: Choose Stronger Position Using M4

Choose which of your two positions is stronger and explain why using Mental Model M4: Claim Hierarchy. Which position has a more sophisticated claim structure? Which addresses more of the complexity?

Part 3: Opposition Paragraph

Write an opposition paragraph for your chosen position: Acknowledge the strongest challenge to your position (from one of the perspectives or elsewhere), then explain why your position holds despite this challenge.

Word count: 0

Independent Practice: Generate All Four Support Types
🔒

This time, you're generating all four types of constructed support for a claim, ranking them by persuasiveness, and drafting an opposition paragraph. No scaffold.

ACT Prompt: Standardized School Dress Codes

Issue:

Many public schools have implemented strict dress codes or uniform policies, arguing that they reduce distractions, minimize bullying over clothing, and create a more focused learning environment. Critics, however, argue that dress codes suppress individuality and are often enforced in ways that disproportionately target certain students. The debate reflects broader tensions between institutional order and personal expression.

Perspective One:

School dress codes and uniform policies benefit students by removing a major source of social comparison and distraction. When students are not competing over brands and fashion, they can focus on learning, and lower-income students are spared the pressure of keeping up with wealthier peers. Schools exist for education, and dress codes help maintain that focus.

Perspective Two:

Dress codes are more about control than education, and their enforcement consistently falls hardest on girls, students of color, and students who do not conform to traditional gender norms. What schools call "appropriate" dress often reflects narrow cultural standards rather than genuine educational needs. A policy that claims to treat everyone equally but punishes some groups more than others is not truly fair.

Perspective Three:

Rather than imposing dress codes from above, schools should involve students in creating community standards for dress that balance self-expression with mutual respect. When students participate in making the rules, they develop ownership over their school culture and practice democratic decision-making. Top-down policies miss an opportunity to teach students how to negotiate shared norms.

Write a unified, coherent essay in which you evaluate multiple perspectives on school dress codes.

Your Task:

Step 1: Choose or develop a position on dress codes (use any strategy: Agree/Extend, Synthesize, or Reframe).

Step 2: Generate all four types of support for your position:

  • Real-World Example
  • Logical Reasoning
  • Hypothetical Scenario
  • Analogy

Step 3: Rank your four pieces of support from most to least persuasive. Briefly explain your ranking (1-2 sentences).

Step 4: Draft an opposition paragraph: acknowledge the strongest objection to your position and explain why your position holds.

Word count: 0

Success Criteria:

  • Position clearly relates to at least one of the three perspectives
  • All four support types generated and clearly labeled
  • Support is specific and developed (not vague assertions)
  • Ranking is justified with reasoning
  • Opposition paragraph acknowledges a strong challenge (not a weak strawman)

Transfer: Constructed vs. Textual Evidence
🔒

You've just practiced constructed support—building evidence from reasoning, examples, and logic rather than from source texts. This is a fundamentally different skill from synthesis writing (L09, L11-L15).

Self-Check:

How is constructed support different from textual evidence?

Textual Evidence (Synthesis Essays):

  • You're given 6-7 sources and must select and integrate the strongest evidence
  • Your job is to synthesize existing arguments into a coherent position
  • You're evaluated on source selection, integration, and attribution
  • The evidence already exists—you're curating and connecting it

Constructed Support (ACT Writing):

  • You're given no sources—only three perspectives on an issue
  • Your job is to generate your own reasoning and examples from scratch
  • You're evaluated on logical coherence, specificity, and persuasiveness
  • The evidence doesn't exist yet—you're constructing it from your own knowledge and reasoning

The Key Difference: In synthesis, you're a curator. In ACT Writing, you're a builder. Both are essential college-level skills.

Why This Matters:

Many students find ACT Writing harder than synthesis essays because there are no sources to lean on. You can't hide weak reasoning behind a strong quote. Everything in your essay has to come from your own thinking.

But this is also why ACT Writing is such a powerful diagnostic of your argumentation skills. When you strip away sources, what's left is pure reasoning. Can you:

  • Generate specific, relevant examples from your knowledge base?
  • Build logical chains of reasoning that hold under scrutiny?
  • Construct hypotheticals that genuinely test your position?
  • Make analogies that illuminate rather than confuse?

These are the skills that every college essay requires—whether you have sources or not. The ACT is testing whether you can argue persuasively with or without external support.
