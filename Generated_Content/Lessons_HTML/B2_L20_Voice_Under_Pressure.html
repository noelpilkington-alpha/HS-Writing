Phase 1: Teach (2 min)

Voice Collapse Under Pressure

You've spent two lessons developing voice. In L18, you analyzed three published voices. In L19, you wrote the same paragraph in two voices and chose yours. You proved voice is controllable.

But here's what happens on test day: under time pressure, voice collapses. Students who write with personality and precision during practice revert to template language during timed essays. "However, this demonstrates that the author effectively utilizes rhetorical strategies to convey their message to the audience."

Why? Because template language is cognitively cheap. When you're stressed, your brain defaults to cached phrases. Writing in your own voice requires conscious attention—which is expensive under time pressure.

The Counter-Strategy: Write your First Sentence in Your Voice. Not the thesis sentence—that's often formulaic. The first sentence of your FIRST BODY PARAGRAPH. If that sentence sounds like you (not like a template), the rest of the paragraph follows. Voice is momentum: once you start in your voice, you stay in your voice.

The Template Language Checklist: Before you finish any timed paragraph, scan for these phrases. If you find them, revise.

  • "This demonstrates that..."
  • "It is important to note..."
  • "The author effectively utilizes..."
  • "In conclusion..."
  • "This example clearly shows..."
  • "Throughout the passage..."

These phrases are not wrong. They're just not YOU. Replace them with sentences that sound like something a human being would say when explaining an idea to another human being.

Phase 2: Independent Practice — Timed Paragraph (7 min)

Voice Under Time Pressure

Your Task: Write one body paragraph analyzing Carrie Chapman Catt's rhetorical strategy in the excerpt below. You have 7 minutes. The paragraph must include S-F-E analysis, a sophistication move, and YOUR VOICE. No template language.

Excerpt: Carrie Chapman Catt, "The Crisis" (1916)

Woman suffrage is coming—you know it. Will you, Honorable Senators and Members of the House of Representatives, help or hinder it? We want your support, but whether you give it to us now or later, or never, suffrage for women is inevitable. The only question left for you to decide is whether it will come under your leadership, with your reputation enhanced by standing with a great cause, or whether it will come in spite of your obstruction, leaving you the inglorious reputation of having stood against civilization and progress.

Carrie Chapman Catt, address to Congress, "The Crisis," 1916 (public domain excerpt)

Analytical Focus: How does Catt use the certainty of suffrage's success (framed as inevitable) to reframe the stakes for her audience? What rhetorical move is she making when she shifts the question from "Will suffrage happen?" to "How will you be remembered when it does?"

Set a 7-minute timer. Start now.

Write your timed body paragraph here. Include: S-F-E analysis, sophistication move, YOUR voice (no template language).

  • "It is important to note..."

Phase 4: Gate Preview (10 min)

B2 Gate: Full AP Practice Exam

Gate Format

Total Time: 2 hours 15 minutes (135 minutes)

Three Essays:

  • Synthesis Essay (55 min): 15 min reading 6 sources + 40 min writing. You must synthesize at least 3 sources to support a position.
  • Rhetorical Analysis Essay (40 min): 5 min reading + 30 min writing + 5 min review. Analyze how an author uses rhetorical strategies to achieve a purpose.
  • Argument Essay (40 min): 5 min planning + 30 min writing + 5 min review. Defend, challenge, or qualify a position using evidence from your knowledge and experience.

Passing Standard: Score 5+ on at least 2 of 3 essays, with NO essay below 4.

Review Your Pattern Diagnosis (from L15)

In L15, you diagnosed your consistent weakness across your Speed Run essays (L08-L10). Return to that diagnosis now. Answer these questions:

Pattern Review:

  • Which Row is consistently your weakest? Row A (Thesis), Row B (Evidence/Analysis), or Row C (Sophistication)?
  • Which essay type is your weakest? Rhetorical Analysis, Argument, or Synthesis?
  • Where does quality typically drop in your essays? Third body paragraph? Conclusion? Evidence analysis in second half?

Based on your pattern diagnosis, build your gate strategy:

Gate Strategy Planner

Question Your Strategy
Essay Order: Which essay type will you write first? Most students write Synthesis first (it has sources to work with). But if Synthesis is your weakest, consider writing Rhetorical Analysis first to build confidence.
Sophistication Placement: Where will your sophistication move go in each essay? Plan this during your 5-minute planning phase. Most effective: end of 2nd body paragraph or start of 3rd.
Your Consistent Weakness: What's the ONE thing you'll monitor throughout? If Row B is weak, monitor S-F-E analysis—reach Effect in every paragraph. If Row C is weak, plan sophistication move before writing. If quality drops, check your pacing every 10 minutes.
Recovery Plan: What will you do if you fall behind? Review L13 (Recovery Drill). Minimum viable essay: thesis + 2 body paragraphs with S-F-E + brief conclusion = score 3-4. A complete mediocre essay beats an unfinished strong essay.
Voice Reminder: How will you maintain voice under pressure? Write your first body paragraph's first sentence in YOUR voice. Not a template. Once you start in your voice, you'll stay in your voice.
Write Your Gate Strategy (3 Sentences)

Summarize your plan for the gate: (1) Essay order, (2) Your consistent weakness and how you'll address it, (3) Your recovery plan if timing goes wrong.
