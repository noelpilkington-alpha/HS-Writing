Anna Julia Cooper, "Womanhood: A Vital Element in the Regeneration and Progress of a Race" (1892)

The race is just twenty-one years removed from the conception and experience of a chattel, just at the age of ruddy manhood. It is well enough to pause a moment for retrospection, introspection, and prospection. We look back, not to become inflated with conceit because of the depths from which we have arisen, but that we may learn wisdom from experience. We look within that we may gather together once more our forces, and, by improved and more practical methods, address ourselves to the tasks before us. We look forward with hope and trust that the same God whose guiding hand led our fathers through and out of the gall and bitterness of oppression, will still lead and direct their children.

Now the fundamental agency under God in the regeneration, the retraining of the race, as well as the groundwork and starting point of its progress upward, must be the black woman. I do not imagine that I can remotely fathom the responsibility that rests upon her, or that I can even suggest the weight and complexity of her situation. The race cannot be elevated faster or farther than its women are developed. With all the wrongs and neglects of her past, with all the weakness, the debasement, the moral thralldom of her present, the black woman of today stands mute and wondering at the Herculean task devolving upon her. But the cycles wait for her. No other hand can move the lever. She must be loosed from her bands and set to work.

Yet how can she set herself at work when she has been systematically degraded, her self-respect shattered, her aspirations crushed? Let our girls feel that we expect something more of them than that they merely look pretty and appear well in society. Teach them that there is a race with special needs which they and only they can help; that the world needs and is already asking for their trained, efficient forces. Finally, if there is an ambitious girl with pluck and brain to take the higher education, encourage her, and, if need be, support her. Let her know that your heart is following her, that your hand, though she sees it not, is ready to support her. Let her know that your prayer is that she may prove herself a genuine woman, scientifically, as well as emotionally, practically, as well as spiritually, a woman whom the world cannot do without.

Anna Julia Cooper, speech delivered to the clergy of the Protestant Episcopal Church, Washington, D.C., 1892. Cooper, born enslaved in North Carolina, became one of the first African American women to earn a doctoral degree and a prominent educator and activist.

Argument Prompt

In her memoir of scientific discovery, a Nobel Prize-winning physicist writes: "Every breakthrough I achieved came through struggle. Easy problems taught me nothing; only when I was forced to confront challenges that seemed insurmountable did I develop the tools to think in genuinely new ways. We do students no favors when we smooth their path."

This perspective suggests that difficulty and struggle are not merely obstacles to overcome but essential components of meaningful learning and achievement. However, others argue that unnecessary difficulty is simply inefficient, that removing barriers allows more people to achieve their potential, and that suffering is not a prerequisite for excellence.

Assignment: Write an essay that argues your position on whether difficulty and struggle are essential to meaningful achievement. Use appropriate evidence from your reading, experience, or observations to support your argument.
