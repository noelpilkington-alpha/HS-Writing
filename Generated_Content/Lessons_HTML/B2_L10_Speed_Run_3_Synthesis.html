Speed Run 3: Synthesis

Full Timed Synthesis Essay with 6 Sources

⏱ ~65 min 📍 KCs: C.HS.04 (Sustain Quality Under Time Pressure) 🧠 Mental Models: M1-M6
Speed Run 3 Focus: Synthesis is the hardest AP essay: 6+ sources in 15 minutes, then 40 minutes writing. Sophistication in synthesis often comes from M6: asking whose perspective the source set represents and whose is missing. This is NOT a criticism of the sources—it's an analytical move that demonstrates you think beyond what's given.

Phase 1: Teach (2 min)

Synthesis + M6: The Missing Perspective

This is your third full-speed essay in B2 and your hardest. Synthesis requires you to read, understand, and cite 3+ sources (out of 6) while developing your own argument—all in 55 minutes.

Sophistication in synthesis: Most students synthesize the sources they're given but never ask what's NOT there. M6 (Who Benefits?) gives you a sophistication strategy: identify whose perspective the source set represents and whose is missing.

Example: A source set about education policy includes voices from teachers, administrators, researchers, and policymakers. Missing: students themselves. A sophistication move: "While these sources represent adult stakeholders, the student perspective—those most directly affected by policy changes—is notably absent. This omission suggests that education debates often proceed as if students are subjects to be managed rather than agents with valuable insights."

This is NOT a criticism of the College Board. It's an analytical move showing you can think beyond the given sources. You're demonstrating metacognitive awareness: "I see what I've been given, and I also see what I haven't been given."

Your task today: Write a full synthesis essay on AI and Employment. Use at least 3 sources. Include a sophistication move (missing perspective or another type). Track your quality drop-off across all 3 Speed Runs.

Phase 2: Independent (55 min)

Full Synthesis Essay: AI and Employment

AP Synthesis Prompt (SYN-09)

Time: 55 minutes (15 min reading + 40 min writing)

Prompt: Write an essay that synthesizes material from at least three of the provided sources and develops your position on the role of artificial intelligence in shaping the future of employment.

Instructions: Your argument should be central. Use the sources to support your reasoning. Avoid merely summarizing sources. Cite sources as Source A, Source B, etc., or by the authors' names.

AP-style synthesis prompt (representative)

Source Set: AI and Employment (6 Sources)

Source A: Lee (Economist — Optimistic with Caveats)

Key Position: Economic research shows AI eliminated 3.2M jobs but created 4.7M new roles (2015-2023). "Technology eliminates tasks, not occupations." However, transition creates "friction costs"—40% skills gap between available jobs and qualified workers. Challenge is not job scarcity but ensuring displaced workers access retraining before "savings and morale are exhausted."

Best Use: Rebutting doom scenarios; highlighting transition support needs; quantifying net job growth.

Key Quote: "The transition is painful for displaced workers, but claims of net job loss ignore the historical pattern."

Source B: Chen (Tech CEO — Strongly Pro-AI)

Key Position: AI serves as "cognitive augmentation," not replacement. "The jobs AI eliminates are precisely the ones draining human potential—repetitive drudgery." Cites hospital deployments (AI handles scheduling, freeing nurses for patient care) and law firms (AI reviews documents, allowing associates to focus on strategy). Dismisses displacement concerns as "Luddite fear-mongering" like anxieties about ATMs replacing bank tellers (proven false).

Best Use: Supporting AI benefits; may be too dismissive for nuanced argument.

Key Quote: "This represents liberation, not layoff."

Source C: National Labor Federation (Union — Worker Protection Focus)

Key Position: Represents 4.2M members facing automation. "Our members are not anti-technology; they are anti-abandonment." Documents cases of mass layoffs with no severance or retraining. Proposes mandatory "transition plans" requiring 18 months paid retraining, relocation assistance, or early retirement packages. Critiques "rosy projections" as irrelevant to 50-year-old workers with families who cannot afford multi-year retraining or cross-country moves.

Best Use: Humanizing displacement costs; advocating regulation; questioning who benefits from efficiency.

Key Quote: "We are told to celebrate AI's efficiency gains, but efficiency for whom? Shareholders reap billions while our members face foreclosure."

Source D: Rodriguez and Patterson (Historians — Cautiously Optimistic)

Key Position: Compares AI to Industrial Revolution, mechanized agriculture, and computerization. Each wave displaced millions, yet unemployment ultimately decreased as economies reorganized (15-30 year transition periods). "Transitional suffering was real—Luddite weavers faced destitution—but societies adapted." Cautions that AI's pace may exceed historical precedents: Industrial Revolution unfolded over 80 years, AI may achieve comparable penetration in 15-20, "compressing transition suffering into a narrower window."

Best Use: Providing historical context; tempering both extremes; warning about pace.

Key Quote: "Each wave displaced millions, yet unemployment rates ultimately decreased as economies reorganized around new technologies."

Source E: U.S. Department of Labor (Government — Moderate/Pragmatic)

Key Position: Balances competitiveness and worker protection. Proposes: tax incentives for companies offering retraining, expansion of adjustment assistance programs, portable benefits for gig workers, 90-day notice before mass layoffs. "Guardrails, not bans." Projects proactive policies could reduce transition hardship by 60% while maintaining innovation. Critics from labor call it "inadequate bandages"; business groups protest "excessive burdens."

Best Use: Policy solutions; balancing innovation and protection; pragmatic middle ground.

Key Quote: "We cannot stop the wave, but we can teach people to swim."

Source F: Bergman (Philosopher — Radical/Post-Work)

Key Position: Questions whether job preservation should be society's goal. "The work ethic—the notion that employment defines human worth—is a recent historical construct, not a timeless truth." Proposes AI-driven abundance could enable "post-work society" with universal basic income freeing humans for creative/relational pursuits. "We mourn job losses, but why? Because we equate employment with dignity, not because the tasks themselves are inherently meaningful." Points to studies showing retirees and wealthy report higher satisfaction than workers.

Best Use: Challenging assumptions; exploring radical alternatives; sophistication via tension.

Key Quote: "The problem is not AI eliminating work, but our cultural inability to imagine purpose beyond paychecks."

Timing Strategy (55 minutes total):

  • 0-15 min: Read all 6 sources. As you read, mark 3-4 you'll cite. Note 1-2 quotable phrases per source. Identify: What perspective is MISSING from this set?
  • 15-20 min: Planning. Write thesis with line of reasoning, 3 body claims, sources for each, sophistication move type and location.
  • 20-50 min: Writing. Introduction (4 min), Body 1 (8 min), Body 2 (8 min), Body 3 with sophistication (8 min), Conclusion (2 min).
  • 50-55 min: Review. Check: Did you cite 3+ sources? Does sophistication appear? Is final paragraph as strong as first?
Before You Write

Source Selection Strategy: You don't need all 6 sources. Choose 3-4 that create a conversation: one optimistic (Lee or Chen), one skeptical (NLF), one complicating (Rodriguez or Bergman), one policy-focused (DOL). This balance shows you can synthesize multiple perspectives, not just cherry-pick sources that agree with you.

Write your essay now. Set a timer for 55 minutes (15 min reading + 40 min writing).

When complete: Proceed to self-scoring below. This is your third Speed Run—compare patterns across all three essays.

Phase 3: Self-Score (5 min)

AP Synthesis Rubric Self-Scoring

Row A: Thesis (0-1 point)

  • 1 point: Responds to the prompt with a defensible thesis that establishes a line of reasoning.
  • 0 points: No defensible thesis or line of reasoning.

Row B: Evidence and Commentary (0-4 points)

  • 4 points: Provides specific evidence from at least 3 sources + explains how evidence supports the argument. Sources are integrated smoothly, not just summarized. Commentary analyzes the relationship between sources and thesis.
  • 3 points: Cites 3+ sources with explanation, but integration may be mechanical or commentary underdeveloped in places.
  • 2 points: Cites 2-3 sources, but explanation is superficial or sources are merely summarized.
  • 1 point: Cites sources but with little or no explanation of how they support the argument.
  • 0 points: Cites fewer than 2 sources or no meaningful engagement with sources.

Row C: Sophistication (0-1 point)

  • 1 point: Demonstrates sophistication through one or more of: broader context, identifying missing perspectives (M6), tension/complexity across sources, vivid and persuasive style.
  • 0 points: Does not meet sophistication criteria.
