They Say/I Say — Template Practice

Mastering three essential moves for engaging with other perspectives

⏱ ~25 min 📋 KC C.10.03, C.10.04, C.10.05 📖 No passages

Lesson Overview

Gerald Graff and Cathy Birkenstein's They Say / I Say demonstrates that academic writing follows recognizable patterns. In this lesson, you'll practice three core conversational moves: agreeing with a difference, disagreeing diplomatically, and complicating the debate. These templates aren't formulas to memorize — they're moves that become instinctive with practice.

Teach • 2 min

Templates as Moves, Not Formulas

Think about basketball footwork or guitar chord transitions. At first, you focus on where your feet or fingers go. With practice, the moves become automatic — you stop thinking about the steps and start focusing on the game or the music.

Writing moves work the same way. Templates give you starting points. As you practice, the templates fade into the background, but the moves — the intellectual gestures of engaging, qualifying, complicating — stay with you.

Three Core Moves for Academic Conversation:

  • Agree with Difference (C.10.03): "While I agree with ___ that ___, I want to add that ___."
  • Disagree Diplomatically (C.10.04): "___'s argument that ___ overlooks the fact that ___."
  • Complicate the Debate (C.10.05): "The debate typically focuses on ___, but we should also consider ___."

These moves signal to readers that you're engaging seriously with ideas, not just asserting your own opinion. They create space for nuance, which is what separates college-level writing from high school binary debates.

Check Your Understanding

Which of the following represents a genuine engagement with an opposing view, rather than a straw man caricature?

  1. "Critics of renewable energy who just want to protect fossil fuel profits ignore climate science completely."
  2. "Critics of rapid renewable energy transition raise legitimate concerns about grid reliability and worker displacement, but these challenges can be addressed through phased timelines and retraining programs."
  3. "Anyone who disagrees with renewable energy is just too lazy to adapt to change."
Show Answer

Answer: B

Explanation: Option B genuinely represents what critics actually argue (grid reliability, worker displacement) and then responds to those real concerns. Options A and C caricature opponents' positions by attributing bad motives (protecting profits, laziness) rather than engaging with actual arguments. Genuine engagement means accurately representing what the other side believes before responding to it.

Model • 4 min

Coping Model: Applying All Three Moves

Given position: "Schools should ban smartphones during the school day to improve student focus and reduce social media distraction."

Task: Write responses using all three They Say/I Say moves.

First attempt (straw man):

"People who disagree with smartphone bans are just addicted to their devices and can't handle being offline for a few hours. They're making excuses for poor self-control."

Wait — that's not genuine engagement. I'm caricaturing the other side instead of representing what they actually believe. Let me think about what opponents of smartphone bans really argue: emergency contact with parents, educational apps, digital literacy, equity concerns. Now let me try each move while fairly representing those views.

Strong responses using all three moves:

Move 1: Agree with Difference
While I agree with opponents of smartphone bans that students need to develop self-regulation skills rather than having devices removed by force, I want to add that learning self-regulation works better when students aren't competing against algorithms designed by billion-dollar companies to maximize engagement. A phone-free environment during class creates space for students to practice focus without fighting that level of engineered distraction.

Move 2: Disagree Diplomatically
The argument that students need smartphones for emergency parent contact overlooks the fact that schools have functioning offices and communication systems that worked for decades before smartphones existed. In genuine emergencies, front office staff can reach students faster than a text message can, and for routine check-ins ("Did you bring your lunch?"), waiting until after school doesn't create real harm.

Move 3: Complicate the Debate
The debate about smartphone bans typically focuses on distraction versus access, but we should also consider what message schools send about sustained attention. When schools allow constant phone checking, they implicitly teach students that eight-second attention spans are acceptable. Banning phones during instruction isn't just about removing distraction — it's about valuing deep focus as a skill worth protecting and cultivating.

Notice: Each response fairly represents what opponents believe before responding. The correction shows the shift from attacking motives to engaging ideas. This is the move that makes your writing credible.

Transfer • 3 min

Reflect on Your Process

Self-check questions:

  • Which of the three moves (Agree with Difference, Disagree Diplomatically, Complicate) felt most natural to you?
  • Which move was hardest? That's the one you need to practice more deliberately.
  • When you reread your responses, did you catch yourself creating any straw man arguments? What did you revise?

Looking ahead: These three moves will appear throughout synthesis tasks, where you'll need to enter conversations between multiple sources. The templates will fade, but the habit of genuine engagement — representing others fairly before responding — stays with you. That's the intellectual move that makes synthesis work.
