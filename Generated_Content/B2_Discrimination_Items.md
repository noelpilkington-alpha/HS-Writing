## 1. SOPHISTICATION MOVE IDENTIFICATION (5 items for L03)

### Item 1: Douglass – Broader Context Move (Contains Sophistication)

**Excerpt:**

In his Fourth of July oration, Frederick Douglass employs biting irony to expose the hypocrisy of celebrating freedom in a slaveholding nation. He asks his white audience, "What, to the American slave, is your Fourth of July?" This rhetorical question forces listeners to confront the gap between American ideals and American reality. Douglass's strategy here mirrors a pattern seen throughout 19th-century reform movements: speakers used the nation's founding documents as a mirror to reflect its failures. By invoking the Declaration of Independence's promise of equality, Douglass doesn't reject American values—he demands America live up to them. This approach proved more persuasive than outright condemnation because it appealed to his audience's patriotism while simultaneously indicting their complicity in slavery.

**Contains sophistication move?** Yes

**If yes, which type?** Broader context

**Explanation:** This excerpt demonstrates the broader context sophistication move by connecting Douglass's specific rhetorical strategy to the wider pattern of 19th-century reform rhetoric. The writer moves beyond analyzing what Douglass does to situating his approach within a larger historical and rhetorical tradition. The phrase "mirrors a pattern seen throughout 19th-century reform movements" explicitly signals this contextualizing move, and the writer explains the strategic advantage of this approach across multiple reform contexts.

### Item 2: Sojourner Truth – No Sophistication Move

**Excerpt:**

**If no, what type could be added?** Broader context or tension/complexity

**Explanation:** This excerpt provides solid rhetorical analysis with clear identification of strategies (repetition, contrast) and effects, but it remains at the level of feature-function description. To add a broader context sophistication move, the writer could connect Truth's rhetorical choices to the intersection of abolitionist and women's rights movements, or to the specific challenges faced by Black women speakers addressing mixed-race audiences. Alternatively, a tension/complexity move could explore how Truth's argument simultaneously challenges gender stereotypes while potentially reinforcing class divisions between laboring and leisured women.

### Item 3: Patrick Henry – Alternative Reading Move (Contains Sophistication)

**Excerpt:**

Patrick Henry's "Give me liberty or give me death" speech relies on a series of rhetorical questions to corner his audience into supporting armed resistance. Questions like "Is life so dear, or peace so sweet, as to be purchased at the price of chains and slavery?" force listeners to either agree with him or admit they value comfort over freedom. On the surface, this appears to be a straightforward appeal to courage and patriotism. However, one could argue that Henry's rhetoric actually reveals his awareness of his audience's hesitation rather than their enthusiasm. The very need for such forceful, binary framing suggests that many in his audience were undecided or even opposed to war. Henry's rhetorical aggression, then, might be read as a response to weakness in his position, not strength. He must make neutrality impossible because he cannot win the argument through calm deliberation.

**Contains sophistication move?** Yes

**If yes, which type?** Alternative reading

**Explanation:** This excerpt demonstrates the alternative reading sophistication move by first presenting the conventional interpretation ("appeal to courage and patriotism") and then offering a different way to understand Henry's choices. The phrase "However, one could argue" explicitly signals the shift to an alternative reading. The writer reinterprets rhetorical aggression not as confidence but as compensation for a weak position, showing sophisticated awareness that rhetorical choices can be read in multiple ways depending on what we assume about context and motive.

### Item 5: Susan B. Anthony – Tension/Complexity Move (Contains Sophistication)

**Excerpt:**

In her speech after being arrested for voting, Susan B. Anthony argues that the Constitution already grants women the right to vote, making her arrest illegal. She opens by declaring, "I not only committed no crime, but instead simply exercised my citizen's rights." This framing is strategically brilliant because it shifts the burden of proof to the prosecution—they must show why she's not a citizen rather than why women can't vote. However, this constitutional argument contains an inherent tension. By claiming voting is already a constitutional right, Anthony leaves no room for gradual reform or compromise. If she's correct, then every day women are denied the vote is not a policy disagreement but an ongoing constitutional crisis. This rhetorical choice wins the logical argument but may have alienated moderate supporters who preferred incremental change. Anthony's legal framing thus demonstrates how a rhetorically powerful argument can simultaneously strengthen and limit a movement's appeal.

**Contains sophistication move?** Yes

**If yes, which type?** Tension/complexity

**Explanation:** This excerpt demonstrates the tension/complexity sophistication move by exploring the inherent contradiction in Anthony's strategic choice. The writer doesn't just praise the strategy as "effective"—instead, they examine how the same rhetorical move that provides logical power ("wins the logical argument") also creates a political problem ("may have alienated moderate supporters"). The final sentence explicitly names this duality with "simultaneously strengthen and limit," showing sophisticated recognition that rhetorical choices involve trade-offs and unintended consequences.

## 2. SCORED ESSAY PAIR: 5 vs. 6 (for L03)

### Essay A: Score 5 (No Sophistication Move)

**Prompt:** Analyze the rhetorical strategies Frederick Douglass uses in his "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" speech to persuade his audience of the hypocrisy of celebrating American independence while slavery persists.

**Essay A:**

In his Fourth of July oration, Frederick Douglass exposes the profound hypocrisy of celebrating freedom while millions remain enslaved. Through bitter irony, strategic questioning, and powerful contrasts, Douglass transforms a patriotic occasion into a moment of moral reckoning.

Douglass's central rhetorical strategy is irony. He begins by acknowledging the significance of July 4th to his white audience, praising the founders and their achievements. This seemingly conventional opening disarms listeners, making them receptive before he pivots to his true purpose. When he asks, "What, to the American slave, is your Fourth of July?" the question lands with devastating force precisely because he has first established common ground. He answers his own question: to the slave, this celebration is "mere bombast, fraud, deception, impiety, and hypocrisy." By first praising and then condemning, Douglass creates cognitive dissonance that his audience cannot easily dismiss.

Douglass also employs strategic contrasts throughout the speech. He juxtaposes America's stated ideals with its brutal realities, the founders' love of liberty with their descendants' tolerance of slavery, and the joy of white celebration with the suffering of enslaved people. These contrasts appear in parallel structures that emphasize the contradictions: "For revolting barbarity and shameless hypocrisy, America reigns without a rival." The balanced syntax makes the critique feel measured rather than emotional, lending it credibility.

Finally, Douglass uses direct address to implicate his audience personally. He shifts from analyzing slavery as an abstract wrong to confronting his listeners as participants in that wrong. By using "you" and "your," he denies them the comfort of moral distance. They are not neutral observers of injustice but active celebrants of a hollow freedom.

Through these strategies, Douglass accomplishes his purpose: he makes it impossible for his audience to celebrate American independence without confronting American slavery. His rhetoric transforms patriotic celebration into moral crisis.

**Row A Score: 1**
**Explanation:** The essay presents a clear, defensible thesis in the opening paragraph: Douglass uses "bitter irony, strategic questioning, and powerful contrasts" to expose hypocrisy and create "a moment of moral reckoning." This thesis responds to the prompt and establishes a line of reasoning that the essay then follows.

**Row B Score: 3**
**Explanation:** The essay provides adequate analysis with specific evidence. Each body paragraph focuses on a clear strategy (irony, contrast, direct address) and explains how it functions. The writer quotes key phrases and explains their effect ("lands with devastating force," "cognitive dissonance"). However, the analysis remains somewhat surface-level. The explanations are competent but don't reach the depth of insight required for a 4. For example, the discussion of irony identifies the strategy and basic effect but doesn't explore the more nuanced implications of why this approach would be more persuasive than direct condemnation for this particular audience.

**Row C Score: 0**
**Explanation:** The essay demonstrates solid rhetorical analysis but includes no sophistication move. It does not situate Douglass's strategies in broader context, offer alternative readings, explore tensions or complexities, or employ particularly vivid or illuminating style. The analysis remains focused on identifying what Douglass does and explaining why it works, without the additional layer of insight required for Row C credit.

**Total Score: 4**

## 3. SURFACE VS. SUBSTANTIVE SORT (5 items for L16)

### Item 1: Grammar Fix

**Revision action:** Changed "Between you and I, this argument is unconvincing" to "Between you and me, this argument is unconvincing."

**Classification:** Surface Editing

**Explanation:** This is surface editing because it corrects a grammatical error (object pronoun after a preposition) without changing the meaning, structure, or quality of the argument. The sentence communicates the same idea before and after the change. While correct grammar matters for credibility, this revision doesn't improve the thinking or rhetorical effectiveness of the passage.

**Substantive version:** A substantive revision would reconsider whether this informal, confidential tone ("between you and me") is appropriate for an academic essay. The writer might replace the entire sentence with something like "This argument fails to convince for three reasons," which removes the colloquial framing and leads more directly into the analysis. The substantive revision addresses rhetorical choice, not just correctness.

"Make his audience feel guilty" suggests an emotional manipulation, while "implicate his audience in slavery's continuation" identifies a more specific rhetorical strategy—establishing complicity and responsibility. The revision shows clearer thinking about what Douglass is doing rhetorically. This changes the quality of the analysis, not just its surface expression.

## 4. CALIBRATION ESSAYS (4 essays for L14)

### Essay 1: Rhetorical Analysis – Sojourner Truth

**Prompt:** In her 1851 speech "Ain't I a Woman?" delivered at the Women's Rights Convention in Akron, Ohio, Sojourner Truth challenges prevailing notions of women's capabilities and rights. Analyze the rhetorical strategies Truth uses to convey her message to her audience.

**Essay:**

In "Ain't I a Woman?" Sojourner Truth argues that women deserve equal rights by showing that women are just as strong and capable as men. She uses repetition, personal examples, and contrast to make her point.

Truth's main strategy is repetition. She repeats the phrase "Ain't I a woman?" throughout the speech. This repetition appears after she describes her physical strength and work experience. Each time she says "Ain't I a woman?" it reminds the audience that she is female despite her strength. The repetition makes the speech memorable and emphasizes her main point. It also creates a rhythm that would have been easy for listeners to remember, which is important in a speech since people can't go back and reread it like they can with written text.

Another strategy Truth uses is personal examples. She talks about how she has "plowed and planted and gathered into barns" and how no man could help her. She also mentions that she has "borne thirteen children" and seen most of them sold into slavery. These examples are concrete and specific, not abstract. By sharing her own experiences, Truth makes her argument more credible because she is speaking from direct knowledge, not theory. Her audience can't dismiss her claims because she is living proof of women's capabilities.

Truth also uses contrast when she compares herself to delicate white women who need help getting into carriages or stepping over puddles. She points out that nobody helps her in these ways, yet she is still a woman. This contrast shows that the stereotype of women as weak and delicate doesn't apply to all women, especially not to Black women like herself. The contrast reveals the hypocrisy in how society defines womanhood.

Truth's rhetorical strategies effectively challenge the audience's assumptions about women's abilities and make a strong case for women's rights.

**Explanation:** The essay presents a defensible thesis in the introduction: Truth "uses repetition, personal examples, and contrast to make her point" (establishing equal rights by demonstrating women's capabilities). This thesis responds to the prompt and establishes a line of reasoning that the body paragraphs follow. The thesis is straightforward but sufficient for Row A credit.

**Row B Score: 2**

The analysis is adequate but not developed enough for a 3.

**Row C Score: 0**
**Explanation:** The essay provides straightforward rhetorical analysis without any sophistication move. ### Essay 2: Rhetorical Analysis – Patrick Henry

**Prompt:** Patrick Henry's 1775 "Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death" speech urged the Virginia Convention to support armed resistance against British rule. Analyze the rhetorical strategies Henry uses to persuade his audience to support military action.

**Essay:**

Patrick Henry's "Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death" speech employs rhetorical questions, religious imagery, and binary framing to corner his audience into supporting war—but his very need for such aggressive rhetoric reveals the weakness of his position among hesitant colonists.

Henry's most prominent strategy is the rhetorical question, which appears throughout the speech: "Is life so dear, or peace so sweet, as to be purchased at the price of chains and slavery?" These questions function as false choices that make neutrality impossible. Henry doesn't ask whether war is wise or winnable; he asks whether listeners value comfort over freedom. By framing the issue this way, he makes any position other than immediate military action seem cowardly. The questions don't invite deliberation—they coerce agreement by making disagreement shameful.

Henry reinforces this either-or thinking through religious imagery that casts the conflict in cosmic terms. He references "the majesty of heaven" and states, "I know not what course others may take, but as for me, give me liberty or give me death!" This biblical language (echoing Joshua's "as for me and my house, we will serve the Lord") transforms a political dispute into a test of faith. If this is God's cause, then opposition isn't just mistaken policy—it's moral failure. The religious framing adds urgency and stakes beyond practical military considerations.

Yet Henry's rhetorical aggression might actually indicate weakness rather than strength. If the Virginia Convention were already convinced of war's necessity, Henry wouldn't need such forceful, binary rhetoric. The very fact that he must make moderation impossible suggests that many in his audience preferred caution or negotiation. His speech, then, represents not the expression of unanimous colonial sentiment but an attempt to manufacture that unanimity through rhetoric that forecloses alternatives. We might read Henry's famous closing as revealing strategic desperation: he can't win through calm deliberation, so he must make deliberation itself seem treasonous.

This alternative reading doesn't diminish Henry's rhetorical skill—it highlights it. He recognizes that his audience is divided and hesitant, so he employs strategies specifically designed to overcome uncertainty by making it socially and morally untenable.

**Row A Score: 1**
**Explanation:** The essay presents a clear, defensible thesis that responds to the prompt: Henry uses rhetorical questions, religious imagery, and binary framing to persuade his audience, and these strategies reveal his response to a resistant audience. The thesis establishes a line of reasoning that the essay follows coherently.

**Row B Score: 4**
**Explanation:** The essay provides sophisticated analysis that goes beyond feature-function description. The writer doesn't just identify rhetorical questions and explain that they "make the audience think"—instead, the analysis explores how the questions function as coercion rather than invitation ("don't invite deliberation—they coerce agreement"). The discussion of religious imagery explains specific effects (transforms political dispute into test of faith) with attention to rhetorical situation. The analysis is specific, insightful, and consistently focused on how strategies work to persuade this particular audience. This is strong Row B work.

**Row C Score: 1**
**Explanation:** The third body paragraph demonstrates a clear sophistication move—alternative reading. The writer explicitly offers a different interpretation of Henry's rhetorical choices: "Yet Henry's rhetorical aggression might actually indicate weakness rather than strength." The essay then develops this alternative reading by showing how Henry's forceful rhetoric could be understood as compensation for a divided audience rather than expression of unanimous support. The final paragraph acknowledges this as an "alternative reading," showing metacognitive awareness. This is clear Row C work.

**Total Score: 6**

**Common scoring errors:** Some students might under-score this essay, thinking sophistication requires longer essays or more examples. Row C doesn't require length—it requires a clear move that demonstrates insight beyond feature-function analysis. This essay's alternative reading is compact but genuine. Other students might confuse the essay's critical stance (suggesting Henry's position was "weak") with being negative about the speech; Row C isn't about praise or criticism but about demonstrating sophisticated thinking about rhetoric.

### Essay 3: Argument – The Value of Difficulty

**Prompt:** Some educators argue that students learn best when challenged with difficult material, even if it causes frustration. Others contend that students need achievable tasks that build confidence through success. Write an essay in which you argue for or against the educational value of difficulty and struggle.

**Essay:**

While confidence-building through success has its place in education, genuine learning requires difficulty, struggle, and even frustration. Students who encounter only achievable tasks develop skills without developing resilience, leaving them unprepared for the authentic challenges they will face beyond school.

First, difficulty forces students to develop problem-solving strategies rather than simply executing known procedures. When my younger brother struggled with algebra, he initially wanted my parents to show him "the right way" to solve each problem type. But his teacher insisted he first attempt problems independently, even if he failed. Through this struggle, he learned to identify what type of problem he was facing, recall relevant principles, and test different approaches—metacognitive skills that wouldn't develop if every problem came with instructions. Research by cognitive psychologist Robert Bjork shows that "desirable difficulties" in learning, such as spacing practice and varying problem types, produce better long-term retention than massed practice on uniform problems. Students need to struggle with retrieval and application precisely because that struggle strengthens memory and understanding.

Second, experiencing difficulty in controlled educational settings prepares students for the unstructured challenges of professional and civic life. In school, students face problems that teachers have designed to be solvable with available resources. But after graduation, they will encounter problems without clear solutions, domains where they lack expertise, and situations where initial attempts fail. Students who have learned to persist through frustration, revise failed approaches, and tolerate ambiguity are far better equipped for these realities than students whose education prioritized constant success. When I interviewed for my first job, the employer asked me to analyze a business case I knew nothing about. My ability to work through confusion and acknowledge what I didn't know—skills I developed struggling through AP courses—mattered more than any specific content knowledge.

Finally, struggle teaches students that intelligence is not fixed but developed through effort. Students who succeed easily often develop a "fixed mindset," believing their abilities are innate rather than cultivated. When they inevitably encounter material that doesn't come easily, they interpret difficulty as evidence of inadequacy rather than opportunity for growth. But students who regularly work through difficult material learn that confusion is temporary and competence develops through persistence. This "growth mindset" may be the most valuable outcome of difficult coursework—not the content mastery itself, but the understanding that mastery is possible through sustained effort.

Difficulty in education must be calibrated—impossible tasks that produce only demoralization serve no purpose. But appropriately challenging work that requires genuine struggle develops problem-solving skills, resilience, and growth mindset that confidence-building through easy success cannot provide.

**Row A Score: 1**
**Explanation:** The essay presents a clear thesis in the opening paragraph: "genuine learning requires difficulty, struggle, and even frustration" because achievable-only tasks leave students "unprepared for the authentic challenges they will face beyond school." This is a defensible position that responds to the prompt. The essay establishes a clear line of reasoning (develops problem-solving skills, prepares for real-world challenges, teaches growth mindset) that it follows throughout.

**Row B Score: 3**
**Explanation:** The essay provides consistent support for its thesis with a mix of personal evidence (brother's algebra struggle, job interview), expert testimony (Robert Bjork's research on desirable difficulties), and reasoning about transfer to non-academic contexts. Each body paragraph develops a distinct reason supporting the thesis. The commentary connects evidence to claims (explaining how struggle develops metacognition, how educational difficulty prepares for professional challenges, how struggle builds growth mindset). However, the analysis doesn't reach the exceptional depth or complexity that would earn a 4—the reasoning is solid but not particularly original or nuanced. The evidence is specific and relevant but not unusually compelling or unexpected.

**Row C Score: 0**
**Explanation:** The essay demonstrates competent argumentation but includes no sophistication move. It doesn't situate the argument in broader intellectual or historical context, explore tensions or complexities in the position (e.g., the paragraph mentions that difficulty "must be calibrated" but doesn't develop this complication), acknowledge alternative perspectives in depth, or employ particularly vivid or illuminating style. The writing is clear and the argument is sound, but it remains within the realm of solid work without the additional layer of insight required for Row C.

**Total Score: 4**

**Common scoring errors:** Students sometimes over-score this essay (5 or 6) because it is well-organized, includes specific evidence, and addresses the prompt thoroughly. However, Row B=3 is appropriate for "adequate" support and commentary—the essay is good but not exceptional in its reasoning or evidence. ### Essay 4: Synthesis – Technology in Education

**Prompt:** Many schools are incorporating technology into classrooms, from tablets for elementary students to online courses for high schoolers. Using the provided sources, develop an argument about the appropriate role of technology in education.

Schools should integrate technology into education not as a replacement for traditional instruction but as a tool that allows teachers to personalize learning and provide immediate feedback.
