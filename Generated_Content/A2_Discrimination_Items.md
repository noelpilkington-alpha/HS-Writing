# A2 Discrimination Items -- Course A2: Argument, Synthesis & ACT Writing

This file contains discrimination items for Grade 10 writing instruction, organized by skill type. All items are designed to build analytical judgment before requiring production.

## 1. THEY SAY/I SAY MOVE CLASSIFICATION ITEMS

**Instructions:** Read each argument opening. Classify it as one of three moves: (1) Agree with a Difference, (2) Disagree Diplomatically, or (3) Complicate the Debate. Pay attention to signal words and phrases that reveal the writer's stance toward the existing conversation.

### ITEM 1 (Supported Practice)

**Opening:**
Many educators argue that group projects teach collaboration skills essential for the workplace. While I agree that modern careers do require teamwork, I want to add that group projects in their current form often reward social skills over intellectual contribution. The student who speaks confidently in meetings may receive the same grade as the student who completes the most rigorous analytical work. True collaboration should involve structured roles and individual accountability, not just dividing tasks and hoping for the best.

**Classification:** Agree with a Difference

**Explanation:** The writer explicitly agrees with the premise ("I agree that modern careers do require teamwork") but then introduces a qualification or extension ("I want to add that..."). This is the classic "Yes, but..." structure. The writer does not reject the value of group projects; instead, they complicate what effective collaboration looks like.

**Signal words/phrases:**
- "While I agree that..."
- "I want to add that..."
- "True collaboration should..." (this phrase redefines rather than rejects)

---

### ITEM 2 (Supported Practice)

**Opening:**
Proponents of universal basic income claim that automation will eliminate most jobs, making a guaranteed income necessary for economic survival. However, this argument overlooks the fact that technological revolutions have historically created more jobs than they destroyed -- just in different sectors. The Industrial Revolution displaced agricultural workers, yet unemployment did not skyrocket; the workforce adapted. Rather than assuming catastrophic job loss, we should invest in education systems that prepare workers to transition into emerging fields.

**Classification:** Disagree Diplomatically

**Explanation:** The writer challenges the foundational claim ("automation will eliminate most jobs") but does so respectfully by using softening language ("this argument overlooks") rather than saying "this is wrong." The writer also provides an alternative explanation grounded in historical evidence. This is a genuine counterargument, not mere agreement with a caveat.

**Signal words/phrases:**
- "However, this argument overlooks..."
- "Rather than assuming... we should..."
- Use of historical evidence to counter the claim

---

### ITEM 3 (Supported Practice)

**Opening:**
The debate over school start times typically focuses on whether teenagers need more sleep or whether early start times prepare them for adult responsibilities. But we should also consider the structural inequalities this debate ignores. Later start times disproportionately benefit families with flexible work schedules and private transportation, while students who rely on district busing or have after-school jobs face new logistical burdens. The question is not just "What time should school start?" but "Whose needs are centered when we make that decision?"

**Classification:** Complicate the Debate

**Explanation:** The writer does not align with either side of the existing binary debate. Instead, they argue that the entire framing of the conversation is inadequate ("this debate ignores"). They introduce a new dimension -- structural inequality -- that neither side has addressed. This is not agreement or disagreement; it is a reframing that complicates the conversation.

**Signal words/phrases:**
- "The debate typically focuses on... But we should also consider..."
- "The question is not just X but Y"
- Introduction of a dimension (structural inequality) absent from both sides

### ITEM 4 (Supported Practice)

**Opening:**
Critics of social media often claim that it isolates people and destroys genuine connection. I would argue, however, that social media is a tool whose effects depend entirely on how it is used. For people with disabilities, chronic illnesses, or social anxiety, online platforms can provide community and belonging that physical spaces do not. The problem is not the technology itself but the compulsive, performative ways many people engage with it. Blaming social media for loneliness is like blaming libraries for making people antisocial.

**Classification:** Disagree Diplomatically

**Explanation:** The writer directly counters the critics' claim ("I would argue, however, that...") but does so by acknowledging the criticism exists and then offering a more nuanced view. The writer does not attack the critics as ignorant; instead, they propose that the critics have misidentified the cause. The analogy at the end reinforces the reframing without being dismissive.

**Signal words/phrases:**
- "I would argue, however, that..."
- "The problem is not X but Y"
- Analogical reasoning to soften disagreement

### ITEM 5 (Supported Practice)

**Opening:**
Environmentalists are correct that individual consumer choices -- buying less, recycling more, choosing sustainable products -- matter for the planet. Yet we must recognize that emphasizing individual responsibility can obscure the vastly larger impact of corporate and governmental policy. The average person's carbon footprint is dwarfed by emissions from industrial agriculture, energy production, and manufacturing. Personal lifestyle changes are important, but they should not distract us from demanding systemic change from the institutions that hold the most power.

**Classification:** Agree with a Difference

**Explanation:** The writer validates the initial claim ("Environmentalists are correct that...") but immediately introduces a significant qualification ("Yet we must recognize that..."). The move here is not to reject individual action but to argue that it is insufficient without systemic change. This is the "Yes, and also..." structure.

**Signal words/phrases:**
- "Environmentalists are correct that..."
- "Yet we must recognize that..."
- "Personal lifestyle changes are important, but..." (acknowledges value while adding complication)

### ITEM 6 (Independent Practice)

**Opening:**
Advocates for banning cell phones in classrooms argue that devices create distractions and undermine learning. While it is true that misuse of phones disrupts focus, I believe that blanket bans ignore the educational potential of the very technology we are removing. Students can access primary sources, collaborate on shared documents, use specialized apps for math or language learning, and fact-check claims in real time. Instead of banning devices, we should teach students when and how to use them responsibly.

**Classification:** Agree with a Difference

**Explanation:** The writer concedes the critics' point about distraction ("While it is true that...") but argues for a different conclusion by highlighting benefits the original argument did not consider ("ignore the educational potential"). This is not a rejection of the problem but a reframing that shifts focus to solutions other than removal.

**Signal words/phrases:**
- "While it is true that... I believe that..."
- "Instead of... we should..."

### ITEM 7 (Independent Practice)

**Opening:**
Many reformers insist that the criminal justice system is fundamentally broken and beyond repair. This perspective, while understandable given the system's many injustices, may underestimate the progress that targeted reforms have already achieved. Sentencing reform, diversion programs, and bail reform have demonstrably reduced incarceration rates and recidivism in states that have adopted them. Abandoning the framework entirely risks discarding institutional knowledge and infrastructure that, with continued pressure and reform, could serve justice more effectively than starting from scratch.

**Classification:** Disagree Diplomatically

**Explanation:** The writer acknowledges the validity of the reformers' frustration ("while understandable given the system's many injustices") but ultimately argues against their conclusion ("may underestimate the progress"). The writer does not dismiss the problem but disputes the solution, offering evidence of what reform has achieved and arguing that abolition is premature.

**Signal words/phrases:**
- "This perspective, while understandable... may underestimate..."
- "Abandoning the framework entirely risks..."
- Concession followed by counterargument

### ITEM 8 (Independent Practice)

**Opening:**
Arguments about the ethics of artificial intelligence often pit those who fear job displacement against those who celebrate efficiency and innovation. What this binary misses is the deeper question of how AI systems are designed and who controls them. The real risk is not automation itself but the concentration of algorithmic power in the hands of a few corporations accountable to shareholders rather than to the public. Whether AI helps or harms society depends less on the technology than on the governance structures surrounding it.

**Classification:** Complicate the Debate

**Explanation:** The writer explicitly identifies a binary debate ("those who fear... against those who celebrate") and argues that both sides are missing the crucial issue ("What this binary misses..."). The writer does not choose a side; instead, they introduce a third dimension -- governance and power -- that reframes the entire conversation.

**Signal words/phrases:**
- "Arguments often pit X against Y. What this binary misses..."
- "The real risk is not X but Y"
- "Whether AI helps or harms depends less on the technology than on..."

### ITEM 9 (Independent Practice)

**Opening:**
Free speech advocates argue that college campuses must tolerate all viewpoints, even offensive ones, to preserve intellectual freedom. I agree that universities should be spaces for challenging ideas, but I would add that the principle of free speech does not require institutions to platform or fund speech that directly threatens the safety and dignity of marginalized students. There is a meaningful difference between permitting speech and endorsing it through institutional support. True intellectual freedom requires not just the absence of censorship but the presence of conditions where all students can participate in debate without fear.

**Classification:** Agree with a Difference

**Explanation:** The writer accepts the premise that universities should be spaces for challenging ideas ("I agree that...") but draws a crucial distinction that the original argument does not make ("but I would add that..."). This is an extension, not a rejection -- the writer deepens the analysis by differentiating between tolerating speech and institutionally supporting it.

**Signal words/phrases:**
- "I agree that... but I would add that..."
- "There is a meaningful difference between X and Y"
- "True X requires not just A but B"

### ITEM 10 (Independent Practice)

**Opening:**
Supporters of standardized testing claim that tests provide objective measures of student learning and hold schools accountable. This argument, however, assumes that what tests measure is the same as what we value in education. Standardized exams reward narrow forms of recall and procedural knowledge while marginalizing critical thinking, creativity, and the ability to tackle novel problems. Furthermore, "objective" measures still reflect the biases of test designers, whose questions often assume cultural knowledge that is not universally shared. If we want accountability systems that reflect what learning truly looks like, we need assessments far more sophisticated than multiple-choice exams.

**Classification:** Disagree Diplomatically

**Explanation:** The writer challenges the core claim of standardized testing advocates ("This argument, however, assumes...") and provides detailed evidence for why that assumption is flawed. The writer does not attack the advocates personally but dismantles the logic of their position while acknowledging their goal of accountability. The final sentence offers an alternative rather than simply rejecting testing altogether.

**Signal words/phrases:**
- "This argument, however, assumes that..."
- "Furthermore, 'objective' measures still reflect..."
- "If we want X, we need Y" (constructive alternative)

## 2. STRAW MAN DETECTION ITEMS

**Instructions:** Read each argument opening. Determine whether the writer is engaging genuinely with the opposing view or creating a straw man (a distorted, oversimplified version of the opposition that is easier to defeat). For straw man items, suggest a revision that engages more fairly.

### ITEM 1

**Opening:**
Some people think that teenagers should have no restrictions on their freedom and should be allowed to do whatever they want. This naive view ignores the reality that adolescent brains are not fully developed, particularly in areas governing impulse control and long-term planning. Without parental guidance and societal boundaries, teenagers would make reckless choices with lifelong consequences. Clearly, some level of restriction is not just appropriate but necessary for young people's wellbeing.

**Classification:** Straw Man

**Explanation:** The writer misrepresents the opposing view as an extreme position ("teenagers should have no restrictions... and should be allowed to do whatever they want") that few people actually hold. This makes it trivially easy to refute. Real advocates for teenage autonomy typically argue for *increased* freedom in specific areas or *age-appropriate* independence, not absolute freedom. The phrase "naive view" also signals that the writer is not engaging respectfully with the opposition.

**How to fix it (revised version):**
Some advocates for teenage autonomy argue that adolescents are capable of making important decisions about their own lives and that overprotective parenting can hinder their development of responsibility. While it is true that adolescent brains are still developing, particularly in areas governing impulse control, this does not mean teenagers are incapable of exercising judgment. The question is not whether teenagers need any boundaries but where we draw the line between protection and excessive control. Treating teenagers as if they have no capacity for responsible decision-making can become a self-fulfilling prophecy.

---

### ITEM 2

**Opening:**
Critics of charter schools claim that allowing school choice will destroy public education and leave vulnerable students behind. However, the evidence suggests otherwise. Studies show that students in well-run charter schools often outperform their peers in traditional public schools, particularly in urban areas where district schools have struggled for decades. Parents in underserved communities consistently choose charters when given the option, demonstrating that school choice empowers families rather than abandoning them. Rather than clinging to a failing system out of fear of change, we should expand access to the schools that are working.

**Classification:** Genuine Engagement

**Explanation:** The writer fairly represents the critics' concern (that charter schools may harm public education and vulnerable students) and responds with evidence rather than caricature. The writer acknowledges the real stakes of the debate -- the wellbeing of students in underserved communities -- and offers data to counter the critics' position. While the phrase "clinging to a failing system" is somewhat charged, the writer has not distorted the opposing argument itself. This is a genuine counterargument, not a straw man.

**Why this is genuine:**
- The opposing view is presented as a coherent concern, not an absurd extreme
- The writer addresses the core worry (harm to vulnerable students) directly
- Evidence is deployed to counter the claim, not to mock it

---

### ITEM 3

**Opening:**
Opponents of raising the minimum wage argue that higher wages will cause mass unemployment and destroy small businesses. But this doomsday scenario has been predicted every single time wage increases have been proposed, and it has never come true. Studies consistently show that modest minimum wage increases have little to no effect on employment levels, and in many cases they stimulate local economies by putting more money in workers' pockets. The real reason some business owners resist wage increases is not economics but the desire to maximize profits at workers' expense.

**Classification:** Straw Man

**Explanation:** The writer characterizes the opposing view as a "doomsday scenario" and implies that all opponents are motivated by greed ("the desire to maximize profits at workers' expense"). While some opponents of minimum wage increases do predict dire consequences, many economists offer more nuanced concerns -- for example, that wage increases may disproportionately affect certain industries, regions, or firm sizes, or that the effects vary depending on the size of the increase. By framing the entire opposition as alarmist and greedy, the writer avoids engaging with more sophisticated economic arguments.

**How to fix it (revised version):**
Opponents of raising the minimum wage argue that higher wages could lead to job losses, particularly in industries with thin profit margins, and may force small businesses to reduce hours or automate positions. While these concerns are worth taking seriously, the evidence suggests they are overstated. Studies of minimum wage increases in cities like Seattle and states like California show that modest increases have had little measurable effect on overall employment, and in some cases have stimulated local economies by increasing consumer spending. The key question is not whether to raise wages but by how much and at what pace, ensuring that workers benefit without causing unintended harm to the businesses that employ them.

### ITEM 4

**Opening:**
Advocates for expanding mental health services in schools argue that emotional wellbeing is just as important as academic achievement and that schools are ideally positioned to provide support for students who might not access it elsewhere. While some worry that schools lack the resources or expertise to take on this role, this concern underestimates what trained school counselors and social workers can accomplish. Many students face anxiety, depression, trauma, or family instability that directly affects their ability to learn. Providing mental health support in schools does not distract from academics -- it enables them.

**Classification:** Genuine Engagement

**Explanation:** The writer acknowledges a legitimate concern from those who might oppose expanding school-based mental health services ("schools lack the resources or expertise") and responds substantively rather than dismissively. The writer does not claim that opponents don't care about students' wellbeing; instead, they argue that the concern is based on an underestimation of what is possible. The response is evidence-based and respectful, making this genuine engagement rather than straw man argumentation.

**Why this is genuine:**
- The opposing concern is stated fairly (schools may lack resources/expertise)
- The writer addresses the concern directly rather than dismissing it
- The counterargument is constructive (trained professionals can address these needs)

## 3. SERIAL VS. SYNTHESIS DETECTION ITEMS

**Instructions:** Read each paragraph. Determine whether the writer is engaging in serial summarizing (discussing sources one at a time, treating each separately) or true synthesis (organizing ideas by theme or concept, with sources serving a unified argument). For serial items, suggest how the paragraph could be reorganized for synthesis.

### ITEM 1

**Paragraph:**
The relationship between screen time and adolescent wellbeing is complex and contested. Dr. Sarah Henderson's 2022 study of 1,500 teenagers found that moderate social media use (1-2 hours daily) correlated with higher self-reported happiness and larger friend networks compared to non-users. However, students who used social media for more than 3 hours daily showed increased rates of anxiety and depression. In contrast, the Patel and Johnson study from 2021 examined the content of social media use rather than just duration and concluded that passive scrolling was associated with negative outcomes while active engagement (commenting, messaging) was associated with positive ones. Meanwhile, Dr. Marcus Lee's longitudinal research spanning five years argues that the direction of causation remains unclear -- students who are already anxious may seek out social media as a coping mechanism, meaning that social media use could be a symptom rather than a cause of poor mental health.

**Classification:** Serial Summarizing

**Explanation:** The paragraph discusses three sources sequentially, using transition words like "In contrast" and "Meanwhile" to move from one source to the next. Each source is treated as a separate unit of information. The paragraph does not organize by *theme* (e.g., "quantity vs. quality of use" or "the causation problem") but by *source*. The reader learns what Henderson says, then what Patel and Johnson say, then what Lee says, but the connections between these findings are left implicit.

**How to reorganize by theme (one-sentence example):**
Rather than organizing by source, the writer should organize by the two key themes: (1) the distinction between quantity and quality of social media use (Henderson vs. Patel/Johnson), and (2) the causation problem that complicates all claims (Lee). Each theme becomes a section, with sources woven together to build the idea.

---

### ITEM 2

**Paragraph:**
Urban tree canopy coverage offers multiple benefits that extend beyond aesthetics. Environmental scientists have demonstrated that trees in cities significantly reduce the urban heat island effect -- neighborhoods with 30% or more tree coverage are on average 5-7 degrees cooler in summer than comparable areas with less than 10% coverage (Martinez et al.). This cooling effect is especially critical for low-income communities, which disproportionately experience heat-related illness and lack access to air conditioning (Reynolds). Trees also improve air quality by filtering pollutants; a single mature tree can absorb up to 48 pounds of carbon dioxide per year while releasing oxygen (Park Service Report). Beyond environmental impacts, urban greenery has measurable effects on human psychology and behavior. Neighborhoods with higher tree density report lower crime rates and higher levels of social cohesion, suggesting that natural environments encourage outdoor activity and community interaction (Thompson & Ward). Given these converging benefits -- environmental, health-related, and social -- investment in urban forestry should be understood not as a luxury but as essential infrastructure.

**Classification:** True Synthesis

**Explanation:** The paragraph is organized by *types of benefits* (environmental, health, social) rather than by source. Sources are woven in to support points as needed, but the reader experiences a unified argument rather than a source-by-source tour. The writer's own analytical claim ("investment in urban forestry should be understood as essential infrastructure") frames the entire paragraph, and sources serve that claim. This is synthesis.

**Why this is synthesis:**
- Organization is thematic (types of benefits), not source-by-source
- Multiple sources appear in service of the same point (e.g., Martinez + Reynolds both support the cooling/health claim)
- The writer's voice and argument are primary; sources are secondary support

---

### ITEM 3

**Paragraph:**
The debate over homework's effectiveness has generated significant research. A 2018 meta-analysis by Dr. Karen Ostroff examined 42 studies and concluded that homework has a positive but modest effect on academic achievement in high school, with an effect size of 0.28. Ostroff's research suggested that homework is most beneficial when it reinforces rather than introduces new material. Another major study, conducted by Lee and Ramirez in 2020, tracked 800 middle school students and found no significant correlation between time spent on homework and test performance, leading the researchers to question whether homework is effective at all for younger students. Meanwhile, Robert Chen's qualitative research involving interviews with 60 teachers revealed that educators assign homework primarily because they believe it builds discipline and responsibility rather than because it directly improves learning. Chen's findings suggest that the perceived benefits of homework may be about habit formation rather than academic skill development.

**Classification:** Serial Summarizing

**Explanation:** The paragraph moves from Ostroff's study to Lee and Ramirez's study to Chen's study, using "Another major study" and "Meanwhile" as source-to-source transitions. Each study is presented as a standalone finding. The paragraph does not organize around themes (e.g., "Does homework actually improve learning?" vs. "Why do teachers assign it?") but around sources. The connections between these studies are left for the reader to infer.

**How to reorganize by theme (one-sentence example):**
The writer should separate the evidence question (Does homework work? Ostroff says modest effects; Lee/Ramirez say unclear for middle school) from the practice question (Why is it assigned? Chen shows teachers value discipline over learning). Each theme gets developed with relevant sources, rather than presenting studies sequentially.

### ITEM 4

**Paragraph:**
While automation is often framed as a threat to employment, the relationship between technology and jobs is more nuanced than simple displacement. Historically, technological revolutions have eliminated certain types of work while creating new categories of employment -- the introduction of ATMs in the 1980s reduced the need for bank tellers per branch, yet the number of bank branches increased because lower operating costs made expansion feasible, ultimately leading to more teller jobs overall (Autor). Today's automation follows a similar pattern: jobs that consist of routine, predictable tasks are vulnerable to replacement, but roles requiring creativity, complex problem-solving, or human interaction remain difficult to automate (Brynjolfsson & McAfee). The challenge is not whether jobs will exist but whether workers displaced from routine roles can access the education and training necessary to transition into the emerging fields. As economist Daron Acemoglu argues, the question is not "Will robots take our jobs?" but "Who will benefit from automation, and what policies can ensure that gains are broadly shared?" (Acemoglu & Restrepo). The anxiety around automation is justified, but it is ultimately a problem of distribution and policy rather than a problem of technology itself.

**Classification:** True Synthesis

**Explanation:** The paragraph is organized around a unified argument: automation's effect on employment is not simple job loss but a distribution problem. Sources are integrated to build this argument rather than presented sequentially. Autor's historical example, Brynjolfsson and McAfee's distinction between routine and complex work, and Acemoglu's policy framing all work together to support the writer's central claim. The writer's own analytical voice ("the relationship is more nuanced," "the challenge is not whether but who") drives the paragraph, with sources serving that argument.

**Why this is synthesis:**
- The paragraph builds a single, unified claim (automation is a distribution problem, not a job-elimination problem)
- Sources are ordered by logical function (historical evidence -> current pattern -> policy implication), not chronologically or by author
- The writer's analysis frames the conversation; sources support rather than dictate it

## 4. CALIBRATION BANK -- 6 SAMPLE ARGUMENT PARAGRAPHS WITH MODEL SCORING

**Instructions:** Each paragraph is scored on a 3-point rubric: Weak (0-1), Adequate (2), or Strong (3). Use the scoring rationale to understand what distinguishes each level.

### 3-Point Rubric for Argument Paragraphs

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **Strong (3)** | Clear claim connected to thesis. Specific, well-integrated evidence. Thorough commentary that explains HOW evidence supports the claim and WHY it matters. Smooth transitions. |
| **Adequate (2)** | Identifiable claim, though may be vague or loosely connected to thesis. Evidence present but may be general or awkwardly integrated. Some commentary, but may be underdeveloped or merely restate the evidence. Functional but mechanical. |
| **Weak (0-1)** | Unclear or missing claim. Little or no evidence, or evidence not relevant to claim. Minimal or absent commentary -- may simply summarize evidence without analysis. Organization issues or unclear purpose. |

---

### ITEM 1 (Supported Practice)

**Paragraph:**
Social media companies should be required to verify users' ages because young people's brains are still developing. Studies show that teenagers are more likely to experience anxiety and depression when they use social media. Also, social media platforms are designed to be addictive, with features like infinite scrolling and notifications that keep users engaged for as long as possible. Many young people spend hours every day on their phones. If companies had to verify ages and restrict access to users under 16, this would protect young people from harmful content and reduce mental health problems.

**Score:** Weak (1)

**Detailed Scoring Rationale:**
This paragraph has an identifiable claim (age verification should be required to protect young people), but the development is weak. The evidence is vague ("studies show") without specific citation or detail. The paragraph lists problems (anxiety, addictive design, excessive use) but does not clearly connect these problems to the proposed solution (age verification). The commentary is minimal -- the writer asserts that age verification would "protect young people" but never explains *how* verification would address the issues raised. The logic gap is significant: even if we accept that social media harms teenagers, the paragraph does not demonstrate why age verification (rather than, say, design changes or digital literacy education) is the appropriate response. The paragraph reads more like a list of concerns than a developed argument.

**One Specific Suggestion for Improvement:**
After presenting evidence about harm to teenagers, add 2-3 sentences of commentary that explicitly connect age verification to the problem. For example: "Age verification addresses this harm by ensuring that users are developmentally ready for the psychological pressures of social media. While older teens may still face risks, restricting access during early adolescence -- when identity formation is most fragile and peer influence is strongest -- would reduce exposure during the highest-risk years."

---

### ITEM 2 (Supported Practice)

**Paragraph:**
Homework can deepen inequality because not all students have equal access to resources at home. According to a 2021 report from the National Center for Education Statistics, 14% of U.S. households with school-age children lack reliable internet access, making it difficult or impossible for students to complete assignments that require online research or digital submission. This "homework gap" disproportionately affects rural and low-income communities, meaning that students are effectively penalized for circumstances beyond their control. When academic success depends on resources that schools do not provide, homework stops being a measure of student effort or learning and becomes a measure of family wealth. Schools that claim to value equity must either ensure that all students have the resources to complete assignments or redesign homework to eliminate the need for unequal home supports.

**Score:** Strong (3)

**Detailed Scoring Rationale:**
This paragraph demonstrates all the characteristics of strong argument writing. The claim is clear, specific, and directly connected to a broader argument about equity ("homework can deepen inequality"). The evidence is concrete and credible (a named source with specific data: 14% of households lack internet), and it is smoothly integrated into the writer's sentence rather than dropped in as a quotation. The commentary is thorough and analytical -- the writer explains *why* the homework gap matters ("students are penalized for circumstances beyond their control"), connects it to larger principles ("a measure of family wealth" rather than learning), and offers a logical conclusion about what equity requires. The paragraph also anticipates and addresses a potential counterargument (schools that "claim to value equity") without derailing the main point. Transitions are smooth, and every sentence advances the argument.

**One Specific Suggestion for Improvement:**
While this paragraph is already strong, it could be even more compelling with a brief acknowledgment of a potential counterargument. For example, after presenting the evidence, the writer could add: "Some might argue that homework teaches responsibility regardless of resources, but this ignores the fact that students without internet access must find libraries or other locations to complete work -- a burden that can take hours and is simply not required of their wealthier peers."

---

### ITEM 3 (Supported Practice)

**Paragraph:**
Public libraries are still important today because they provide free access to information. A lot of people can't afford to buy books or pay for internet, and libraries let them use computers and check out materials. Libraries also have programs for kids and help people find jobs. Some people think libraries are outdated now that we have the internet, but not everyone has access to technology at home. Libraries are important community spaces where people can learn and get help. They should continue to be funded because they serve people who need them.

**Score:** Weak (0)

**Detailed Scoring Rationale:**
This paragraph has a discernible topic (libraries are important) but lacks a precise, arguable claim connected to a larger thesis. The evidence is entirely general ("a lot of people," "programs for kids," "help people find jobs") with no specific examples, data, or sources. Most critically, the paragraph offers almost no commentary -- it lists things libraries do but never explains *why* those things matter, *how* they connect to a larger argument about equity or access, or *what* would be lost without libraries. The acknowledgment of the opposing view ("some people think libraries are outdated") is present but undeveloped -- the response is simply to reassert the original claim rather than engaging with the opposition's logic. The paragraph reads like a rough draft that outlines points but does not develop them into analysis.

**One Specific Suggestion for Improvement:**
Choose one specific service libraries provide (e.g., free internet access) and develop it fully with evidence and commentary. For example: "Public libraries are critical equalizers in the digital age because they provide free internet access to populations who cannot afford it. According to a 2022 Pew Research study, 28% of library users cite internet access as a primary reason for their visit, and many of these users are job seekers who need to submit applications, write resumes, or access training resources online. Without libraries, these individuals would be excluded from the digital economy entirely, widening the already significant gap between those who can afford connectivity and those who cannot."

### ITEM 4 (Independent Practice)

**Paragraph:**
Paying college athletes as employees would fundamentally change the purpose of higher education. Universities exist to educate students, not to run professional sports franchises. College athletes already receive significant compensation in the form of scholarships that cover tuition, room, board, and access to world-class coaching and facilities -- benefits worth tens of thousands of dollars per year. According to the NCAA, the average Division I athletic scholarship is valued at $38,000 annually. If athletes were paid as employees, the line between college sports and professional leagues would blur, undermining the educational mission that justifies universities' tax-exempt status. More importantly, only a small fraction of college athletes compete in revenue-generating sports; paying football and basketball players while leaving swimmers, track athletes, and soccer players uncompensated would create a two-tiered system that contradicts the principles of collegiate athletics.

**Score:** Adequate (2)

**Detailed Scoring Rationale:**
This paragraph has a clear claim (paying athletes would undermine the educational mission of universities) and provides relevant evidence (scholarship value, tax-exempt status, disparities between sports). The evidence is more specific than a weak paragraph (e.g., the $38,000 figure) and is integrated reasonably well. However, the commentary is underdeveloped. The writer asserts that payment would "blur the line" and "undermine the educational mission" but does not fully explain *how* or *why* this would happen. What specifically about the educational mission is threatened? Why does tax-exempt status depend on athletes remaining unpaid? The paragraph also introduces a second concern (inequity between athletes in different sports) but does not connect it back to the thesis or explain why this two-tiered system is worse than the current system, where all athletes are unpaid despite vastly different revenue generation. The paragraph is functional and makes a coherent argument, but it stops short of the deeper analysis that would make it strong.

**One Specific Suggestion for Improvement:**
Expand the commentary on the "educational mission" claim. For example: "The educational mission of universities is premised on the idea that students are there to learn and develop, not to generate profit for institutions. Once athletes become employees, universities would be legally accountable for labor conditions, wages, and worker protections -- effectively acknowledging that these students are revenue-generating labor first and learners second. This shift would prioritize athletics over academics in ways that would be difficult to reverse."

---

### ITEM 5 (Independent Practice)

**Paragraph:**
Renewable energy is important, but we need to think about the workers in coal and oil industries. ### ACT-P07 | The Role of Arts Education in Public Schools

**Issue:**
As public school budgets face increasing pressure, many districts have reduced or eliminated funding for arts programs -- music, theater, visual arts, and dance -- in favor of expanding STEM subjects, standardized test preparation, and core academic courses. Supporters of arts education argue that creative disciplines are essential to well-rounded development and future success, while critics contend that schools must prioritize subjects with clearer pathways to college and career readiness. The debate raises fundamental questions about what schools should teach and how we define an educated person.

**Perspective One:**
Arts education is not a luxury but a necessity. The skills developed through music, theater, and visual arts -- creativity, collaboration, emotional expression, and problem-solving -- are precisely the skills that employers say they need but cannot find in graduates. Furthermore, arts programs provide students who struggle in traditional academic subjects with opportunities to excel and build confidence. Cutting arts funding to focus on test scores and STEM reflects a dangerously narrow view of human potential and economic value. A society that does not invest in creativity will not remain competitive or innovative.

**Perspective Two:**
While arts programs may be valuable, schools have limited resources and must make difficult choices about what to prioritize. The evidence is clear: students with strong math, science, literacy, and technology skills have better college and career outcomes than those without them. When budgets are constrained, schools cannot afford to fund programs that serve only a small subset of students or that do not directly contribute to measurable academic achievement. Arts education is something families who value it can pursue outside of school, but schools themselves must focus on core competencies that benefit all students equally.

**Perspective Three:**
The debate over arts versus STEM is a false choice created by inadequate school funding. Wealthy districts manage to offer robust arts programs alongside strong STEM instruction because they have the resources to do both. Rather than asking which subjects to cut, we should be asking why schools are chronically underfunded in the first place and demanding that governments invest enough to provide comprehensive education. Forcing schools to choose between creativity and technical skills guarantees that students -- especially those in underfunded districts -- receive an incomplete education no matter which option is chosen.

**Essay Task:**
Write a unified, coherent essay in which you evaluate multiple perspectives on the role of arts education in public schools. In your essay, be sure to:
- clearly state your own perspective on the issue and analyze the relationship between your perspective and at least one other perspective
- develop and support your ideas with reasoning and examples
- organize your ideas clearly and logically
- communicate your ideas effectively in standard written English

**Designer Notes:**
- **Core assumptions:** P1 assumes that creativity and emotional skills taught in arts classes cannot be effectively taught elsewhere and that employers genuinely value these skills as much as technical ones; P2 assumes that "core competencies" are easily defined, measurable, and universally necessary, and that families have equal access to private arts instruction; P3 assumes that adequate funding is politically achievable and that the problem is purely financial rather than also ideological (i.e., some people genuinely believe STEM is more important, regardless of budget)
