# B1L Templates and References
**Course B1L: AP Language Essay Writing (Grade 11)**

Students entering B1L have completed A1 (argumentative/analytical writing) and A2 (sophisticated argument, synthesis, ACT writing). They know mental models M1-M4. B1L introduces M5 (Reading as Reverse Engineering) and previews M6 (Who Benefits?).

---

## 1. M5 Decision Reconstruction Template

**Instructions:** Use this template when analyzing AP passages to deploy M5 (Reading as Reverse Engineering). For each paragraph or major section, identify the writer's rhetorical problem and how they solved it. This helps you see the passage as a series of strategic choices rather than just content. Deploy M1-M4 simultaneously:
- **M1 (Everything Is a Choice)**: What did the writer choose to do here?
- **M2 (S-F-E)**: What strategy did they use? What effect does it have?
- **M3 (Force Field)**: How does this choice respond to audience resistance or constraints?
- **M4 (Claim Hierarchy)**: How does this choice support their overarching thesis?

**"If I Were Writing This" Technique:** Before analyzing each paragraph, ask yourself: "If I were writing this piece, what problem would I be facing at this point?" This puts you in the writer's shoes and helps you see their decisions as strategic responses.

| Paragraph/Section | Writer's Problem | Writer's Solution |
|-------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| Opening (¶1) | Need to hook audience and establish stakes without alienating skeptics | [Strategy: Opens with vivid anecdote/surprising statistic/common misconception/etc. Effect: ___] |
| ¶2 | Need to establish credibility and preview position | [Strategy: ___ Effect: ___] |
| ¶3 | Need to develop first major claim while maintaining audience engagement | [Strategy: ___ Effect: ___] |
| ¶4 | Need to address counterargument before audience thinks of it | [Strategy: ___ Effect: ___] |
| ¶5 | Need to shift from concession back to main argument | [Strategy: ___ Effect: ___] |
| ¶6 | Need to escalate/deepen the argument | [Strategy: ___ Effect: ___] |
| Conclusion (¶7) | Need to leave lasting impression beyond just summarizing | [Strategy: ___ Effect: ___] |

**Tips for Strong Analysis:**
- The "problem" should be rhetorical (about persuading THIS audience), not just content (what to say next)
- The "solution" should name a specific strategy AND explain its effect using S-F-E
- Look for problems at structural moments: openings, shifts, concessions, climaxes, closings
- Ask: "Why THIS strategy for THIS problem?" (not just "What strategy is this?")

## 2. AP Rhetorical Situation Template (Streamlined)

**Instructions:** Use this streamlined Force Field map during the 5-minute reading phase for AP Rhetorical Analysis. Focus on identifying the forces that explain the writer's BIG choices (not every small detail). Under "Constraints," list what each force prevents or demands. Under "Enabled Moves," list what the writer CAN do because of this situation.

**PASSAGE:** [Author, title, brief context]

| Force | Description | Constraints (What this prevents/demands) | Enabled Moves (What this allows) |
|-------|-------------|------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Exigence** | [What prompted this writing? What problem/issue/occasion?] | - Must address ___ directly<br>- Cannot ignore ___<br>- Must establish urgency | - Can tap into existing concern<br>- Can leverage timeliness<br>- Can position as solution |
| **Audience** | [Who is the target reader? What do they believe/value/fear?] | - Cannot assume ___<br>- Must overcome resistance to ___<br>- Must speak to their values: ___ | - Can appeal to shared ___<br>- Can use their language<br>- Can address their concerns |
| **Speaker** | [Who is the writer? What's their position/credibility/stake?] | - Cannot claim expertise in ___<br>- Must establish credibility via ___<br>- Must navigate their position as ___ | - Can leverage ethos as ___<br>- Can use personal experience<br>- Can speak with authority on ___ |
| **Context** | [When/where written? What's the broader situation?] | - Must acknowledge ___<br>- Cannot ignore recent ___<br>- Must position within debate about ___ | - Can reference current ___<br>- Can build on existing ___<br>- Can position as timely |

**Key Question:** Which of these forces best explains the passage's MAJOR strategic choices (structure, tone, appeals, evidence selection)? Focus your analysis there.

## 3. AP Rhetorical Analysis Planner

**Instructions:** Complete this planner during your 5-minute planning time for the Rhetorical Analysis FRQ. Focus on 2-3 strategies that MATTER MOST (see Strategy Selection Criteria, Section 15). Each body paragraph should analyze one strategy using S-F-E.

```
PASSAGE: [Author, title, context]

RHETORICAL SITUATION (Quick Force Field):
  Audience: ___
    -> Constraints: ___
  Exigence: ___
    -> Constraints: ___
  Writer: ___
    -> Enabled moves: ___

THESIS:
[Author] uses [strategy 1], [strategy 2], and [strategy 3]
to [purpose/effect] for [audience].

LINE OF REASONING (how the strategies work together):
___

---

BODY PARAGRAPH 1: [Strategy name]
  Evidence (quote or paraphrase): "___" (¶___)
  S-F-E Analysis:
    Strategy: [Name the technique specifically]
    Function: [What rhetorical work does it do?]
    Effect: [How does it impact THIS audience given the rhetorical situation?]

BODY PARAGRAPH 2: [Strategy name]
  Evidence (quote or paraphrase): "___" (¶___)
  S-F-E Analysis:
    Strategy: ___
    Function: ___
    Effect: ___

BODY PARAGRAPH 3: [Strategy name / Shift / Culmination]
  Evidence (quote or paraphrase): "___" (¶___)
  S-F-E Analysis:
    Strategy: ___
    Function: ___
    Effect: ___
  [Optional: How does this build on or complicate the earlier strategies?]
```

**Planning Tips:**
- Your thesis should name strategies AND state the writer's purpose
- Each body paragraph = ONE strategy deeply analyzed (not a list of 5 strategies)
- Quote sparingly; paraphrase + paragraph reference is often better
- Connect each strategy back to the rhetorical situation (Why THIS strategy for THIS audience?)

## 4. AP Argument Essay Planner

**Instructions:** Complete this planner during your 5-minute planning time for the Argument FRQ. The "THEY SAY / I SAY" frame helps you position your argument within the debate. Plan 3 claims + 1 naysayer for a strong Row B score (4 points).

```
PROMPT DECOMPOSITION:
  [Claim being debated]: ___
  [Complexity required]: ___
  [Expected response type]: ___

MY POSITION (one sentence): ___

---

THEY SAY (what's the common view or counterargument?): ___

I SAY (your response/complication/position): ___

---

THESIS (defensible claim + line of reasoning):
___

---

BODY PARAGRAPH 1:
  CLAIM 1: ___
  Evidence (type + source): [Historical example / Literary reference / Current event / etc.]
    Specific example: ___
  Why persuasive for AP audience: ___
  S-F-E: Strategy ___ -> Function ___ -> Effect ___

BODY PARAGRAPH 2:
  CLAIM 2: ___
  Evidence (type + source): ___
    Specific example: ___
  Why persuasive for AP audience: ___
  S-F-E: Strategy ___ -> Function ___ -> Effect ___

BODY PARAGRAPH 3 (Naysayer):
  NAYSAYER: Some might argue that ___
  CONCESSION: It's true that ___
  REBUTTAL: However, ___ [pivot word] ___
  Evidence for rebuttal: ___

BODY PARAGRAPH 4:
  CLAIM 3 (often your strongest/most sophisticated): ___
  Evidence (type + source): ___
    Specific example: ___
  Why persuasive for AP audience: ___
  S-F-E: Strategy ___ -> Function ___ -> Effect ___
  [Optional sophistication move: broader context / tension / alternative interpretation]
```

**Planning Tips:**
- Choose evidence from DIFFERENT domains (don't use 3 historical examples)
- See Evidence Strength Ranking (Section 10) for AP audience preferences
- Place naysayer paragraph where it feels most strategic (often paragraph 3)
- Save your strongest, most sophisticated claim for last

## 5. AP Synthesis Essay Planner

| Source | Position (For/Against/Complicates) | Key Evidence/Data | Credibility (Expert/Study/Anecdote) | Use? (Y/N) | Notes |
|--------|---------------------------------------|-------------------|-------------------------------------|------------|-------|
| Source A | | | | | |
| Source B | | | | | |
| Source C | | | | | |
| Source D | | | | | |
| Source E | | | | | |
| Source F | | | | | |
| Source G | | | | | |

**Matrix Tips:**
- Don't read sources in order; skim all first, then dive into the best ones
- Mark "N" for sources that are redundant, weak, or off-topic
- Look for sources that offer DATA, not just opinions
- Identify 1-2 sources you can quote; others you'll paraphrase

### Synthesis Outline (5-minute planning)

```
PROMPT: [Restate the question/task]

MY POSITION: ___

THESIS (with line of reasoning):
___

---

BODY PARAGRAPH 1: [First claim]
  My reasoning: ___
  Source support: [Source X] argues that "___" (paraphrase/quote)
  Source support: [Source Y] provides data showing ___ (paraphrase)
  S-F-E: Using these sources together ___

BODY PARAGRAPH 2: [Second claim]
  My reasoning: ___
  Source support: [Source ___]: ___
  Source support: [Source ___]: ___
  S-F-E: ___

BODY PARAGRAPH 3: [Counterargument/Complication]
  Naysayer: [Source ___] suggests that ___
  My response: However, this overlooks ___ [Source ___] ___
  S-F-E: ___

BODY PARAGRAPH 4: [Third claim, often most sophisticated]
  My reasoning: ___
  Source support: [Source ___]: ___
  Source support: [Source ___]: ___
  Sophistication move: [Broader context / Tension within position / Alternative interpretation]
  S-F-E: ___
```

**Synthesis Planning Tips:**
- Don't let sources drive your argument; use them to support YOUR claims
- Vary attribution patterns (see Section 11)
- Cross-source synthesis = using 2+ sources in same sentence/paragraph
- Cite parenthetically: (Source A), (Source B), etc.

## 6. AP Row A Scoring Guide (Thesis)

**Instructions:** Row A is worth 1 point (0 or 1). Your thesis must state a DEFENSIBLE CLAIM and establish a LINE OF REASONING. It should appear in the introduction (usually the last sentence).

| Score | Criteria | What This Means |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **0 points** | - No thesis<br>- Restates prompt only<br>- Presents claim without line of reasoning | The thesis doesn't take a position OR doesn't preview how the essay will develop that position. |
| **1 point** | - Defensible claim<br>- Line of reasoning | The thesis takes a clear position AND previews the key ideas/strategies that will support it. |

### Rhetorical Analysis Thesis Examples

| Score | Example | Why? |
|-------|---------|------|
| **0** | "The author uses many rhetorical strategies to persuade the audience." | No specific claim about what the author achieves; no line of reasoning (which strategies? to what effect?). |
| **0** | "In this passage, the author uses ethos, pathos, and logos." | Lists strategies but doesn't state the PURPOSE or EFFECT (line of reasoning is missing). |
| **1** | "Through vivid imagery, ethical appeals, and a shift from nostalgia to urgency, the author persuades environmentally skeptical readers that conservation is economically necessary." | Defensible claim (what the author achieves) + line of reasoning (which strategies create that effect). |

### Argument Thesis Examples

| Score | Example | Why? |
|-------|---------|------|
| **0** | "There are many benefits and drawbacks to social media." | Not a defensible CLAIM; just acknowledges complexity without taking a position. |
| **0** | "Social media has negative effects on teenagers." | Defensible claim, but no line of reasoning (which effects? why?). |
| **1** | "Social media undermines teenage well-being by fragmenting attention, distorting social comparison, and replacing substantive connection with performative self-presentation." | Defensible claim + line of reasoning (three specific ways the harm occurs). |

### Synthesis Thesis Examples

| Score | Example | Why? |
|-------|---------|------|
| **0** | "There are both advantages and disadvantages to implementing a four-day school week." | Acknowledges debate but doesn't take a position. |
| **0** | "Schools should adopt a four-day week because it has benefits." | Claim without line of reasoning (which benefits? how do they matter?). |
| **1** | "Schools should adopt a four-day week because it reduces operational costs, improves teacher retention, and allows students more time for deep learning, despite concerns about childcare disruptions." | Defensible claim + line of reasoning (three reasons + acknowledgment of counterargument). |

**Key Insight:** The line of reasoning is what distinguishes Row A 1 from Row A 0. Don't just state your position—preview HOW you'll develop it.

## 7. AP Row B Scoring Guide (Evidence & Commentary)

**Instructions:** Row B is worth 4 points (0-4 scale). This is where you demonstrate your ability to support claims with evidence and analyze that evidence. The key distinction: FEATURE-SPOTTING (Row B 2) vs. S-F-E ANALYSIS (Row B 3-4).

### Rhetorical Analysis Row B

| Score | Criteria | What This Means |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **0 points** | No evidence or commentary | Essay doesn't reference the passage or explain anything. |
| **1 point** | Evidence without commentary OR commentary without evidence | You quote/summarize the passage but don't analyze it, OR you make claims about strategies without evidence. |
| **2 points** | Evidence + feature-spotting commentary | You identify strategies ("The author uses a metaphor") but don't explain FUNCTION or EFFECT. This is the trap—naming techniques isn't analysis. |
| **3 points** | Evidence + consistent S-F-E commentary | You explain HOW strategies work (function) and WHY they're effective for THIS audience/purpose (effect). Most of your paragraphs do this. |
| **4 points** | Evidence + sophisticated S-F-E commentary | Your analysis is consistently insightful. You connect strategies to the rhetorical situation, explain nuances, and show how strategies work together. |

**Row B 2 vs. Row B 3 Examples:**

| Score | Example Commentary | Why? |
|-------|-------------------|------|
| **Row B 2** | "The author uses a metaphor in paragraph 3 comparing the economy to a 'house of cards.' The author also uses repetition of the word 'collapse' three times." | Feature-spotting: names techniques but doesn't explain function or effect. |
| **Row B 3** | "The author's metaphor comparing the economy to a 'house of cards' emphasizes its fragility and precariousness, making the abstract concept of economic instability visceral and alarming for readers who may have felt insulated from risk." | S-F-E analysis: names strategy + explains function (making abstract visceral) + explains effect (alarming readers). |

### Argument/Synthesis Row B

| Score | Criteria | What This Means |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **0 points** | No evidence | Essay makes claims without support. |
| **1 point** | Evidence without clear connection to argument | You include examples but don't explain how they support your claims. |
| **2 points** | Some evidence with basic explanation | You support claims with examples and explain them briefly, but the commentary is thin or repetitive. |
| **3 points** | Multiple pieces of evidence with consistent explanation | You support claims with varied evidence (for synthesis: 3+ sources) and explain how each piece supports your reasoning. |
| **4 points** | Multiple pieces of evidence with sophisticated explanation | Your evidence is well-chosen, varied, and deeply explained. You show nuance, anticipate objections, and connect evidence to broader implications. |

**Key Distinction:** Row B 3-4 requires COMMENTARY that explains the "So what?" Don't just present evidence—analyze it using S-F-E.

## 8. AP Row C Scoring Guide (Sophistication)

**Instructions:** Row C is worth 1 point (0 or 1). This is the hardest point to earn. You demonstrate sophistication by going beyond a competent essay to show insight, nuance, or stylistic control. There are FOUR main ways to earn Row C.

| Score | Criteria | What This Means |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **0 points** | No sophistication | Essay is competent but predictable; analysis stays on the surface; no complexity or insight. |
| **1 point** | Demonstrates sophistication via one or more of these moves: | See below for the four types. You only need ONE move done well (not all four). |

### Four Types of Sophistication Moves

| Type | Description | Example Context |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| **1. Broader Context** | Place the argument/passage within a larger conversation, historical trend, or philosophical framework | "This debate echoes earlier tensions between individual liberty and collective welfare during the New Deal era, suggesting that Americans perpetually struggle to balance autonomy with interdependence." |
| **2. Tension/Complexity Within Position** | Acknowledge genuine complexity within your own position (not just counterarguments); explore trade-offs or limitations | "While regulating AI may protect jobs in the short term, this position risks stifling the innovation that creates entirely new industries—a tension that policymakers must navigate rather than resolve." |
| **3. Alternative Interpretation** | Offer a second reading of evidence or suggest how opponents might reasonably interpret the same facts differently | "Proponents might view this data as evidence of progress, but the same statistics reveal widening disparities that suggest systemic inequities persist beneath surface-level gains." |
| **4. Vivid/Persuasive Style** | Craft prose that is precise, vivid, and rhetorically controlled; use sentence variety, purposeful word choice, and rhetorical devices | "The author doesn't merely argue—she orchestrates a gradual crescendo from personal anecdote to statistical indictment to moral imperative, leaving readers no rhetorical exit." |

**M6 Connection (Preview):** The "Who Benefits?" mental model (M6) is particularly useful for Row C Type 2 and Type 3 sophistication. When you ask "Who benefits from this policy/argument?" you often uncover tensions, trade-offs, and alternative interpretations that elevate your analysis.

**Row C Tips:**
- Don't force sophistication; it should emerge naturally from deep thinking
- One well-executed sophistication move is enough
- The conclusion is a great place for broader context or tension/complexity
- Vivid style requires precise verbs, varied syntax, and purposeful word choice throughout

## 9. AP Lang Rhetorical Device Reference

**Instructions:** Use this reference to identify and name strategies when analyzing passages. Don't just spot devices—explain their FUNCTION and EFFECT using S-F-E. Devices are organized by category.

### Appeals (Aristotelian)

| Device | Definition |
|--------|------------|
| **Ethos** | Appeal to credibility, character, or authority; establishes writer's trustworthiness |
| **Pathos** | Appeal to emotion; evokes feelings to persuade (fear, sympathy, anger, hope, pride, etc.) |
| **Logos** | Appeal to logic and reason; uses evidence, data, reasoning, cause-effect |
| **Kairos** | Appeal to timeliness or appropriateness; seizing the opportune moment to argue |

### Diction (Word Choice)

| Device | Definition |
|--------|------------|
| **Connotation** | Emotional associations of words beyond literal meaning (positive, negative, neutral) |
| **Diction register** | Level of formality (formal, informal, colloquial, slang, technical, archaic) |
| **Concrete vs. Abstract** | Specific, tangible words (concrete) vs. conceptual, intangible words (abstract) |
| **Imagery** | Vivid sensory language that creates mental pictures (visual, auditory, tactile, etc.) |
| **Figurative language** | Non-literal language (see below for specific types) |

### Syntax (Sentence Structure)

| Device | Definition |
|--------|------------|
| **Parallelism** | Repetition of grammatical structure; creates rhythm and emphasizes connection |
| **Juxtaposition** | Placing contrasting ideas/images side-by-side to highlight differences |
| **Antithesis** | Parallel structure that contrasts opposing ideas ("Ask not what your country can do for you...") |
| **Anaphora** | Repetition of words/phrases at the beginning of successive clauses ("We shall fight... We shall fight...") |
| **Epistrophe** | Repetition of words/phrases at the end of successive clauses |
| **Cumulative sentence** | Sentence that starts with main clause, then adds details/modifiers (loose sentence) |
| **Periodic sentence** | Sentence that delays main clause until the end, building suspense |
| **Short sentences** | Brief, punchy sentences; create emphasis, urgency, clarity |
| **Long sentences** | Extended sentences; create complexity, accumulation, flow |
| **Rhetorical question** | Question asked for effect, not expecting an answer; engages audience |

### Figurative Language

| Device | Definition |
|--------|------------|
| **Metaphor** | Implicit comparison between two unlike things ("Time is a thief") |
| **Simile** | Explicit comparison using "like" or "as" ("Her words cut like a knife") |
| **Analogy** | Extended comparison to explain or clarify (often logical/instructive) |
| **Personification** | Giving human qualities to non-human things ("The wind whispered secrets") |
| **Hyperbole** | Deliberate exaggeration for emphasis or effect |
| **Understatement** | Deliberate minimizing for ironic or emphatic effect (often called litotes) |
| **Irony** | Contrast between expectation and reality (verbal, situational, dramatic) |
| **Allusion** | Brief reference to historical, literary, religious, or cultural person/event/text |

### Structure & Organization

| Device | Definition |
|--------|------------|
| **Narrative structure** | Organizing via storytelling (chronological, flashback, framing story) |
| **Cause-effect structure** | Organizing to show causal relationships |
| **Problem-solution structure** | Presenting problem, then proposing solution |
| **Compare-contrast structure** | Organizing around similarities and differences |
| **Inductive reasoning** | Moving from specific examples to general conclusion |
| **Deductive reasoning** | Moving from general principle to specific application |
| **Concession-refutation** | Acknowledging opposing view before rebutting it |
| **Shift/Turn** | Pivotal moment where tone, focus, or direction changes (often signaled by "but," "yet," "however") |

**Usage Tip:** Don't list devices. Choose 2-3 that MATTER MOST (see Strategy Selection Criteria, Section 15) and analyze them deeply using S-F-E.

## 10. Evidence Strength Ranking Reference (AP Argument)

**Instructions:** For AP Argument essays, choose evidence that AP readers will find credible and substantive. Historical and scientific evidence ranks highest because it demonstrates knowledge depth and analytical thinking. Avoid relying primarily on personal anecdotes.

### Evidence Ranking (Strongest to Weakest for AP Audience)

| Rank | Evidence Type | Why Strong/Weak for AP | Example |
|------|---------------|------------------------|---------|
| **1** | **Historical examples (specific, detailed)** | Demonstrates knowledge, analytical thinking, and ability to draw parallels across time; highly persuasive for academic audience | "During the Great Depression, FDR's New Deal programs like the CCC and WPA employed millions in public works projects, demonstrating that government intervention can mitigate economic collapse while building lasting infrastructure." |
| **2** | **Scientific/research evidence** | Objective, data-driven; shows engagement with empirical inquiry; credible for academic audience | "Neuroscience research at MIT has shown that adolescent brains exhibit heightened reward sensitivity in the presence of peers, explaining why teenagers take greater risks in group settings (Steinberg, 2008)." |
| **3** | **Literary references** | Shows intellectual breadth; useful for exploring themes, human nature, ethics; culturally credible | "In *Frankenstein*, Shelley explores the consequences of unchecked scientific ambition, illustrating how innovation divorced from ethical responsibility leads to destruction—a warning relevant to contemporary AI development." |
| **4** | **Current events** | Demonstrates awareness of contemporary issues; timely and relatable; BUT can be superficial if not analyzed deeply | "The 2023 UPS strike negotiations, which secured higher wages and worker protections against AI automation, demonstrate labor's power to shape technological implementation rather than passively accept displacement." |
| **5** | **Logical reasoning/hypotheticals** | Useful for exploring consequences and thought experiments; BUT lacks empirical grounding | "If schools eliminate arts programs to fund STEM, students lose crucial opportunities to develop creativity and emotional intelligence—skills that even technical careers increasingly demand." |
| **6** | **Personal anecdotes (weakest)** | Shows authenticity and lived experience; BUT lacks broader applicability; seen as less rigorous for academic argument | "When my school cut music funding, I watched talented peers lose their only creative outlet, which made me realize how arts programs provide more than entertainment." |

### Strategic Evidence Selection Tips

**DO:**
- Use evidence from DIFFERENT domains (don't stack 3 historical examples)
- Develop historical/scientific evidence with SPECIFICITY (names, dates, details matter)
- Connect evidence explicitly to your claim (don't assume the connection is obvious)
- Use logical reasoning to EXTEND evidence (what does this historical example reveal about human nature?)

**DON'T:**
- Lead with personal anecdotes (if you use them, place them strategically and pair with stronger evidence)
- Use only current events (they're often thin without historical/philosophical depth)
- Name-drop evidence without development ("Einstein said..."; "In *1984*...") — these need analysis

**Row C Sophistication Connection:** The STRONGEST essays often layer evidence types. Example: Start with historical example, connect to current event, then use logical reasoning to explore broader implications. This demonstrates range and intellectual flexibility.

## 11. AP Attribution Patterns Reference

**Instructions:** For Synthesis essays, vary your attribution patterns to create sophisticated, readable prose. Don't use "Source A says..." repeatedly. These patterns build on A2 attribution work but add complexity for AP-level synthesis.

| Pattern Type | Example Sentence | When to Use |
|--------------|------------------|-------------|
| **1. Standard attribution** | According to Source C, renewable energy costs have declined 89% since 2010. | Opening a paragraph; first time citing a source |
| **2. Embedded credentials** | Dr. Maria Chen, a climate economist at Stanford (Source B), argues that carbon pricing mechanisms have proven effective in 27 countries. | When the source's expertise matters; builds ethos |
| **3. Parenthetical citation** | Renewable energy now employs more workers than fossil fuels in the U.S. (Source D). | After presenting data/facts; keeps focus on information |
| **4. Attribution verb variety** | Source E contends / suggests / demonstrates / reveals / acknowledges / warns that... | Throughout essay; avoids repetitive "Source X says..." |
| **5. Attributing data vs. interpretation** | While Source A provides data showing test scores rising, Source F interprets this as evidence of "teaching to the test" rather than genuine learning gains. | When sources offer different interpretations of the same facts |
| **6. Cross-source synthesis (agreement)** | Both Source B and Source D confirm that income inequality has widened over the past two decades. | When multiple sources support the same point |
| **7. Cross-source synthesis (tension)** | While Source C emphasizes the economic benefits of automation, Source F highlights the social costs of job displacement—a tension that policymakers must navigate. | When sources present competing values or priorities |
| **8. Attributing while disagreeing** | Source E claims that school uniforms improve academic performance, but this conclusion rests on correlation rather than causation, as Source G's longitudinal study reveals. | When you need to refute or complicate a source |
| **9. Attribution with commentary** | Source B's survey of 10,000 teenagers reveals widespread mental health concerns, a finding that should alarm policymakers but has been largely ignored in budget debates. | When you want to extend or evaluate the source's significance |
| **10. Multi-source synthesis in one sentence** | The consensus across Sources A, C, and F—despite their different political leanings—suggests that infrastructure investment yields long-term economic returns. | When synthesizing multiple sources to establish broad agreement or patterns |

**Synthesis Writing Tips:**
- Use 2-3 different attribution patterns per paragraph (variety = sophistication)
- Embed credentials (Source B's author is a Stanford economist) when it strengthens your point
- Don't just quote; PARAPHRASE most of the time and save quotes for language that's particularly striking or precise
- Cross-source synthesis (patterns 6, 7, 10) is a sophistication move that can earn Row C

## 12. AP Timing Strategy Reference

**Instructions:** Time management is critical for AP Lang FRQ success. Each essay is 40-55 minutes. Budget your time across reading, planning, writing, and review phases. Practice these timings in mock exams.

### Time Allocation by FRQ Type

| FRQ Type | Reading | Planning | Writing | Review | Total |
|----------|---------|----------|---------|--------|-------|
| **Synthesis** | 15 min | 5 min | 30 min | 5 min | **55 min** |
| **Rhetorical Analysis** | 5 min | (integrated) | 30 min | 5 min | **40 min** |
| **Argument** | — | 5 min | 30 min | 5 min | **40 min** |

### Detailed Timing Strategies

#### Synthesis Essay (55 minutes total)
**15 minutes: Source Reading**
- Skim all 7 sources first (1 min each) to get the landscape
- Mark 1-2 sources you won't use (redundant, weak, off-topic)
- Deep-read your 4-5 strongest sources (3 min each)
- Complete Rapid Synthesis Matrix (Section 5) as you read
- Mark quotable passages with asterisks

**5 minutes: Planning**
- Write thesis
- Outline 3-4 body paragraphs with source assignments
- Note WHERE in each source you'll find support (paragraph numbers)

**30 minutes: Writing**
- Intro + thesis: 5 min
- Body paragraphs: 6-7 min each × 3-4 paragraphs = 20-25 min
- Conclusion: 3-5 min

**5 minutes: Review**
- Check that you've cited 3+ sources (required for Row B 3-4)
- Add transitions if paragraphs feel choppy
- Fix obvious errors, but don't rewrite

#### Rhetorical Analysis (40 minutes total)
**5 minutes: Reading & Planning (Integrated)**
- Read passage once for gist (2 min)
- Complete Rhetorical Situation Template (Section 2): 1 min
- Identify 2-3 strategies that matter most: 1 min
- Write thesis and rough outline: 1 min

**30 minutes: Writing**
- Intro + thesis: 5 min
- Body paragraphs: 7-8 min each × 3 paragraphs = 21-24 min
- Conclusion: 3-5 min

**5 minutes: Review**
- Ensure you've analyzed strategies (not just identified them)
- Check that each body paragraph has S-F-E commentary
- Fix obvious errors

#### Argument (40 minutes total)
**5 minutes: Planning**
- Decompose prompt (1 min)
- Brainstorm position + evidence (2 min)
- Write thesis and outline with 3 claims + naysayer (2 min)

**30 minutes: Writing**
- Intro + thesis: 5 min
- Body paragraphs: 6 min each × 4 paragraphs = 24 min
- Conclusion: 3-5 min (opportunity for Row C sophistication move)

**5 minutes: Review**
- Check that you've developed claims with specific evidence
- Add sophistication move if you didn't include one yet
- Fix obvious errors

### Recovery Strategies (When Running Out of Time)

| Situation | Recovery Strategy |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **5 minutes left, no conclusion written** | Write 2-3 sentence conclusion: (1) Restate thesis differently, (2) Add one sophistication move (broader context or complexity), (3) Final thought. DON'T just summarize. |
| **10 minutes left, one body paragraph not written** | Write a shorter version (4-5 sentences): Topic sentence, one piece of evidence, brief S-F-E analysis. Shorter and complete beats missing entirely. |
| **15 minutes left, two body paragraphs not written** | Finish the stronger of the two paragraphs fully. For the weaker one, write a topic sentence and one developed example. Then write conclusion. |
| **Running long on first body paragraph** | Set a timer. Cap each body paragraph at 7 minutes. It's better to have 3 complete paragraphs at 7 min each than 1 perfect paragraph at 15 min + 2 rushed paragraphs. |

**Key Timing Insight:** AP readers score holistically. A complete essay with shorter body paragraphs scores better than an incomplete essay with one perfect paragraph. FINISH the essay.

## 13. Row C Sophistication Moves Reference

**Instructions:** This section provides detailed before/after examples for each of the four Row C sophistication types. Use these as models for elevating your own writing. You only need ONE sophistication move done well to earn Row C—don't force all four.

### Type 1: Broader Context

**What It Is:** Situating the argument/passage within a larger historical trend, philosophical debate, or ongoing conversation. Shows that you see beyond the immediate topic to its broader significance.

**Before (Row B 3-4, but no Row C):**
> Social media platforms should be regulated because they spread misinformation, harm teenage mental health, and undermine democratic discourse. These problems are serious and require government intervention.

**After (Row C with broader context):**
> Social media platforms should be regulated because they spread misinformation, harm teenage mental health, and undermine democratic discourse. This debate mirrors earlier regulatory struggles over radio, television, and even the printing press—each new medium has demanded a renegotiation of the boundary between free expression and social harm. What makes this moment distinctive is the algorithmic amplification of content, which accelerates harm at a scale previous media regulators never confronted.

**Why This Earns Row C:** You've placed the debate in historical context (radio, TV, printing press regulations) AND identified what's genuinely NEW about this iteration (algorithmic amplification). This shows intellectual range.

**M6 Connection:** Asking "Who benefits from regulation vs. non-regulation?" often reveals broader patterns about power, speech, and technology.

---

### Type 2: Tension/Complexity Within Your Position

**What It Is:** Acknowledging genuine trade-offs, costs, or limitations within your own argument (NOT just counterarguments from opponents). Shows intellectual honesty and nuance.

**Before (Row B 3-4, but no Row C):**
> Schools should adopt four-day weeks because they reduce costs, improve teacher retention, and give students more time for deep learning. While critics worry about childcare disruptions, schools can partner with community organizations to provide supervision on the fifth day.

**After (Row C with tension/complexity):**
> Schools should adopt four-day weeks because they reduce costs, improve teacher retention, and give students more time for deep learning. Yet this position requires confronting an uncomfortable tension: the families most harmed by childcare disruptions—low-income, single-parent households—are precisely those who most need the cost savings and teacher stability that four-day weeks provide. Policymakers must navigate this trade-off rather than pretend it doesn't exist, perhaps by phasing in four-day weeks with targeted support for affected families.

**Why This Earns Row C:** You've acknowledged a tension WITHIN your own position (helping some while harming others) rather than dismissing it. You've also suggested a nuanced path forward. This shows sophisticated thinking.

---

### Type 3: Alternative Interpretation of Evidence

**What It Is:** Offering a second, reasonable reading of your evidence or showing how opponents might interpret the same facts differently. Demonstrates that you understand evidence is rarely univocal.

**Before (Row B 3-4, but no Row C):**
> Historical evidence supports investment in arts education. During the 1960s, federal arts funding expanded dramatically, and this period saw an explosion of American creative innovation—supporting the claim that public investment cultivates artistic excellence.

**After (Row C with alternative interpretation):**
> Historical evidence supports investment in arts education. During the 1960s, federal arts funding expanded dramatically, and this period saw an explosion of American creative innovation. Yet skeptics might reasonably interpret this correlation differently: perhaps economic prosperity and Cold War cultural competition drove both arts funding AND innovation, meaning the funding was symptom rather than cause. Even granting this alternative reading, however, federal support provided infrastructure—theaters, grants, training programs—that channeled creative energy into lasting institutions.

**Why This Earns Row C:** You've presented an alternative interpretation (correlation vs. causation), acknowledged its reasonableness, then explained why your position still holds. This shows you're thinking critically about evidence, not just asserting claims.

---

### Type 4: Vivid/Persuasive Style

**What It Is:** Prose that is precise, rhetorically controlled, and purposefully crafted. Uses sentence variety, vivid verbs, purposeful word choice, and rhetorical devices to create impact.

**Before (Competent but plain):**
> The author uses many strategies to convince the audience. First, she uses an anecdote about her childhood to build credibility. Then she uses statistics to support her claim. Finally, she uses a rhetorical question to make the audience think.

**After (Row C with vivid style):**
> The author doesn't merely argue—she orchestrates. Opening with a childhood anecdote, she disarms skeptical readers, establishing herself as relatable rather than preachy. Then, mid-passage, she pivots: statistics transform personal concern into societal crisis, the numbers indicting not just individual choices but systemic failures. By the conclusion, her rhetorical question—"How long will we tolerate this?"—leaves readers no exit, the question hanging like an accusation.

**Why This Earns Row C:** The prose is vivid and rhetorically controlled. Note the purposeful verbs (orchestrates, disarms, pivots, indicting, hanging), varied sentence structures (short "she pivots" vs. long final sentence), and metaphorical language (numbers "indicting," question "hanging like an accusation"). This is craft.

**Stylistic Moves to Practice:**
- **Purposeful verbs:** Instead of "uses," try: deploys, orchestrates, leverages, wields, dismantles, escalates
- **Sentence variety:** Mix short punchy sentences with longer cumulative/periodic sentences
- **Metaphor:** "The author's argument builds like a legal case" / "unravels like a thread" / "crescendos to moral imperative"
- **Parallel structure:** "First X, then Y, finally Z" / "Not just A but B" / "Neither X nor Y, but Z"

## 14. Weakest Row Fix Guide

**Instructions:** Use this guide to diagnose and fix the weakest dimension of your essay. AP essays are scored holistically, but each row (A, B, C) contributes. If you consistently lose points in one row, target that weakness.

### Row A (Thesis) Fixes

**Problem:** Thesis earns 0 points (no defensible claim or no line of reasoning).

**How to Diagnose:**
- Read your thesis aloud. Does it take a clear POSITION, or just describe/acknowledge?
- Does your thesis PREVIEW how you'll develop that position (line of reasoning)?
- Could someone disagree with your thesis? (If not, it's not defensible.)

**Fix Strategy:**
1. **For Rhetorical Analysis:** Use this formula: "[Author] uses [strategy 1], [strategy 2], and [strategy 3] to [purpose/effect] for [audience]."
2. **For Argument:** Use this formula: "[Position] because [reason 1], [reason 2], and [reason 3], despite [acknowledgment of complexity]."
3. **For Synthesis:** Same as Argument, but preview which sources/perspectives you'll engage.

**Revised Thesis Checklist:**
- [ ] Takes a defensible position (not just "there are pros and cons")
- [ ] Previews 2-3 key ideas/strategies that will structure the essay
- [ ] Is specific enough that someone could disagree

---

### Row B (Evidence & Commentary) Fixes

**Problem:** Thesis earns 2 points (feature-spotting) instead of 3-4 (S-F-E analysis).

**How to Diagnose:**
- Reread one body paragraph. Do you NAME strategies but not explain FUNCTION or EFFECT?
- Does your commentary answer "What strategy?" but not "How does it work?" or "Why is it effective?"
- Do you list 3-4 strategies per paragraph instead of analyzing 1 strategy deeply?

**Fix Strategy: The S-F-E Expansion**

Take one sentence of feature-spotting commentary and expand it into S-F-E:

**Before (Row B 2):**
> "The author uses a metaphor comparing social media to a 'slot machine' in paragraph 4."

**After (Row B 3-4):**
> "The author's metaphor comparing social media to a 'slot machine' (paragraph 4) renders the abstraction of 'addictive design' visceral and alarming. By evoking the image of gambling—an activity culturally coded as predatory—the author positions tech companies not as neutral platforms but as manipulators exploiting psychological vulnerabilities. For an audience of parents skeptical that apps could truly harm children, this metaphor reframes the issue from parental overreaction to corporate exploitation, making regulation seem not just justified but necessary."

**S-F-E Formula to Apply:**
1. **Strategy:** Name it precisely (not just "metaphor" but "metaphor comparing X to Y")
2. **Function:** What rhetorical WORK does it do? (makes abstract visceral, reframes issue, establishes urgency, etc.)
3. **Effect:** How does it impact THIS AUDIENCE given the rhetorical situation? (changes how they see the issue, overcomes resistance, builds ethos, etc.)

**Quick Fix:** After every piece of evidence in your next essay, ask: "So what? How does this evidence support my claim?" Write 2-3 sentences answering that question.

---

### Row C (Sophistication) Fixes

**Problem:** Essay earns 0 points for Row C (competent but not sophisticated).

**How to Diagnose:**
- Does your essay stay on the surface, making predictable points?
- Do you avoid complexity, tensions, or alternative interpretations?
- Is your prose functional but plain (no sentence variety, vivid verbs, or rhetorical control)?

**Fix Strategy: Add ONE Sophistication Move**

You don't need all four types—just ONE done well. Choose the move that fits your essay:

| If your essay... | Try this sophistication move | Where to add it |
|------------------|------------------------------|-----------------|
| Discusses a contemporary issue | **Broader context**: Connect to historical parallel or ongoing philosophical debate | Introduction or conclusion |
| Takes a strong position | **Tension/complexity**: Acknowledge trade-offs or costs within your own position | Body paragraph 3 or 4, or conclusion |
| Relies heavily on one interpretation of evidence | **Alternative interpretation**: Offer a second reading, then explain why yours is stronger | Body paragraph 2 or 3 |
| Has solid analysis but plain prose | **Vivid style**: Revise 2-3 sentences for purposeful verbs, sentence variety, metaphor | Throughout, especially in topic sentences and conclusion |

**Easiest Entry Point for Row C:** Add 2-3 sentences to your conclusion that place your argument in broader context or acknowledge a genuine tension within your position. Example:

**Before (Row C 0):**
> In conclusion, schools should adopt four-day weeks because they save money, retain teachers, and improve learning.

**After (Row C 1):**
> In conclusion, schools should adopt four-day weeks because they save money, retain teachers, and improve learning. This debate is ultimately about competing values: efficiency versus equity, innovation versus stability. The question is not whether four-day weeks work in theory—pilot programs have demonstrated their benefits—but whether we can implement them without exacerbating the inequities that already divide American education.

**Why This Works:** You've acknowledged the underlying values conflict (efficiency vs. equity) and identified the real challenge (implementation without harm). This is sophisticated thinking.

## 15. Strategy Selection Criteria

**Instructions:** For Rhetorical Analysis essays, you must choose 2-3 strategies to analyze deeply (not 7 strategies briefly). Use these criteria to identify which strategies MATTER MOST. The strongest essays analyze strategies that are central to the writer's purpose and responsive to the rhetorical situation.

### Four Questions for Strategy Selection

Ask these four questions about each potential strategy. If a strategy scores "yes" on 3-4 questions, it's worth analyzing. If it scores "yes" on 1-2 questions, skip it.

| Question | What This Means | Example |
|----------|-----------------|---------|
| **1. Does this strategy directly serve the writer's stated purpose?** | The strategy is central to achieving the writer's goal (persuade, inform, inspire, warn, etc.), not just decorative. | If the writer's purpose is to alarm complacent readers about climate change, a strategy that establishes urgency (vivid imagery of consequences, countdown structure, shift to imperative mood) directly serves that purpose. A passing metaphor about seasons doesn't. |
| **2. Does this strategy address the audience's specific resistance?** | The strategy responds to what THIS audience believes, values, or fears; it overcomes a barrier to persuasion. | If the audience is skeptical parents who think "kids are too sensitive," a strategy that reframes mental health struggles as biologically rooted (using scientific evidence + medical diction) directly addresses their resistance. |
| **3. Does this strategy appear at a key structural moment?** | The strategy occurs at the opening (hooks audience), shift (changes direction/tone), climax (strongest appeal), or closing (lasting impression). | An anaphora that appears in the opening paragraph (establishing rhythm and theme) or in the conclusion (building to crescendo) likely matters more than a single metaphor in paragraph 4. |
| **4. Is this strategy unusual or distinctive enough to generate deep analysis?** | The strategy is sophisticated, layered, or unexpected—not just "the author uses logos." It gives you something interesting to say. | A shift from second person ("you") to first person plural ("we") that repositions the audience from blamed party to collaborative problem-solvers is distinctive. Simply "using statistics" is not. |

### Examples: Applying Strategy Selection Criteria

**Passage Context:** An environmental scientist writing to suburban homeowners about lawn care practices that harm local ecosystems.

| Strategy | Q1: Serves purpose? | Q2: Addresses resistance? | Q3: Key structural moment? | Q4: Distinctive? | **Analyze?** |
|----------|---------------------|---------------------------|----------------------------|------------------|--------------|
| Opening anecdote about personal lawn care mistake | YES (establishes ethos, builds relatability) | YES (overcomes "preachy expert" resistance) | YES (opening) | YES (positions writer as fellow learner, not judge) | **YES** |
| Use of statistics about chemical runoff | YES (establishes problem's scope) | Somewhat (proves harm, but may not overcome values resistance) | NO (mid-passage data) | NO (expected) | Maybe |
| Shift from "your lawn" to "our shared watershed" (2nd person to 1st person plural) | YES (reframes issue from individual blame to collective responsibility) | YES (addresses homeowners' defensiveness) | YES (structural shift at paragraph 5) | YES (distinctive pronoun choice with rhetorical effect) | **YES** |
| Single metaphor comparing lawns to "green deserts" | YES (vivid, reframes lawns as lifeless) | Somewhat (challenges aesthetic values) | NO (one sentence in paragraph 3) | Somewhat (vivid, but brief) | Maybe |
| Concrete diction + imperative mood in conclusion ("Choose native plants. Reduce mowing. Eliminate pesticides.") | YES (provides actionable steps, urgent tone) | YES (offers solutions, not just criticism) | YES (conclusion) | YES (imperative mood is bold for this audience) | **YES** |
| Use of logos throughout | YES (serves purpose) | NO (doesn't address specific resistance) | NO (not structural) | NO (not distinctive) | **NO** |

**Result:** Analyze the opening anecdote, the pronoun shift, and the imperative conclusion. These three strategies score highly on the criteria and will generate deep S-F-E commentary.

### Common Mistakes in Strategy Selection

| Mistake | Why It's Weak | Better Approach |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Analyzing "ethos, pathos, logos"** | Too broad; every passage uses all three appeals. This is feature-spotting, not analysis. | Analyze a SPECIFIC ethos-building move (e.g., "The author's opening concession to opponents' concerns establishes her as fair-minded, overcoming audience skepticism") |
| **Listing 7 devices without development** | Breadth without depth; looks like feature-spotting (Row B 2). | Choose 2-3 devices and analyze each for a full paragraph using S-F-E. |
| **Analyzing decorative devices that don't serve purpose** | If the device doesn't connect to the writer's purpose or audience's resistance, it's not worth analyzing. | Use Questions 1-2 above to filter. |
| **Ignoring structural strategies** | Structure is often the most important strategic choice (where the shift happens, how the conclusion reframes, etc.), but students overlook it. | Always ask: "What's the structure of this passage, and why did the writer structure it this way?" |

### Quick Strategy Selection Process (During 5-Minute Planning)

1. **Skim passage:** Identify the writer's PURPOSE and the audience's likely RESISTANCE.
2. **Mark structural moments:** Opening, shifts, climax, conclusion. What strategies appear there?
3. **Ask the 4 questions:** Which strategies score highest?
4. **Choose 2-3 strategies:** Pick the ones you can develop with S-F-E commentary.
5. **Write thesis:** Name those 2-3 strategies and the effect they create.

**Final Insight:** The best rhetorical analysis essays don't catalog everything the writer does. They explain the 2-3 MOST IMPORTANT strategic choices and analyze them deeply. Quality over quantity.
