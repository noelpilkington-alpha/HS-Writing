# Style Discrimination Items -- A1 Lesson 17

## Section A: Parallelism (8 items)

**Student task:** For each pair, (1) identify which version is correct, and (2) name what is faulty in the other.

---

### PAR-01 | Coordinating Conjunction

**Version A:** The essayist develops her argument by providing historical examples, citing expert testimony, and she includes personal anecdotes.

**Version B:** The essayist develops her argument by providing historical examples, citing expert testimony, and including personal anecdotes.

**Correct:** B
**Error in A:** The third item in the list shifts from a gerund phrase ("providing," "citing") to an independent clause ("she includes"), breaking parallel structure.

---

### PAR-02 | Coordinating Conjunction

**Version A:** The speaker's tone is urgent, direct, and it conveys a sense of authority.

**Version B:** The speaker's tone is urgent, direct, and authoritative.

**Correct:** B
**Error in A:** The third item shifts from a predicate adjective ("urgent," "direct") to an independent clause ("it conveys a sense of authority"), breaking the parallel series of adjectives.

---

### PAR-03 | Correlative Conjunction (both/and)

**Version A:** The author both acknowledges the opposition's strongest evidence and refutes it with recent data.

**Version B:** The author both acknowledges the opposition's strongest evidence and is refuting it with recent data.

**Correct:** A
**Error in B:** After "both...and," the verb forms must match. "Acknowledges" (simple present) is paired with "is refuting" (present progressive) instead of the parallel "refutes."

---

### PAR-04 | Coordinating Conjunction

**Version A:** In the opening paragraph, the writer establishes credibility through a relevant anecdote, poses a provocative question, and clearly stating the thesis.

**Version B:** In the opening paragraph, the writer establishes credibility through a relevant anecdote, poses a provocative question, and clearly states the thesis.

**Correct:** B
**Error in A:** The third verb in the series shifts from conjugated present tense ("establishes," "poses") to a participial form ("stating"), breaking the parallel predicate structure.

---

### PAR-05 | Correlative Conjunction (not only/but also)

**Version A:** The editorial not only challenges the mayor's fiscal policy but also questions the transparency of the budget process.

**Version B:** The editorial not only challenges the mayor's fiscal policy but also the transparency of the budget process is questioned.

**Correct:** A
**Error in B:** After "not only [verb phrase]...but also," the structure requires a matching verb phrase. Version B shifts to a passive independent clause ("the transparency...is questioned") instead of a parallel active verb phrase.

---

### PAR-06 | Than/As Comparison

**Version A:** Analyzing the author's use of metaphor is more revealing than to simply summarize the plot.

**Version B:** Analyzing the author's use of metaphor is more revealing than simply summarizing the plot.

**Correct:** B
**Error in A:** The comparison links a gerund phrase ("Analyzing...") with an infinitive phrase ("to simply summarize"), which are not parallel. Both sides of "than" should use the same verb form.

---

### PAR-07 | Correlative Conjunction (either/or)

**Version A:** The reader must either interpret the narrator's silence as complicity or as a deliberate rhetorical withholding.

**Version B:** The reader must interpret the narrator's silence as either complicity or a deliberate rhetorical withholding.

**Correct:** B
**Error in A:** Placing "either" before the verb ("either interpret...as complicity") promises the "or" will introduce a second verb phrase, but "or" instead introduces another prepositional object ("as a deliberate rhetorical withholding"). Moving "either" after "as" makes both branches parallel noun phrases.

---

### PAR-08 | Than/As Comparison

**Version A:** The writer's decision to structure the essay as a series of vignettes is as strategically important as her choosing which details to omit.

**Version B:** The writer's decision to structure the essay as a series of vignettes is as strategically important as her decision about which details to omit.

**Correct:** B
**Error in A:** The "as...as" comparison pairs a noun phrase ("The writer's decision to structure...") with a gerund phrase ("her choosing which details to omit"). Version B maintains parallel noun phrases on both sides ("decision...decision").

## Section B: Sentence Combining (6 items)

---

### SC-01 | Coordination

**Original sentences:**
1. The author opens with a vivid anecdote about factory workers.
2. She immediately pivots to national unemployment statistics.

**Task:** Combine into one sentence using coordination.

**Model Answer:** The author opens with a vivid anecdote about factory workers, and she immediately pivots to national unemployment statistics.

**Why this works:** The two ideas carry equal weight -- both describe deliberate moves the author makes in sequence. A coordinating conjunction ("and") preserves that equality while showing the sequence.

---

### SC-02 | Coordination

**Original sentences:**
1. The speech relies heavily on emotional appeals.
2. It offers almost no verifiable evidence.

**Task:** Combine into one sentence using coordination.

**Model Answer:** The speech relies heavily on emotional appeals, yet it offers almost no verifiable evidence.

**Why this works:** The two observations are equally important and exist in tension. The coordinating conjunction "yet" signals contrast between the emotional strategy and the lack of evidence, giving neither idea more weight than the other.

---

### SC-03 | Subordination

**Original sentences:**
1. The columnist shifts from third person to first person midway through the piece.
2. The shift makes the argument feel more personal and urgent.

**Task:** Combine into one sentence using subordination.

**Model Answer:** When the columnist shifts from third person to first person midway through the piece, the argument begins to feel more personal and urgent.

**Why this works:** The point-of-view shift is the cause; the change in tone is the effect the writer wants to analyze. Subordinating the shift with "when" keeps the analytical focus on the result -- the heightened sense of urgency -- which is the more important claim.

---

### SC-04 | Subordination

**Original sentences:**
1. The author concedes that standardized testing has some diagnostic value.
2. She argues that its costs to student well-being far outweigh that benefit.

**Task:** Combine into one sentence using subordination.

**Model Answer:** Although the author concedes that standardized testing has some diagnostic value, she argues that its costs to student well-being far outweigh that benefit.

**Why this works:** The concession is background information; the author's main argument is the dominant idea. Subordinating the concession with "although" signals to the reader that the second clause carries the real analytical weight.

---

### SC-05 | Student Choice

**Original sentences:**
1. The essayist arranges her evidence chronologically.
2. This structure allows readers to watch the problem worsen over time.
3. The cumulative effect strengthens her call to action.

**Task:** Combine into one or two sentences using coordination, subordination, or a mix. Be prepared to explain your choice.

**Model Answer:** Because the essayist arranges her evidence chronologically, readers watch the problem worsen over time, and the cumulative effect strengthens her call to action.

**Why this works:** The chronological arrangement is the strategic cause, so it belongs in a subordinate clause ("Because..."). The two results -- the reader experience and the rhetorical payoff -- are equally important consequences, so they are coordinated with "and." Subordination highlights the cause-effect relationship; coordination keeps the two effects on equal footing.

---

### SC-06 | Student Choice

**Original sentences:**
1. The writer uses short, declarative sentences in the final paragraph.
2. The rest of the essay relies on long, complex sentences.
3. The contrast in sentence length signals a shift in tone.

**Task:** Combine into one or two sentences using coordination, subordination, or a mix. Be prepared to explain your choice.

**Model Answer:** Whereas the rest of the essay relies on long, complex sentences, the writer uses short, declarative sentences in the final paragraph; this contrast in sentence length signals a shift in tone.

**Why this works:** Subordination with "whereas" sets up the comparison between the two sentence styles, placing the more surprising choice (the short sentences) in the main clause. A semicolon then coordinates the analytical observation about tone, which follows logically as an equal partner to the description of the technique itself.
