# Mentor Sentences for Sentence Imitation (M.9.02)

> **Purpose:** Study each sentence's *structure*, then write your own sentence imitating that structure on a different topic. The goal is not to copy content but to internalize syntactic moves that strengthen analytical writing.

---

## Periodic Sentences
*Main clause delayed until the end -- the reader must hold the whole sentence in mind, building anticipation.*

---

### MS-01 | Pattern: Periodic Sentence

**Source:** Frederick Douglass, *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass*, 1845

**Sentence:** "From my earliest recollection, I date the entertainment of a deep conviction that slavery would not always be able to hold me within its foul embrace; and in the darkest hours of my career in slavery, this living word of faith and spirit of hope departed not from me, but remained like ministering angels to cheer me through the gloom."

**Structural Diagram:** "[Prepositional phrase of time], [MAIN CLAUSE]; and [prepositional phrase of contrast], [SUBJECT] + [verb phrase] + but [verb phrase with simile extending the idea]."

**Why This Works:** Douglass delays his assertion of inner freedom by first anchoring us in the conditions of enslavement, so that when the main clause arrives, his conviction feels earned rather than naive. The periodic structure mirrors the endurance it describes -- the reader must persist through the sentence just as Douglass persisted through bondage.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence about a long-term academic or personal goal using the same structure: *From [earliest time marker], [state the conviction]; and in [the hardest moments], [that conviction] did not [leave], but [remained like something vivid].*"

---

### MS-02 | Pattern: Periodic Sentence

**Source:** Mary Wollstonecraft, *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*, 1792

**Sentence:** "If the abstract rights of man will bear discussion and explanation, those of woman, by a parity of reasoning, will not shrink from the same test."

**Structural Diagram:** "If [conditional clause setting up the premise], [MAIN CLAUSE with logical conclusion] + [parenthetical phrase reinforcing logic]."

**Why This Works:** By withholding her conclusion until after the conditional, Wollstonecraft forces readers to accept the premise before they encounter its radical implication. The periodic delay is itself a logical move -- it makes disagreement feel irrational.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence about fairness in school policy using the same structure: *If [accepted principle] will bear [test], then [your parallel case], by [a stated logic], will not shrink from [the same test].*"

## Cumulative Sentences
*Main clause first, then trailing modifiers that add detail, nuance, or evidence.*

---

### MS-03 | Pattern: Cumulative Sentence

**Source:** Virginia Woolf, *A Room of One's Own*, 1929

**Sentence:** "One could not but play for a moment with the thought of what might have happened if Charlotte Bronte had possessed say three hundred a year -- but the foolish woman sold the copyright of her novels outright for fifteen hundred pounds; had somehow possessed more knowledge of the busy world, and towns and regions full of life; more practical experience, and intercourse with her kind and acquaintance with a variety of character."

**Structural Diagram:** "[MAIN CLAUSE -- one could not but play with a thought] + [trailing hypothetical elaboration] -- [interjected fact] + [series of parallel noun phrases accumulating what was lacking]."

**Why This Works:** The cumulative structure lets Woolf pile up everything Bronte was denied, so that the sheer weight of the trailing modifiers becomes the argument. Each addition makes the deprivation more concrete and more damning.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence about what a person could have achieved given different circumstances: *One cannot help but wonder what might have happened if [person] had possessed [resource] -- [contrasting fact]; had somehow possessed [more of X], and [Y], and [Z].*"

---

### MS-04 | Pattern: Cumulative Sentence

**Source:** Henry David Thoreau, *Civil Disobedience*, 1849

**Sentence:** "The government itself, which is only the mode which the people have chosen to execute their will, is equally liable to be abused and perverted before the people can act through it."

**Structural Diagram:** "[SUBJECT with embedded relative clause defining it], [MAIN VERB PHRASE] + [temporal modifier limiting the claim]."

**Why This Works:** Thoreau delivers his main assertion up front -- government is liable to abuse -- then trails off into a qualifier that deepens the critique. The cumulative "before the people can act through it" transforms a general complaint into a specific structural argument about timing and power.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence critiquing a system or institution: *[The system] itself, which is only [its stated purpose], is equally liable to [fail in some way] before [the people it serves] can [act through it].*"

## Parallel Structure
*Repeated grammatical pattern for emphasis -- the rhythm carries the argument.*

---

### MS-05 | Pattern: Parallel Structure

**Source:** Frederick Douglass, *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass*, 1845

**Sentence:** "I have found that, to make a contented slave, it is necessary to make a thoughtless one. It is necessary to darken his moral and mental vision, and, as far as possible, to annihilate the power of reason."

**Structural Diagram:** "To [make X], it is necessary to [make Y]. It is necessary to [verb + object], and, [parenthetical qualifier], to [verb + object]."

**Why This Works:** The repeated "it is necessary to" structure forces the reader to confront each step of the slaveholder's project as a deliberate, methodical act. Parallelism turns description into indictment -- each repetition is another charge.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write two sentences about what it takes to create a specific outcome (positive or negative) using the same structure: *I have found that, to [achieve X], it is necessary to [do Y]. It is necessary to [verb + object], and, [qualifier], to [verb + object].*"

---

### MS-06 | Pattern: Parallel Structure

**Source:** Thomas Paine, *Common Sense*, 1776

**Sentence:** "Society is produced by our wants, and government by our wickedness; the former promotes our happiness positively by uniting our affections, the latter negatively by restraining our vices."

**Structural Diagram:** "[Subject A] is [produced by X], and [Subject B] [by Y]; [the former] [does Z] positively by [gerund phrase], [the latter] [does Z] negatively by [gerund phrase]."

**Why This Works:** The symmetrical parallelism makes Paine's distinction between society and government feel not like an opinion but like a natural law. The balanced structure implies that the contrast is inherent, not invented.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence that draws a sharp contrast between two related concepts: *[A] is produced by [X], and [B] by [Y]; the former promotes [goal] by [method], the latter [goal] by [method].*"

## Embedded Appositive
*A noun phrase renamed or elaborated mid-sentence, adding information without breaking the sentence's momentum.*

---

### MS-07 | Pattern: Embedded Appositive

**Source:** W.E.B. Du Bois, *The Souls of Black Folk* ("Of Our Spiritual Strivings"), 1903

**Sentence:** "The history of the American Negro is the history of this strife, -- this longing to attain self-conscious manhood, to merge his double self into a better and truer self."

**Structural Diagram:** "[MAIN CLAUSE: A is B], -- [APPOSITIVE renaming 'this strife' as 'this longing'] + [parallel infinitive phrases elaborating the longing]."

**Why This Works:** The appositive redefines "strife" as "longing," shifting the emotional register from conflict to aspiration. Du Bois uses the grammatical pause to reframe his entire argument -- what looks like a fight is actually a yearning.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence that defines a struggle and then renames it as something deeper: *The history of [group or experience] is the history of this [initial term], -- this [redefining term] to [infinitive], to [infinitive].*"

---

### MS-08 | Pattern: Embedded Appositive

**Source:** Ralph Waldo Emerson, *Self-Reliance*, 1841

**Sentence:** "Society everywhere is in conspiracy against the manhood of every one of its members. Society is a joint-stock company, in which the members agree, for the better securing of his bread to each shareholder, to surrender the liberty and culture of the eater."

**Structural Diagram:** "[MAIN CLAUSE: Society is X], [APPOSITIVE renaming 'conspiracy' as 'a joint-stock company'], in which [relative clause with embedded purpose phrase] + [infinitive phrase revealing the cost]."

**Why This Works:** The appositive metaphor -- "a joint-stock company" -- reframes social conformity in economic terms, making the abstract threat of "conspiracy" suddenly concrete. The embedded purpose phrase ("for the better securing of his bread") delays the true cost ("to surrender the liberty and culture"), so the reader feels the trade-off structurally.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence that names a social phenomenon and then renames it through a metaphor: *[Institution] is [abstract claim]. [Institution] is a [concrete metaphor], in which [members] agree, for [stated benefit], to [hidden cost].*"

## Colon Introduction
*Independent clause, then a colon, then elaboration -- the colon says "here is the proof" or "here is what I mean."*

---

### MS-09 | Pattern: Colon Introduction

**Source:** W.E.B. Du Bois, *The Souls of Black Folk* ("Of Our Spiritual Strivings"), 1903

**Sentence:** "It is a peculiar sensation, this double-consciousness, this sense of always looking at one's self through the eyes of others, of measuring one's soul by the tape of a world that looks on in amused contempt and pity."

**Structural Diagram:** "[MAIN CLAUSE: It is a peculiar sensation], [APPOSITIVE naming it], [APPOSITIVE expanding it through two parallel 'of' phrases that elaborate what double-consciousness feels like]."

**Why This Works:** Du Bois states the abstraction first -- "a peculiar sensation" -- then unpacks it in layers of increasing specificity. Each trailing phrase after the comma functions like elaboration after a colon: it answers the implicit question "what do you mean?" The sentence makes the reader experience the very layering of perspective it describes.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence that names a feeling or experience, then unpacks it in increasingly specific terms: *It is a [adjective] [sensation/experience], this [name for it], this sense of [gerund phrase], of [gerund phrase that deepens the first].*"

---

### MS-10 | Pattern: Colon Introduction

**Source:** Jonathan Swift, *A Modest Proposal*, 1729

**Sentence:** "I grant this food will be somewhat dear, and therefore very proper for landlords, who, as they have already devoured most of the parents, seem to have the best title to the children."

**Structural Diagram:** "[MAIN CLAUSE: I grant X], and therefore [logical extension] + [relative clause with embedded ironic metaphor delivering the real argument]."

**Why This Works:** The colon-like logical connector "and therefore" pretends to follow rational economic argument, but the relative clause ("who, as they have already devoured most of the parents") detonates the sentence's real meaning. Swift uses the structure of reasonable prose to deliver savage critique -- the form is the satire.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence that uses mock-logical reasoning to expose an absurdity: *I grant that [seemingly reasonable premise], and therefore [logical-sounding extension], who, as they have already [done something revealing], seem to have the best [claim/right] to [consequence].*"

---

### MS-11 | Pattern: Colon Introduction

**Source:** Ralph Waldo Emerson, *Self-Reliance*, 1841

**Sentence:** "There is a time in every man's education when he arrives at the conviction that envy is ignorance; that imitation is suicide."

**Structural Diagram:** "[MAIN CLAUSE: There is a time] + [temporal relative clause] when [he arrives at the conviction] that [bold claim]; that [bolder claim]."

**Why This Works:** The sentence builds to its two declarations through a frame of inevitability -- "there is a time" suggests this is universal, not personal opinion. The colon-like structure of "the conviction that... ; that..." delivers two maxims as if they are discoveries, not assertions. The semicolon between them gives each its own weight.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence that frames a personal realization as a universal moment of understanding: *There is a time in every [person's experience] when [they] arrive at the conviction that [claim]; that [stronger claim].*"

## Dash Interruption
*A parenthetical aside set off by dashes -- it breaks the sentence's flow to insert something the reader must reckon with.*

---

### MS-12 | Pattern: Dash Interruption

**Source:** Virginia Woolf, *A Room of One's Own*, 1929

**Sentence:** "A woman must have money and a room of her own if she is to write fiction -- and that, as you will see, leaves the great problem of the true nature of woman and the true nature of fiction unsolved."

**Structural Diagram:** "[MAIN CLAUSE with conditional] -- and [dash-interrupted qualifier that reframes the whole argument as insufficient]."

**Why This Works:** The dash interruption undercuts the very thesis Woolf has just stated. She offers her famous claim, then immediately admits its inadequacy. The dash performs intellectual honesty -- it shows a mind that refuses to let its own argument go unexamined.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence that states a thesis and then immediately qualifies or complicates it with a dash interruption: *[Clear thesis statement] -- and that, as [acknowledgment], leaves [the larger problem] unsolved.*"

---

### MS-13 | Pattern: Dash Interruption

**Source:** Frederick Douglass, *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass*, 1845

**Sentence:** "I was now about twelve years old, and the thought of being a slave for life began to bear heavily upon my heart."

**Structural Diagram:** "[Simple time marker], and [MAIN CLAUSE: emotional revelation]."

*Note: The following sentence from Douglass better illustrates the dash interruption.*

**Sentence:** "In a very short time after I went to live at Baltimore, my old master's youngest son Richard died; and in about three years and six months after his death, my old master, Captain Anthony, died, leaving only his son, Andrew, and daughter, Lucretia, to share his estate."

**Structural Diagram:** "[Temporal phrase], [CLAUSE 1 -- event]; and [temporal phrase], [CLAUSE 2], [participial phrase] + [appositives embedded with commas functioning as dashes, naming the heirs]."

**Why This Works:** The interrupting appositives -- "Captain Anthony" renaming "my old master," and "Andrew" and "Lucretia" naming the heirs -- force the reader to see these people as both specific individuals and as figures in a system of property. The interruptions humanize what the sentence's content dehumanizes.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence that narrates a sequence of events while using embedded names or titles as dash-like interruptions: *In [time frame], [event 1]; and in [time frame], [person] -- [title or renaming] -- [event 2], leaving [list with appositives].*"

## Inverted Syntax
*Subject-verb order reversed for emphasis -- what comes first is what matters most.*

---

### MS-14 | Pattern: Inverted Syntax

**Source:** W.E.B. Du Bois, *The Souls of Black Folk* ("Of Our Spiritual Strivings"), 1903

**Sentence:** "Between me and the other world there is ever an unasked question."

**Structural Diagram:** "[Prepositional phrase fronted for emphasis] there is [SUBJECT delayed: an unasked question]."

**Why This Works:** By inverting the natural order ("An unasked question exists between me and the other world"), Du Bois places the spatial and social divide -- "between me and the other world" -- before the reader even knows what occupies that divide. The inversion makes the gap itself the first thing we feel.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence about a divide or barrier using inverted syntax: *Between [X] and [Y] there is [something unspoken or unresolved].*"

---

### MS-15 | Pattern: Inverted Syntax

**Source:** Thomas Paine, *Common Sense*, 1776

**Sentence:** "In the following pages I offer nothing more than simple facts, plain arguments, and common sense."

**Structural Diagram:** "[Prepositional phrase fronted] [SUBJECT] [VERB] + [series of deliberately understated objects]."

**Why This Works:** The inversion places "In the following pages" first, framing what follows as a spatial and rhetorical offering. By leading with the location rather than the speaker, Paine deflects attention from himself and onto the text -- a move of strategic humility that makes his "simple facts" sound all the more trustworthy.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence that introduces your argument by inverting syntax and understating your claims: *In [this essay / the following analysis] I offer nothing more than [modest term], [modest term], and [modest term].*"

## Compound-Complex with Subordination
*At least two independent clauses and one dependent clause -- for arguments that need to hold multiple ideas in relation.*

---

### MS-16 | Pattern: Compound-Complex with Subordination

**Source:** Mary Wollstonecraft, *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*, 1792

**Sentence:** "Women are told from their infancy, and taught by the example of their mothers, that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning, softness of temper, outward obedience, and a scrupulous attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man."

**Structural Diagram:** "[SUBJECT] are [VERB 1 -- passive] + [VERB 2 -- passive with prepositional phrase], that [noun clause functioning as object: a list of 'virtues' with embedded appositive 'justly termed cunning'] will [obtain the promised reward]."

**Why This Works:** The compound passive construction ("are told... and taught") shows women as objects of instruction, not agents. The long noun clause -- with its devastating parenthetical "justly termed cunning" -- catalogues the false virtues with such precision that the list itself becomes the critique. The sentence performs the very process it describes: it buries the real lesson inside layers of apparent propriety.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a sentence about what a group is taught to value, revealing the hidden cost: *[Group] are told from [early on], and taught by [source], that [list of expected behaviors], will obtain for them [the promised reward].*"

---

### MS-17 | Pattern: Compound-Complex with Subordination

**Source:** Jonathan Swift, *A Modest Proposal*, 1729

**Sentence:** "I have been assured by a very knowing American of my acquaintance in London, that a young healthy child well nursed is at a year old a most delicious, nourishing, and wholesome food, whether stewed, roasted, baked, or boiled; and I make no doubt that it will equally serve in a fricassee or a ragout."

**Structural Diagram:** "[INDEPENDENT CLAUSE 1: I have been assured] + [by phrase with embedded details establishing false credibility] + [dependent noun clause: that a child is food] + [subordinate concessive phrase: whether...]; and [INDEPENDENT CLAUSE 2: I make no doubt] + [dependent noun clause extending the horror]."

**Why This Works:** The compound-complex structure allows Swift to sustain his deadpan persona across multiple clauses -- each new grammatical unit adds another layer of horror delivered in the voice of calm reason. The subordination ("whether stewed, roasted, baked, or boiled") mimics the structure of a cookbook, making the satire unbearable precisely because the syntax is so polite.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a compound-complex sentence that uses an overly reasonable tone to expose something unreasonable: *I have been assured by [credible-sounding source] that [outrageous claim], whether [option A], [option B], or [option C]; and I make no doubt that it will equally serve [in yet another context].*"

## Short Declarative for Impact
*Under 10 words, following longer sentences -- the brevity hits like a hammer.*

---

### MS-18a | Pattern: Short Declarative for Impact

**Source:** Ralph Waldo Emerson, *Self-Reliance*, 1841

**Sentence (in context):** "A foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds, adored by little statesmen and philosophers and divines. With consistency a great soul has simply nothing to do."

**Structural Diagram:** "[Long sentence with trailing modifiers cataloguing the worshippers of consistency]. [SHORT DECLARATIVE that dismisses the entire concept]."

**Why This Works:** After the elaborate first sentence names all the people who worship consistency, the second sentence -- blunt, direct, final -- simply walks away from the argument. The short declarative does not refute; it refuses. That structural move *is* the self-reliance Emerson preaches.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a longer sentence cataloguing something people value, followed by a short declarative that dismisses or reframes it: *[Longer sentence about a common belief, with examples]. [Short, blunt sentence that overturns it].*"

---

### MS-18b | Pattern: Short Declarative for Impact

**Source:** Henry David Thoreau, *Civil Disobedience*, 1849

**Sentence (in context):** "How does it become a man to behave toward this American government to-day? I answer, that he cannot without disgrace be associated with it."

**Structural Diagram:** "[Rhetorical question -- long, formal, deliberate]. [SHORT ANSWER -- declarative, absolute, cutting]."

**Why This Works:** The rhetorical question is measured and philosophical; the answer is not. Thoreau's short declarative collapses the distance between question and condemnation. The contrast in length — a sprawling question met by a swift verdict — signals that no further deliberation is needed. The impact comes from the structural compression, not raw word count.

**Student Imitation Prompt:** "Write a formal rhetorical question about a current issue, followed by a short, decisive answer: *How does it become [a person] to [act] toward [institution or situation]? I answer that [short, firm declaration].*"
