### HN-01: PO-026 Yeats, "Easter 1916"

On April 24, 1916, members of the Irish Republican Brotherhood and the Irish Citizen Army seized the General Post Office and other key buildings in Dublin, declaring an independent Irish Republic. The British military suppressed the uprising within six days, and in the weeks that followed, sixteen of the rebellion's leaders were court-martialed and executed by firing squad — a severity that shifted Irish public opinion dramatically toward the nationalist cause. Yeats was personally acquainted with several of the executed leaders, including Patrick Pearse and Thomas MacDonagh.

### HN-02: ES-005 Swift, "A Modest Proposal" (1729)

This essay is a work of satire: Swift does not literally propose that the Irish poor sell their children as food. He adopts the voice of a coldly rational economic projector to expose and condemn the way English colonial policy had dehumanized Ireland's impoverished population.

---

### HN-03: ES-027 Macaulay, "Minute on Indian Education" (1835)

Thomas Babington Macaulay was a British historian, politician, and colonial official who served on the Governor-General's Supreme Council in India. In his 1835 "Minute on Indian Education," he argued that the British government should fund English-language instruction rather than traditional education conducted in Sanskrit and Arabic, claiming that European learning was superior in every respect. The Minute was adopted as policy, and it became the foundational document of British colonial education in India for the next century.

## SECTION B: COMPOSITE WORKER NARRATIVE (SYN-05-G)

---

**COMPOSITE NARRATIVE: Based on published accounts from Department of Labor workforce development reports and journalistic coverage of automation displacement. This is an illustrative composite, not a specific individual's account.**

I ran a quality-control station on the bumper-assembly line at a mid-sized auto-parts plant in northeastern Ohio for fourteen years. My job was to inspect painted bumper fascias as they came off the line — checking for orange peel in the clear coat, sanding marks that hadn't buffed out, masking-tape residue along the trim edges. I could spot a fisheye in the paint from six feet away. I made twenty-three dollars an hour plus benefits, which in our area was enough to cover the mortgage, keep two kids in the school-sports boosters, and put a little into a 401(k) every paycheck.

In 2021 the company installed a vision-inspection system — a set of high-resolution cameras and machine-learning software that could photograph every square inch of a fascia in the time it took me to check one quarter-panel. They kept a few of us on for the first six months to verify the system's calls, but by October they posted the WARN Act notice. Eighty-seven of us were laid off in a single round.

The first three months were worse than I expected. Unemployment covered about forty percent of my previous take-home. My wife picked up extra shifts at the hospital where she works as a phlebotomist, and we stopped eating out entirely. What I hadn't anticipated was how disorienting it would feel to have nowhere to be at six in the morning. I'd spent my entire adult life on a shift schedule, and suddenly the days had no edges.

In February I enrolled in a workforce retraining program through the local community college, funded partly through the Trade Adjustment Assistance program and partly through a state displaced-worker grant. I started in an industrial-maintenance technology track — twelve months of coursework covering programmable logic controllers, hydraulic and pneumatic systems, electrical troubleshooting, and, ironically, the calibration and upkeep of automated inspection equipment. The classes met four days a week, eight to two-thirty, which at least gave the days some shape again.

I won't pretend the program was easy. I was forty-one, sitting in a classroom with twenty-two-year-olds who had grown up writing code. The electrical-theory coursework was the hardest thing I'd done academically. I failed my first PLC programming midterm and had to go to the tutoring lab three nights a week for a month to catch up. There were moments — usually around eleven at night, with a textbook open and the mortgage statement on the counter — when I was not sure I would finish.

But I did finish. I earned an industrial-maintenance technology certificate in March of the following year and passed the Certified Maintenance and Reliability Technician exam on my second attempt. I now work as a maintenance technician at a packaging plant about thirty minutes from my old job. The pay is close to what I made before — not quite equal yet, but the trajectory is upward, and the job is more varied. I troubleshoot conveyor systems, reprogram PLCs when the line changes over, and maintain the very kind of automated inspection equipment that replaced me.

I don't tell this story as a simple success. The transition cost my family a year of lost income, most of our savings, and a level of stress that is hard to quantify. The retraining worked for me, but I watched classmates drop out because they couldn't afford the months without a real paycheck, or because the childcare math didn't add up, or because the forty-minute commute to the college was one obligation too many. A program is only as good as the support structure around it. I got through because my wife had a job with health insurance and because my mother-in-law could watch the kids on class nights. Not everyone has that.

## SECTION C: STRAW MAN DETECTION ITEMS (4 items for C2)

---

### SM-01 | Genuine

**Opening:** "While Du Bois advocated for broad liberal-arts education and full political rights for African Americans, Booker T. Washington argued that, given the violent realities of the post-Reconstruction South, economic self-sufficiency through industrial training offered the most practical and immediate path toward racial progress. Washington believed that once Black communities demonstrated economic value and moral character, civil rights would follow."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Genuine
- **Explanation:** This accurately represents Washington's strategic reasoning. It acknowledges the historical context (post-Reconstruction violence) that shaped his position and correctly identifies his belief in a sequential path — economic stability first, political rights following. It does not reduce his position to mere capitulation.

---

### SM-02 | Straw Man

**Opening:** "Booker T. Washington essentially told Black Americans to accept segregation and second-class citizenship without complaint. His Atlanta Compromise speech was a promise to white Southerners that African Americans would stay in their place and never demand equality, as long as they were allowed to work menial jobs."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Straw Man
- **Explanation:** This distorts Washington's position in several ways. It characterizes his strategy as passive acceptance ("without complaint") when Washington framed it as active economic self-building. It reduces industrial education to "menial jobs," ignoring that Washington saw skilled trades as a route to ownership and independence. And it claims he promised Black Americans would "never demand equality," when Washington's actual argument was about sequencing — economic power first — not permanent renunciation of civil rights.

---

### SM-03 | Genuine

**Opening:** "Du Bois contended that Washington's program, by conceding political rights and accepting social segregation in exchange for economic opportunity, actually surrendered the very tools African Americans would need to protect any economic gains they achieved. In 'The Talented Tenth,' Du Bois argued that a classically educated Black leadership class was necessary to guide the race's advancement and to challenge the legal and political structures of Jim Crow."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Genuine
- **Explanation:** This is a fair representation of Du Bois's critique. It accurately identifies his concern that without political power, economic gains would remain vulnerable. It correctly describes the Talented Tenth concept as a leadership argument and connects it to the broader goal of challenging systemic oppression, rather than caricaturing Du Bois as elitist or impractical.

---

### SM-04 | Straw Man

**Opening:** "W. E. B. Du Bois was an elitist academic who looked down on ordinary Black working people and thought only the top ten percent of the race mattered. His 'Talented Tenth' idea was basically a plan to help a few privileged Black intellectuals get into white universities while abandoning the vast majority of African Americans to poverty and ignorance."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Straw Man
- **Explanation:** This misrepresents Du Bois's argument by framing the Talented Tenth as a self-serving plan for elites rather than a leadership model meant to uplift the entire race. Du Bois explicitly argued that the educated ten percent had a *responsibility* to the other ninety percent. The straw man also falsely implies Du Bois was indifferent to the broader Black population, when his actual argument was that broadly educated leaders were the mechanism through which the whole community would advance.

## SECTION D: SERIAL VS. SYNTHESIS DETECTION ITEMS (4 items for C2)

---

### SD-01 | Serial

**Paragraph:** "Jean Twenge's research shows that teens who spend more than two hours a day on social media are significantly more likely to report symptoms of depression and anxiety. She uses large datasets to argue that the rise of smartphones correlates with a sharp increase in teen mental health problems starting around 2012. Meanwhile, danah boyd argues that social media is not inherently harmful and that teens use it to navigate normal developmental challenges like identity formation and peer relationships. Boyd points out that moral panics about new technology are nothing new and that adults often misunderstand how young people use digital platforms. Andrew Przybylski's study found that moderate screen time is actually associated with slightly higher well-being than no screen time at all. His research suggests that the relationship between technology use and mental health follows a curvilinear pattern rather than a simple negative one."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Serial
- **Explanation:** The paragraph is organized by source, not by theme. It moves from Twenge's full argument, to boyd's full argument, to Przybylski's full argument, connected only by transition words ("meanwhile," "Andrew Przybylski's study"). There is no overarching claim that the sources collectively support, and no moment where two sources are brought into direct conversation with each other.
- **If Serial, Model Revision:** "Restructure around the question: *How strong is the evidence that social media harms teen mental health?* Lead with the claim that the relationship is more complex than popular accounts suggest. Use Przybylski's curvilinear finding and boyd's developmental argument together to complicate Twenge's correlation data. The paragraph's argument becomes: while large-scale correlations exist (Twenge), they may obscure a more nuanced reality in which moderate use is benign (Przybylski) and social functions matter as much as screen time (boyd)."

---

### SD-02 | Synthesis

**Paragraph:** "The debate over social media's effects on adolescents often stalls on a deceptively simple question: does more screen time cause more depression? The large-scale correlational data that Twenge cites — linking rising smartphone adoption after 2012 to rising rates of teen anxiety — would seem to answer yes, but Przybylski's research complicates that conclusion by showing that the relationship between screen time and well-being is curvilinear: moderate users actually report slightly better outcomes than abstainers. This suggests that the quantity of use matters less than its context and quality, a point danah boyd reinforces when she argues that teens often use social media to perform the same developmental work — identity exploration, peer bonding — that previous generations conducted face-to-face. Taken together, these findings imply that effective policy cannot simply target hours of use but must account for how and why adolescents engage with digital platforms."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Synthesis
- **Explanation:** The paragraph is organized around an argumentative claim (that the screen-time-causes-harm narrative is too simple), not around individual sources. Sources are introduced in service of that argument: Twenge provides the data to be complicated, Przybylski supplies the complication, and boyd extends the point in a new direction. The final sentence synthesizes all three into a policy implication that none of the sources states alone.
- **If Serial, Model Revision:** N/A

---

### SD-03 | Serial

**Paragraph:** "Social media affects teen mental health in several ways that researchers have studied. Twenge found that girls are especially vulnerable to the negative effects of social media, with rates of depression among teen girls rising dramatically since 2012. She argues that social media creates constant opportunities for social comparison and cyberbullying. On the other hand, boyd conducted ethnographic research with teens and found that most of them see social media as a normal part of their social lives rather than something harmful. Boyd emphasizes that teens are more sophisticated in their use of technology than adults give them credit for. Przybylski's work adds another perspective by suggesting that small amounts of screen time can actually be beneficial, and that only excessive use becomes problematic. His 'Goldilocks hypothesis' proposes that there is a moderate amount of screen time that is 'just right.'"

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Serial
- **Explanation:** Despite its opening sentence gesturing toward a theme ("several ways"), the paragraph immediately falls into a source-by-source pattern: Twenge (two sentences), then boyd (two sentences), then Przybylski (two sentences). Each researcher's findings are reported in a self-contained block. The phrase "on the other hand" and "adds another perspective" signal transitions between sources rather than between ideas.
- **If Serial, Model Revision:** "Identify the implicit theme: *Who is most at risk, and under what conditions?* Open with the claim that vulnerability to social media's effects depends on both the user and the usage pattern. Interweave Twenge's finding about girls' specific vulnerability with Przybylski's Goldilocks hypothesis to argue that both population and dosage matter. Then bring in boyd's ethnographic evidence to add a third variable — the user's own agency and purpose. The result is a paragraph organized around the question of *conditions* rather than around *researchers*."

---

### SD-04 | Synthesis

**Paragraph:** "One reason the social media debate remains unresolved is that the strongest claims on each side rely on fundamentally different kinds of evidence. Twenge's alarming correlations draw on nationally representative survey data — large samples, clear trend lines — but they cannot establish causation, and they measure screen time as a single undifferentiated variable. Boyd's ethnographic work provides the granularity that Twenge's data lacks, revealing that a teen scrolling through Instagram to maintain a friendship is engaged in a qualitatively different activity than a teen doomscrolling at 2 a.m., but ethnography trades statistical power for depth. Przybylski attempts to bridge this gap with preregistered studies and transparent methodology, yet even his curvilinear findings explain only a small percentage of the variance in well-being. The implication is not that any single method fails, but that the phenomenon itself — millions of adolescents using dozens of platforms for hundreds of different purposes — may resist the kind of clean, unitary answer that policymakers and parents want."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Synthesis
- **Explanation:** The paragraph's organizing principle is methodological: it argues that the debate is unresolved because each source uses a different type of evidence with different strengths and limitations. Sources are not summarized sequentially; they are placed in conversation around the question of *why we don't have a definitive answer*. Each source is discussed in terms of what it can and cannot show, and the concluding sentence draws a synthetic inference about the nature of the problem itself.
- **If Serial, Model Revision:** N/A

## SECTION F: SURFACE VS. SUBSTANTIVE REVISION EXAMPLES (10 pairs for C4)

---

### RV-01 | Surface

**Before:** "Paine uses metaphors to make his argument more persuasive and convincing to the reader."
**After:** "Paine employs metaphors to render his argument more compelling for the audience."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Surface
- **Explanation:** Every noun-for-noun and adjective-for-adjective swap ("uses/employs," "persuasive and convincing/compelling," "reader/audience") changes diction but not the analytical claim. The sentence still says the same thing: Paine uses metaphors for persuasion. No new idea has been introduced.

---

### RV-02 | Substantive

**Before:** "Paine uses metaphors to make his argument more persuasive."
**After:** "Paine's metaphors reframe a political dispute as a moral crisis, making neutrality feel like complicity."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Substantive
- **Explanation:** The revision transforms a generic observation (metaphors = persuasion) into a specific analytical claim about *how* the metaphors function (reframing politics as morality) and *what effect* that produces (neutrality becomes complicity). The idea itself has changed, not just the words.

---

### RV-03 | Surface

**Before:** "Douglass's speach about the Fourth of July is a really powerfull critique of American slavery and hypocracy."
**After:** "Douglass's speech about the Fourth of July is a powerful critique of American slavery and hypocrisy."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Surface
- **Explanation:** The revision corrects three spelling errors ("speach/speech," "powerfull/powerful," "hypocracy/hypocrisy") and removes the informal intensifier "really." The analytical claim — that the speech is a powerful critique of slavery and hypocrisy — is unchanged.
