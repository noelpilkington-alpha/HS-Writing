# A1 L03: Supporting with Evidence and Reasoning — Content Items

---

## Key Concept: Evidence vs. Reasoning

**Evidence** = Facts, examples, data, personal experience — the WHAT.
**Reasoning** = Logical connections that explain WHY the evidence supports the claim — the SO WHAT.

- Evidence without reasoning = a list of facts (reader thinks: "So?")
- Reasoning without evidence = unsupported opinion (reader thinks: "Prove it.")
- Both together = a convincing argument

---

## Coping Model: Building a Support Paragraph

Show a step-by-step construction of one supporting paragraph. Topic: screen time and sleep.

**Claim:** "Schools should educate students about the effects of screen time on sleep quality."

**Step 1 — Evidence only (weak):**

"Studies show that blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production. Teenagers who use phones before bed report lower sleep quality. The average teen spends over three hours on screens after 8 PM."

**Annotation:** This is a list of facts. The reader knows WHAT the evidence says but not WHY it matters for the claim. There's no reasoning connecting the dots.

**Step 2 — Add reasoning (strong):**

"Studies show that blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production, which means teenagers who scroll through social media before bed are actively undermining their body's ability to fall asleep. This matters because adolescent brain development depends on consistent, high-quality sleep — so the harm isn't just feeling tired the next morning, but potentially affecting cognitive growth during a critical developmental window. If students understood this connection between their nightly habits and their long-term health, they would have a concrete reason to change their behavior, which is exactly why schools should build this into health education."

**Annotation:** Now each piece of evidence is followed by reasoning that explains WHY it supports the claim. The word "because" is doing heavy lifting — it signals the move from evidence to reasoning.

**Signal Words for Reasoning:** because, this matters because, which means, therefore, this suggests, the significance is, as a result, consequently

## Practice Item: Claim + Evidence Generation (1 worked example + 3 practice items)

### Worked Example: ER-00

**Claim:** "High schools should require all students to take a personal finance course before graduation."

**Evidence 1:** Many young adults report feeling unprepared to manage budgets, student loans, and credit cards after high school.
**Reasoning 1:** This gap between what students know and what adult life demands shows that the current curriculum fails to equip them with essential survival skills — a failure that a mandatory finance course would directly address.

**Evidence 2:** States that have implemented financial literacy requirements have seen measurable improvements in young adults' credit scores and savings rates.
**Reasoning 2:** These outcomes demonstrate that financial education doesn't just teach abstract concepts — it produces tangible behavioral change, making the case that such courses are not merely informative but genuinely transformative.

**Evidence 3:** Students from lower-income families are disproportionately affected by financial illiteracy because they are less likely to learn money management at home.
**Reasoning 3:** This means a required finance course wouldn't just benefit all students — it would serve as an equalizer, giving students who lack family resources the same foundational knowledge as their wealthier peers.

### ER-01

**Claim:** "Standardized school start times of 8:30 AM or later should be adopted nationwide."

**Your task:** Generate 3 pieces of evidence from your knowledge. For each, write one sentence of reasoning explaining WHY it supports the claim.

**Model Evidence + Reasoning:**

**Evidence 1:** Research on adolescent biology shows that teenagers' circadian rhythms shift during puberty, making it physically difficult for most teens to fall asleep before 11 PM.
**Reasoning 1:** Because their bodies are biologically wired for a later sleep cycle, forcing students into classrooms at 7:30 AM means they are trying to learn during what is effectively the middle of their night — a policy that works against science rather than with it.

**Evidence 2:** Schools that have shifted to later start times have reported noticeable drops in tardiness, absenteeism, and students falling asleep in class.
**Reasoning 2:** These improvements suggest that early start times were not a discipline problem but a sleep problem, which means changing the schedule addresses the root cause instead of punishing students for something beyond their control.

**Evidence 3:** Many students who wake up before 6 AM for school skip breakfast or rely on caffeine and energy drinks to stay alert through their morning classes.
**Reasoning 3:** This pattern shows that early start times create a chain reaction of unhealthy habits — poor nutrition, stimulant dependence, and diminished focus — all of which a later start time could interrupt before they become ingrained.

### ER-02

**Claim:** "Schools should allow students to use AI tools like ChatGPT for homework, with proper guidelines."

**Your task:** Generate 3 pieces of evidence from your knowledge. For each, write one sentence of reasoning explaining WHY it supports the claim.

**Model Evidence + Reasoning:**

**Evidence 1:** AI tools are already widely used in workplaces across industries including medicine, law, engineering, and journalism, and that trend is accelerating.
**Reasoning 1:** Because students will be expected to work alongside AI in their future careers, banning these tools in school creates a gap between what education teaches and what the real world requires — making guided classroom use a matter of preparation, not laziness.

**Evidence 2:** When students use AI without any training, they tend to copy outputs word-for-word without evaluating whether the information is accurate or well-argued.
**Reasoning 2:** This is actually an argument for structured use rather than a ban, because teaching students how to critically assess AI-generated content builds a skill they desperately need — the ability to distinguish reliable information from convincing-sounding nonsense.

**Evidence 3:** Calculators were once considered a threat to math education, yet schools eventually adopted them with clear rules about when and how they could be used.
**Reasoning 3:** This historical parallel suggests that the solution to a powerful new tool is not prohibition but thoughtful integration, and that schools that establish AI guidelines now will be ahead of those that resist until they are forced to catch up.

### ER-03

**Claim:** "Competitive sports should not be the primary focus of school athletic programs — participation-based fitness programs would benefit more students."

**Your task:** Generate 3 pieces of evidence from your knowledge. For each, write one sentence of reasoning explaining WHY it supports the claim.

**Model Evidence + Reasoning:**

**Evidence 1:** At most high schools, the majority of athletic funding, facilities, and coaching staff are devoted to varsity teams that serve a small fraction of the student body.
**Reasoning 1:** This means the students who are already the most physically active receive the most resources, while the larger population of students who need fitness support the most are effectively left out — an allocation that prioritizes spectacle over student health.

**Evidence 2:** Many students stop participating in physical activity altogether after they are cut from a team or decide they are "not athletic enough" to compete.
**Reasoning 2:** This dropout effect reveals that competitive-only programs can actually discourage physical activity by framing fitness as something reserved for the talented few, when the goal of a school program should be to build lifelong health habits in every student.

**Evidence 3:** Participation-based programs like intramural leagues, group fitness classes, and outdoor recreation clubs allow students of all skill levels to be active without the pressure of tryouts or rankings.
**Reasoning 3:** Because these programs remove the barrier of selection, they reach the students who benefit most from regular exercise — those who would never try out for a varsity team but still need movement, stress relief, and social connection that physical activity provides.
