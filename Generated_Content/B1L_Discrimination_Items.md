# B1L Discrimination Items -- Course B1L: AP Language Essay Writing

## 1. FEATURE-SPOTTING VS. ANALYSIS SORT (5 items for L04)

Each item presents a sentence about a rhetorical passage. Students classify as "Feature-Spotting" (merely names the device) or "Analysis" (explains function and effect via S-F-E—Strategy, Function, Effect).

---

### Item 1: Douglass "Fourth of July"

**Passage Context:** Frederick Douglass's 1852 speech "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" addresses an abolitionist audience on Independence Day, speaking as a formerly enslaved person.

**Sentence:** "Douglass uses irony in his speech."

**Classification:** Feature-Spotting

**Explanation:** This sentence merely names a rhetorical device (irony) without explaining how Douglass deploys it, what rhetorical work it accomplishes, or what effect it creates for the audience. It's an observation, not an analysis. Feature-spotting tells *what* the author does; analysis tells *what* the author does, *how* it works, and *why* it matters to the audience. This sentence stops at identification.

**What's Missing:** The sentence needs to specify (1) the MOMENT of irony (Strategy), (2) what rhetorical FUNCTION that irony serves in context (Function), and (3) what cognitive or emotional EFFECT it produces in the audience (Effect). Currently, it provides none of these elements.

**Signal of Feature-Spotting:** The verb "uses" + device name is the classic flag. The sentence has no subordinate clause explaining function, no result clause showing effect, no causal connection between strategy and audience response.

**Revised to Analysis:** "Douglass's ironic address to his audience as 'fellow citizens' while speaking as a formerly enslaved person in a slaveholding nation (S) exposes the hypocrisy at the heart of American independence celebrations (F), forcing the audience to confront the unbridgeable gap between their professed ideals of liberty and their complicity in the institution of slavery (E)."

**Why the Revision Works:** It identifies the specific MOMENT (addressing them as "fellow citizens"), names what that moment DOES rhetorically (exposes hypocrisy), and articulates the resulting cognitive EFFECT on the audience (forces confrontation with contradiction). The strategy is linked causally to the effect.

---

### Item 2: Patrick Henry "Give Me Liberty"

**Passage Context:** Patrick Henry's 1775 speech urged Virginia delegates to support armed resistance against British rule. His audience consisted of colonial leaders weighing the risks of revolution.

**Sentence:** "Henry's barrage of rhetorical questions in the speech's climactic section serves to eliminate alternative courses of action one by one, systematically foreclosing the possibility of compromise and leaving his audience with only the stark choice he presents: liberty or death."

**Classification:** Analysis

**Explanation:** This sentence achieves full S-F-E analysis. It identifies the STRATEGY (barrage of rhetorical questions in the climactic section), names the FUNCTION (eliminate alternatives, foreclose compromise), and articulates the resulting EFFECT on the audience (leaves them with only the binary choice Henry wants). The sentence explains *how* the strategy works (systematically) and *what it accomplishes* in the audience's decision-making process.

**Signal of Analysis:** The structure "Strategy + serves to + Function + resulting Effect" is a classic analysis move. The causal connectors ("serves to," "leaving") create the chain from author's choice to audience response. The sentence doesn't just name what Henry does—it explains the rhetorical mechanism and consequence.

**Why This Is Not Feature-Spotting:** Unlike "Henry uses rhetorical questions," this sentence embeds the device name within a complete explanation of its rhetorical work. The focus is not on identification but on function and effect. The device is a means to an end, not the end itself.

---

### Item 3: Ida B. Wells "Lynch Law"

**Passage Context:** Ida B. Wells's 1893 speech "Lynch Law in All Its Phases" documented the epidemic of lynching in the American South and challenged justifications for racial violence. Her audience included Northern white reformers.

**Sentence:** "Wells employs evidence accumulation throughout the speech."

**Classification:** Feature-Spotting

**Explanation:** This sentence names a rhetorical approach (evidence accumulation) but provides no analysis of how Wells uses that evidence, what rhetorical function it serves, or what effect it creates. It's a label, not an argument. The reader learns *that* Wells uses evidence but not *why* or *how* it matters rhetorically. The sentence stops at the level of description.

**What's Missing:** The sentence needs to explain (1) the TYPE or PATTERN of evidence Wells accumulates (Strategy detail), (2) the rhetorical FUNCTION that accumulation serves (e.g., to refute a claim, establish credibility, build moral urgency), and (3) the EFFECT on the audience's beliefs, emotions, or willingness to act (Effect). None of these elements appear.

**Signal of Feature-Spotting:** "Employs" + abstract strategy name + no subordinate explanation. The sentence has no causal structure, no explanation of mechanism, no audience-focused consequence.

**Revised to Analysis:** "Wells's systematic accumulation of documented evidence—names, dates, locations, and newspaper accounts of lynchings—throughout the speech (S) refutes the prevailing justification that lynching punished actual crimes (F), forcing her Northern white audience to confront the reality that lynching functioned as racial terrorism designed to maintain white supremacy rather than as vigilante justice (E)."

**Why the Revision Works:** It specifies the NATURE of the evidence (documented, systematic, specific details), names what that evidence DOES (refutes a justification), and articulates the cognitive EFFECT (forces recognition of lynching's true function). The analysis shows how the strategy changes audience understanding.

### Item 4: Emma Goldman "Woman's Emancipation"

**Passage Context:** Emma Goldman's 1906 essay "The Tragedy of Woman's Emancipation" argued that political and economic equality for women would be hollow without internal liberation from traditional gender roles. Her audience included feminists who focused primarily on suffrage.

**Sentence:** "Goldman's redefinition of 'emancipation' at the essay's opening from merely political and economic rights to include psychological and sexual liberation (S) reframes the entire debate about women's progress (F), challenging her feminist audience to recognize that winning the vote and property rights may leave women still imprisoned by internalized ideas about femininity, duty, and self-sacrifice (E)."

**Classification:** Analysis

**Explanation:** This sentence delivers complete S-F-E analysis. It identifies the specific STRATEGY (redefinition of "emancipation" and the direction of that redefinition), names the FUNCTION (reframes the debate), and articulates a detailed EFFECT that specifies what cognitive shift occurs in the audience (recognition that external rights don't guarantee internal freedom). The analysis shows *how* the redefinition does rhetorical work and *why* it matters for this specific audience.

**Signal of Analysis:** The sentence uses a three-part causal chain: Strategy "(S)" explicitly marked, Function "(F)" shows what the strategy does, Effect "(E)" explains the consequence for audience beliefs. The subordinate clause "challenging her audience to recognize that..." extends the effect explanation, showing precisely what shift in understanding occurs.

**Why This Is Not Feature-Spotting:** The sentence doesn't stop at "Goldman redefines 'emancipation.'" It explains *how* she redefines it (from what to what), *what* that redefinition accomplishes (reframes debate), and *why* it matters (forces recognition of limits of political-only feminism). The device serves a larger rhetorical purpose.

### Item 5: Frances Harper "We Are All Bound Up Together"

**Passage Context:** Frances E.W. Harper's 1866 speech "We Are All Bound Up Together" addressed a women's rights convention, arguing that Black women's liberation required both racial justice and gender equality. Her audience was primarily white suffragists.

**Sentence:** "Harper uses personal testimony in her speech."

**Classification:** Feature-Spotting

**Explanation:** This sentence identifies a rhetorical strategy (personal testimony) but offers no analysis of how Harper deploys that testimony, what function it serves in her argument, or what effect it produces on her audience. A reader learns *that* Harper includes personal experiences but not *why* that matters rhetorically or *what* it accomplishes. The sentence is pure identification.

**What's Missing:** The sentence needs (1) specificity about the CONTENT or PATTERN of the personal testimony (Strategy), (2) explanation of the rhetorical FUNCTION that testimony serves (e.g., establish credibility, create emotional connection, provide evidence), and (3) articulation of the EFFECT on the audience's understanding or beliefs (Effect). The current sentence provides none of these.

**Signal of Feature-Spotting:** The generic verb "uses" + device name + no causal or explanatory structure. No subordinate clause explaining function, no result clause showing effect, no connection to audience response. The sentence ends where analysis should begin.

**Revised to Analysis:** "Harper's use of personal testimony—recounting her experience of being ejected from a Philadelphia streetcar despite her status as a respectable, educated woman—(S) establishes her authority to speak on the intersection of racism and sexism while providing concrete evidence that legal freedom after the Civil War did not guarantee dignity or equal treatment (F), compelling her white suffragist audience to recognize that gender equality alone would leave Black women still subject to racial violence and exclusion (E)."

**Why the Revision Works:** It specifies the CONTENT of the testimony (streetcar incident, status as educated woman), names what that testimony DOES (establishes authority + provides evidence), and articulates the EFFECT (forces recognition of intersection between race and gender). The analysis shows how personal narrative functions as argument.

## 2. ANALYTICAL SENTENCE DRILL ITEMS (5 items for L04)

These items provide practice constructing analytical sentences that follow the S-F-E (Strategy-Function-Effect) framework. Each item gives passage context and identifies a rhetorical strategy, then provides a model analytical sentence broken down by component.

---

### Item 1: Douglass "Fourth of July" — Irony

**Passage Context:** Frederick Douglass delivered "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" on July 5, 1852, to an abolitionist audience in Rochester, New York. Speaking as a formerly enslaved person, Douglass was invited to give a speech celebrating Independence Day. Instead, he used the occasion to expose the hypocrisy of celebrating freedom in a nation that enslaved millions.

**Strategy Identified:** Irony (specifically, situational and verbal irony in addressing the audience as "fellow citizens")

**Model S-F-E Sentence:**

"Douglass's ironic address to his audience as 'fellow citizens' while speaking from his position as a formerly enslaved Black man (S) exposes the hypocrisy embedded in America's celebration of independence and equality (F), forcing the audience to confront the contradiction between their professed values of liberty and their nation's continuation of slavery (E)."

**Breakdown:**

- **Strategy (S):** "Douglass's ironic address to his audience as 'fellow citizens' while speaking from his position as a formerly enslaved Black man" — This identifies the specific rhetorical move (irony), locates it precisely (the address to audience), and provides essential context (his status as formerly enslaved, which makes the address ironic).

- **Function (F):** "exposes the hypocrisy embedded in America's celebration of independence and equality" — This names what the irony *does* rhetorically. It's not just present; it performs specific work. The function is exposure of contradiction.

- **Effect (E):** "forcing the audience to confront the contradiction between their professed values of liberty and their nation's continuation of slavery" — This articulates the consequence for the audience. What cognitive or emotional response does the function produce? The effect is forced confrontation with an uncomfortable truth.

**Why This Sentence Works:** It doesn't stop at "Douglass uses irony." It shows HOW he uses it (addressing them as fellow citizens from his position), WHAT that usage accomplishes (exposes hypocrisy), and WHY it matters (forces confrontation). The causal chain is clear: strategy → function → effect.

---

### Item 2: Patrick Henry "Give Me Liberty" — Rhetorical Questions

**Passage Context:** Patrick Henry delivered "Give Me Liberty, or Give Me Death!" to the Virginia Convention in March 1775. The delegates were divided on whether Virginia should join the armed resistance against Britain. Henry spoke late in the debate, after moderates had argued for continued negotiation. His rhetorical questions aimed to foreclose the possibility of compromise.

**Strategy Identified:** Rhetorical questions (a series of questions in the climactic section that demand the answer "none" or "impossible")

**Model S-F-E Sentence:**

"Henry's barrage of rhetorical questions in the speech's climax—'Is life so dear, or peace so sweet, as to be purchased at the price of chains and slavery?'—(S) systematically eliminates the possibility of compromise or continued negotiation (F), leaving his audience facing the stark binary choice he demands: surrender to tyranny or fight for liberty (E)."

**Breakdown:**

- **Strategy (S):** "Henry's barrage of rhetorical questions in the speech's climax—'Is life so dear, or peace so sweet, as to be purchased at the price of chains and slavery?'—" — This identifies the device (rhetorical questions), notes the pattern (barrage), locates it structurally (climax), and provides a representative example. The term "barrage" captures the overwhelming quantity and pace.

- **Function (F):** "systematically eliminates the possibility of compromise or continued negotiation" — This names the rhetorical work the questions perform. They don't invite answers; they foreclose options. The function is elimination of alternatives.

- **Effect (E):** "leaving his audience facing the stark binary choice he demands: surrender to tyranny or fight for liberty" — This articulates the consequence. After the questions eliminate middle ground, what position is the audience in? They face a forced choice. The effect is strategic narrowing of options.

**Why This Sentence Works:** It captures the QUANTITY of questions (barrage), explains their FUNCTION (eliminate alternatives), and shows the resulting EFFECT (binary choice). The analysis reveals how the questions manipulate the decision-making space available to the audience.

---

### Item 3: Ida B. Wells "Lynch Law" — Evidence Accumulation

**Passage Context:** Ida B. Wells's 1893 speech "Lynch Law in All Its Phases" was delivered to a predominantly white Northern audience unfamiliar with the scale and reality of lynching in the South. Wells had spent years documenting lynchings through newspaper accounts and personal investigation. The prevailing Northern narrative excused lynching as rough justice for Black men who assaulted white women. Wells's evidence aimed to dismantle that narrative.

**Strategy Identified:** Evidence accumulation (systematic compilation of documented cases)

**Model S-F-E Sentence:**

"Wells's systematic accumulation of documented evidence—specific names, dates, locations, and newspaper accounts of lynchings across multiple states, including cases where victims were accused of crimes ranging from 'being saucy' to testifying against white men—(S) refutes the dominant justification that lynching punished actual violent crimes (F), forcing her Northern white audience to confront the reality that lynching functioned as racial terrorism designed to maintain white supremacy through fear rather than as vigilante justice (E)."

**Breakdown:**

- **Strategy (S):** "Wells's systematic accumulation of documented evidence—specific names, dates, locations, and newspaper accounts of lynchings across multiple states, including cases where victims were accused of crimes ranging from 'being saucy' to testifying against white men—" — This identifies both the strategy (evidence accumulation) and its specific character (systematic, documented, varied). The examples of alleged "crimes" are crucial because they show the absurdity of the justification.

- **Function (F):** "refutes the dominant justification that lynching punished actual violent crimes" — This names what the evidence DOES. It's not just informative; it's argumentative. The function is refutation of a specific claim.

- **Effect (E):** "forcing her Northern white audience to confront the reality that lynching functioned as racial terrorism designed to maintain white supremacy through fear rather than as vigilante justice" — This articulates the cognitive shift Wells demands. The effect is recognition of lynching's true systemic function, replacing the comfortable narrative with a disturbing truth.

**Why This Sentence Works:** It doesn't just say "Wells uses evidence." It characterizes the NATURE of that evidence (documented, systematic, varied), names its FUNCTION (refutes justification), and shows the EFFECT (forces recognition of systemic terrorism). The analysis reveals how evidence shapes audience understanding.

---

### Item 4: Emma Goldman "Woman's Emancipation" — Redefinition

**Passage Context:** Emma Goldman's 1906 essay "The Tragedy of Woman's Emancipation" was written during the height of the suffrage movement, when mainstream feminists focused on winning political and economic rights for women. Goldman, an anarchist, argued that this narrow focus was insufficient. She opened the essay by redefining "emancipation" to include psychological and sexual liberation from traditional gender roles, not just legal equality.

**Strategy Identified:** Redefinition (reframing the key term "emancipation" to expand its meaning)

**Model S-F-E Sentence:**

"Goldman's opening redefinition of 'emancipation' from the narrow focus on political and economic rights (the vote, property ownership, professional access) to include psychological and sexual liberation from internalized ideas about women's 'proper' roles (S) reframes the entire feminist project as incomplete if it achieves only external legal changes (F), challenging her suffragist audience to recognize that women who win the vote but remain imprisoned by ideas of duty, self-sacrifice, and sexual purity have not achieved true freedom (E)."

**Breakdown:**

- **Strategy (S):** "Goldman's opening redefinition of 'emancipation' from the narrow focus on political and economic rights (the vote, property ownership, professional access) to include psychological and sexual liberation from internalized ideas about women's 'proper' roles" — This identifies the strategy (redefinition), specifies the direction (from what to what), and provides concrete examples of both the original and expanded definitions.

- **Function (F):** "reframes the entire feminist project as incomplete if it achieves only external legal changes" — This names the rhetorical work the redefinition performs. It's not just a clarification; it's a challenge to the movement's priorities. The function is reframing the entire debate.

- **Effect (E):** "challenging her suffragist audience to recognize that women who win the vote but remain imprisoned by ideas of duty, self-sacrifice, and sexual purity have not achieved true freedom" — This articulates what cognitive shift Goldman demands. The effect is recognition of an internal dimension of oppression that political rights cannot address.

**Why This Sentence Works:** It captures the DIRECTION of redefinition (from narrow to expansive), names its FUNCTION (reframes the project), and shows the EFFECT (forces recognition of limitations). The analysis reveals how redefinition changes the terms of debate.

---

### Item 5: Frances Harper "We Are All Bound Up Together" — Personal Testimony

**Passage Context:** Frances E.W. Harper addressed the Eleventh National Woman's Rights Convention in May 1866, just after the Civil War ended. The convention was debating whether to prioritize Black male suffrage (proposed in the 15th Amendment) or universal suffrage including women. Most attendees were white suffragists. Harper, a Black woman, used personal experiences to illustrate that Black women faced oppression on both racial and gender grounds simultaneously.

**Strategy Identified:** Personal testimony (narrative of personal experience used as evidence)

**Model S-F-E Sentence:**

"Harper's recounting of being forcibly ejected from a Philadelphia streetcar despite her status as a respectable, educated, middle-class woman (S) establishes her authority to speak on the intersection of racism and sexism while providing concrete, embodied evidence that legal emancipation did not guarantee Black people dignity or equal treatment (F), compelling her white suffragist audience to recognize that gender equality alone would leave Black women still vulnerable to racial violence, exclusion, and humiliation (E)."

**Breakdown:**

- **Strategy (S):** "Harper's recounting of being forcibly ejected from a Philadelphia streetcar despite her status as a respectable, educated, middle-class woman" — This identifies the strategy (personal testimony), specifies the content (streetcar ejection), and includes the crucial detail that her middle-class status offered no protection. This detail matters because it shows racism overrides class and respectability.

- **Function (F):** "establishes her authority to speak on the intersection of racism and sexism while providing concrete, embodied evidence that legal emancipation did not guarantee Black people dignity or equal treatment" — This names what the testimony DOES. It performs double work: ethos-building (establishes authority) and evidentiary (provides proof). The function is both personal and argumentative.

- **Effect (E):** "compelling her white suffragist audience to recognize that gender equality alone would leave Black women still vulnerable to racial violence, exclusion, and humiliation" — This articulates the consequence for audience understanding. The effect is recognition of what we now call intersectionality: that race and gender cannot be addressed separately.

**Why This Sentence Works:** It specifies the CONTENT of testimony (streetcar incident + status details), names its dual FUNCTION (authority + evidence), and shows the EFFECT (forces recognition of intersection). The analysis reveals how personal narrative functions as political argument.

## 3. AP THESIS SCORING ITEMS (6 items for L05)

These items provide practice scoring thesis statements according to the AP Language Rhetorical Analysis Row A rubric. Row A awards 0 or 1 point. To earn 1 point, a thesis must respond to the prompt with a defensible thesis that analyzes the writer's rhetorical choices. In practice, this means the thesis must: (1) name specific rhetorical strategies/choices, (2) state the writer's purpose or main argument, and (3) establish a line of reasoning connecting the strategies to the purpose.

---

### Item 1: Douglass "Fourth of July" — Score 0

**Thesis:** "Frederick Douglass's speech is very powerful and makes the audience think about slavery."

**Passage:** SP-001, Frederick Douglass, "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" (1852)

**Score:** 0

**Explanation:** This thesis earns 0 points because it fails to meet the Row A requirement of analyzing the writer's rhetorical choices. While it makes a claim about the speech's effect ("powerful," "makes the audience think"), it does not name any specific rhetorical strategies or choices Douglass employs. The thesis is too vague: it could apply to virtually any anti-slavery speech. It lacks specificity about HOW Douglass achieves power (what strategies?) and WHAT he makes the audience think (what purpose or argument?). There is no line of reasoning connecting specific choices to specific effects.

**What's Missing:**
- **No named strategies:** No mention of irony, juxtaposition, audience address, structural choices, diction, imagery, or any other specific rhetorical move.
- **No stated purpose:** "Makes the audience think about slavery" is not a purpose; it's a vague gesture toward effect. A purpose statement would specify WHAT Douglass wants the audience to conclude or do (e.g., "to expose the hypocrisy of celebrating freedom in a slaveholding nation").
- **No line of reasoning:** No connection between strategy and purpose. The thesis doesn't establish HOW Douglass creates power or thought.

**To Reach Score 1, This Thesis Needs:** Specific strategy names (e.g., "Through his use of irony, juxtaposition, and direct audience address..."), a clear purpose statement (e.g., "...Douglass exposes the hypocrisy of American independence celebrations..."), and a line of reasoning connecting them (e.g., "...by forcing his audience to confront the contradiction between their professed ideals and their complicity in slavery").

---

### Item 2: Patrick Henry "Give Me Liberty" — Score 1

**Thesis:** "Through his use of rhetorical questions, emotional appeals, and stark binary framing, Patrick Henry eliminates the possibility of compromise and compels his Virginia Convention audience to view armed resistance as the only honorable response to British tyranny."

**Passage:** SP-008, Patrick Henry, "Give Me Liberty, or Give Me Death!" (1775)

**Score:** 1

**Explanation:** This thesis earns 1 point because it meets all Row A requirements. It names three specific rhetorical strategies (rhetorical questions, emotional appeals, binary framing), states Henry's clear purpose (eliminate compromise, compel acceptance of armed resistance), and establishes a line of reasoning connecting strategies to purpose (through these strategies → elimination → compulsion). The thesis is defensible and provides a roadmap for analysis. It passes the specificity test: it could not apply to any other speech because it names Henry's particular strategies and particular goal.

**Why It Works:**
- **Named strategies:** "rhetorical questions, emotional appeals, and stark binary framing" — three specific, identifiable choices
- **Stated purpose:** "eliminates the possibility of compromise and compels his Virginia Convention audience to view armed resistance as the only honorable response to British tyranny" — clear argument about what Henry aims to accomplish
- **Line of reasoning:** "Through his use of [strategies]... Henry [accomplishes purpose]" — the structure establishes causality between choices and effect

**This Thesis Sets Up Analysis:** A body paragraph could analyze how the rhetorical questions eliminate middle ground, how emotional appeals make compromise seem cowardly, and how binary framing ("liberty or death") forces a stark choice. The thesis provides clear guideposts.

---

### Item 3: Ida B. Wells "Lynch Law" — Score 0

**Thesis:** "Ida B. Wells uses many persuasive techniques in her speech about lynching."

**Passage:** SP-013, Ida B. Wells, "Lynch Law in All Its Phases" (1893)

**Score:** 0

**Explanation:** This thesis earns 0 points because it fails to analyze specific rhetorical choices. "Many persuasive techniques" is maximally vague—it provides no information about WHAT techniques Wells uses. The thesis could apply to literally any persuasive speech. It also lacks a purpose statement: it doesn't say what Wells is trying to persuade her audience TO BELIEVE or DO. Finally, there's no line of reasoning because no specific strategies are connected to any specific purpose. The thesis is a placeholder, not an argument.

**What's Missing:**
- **No named strategies:** "Many persuasive techniques" names nothing. Which techniques? Evidence accumulation? Refutation of counterargument? Statistical data? Narrative testimony? Moral framing? The thesis must name specific choices.
- **No stated purpose:** "Her speech about lynching" describes the topic but not Wells's argument. What is she trying to accomplish? To refute the justification for lynching? To document its scale? To mobilize Northern white support for anti-lynching legislation? The thesis must state what Wells wants the audience to believe or do.
- **No line of reasoning:** Because no strategies or purpose are specified, no connection between them can exist.

**To Reach Score 1, This Thesis Needs:** Specific strategies (e.g., "Through her systematic accumulation of documented evidence and her refutation of the 'Black rapist' myth..."), a clear purpose (e.g., "...Wells exposes lynching as racial terrorism designed to maintain white supremacy..."), and a line of reasoning ("...thereby compelling her Northern white audience to reject the justifications for lynching and recognize it as a tool of systematic oppression").

---

### Item 4: Emma Goldman "Woman's Emancipation" — Score 1

**Thesis:** "By redefining 'emancipation' to include psychological and sexual freedom, challenging the suffrage movement's exclusive focus on political rights, and using examples of 'emancipated' women who remain imprisoned by traditional gender norms, Emma Goldman argues that true women's liberation requires internal transformation, not merely legal reform."

**Passage:** SP-017, Emma Goldman, "The Tragedy of Woman's Emancipation" (1906)

**Score:** 1

**Explanation:** This thesis earns 1 point because it satisfies all Row A criteria. It names three specific rhetorical strategies (redefinition, challenging the movement's focus, using examples of false emancipation), articulates Goldman's clear purpose/argument (true liberation requires internal transformation beyond legal reform), and establishes a line of reasoning (By using these strategies... Goldman argues...). The thesis is defensible, specific, and provides a clear roadmap for the essay. It could not apply to another text because it captures Goldman's particular argument and particular rhetorical moves.

**Why It Works:**
- **Named strategies:** "redefining 'emancipation,'" "challenging the suffrage movement's exclusive focus," "using examples of 'emancipated' women who remain imprisoned" — three identifiable rhetorical choices with sufficient specificity
- **Stated purpose:** "argues that true women's liberation requires internal transformation, not merely legal reform" — clear statement of Goldman's central argument
- **Line of reasoning:** "By [strategies]... Goldman argues [purpose]" — causal structure connecting choices to argument

**This Thesis Sets Up Analysis:** Body paragraphs could examine how the redefinition reframes the debate, how the challenge to suffragists creates tension with her audience, and how the examples demonstrate the limits of legal-only feminism. The thesis provides organizational structure.

---

### Item 5: Frances Harper "We Are All Bound Up Together" — Score 0

**Thesis:** "Frances Harper talks about both race and gender issues in her speech."

**Passage:** SP-024, Frances E.W. Harper, "We Are All Bound Up Together" (1866)

**Score:** 0

**Explanation:** This thesis earns 0 points because it describes the speech's content (topics: race and gender) without analyzing Harper's rhetorical choices. It does not name any strategies Harper uses to address these topics. It also lacks a purpose statement: it doesn't articulate what Harper is arguing about race and gender or what she wants her audience to conclude. There is no line of reasoning because no strategies are identified and no purpose is stated. The thesis is purely descriptive, not analytical.

**What's Missing:**
- **No named strategies:** "Talks about" is not a rhetorical strategy. How does Harper address race and gender? Through personal testimony? Through direct challenges to her white suffragist audience? Through examples? Through redefinition of women's rights to include racial justice? The thesis must name specific rhetorical moves.
- **No stated purpose:** What is Harper's argument about race and gender? That they are intertwined? That white suffragists must prioritize Black women's concerns? That gender equality without racial justice is insufficient? The thesis must state Harper's argument or purpose.
- **No line of reasoning:** With no strategies and no purpose, there can be no connection between them. The thesis provides no analytical framework.

**To Reach Score 1, This Thesis Needs:** Specific strategies (e.g., "Through her use of personal testimony, direct address to her white suffragist audience, and systematic challenges to the convention's prioritization of white women's suffrage over Black voting rights..."), a clear purpose (e.g., "...Harper argues that gender equality and racial justice are inseparable and that white feminists must recognize that Black women face oppression on both grounds simultaneously..."), and a line of reasoning connecting strategies to purpose ("...thereby compelling her audience to acknowledge that they cannot achieve women's liberation while accepting racial hierarchy").

### Item 6: Abraham Lincoln Second Inaugural Address — Score 1

**Thesis:** "In his Second Inaugural Address, Abraham Lincoln uses biblical allusion, parallel structure, and a tone of national reconciliation to reframe the Civil War as divine punishment for slavery and to call both North and South to move forward with 'malice toward none' and 'charity for all.'"

**Passage:** SP-003, Abraham Lincoln, Second Inaugural Address (1865)

**Score:** 1

**Explanation:** This thesis earns 1 point because it meets all Row A requirements. It names three specific rhetorical strategies (biblical allusion, parallel structure, reconciliatory tone), articulates Lincoln's dual purpose (reframe the war as divine punishment; call for reconciliation), and establishes a line of reasoning connecting strategies to purposes (Lincoln uses these strategies... to reframe... and to call). The thesis is defensible, specific to Lincoln's text, and provides clear organizational structure. The inclusion of quoted language ("malice toward none," "charity for all") strengthens specificity.

**Why It Works:**
- **Named strategies:** "biblical allusion, parallel structure, and a tone of national reconciliation" — three identifiable rhetorical choices with clear referents
- **Stated purpose:** "reframe the Civil War as divine punishment for slavery and to call both North and South to move forward with 'malice toward none' and 'charity for all'" — two related purposes (reframing + forward-looking appeal) that together capture Lincoln's complex rhetorical goal
- **Line of reasoning:** "Lincoln uses [strategies]... to reframe... and to call..." — causal structure establishing that the strategies accomplish the purposes

**This Thesis Sets Up Analysis:** Body paragraphs could analyze how biblical allusion creates moral authority for the reframing, how parallel structure equalizes North and South's guilt, and how the reconciliatory tone makes the forward-looking appeal possible. The dual-purpose structure allows for sophisticated organization.

## Summary: Discrimination Before Production

These three sets of items embody a core principle of the SRSD approach: **discrimination before production**. Students must learn to recognize the difference between feature-spotting and analysis (Item Set 1), see how S-F-E sentences are constructed (Item Set 2), and understand what distinguishes a scoreable thesis from an unscorable one (Item Set 3) before they are asked to produce their own analytical writing.

The progression moves from:
1. **Binary classification** (Feature-Spotting vs. Analysis Sort) — Can you tell the difference?
2. **Model study** (Analytical Sentence Drill) — What does good analysis look like?
3. **Scoring practice** (AP Thesis Scoring) — Can you evaluate thesis quality using the rubric?

Only after students demonstrate discrimination competence do lessons ask them to write their own analytical sentences and thesis statements. This sequence builds the internal "quality control" system students need to self-monitor and revise their own writing.
