### Prompt 1: Knowledge and Action (L09 Speed Run 2)

**Topic**: Whether understanding a problem is sufficient motivation to act, or whether knowledge and action are fundamentally separate.

#### Student-Facing Prompt

> "We are drowning in information, while starving for wisdom. The world henceforth will be run by synthesizers, people able to put together the right information at the right time, think critically about it, and make important choices wisely."
> — E.O. Wilson, *Consilience: The Unity of Knowledge*

Many thinkers argue that knowledge naturally leads to action—that once we understand a problem deeply, we are compelled to address it. Others contend that knowledge and action are fundamentally separate: understanding a crisis does not guarantee the will or capacity to respond. Still others suggest the relationship is more complex, varying by context, individual, or type of knowledge.

Write an essay that argues your position on the relationship between knowledge and action. Use appropriate evidence from your reading, experience, or observations to support your argument.

#### Designer Notes

**Core Claim**: This prompt asks students to examine whether understanding generates motivation and capacity for action, or whether these are separate human faculties.

**Complexity**: The prompt invites nuanced thinking about *types* of knowledge (intellectual vs. experiential), *types* of action (individual vs. collective, immediate vs. systemic), and mediating factors (willpower, resources, psychological distance, competing priorities). The Wilson quotation emphasizes *synthesis* and *wisdom* rather than mere information, suggesting that the nature of understanding matters.

**Three Positions**:
- **Agree**: Knowledge inherently motivates action. Examples: learning about climate change shifts behavior, medical diagnosis prompts treatment, understanding injustice drives activism. Ignorance, not apathy, is the primary barrier.
- **Disagree**: Knowledge and action are separate. Examples: widespread awareness of climate crisis without commensurate response, "information overload" leading to paralysis, knowing health risks while maintaining harmful habits. Action requires willpower, resources, systems.
- **Complicate**: The relationship depends on type of knowledge, proximity to problem, or individual capacity. Examples: experiential knowledge (witnessing injustice) vs. abstract knowledge (reading statistics), personal relevance vs. distant issues, structural barriers vs. individual agency.

**Evidence Possibilities**: Climate science and public response, public health campaigns, historical social movements, personal habit change, ethical awareness vs. ethical action, education and social mobility, technology and behavior change.

**Row C Opportunities**: Precise distinctions (intellectual vs. moral knowledge, awareness vs. understanding, motivation vs. capacity), recognition that Wilson's "synthesis" implies active processing rather than passive accumulation, acknowledgment of structural and psychological mediating factors, sophisticated treatment of how *depth* of understanding relates to likelihood of action.

### Prompt 2: Tradition and Progress (L12 Plan-to-Essay Bridge 2)

**Topic**: Whether tradition serves or hinders progress.

#### Student-Facing Prompt

> "Tradition is not the worship of ashes, but the preservation of fire."
> — Gustav Mahler

Societies constantly negotiate between preserving traditions and pursuing progress. Some argue that traditions provide essential continuity, wisdom, and identity—anchoring communities through change and preventing reckless innovation. Others contend that tradition is inherently conservative, resisting necessary evolution and privileging the past over the future. Still others suggest the relationship between tradition and progress is not oppositional but complementary or context-dependent.

Write an essay that argues your position on whether tradition serves or hinders progress. Use appropriate evidence from your reading, experience, or observations to support your argument.

#### Designer Notes

**Core Claim**: This prompt asks students to examine whether tradition functions as stabilizing wisdom or as ossified resistance to change—and whether this is a false binary.

**Complexity**: The prompt invites nuanced thinking about *types* of tradition (cultural, institutional, intellectual, aesthetic), *types* of progress (technological, moral, social, economic), and the mechanisms by which tradition either enables or obstructs change. The Mahler quotation reframes tradition as *dynamic preservation*—keeping alive the essential spirit rather than the static form—which opens space for sophisticated positions.

**Three Positions**:
- **Tradition serves progress**: Traditions provide tested frameworks, continuity during disruption, ethical guardrails against reckless innovation, cultural identity that grounds experimentation. Examples: scientific method as intellectual tradition, constitutional principles enabling legal evolution, artistic traditions providing foundation for innovation, indigenous ecological knowledge informing modern conservation.
- **Tradition hinders progress**: Traditions entrench power structures, resist necessary change, privilege ancestral authority over present needs, slow response to new challenges. Examples: gender/racial hierarchies justified by tradition, religious resistance to scientific advancement, economic systems protecting established interests, educational models resistant to reform.
- **Complicate**: The relationship depends on how tradition is held (rigid vs. adaptive), type of progress sought, or cultural context. Examples: distinguish "dead" tradition (empty ritual) from "living" tradition (evolving practice), recognize that some progress requires radical break while other progress requires rootedness, acknowledge that "progress" itself is value-laden and tradition provides critical perspective.

**Evidence Possibilities**: Scientific revolutions and paradigm shifts, social movements (civil rights, suffrage, LGBTQ+ rights), technological disruption and cultural adaptation, constitutional interpretation, artistic movements (modernism, jazz, etc.), indigenous knowledge systems, educational reform, environmental movements.

**Row C Opportunities**: Precise distinctions (form vs. essence, preservation vs. worship, continuity vs. stasis), recognition that Mahler's metaphor reframes the question entirely, acknowledgment that "progress" is not self-evidently good and tradition can provide critical distance, sophisticated treatment of how *manner* of holding tradition (rigid enforcement vs. adaptive interpretation) determines its relationship to change.

### Prompt 3: Certainty and Understanding (L22 Gate — RESERVED)

**Topic**: Whether pursuing certainty helps or hinders understanding.

**STATUS**: RESERVED FOR GATE ASSESSMENT (L22)

#### Student-Facing Prompt

> "The whole problem with the world is that fools and fanatics are always so certain of themselves, and wiser people so full of doubts."
> — Bertrand Russell

In many domains—science, ethics, politics, personal life—we seek certainty: definitive answers, clear principles, reliable predictions. Some argue that the pursuit of certainty drives rigorous inquiry, decisiveness, and confidence. Others contend that certainty is the enemy of understanding: it closes inquiry prematurely, creates dogmatism, and ignores complexity. Still others suggest the relationship between certainty and understanding varies by domain or that both certainty and doubt play essential roles.

Write an essay that argues your position on whether pursuing certainty helps or hinders understanding. Use appropriate evidence from your reading, experience, or observations to support your argument.

#### Designer Notes

**Core Claim**: This prompt asks students to examine whether the desire for definitive answers advances or obstructs deeper comprehension—and whether this varies by field of knowledge.

**Complexity**: The prompt invites nuanced thinking about *types* of certainty (mathematical proof, empirical confidence, moral conviction, subjective confidence), *types* of understanding (factual knowledge, explanatory models, wisdom, empathy), and the relationship between cognitive confidence and epistemic humility. The Russell quotation links certainty to *character traits* (foolishness/fanaticism vs. wisdom/doubt), suggesting that certainty may be more about psychological disposition than epistemic rigor.

**Three Positions**:
- **Certainty helps understanding**: Pursuit of certainty drives rigor, testing, and precision. Without seeking definitive answers, inquiry becomes unfocused or paralyzed. Examples: mathematical proof, experimental science, legal standards of evidence, moral clarity enabling action, engineering requiring reliable predictions. Certainty represents *earned* confidence after thorough investigation.
- **Certainty hinders understanding**: Certainty closes inquiry prematurely, creates dogmatism, and ignores complexity. Examples: scientific paradigms resisting evidence, moral absolutism preventing empathy, political tribalism shutting down dialogue, overconfidence bias in decision-making, expert predictions failing spectacularly. Russell's "wiser people" remain open because reality resists final answers.
- **Complicate**: The relationship depends on domain (mathematics vs. ethics), stage of inquiry (certainty as goal vs. certainty as method), or balance between confidence and openness. Examples: distinguish *certainty-seeking* (methodological rigor) from *certainty-claiming* (premature closure), recognize that some domains warrant high confidence (physics) while others require perpetual doubt (human nature), acknowledge that functional understanding often requires *provisional* certainty—confidence sufficient for action despite residual uncertainty.

**Evidence Possibilities**: Scientific revolutions and paradigm resistance, mathematical foundations and Gödel's theorems, ethical dilemmas and moral philosophy, political polarization and epistemic closure, expert prediction failures, judicial standards (beyond reasonable doubt vs. preponderance), religious faith and doubt, personal relationships and trust, cognitive biases (overconfidence, confirmation bias), technological risk and precautionary principle.

**Row C Opportunities**: Precise distinctions (certainty-seeking vs. certainty-claiming, confidence vs. dogmatism, provisional certainty vs. absolute certainty, *feeling* certain vs. *being* certain), recognition that Russell's critique targets *unearned* certainty rather than rigorous confidence, acknowledgment that different domains have different relationships to certainty (formal systems vs. human behavior), sophisticated treatment of how certainty and doubt might operate dialectically (thesis-antithesis-synthesis) rather than as simple opposition, recognition that "understanding" itself is ambiguous (factual knowledge, explanatory power, wisdom, empathy) and different senses may have different relationships to certainty.

## VOICE ANALYSIS EXCERPTS (3 for L18)

**Purpose**: For L18 (Personal Style and Voice), students study three analytical voices—each analyzing a rhetorical/literary topic—to identify stylistic dimensions (sentence length, diction, syntax, rhythm) and explore effects. These are original excerpts written to exemplify distinct voices, not drawn from published critics.

**Hypothetical Analysis Topic**: All three excerpts analyze the same moment—the opening paragraph of George Orwell's "Politics and the English Language"—to enable direct comparison of how voice shapes analytical reading.

---

### Excerpt A: Spare and Direct Voice

#### Text

Most people who bother with the matter at all would admit that the English language is in a bad way, but it is generally assumed that we cannot by conscious action do anything about it. Our civilization is decadent and our language—so the argument runs—must inevitably share in the general collapse. It follows that any struggle against the abuse of language is a sentimental archaism, like preferring candles to electric light or hansom cabs to aeroplanes.

Orwell opens with a concession. This is tactical. He grants his opponents their pessimism immediately. The language is in a bad way—he won't dispute that. But then notice the shift. What seems like a statement of fact ("it is generally assumed") is actually a claim he's about to demolish. The metaphor sequence exposes the absurdity: if fighting bad language is like preferring candles to electric bulbs, then improvement is backward. But the metaphor collapses on inspection. Light and transport improved. Language, in this view, only decays. The metaphor doesn't fit. Orwell lets his opponents overreach. He gives them rope. Then he shows that their own metaphor defeats them. The whole opening is a trap.

#### Annotations

**Sentence Length**: Average 11 words. The longest sentence is 22 words ("The metaphor sequence exposes..."); most are 7-15. Short sentences create staccato rhythm and force clarity.

**Diction**: Plain Anglo-Saxon words dominate: "grants," "shift," "claim," "fighting," "collapses," "defeats," "trap." Only one Latinate term: "tactical." Verb-heavy prose. Almost no abstract nouns.

**Syntax**: Simple declarative sentences. Subject-verb-object. Frequent use of fragments for emphasis ("This is tactical." "The language is in a bad way." "The whole opening is a trap."). Minimal subordination. Ideas presented as sequence of independent observations rather than nested relationships.

**Register**: Informal but authoritative. Uses contractions ("won't," "doesn't"). Addresses reader directly ("notice the shift"). Reads like spoken explanation—a teacher walking you through the passage.

**Rhythm**: Punchy. Short bursts. Staccato. Occasional longer sentence provides variety but quickly returns to short declarations. Emphasis through brevity. Key ideas get their own sentences.

### Excerpt B: Elaborate and Layered Voice

#### Text

Most people who bother with the matter at all would admit that the English language is in a bad way, but it is generally assumed that we cannot by conscious action do anything about it. Our civilization is decadent and our language—so the argument runs—must inevitably share in the general collapse. It follows that any struggle against the abuse of language is a sentimental archaism, like preferring candles to electric light or hansom cabs to aeroplanes.

The opening paragraph establishes the rhetorical architecture that will govern the entire essay, positioning Orwell as a reformer challenging linguistic determinism. By granting the premise of linguistic decline ("the English language is in a bad way") while disputing the fatalism that typically accompanies it, Orwell occupies a middle position between those who deny the crisis and those who consider it irreversible—a characteristic move that allows him to claim both realism and agency. The concessive structure ("Most people...would admit...but it is generally assumed") enacts a model of critical thinking that recurs throughout: acknowledge the partial truth in received wisdom, then expose the non sequitur that derives from it. The metaphor sequence (candles/light, hansom cabs/aeroplanes) functions on multiple levels: ostensibly, it represents the defeatist position he opposes, but by selecting metaphors of technological *improvement* to represent linguistic *decay*, Orwell constructs an analogy whose internal contradictions betray the incoherence of the position itself. The paragraph thus establishes not merely a thesis but a methodology: patient attention to logical structure and metaphoric implication reveals conceptual confusion even in arguments that present themselves as common sense.

#### Annotations

**Sentence Length**: Average 38 words. The longest sentence is 78 words (beginning "The concessive structure..."). Only one sentence under 25 words. Extended sentences allow complex embedding of qualifications and relationships.

**Diction**: Latinate and abstract: "establishes," "rhetorical architecture," "determinism," "fatalism," "concessive structure," "non sequitur," "ostensibly," "methodology," "conceptual confusion." Vocabulary of academic criticism. Nominalizations transform actions into concepts ("decline," "improvement," "decay").

**Syntax**: Heavily subordinated. Embedded clauses nest inside clauses. Frequent use of colons and dashes to extend sentences with clarification or elaboration. Participial phrases ("positioning Orwell," "acknowledge the partial truth," "selecting metaphors") pack multiple actions into single sentences. Ideas presented as complex relationships rather than linear sequence.

**Register**: Formal academic prose. No contractions. Third-person analytical distance (discusses "Orwell" as subject rather than addressing reader). Vocabulary assumes educated audience familiar with rhetorical terminology.

**Rhythm**: Flowing and cumulative. Long waves of thought. Clauses build upon each other, delaying resolution. Reader must hold multiple ideas in suspension before reaching the main verb. Emphasis through elaboration rather than brevity.

### Excerpt C: Balanced and Rhythmic Voice

#### Text

Most people who bother with the matter at all would admit that the English language is in a bad way, but it is generally assumed that we cannot by conscious action do anything about it. Our civilization is decadent and our language—so the argument runs—must inevitably share in the general collapse. It follows that any struggle against the abuse of language is a sentimental archaism, like preferring candles to electric light or hansom cabs to aeroplanes.

Orwell begins by granting what his opponents believe: the language is deteriorating, and we are powerless to stop it. This is strategic concession, not genuine agreement. Notice the structure: first, he lets the defeatists speak ("Most people...would admit," "it is generally assumed," "so the argument runs"). Then he lets them overreach. The metaphor they choose—candles versus electric light, hansom cabs versus aeroplanes—is supposed to make reform look ridiculous, a nostalgic preference for obsolete technologies. But the metaphor betrays them. Light and transportation improved; in this view, language only decays. If language is like candles being replaced by bulbs, then change represents progress, not collapse. The metaphor says the opposite of what it intends. Orwell doesn't point this out yet—he's too subtle for that. He simply presents the argument and lets the contradiction sit there, waiting. The entire essay will be a demonstration of how muddled thinking produces muddled language, and muddled language reinforces muddled thinking. But here, in the opening paragraph, he shows rather than tells: he lets bad logic speak for itself.

#### Annotations

**Sentence Length**: Average 19 words. Range from 7 to 33 words. Deliberate variation: short sentences for key claims ("This is strategic concession, not genuine agreement." "But the metaphor betrays them.") and longer sentences for development. Creates rhythmic variety.

**Diction**: Mix of plain and precise language. Anglo-Saxon clarity ("lets them speak," "overreach," "betray," "sits there") combined with analytical precision ("strategic concession," "obsolete technologies," "contradiction"). Avoids both excessive plainness and excessive formality.

**Syntax**: Varied structures. Simple declaratives alternate with complex sentences. Uses parallelism for emphasis ("Light and transportation improved; in this view, language only decays"). Employs fragments sparingly ("Then he lets them overreach."). Balance between subordination and independence.

**Register**: Clear but sophisticated. No contractions, but direct address to reader ("Notice the structure"). Analytical vocabulary accessible to educated general reader. Tone is explanatory but assumes reader's intelligence.

**Rhythm**: Balanced and controlled. Sentences build and release tension. Parallel structures create echoes ("muddled thinking produces muddled language, and muddled language reinforces muddled thinking"). Variation in sentence length prevents monotony. Key ideas get emphasis through placement (often sentence-final) rather than through sentence length alone.

## Design Notes for Instructors

**Argument Prompts**: All three prompts follow AP FRQ format and target Row C thinking: precise distinctions, recognition of complexity, sophisticated line of reasoning. Prompts 1 and 2 are for practice (speed run, planning); Prompt 3 is reserved for gate assessment. Each prompt includes designer notes identifying complexity, positions, evidence possibilities, and Row C opportunities—these are for instructor planning, not for student distribution.

**Voice Excerpts**: These three excerpts provide models of stylistic range within analytical writing. All analyze the same passage (Orwell's opening paragraph), enabling direct comparison. Students in L18 will annotate these excerpts, identify stylistic features, and consider effects—then experiment with different voices in their own analytical writing. The excerpts are not *better* or *worse*—they demonstrate different rhetorical choices appropriate for different contexts and audiences. The goal is conscious control of voice, not imitation of a single "correct" style.
