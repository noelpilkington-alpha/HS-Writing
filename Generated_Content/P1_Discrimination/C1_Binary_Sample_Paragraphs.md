# C.9.11 Score-Then-Compare: Binary Calibration Paragraphs

## Instructions

For each pair below, read both paragraphs carefully. Score each paragraph as **Analysis** or **Summary** using binary calibration. Then check the answer key.

**Reminder -- The S-F-E Test:**
- **Surface:** What device or technique is present?
- **Function:** What does the device DO in the passage (how does it work)?
- **Effect:** What is the IMPACT on the reader, the argument, or the larger meaning?

A true analysis paragraph reaches all three levels. A summary paragraph may name a device (Surface) but fails to explain its Function and Effect.

### PAIR 1 | Passage: SP-030 | Moment: Opening line and emotional urgency

**Paragraph A:**
"Paine opens 'The Crisis, No. 1' by declaring that 'these are the times that try men's souls,' a line that immediately establishes the gravity of the revolutionary moment. He talks about how difficult things are for the colonists and mentions that not everyone is willing to fight. He then discusses the difference between dedicated soldiers and those who only support the cause when it is easy. Paine also brings up the idea that Britain is acting like a tyrant, and he uses strong language throughout the paragraph to make his point."

**Paragraph B:**
"Paine's opening declaration that 'these are the times that try men's souls' functions as both a rallying cry and a filter, immediately sorting his audience into those who will rise to the occasion and those who will shrink from it. The word 'try' is deliberately chosen -- it does not mean 'attempt' but rather 'test' or 'put on trial,' transforming the Revolution from a political event into a moral crucible. This framing forces readers to see inaction not as a neutral choice but as a failure of character, which pressures fence-sitters in the colonial audience to commit to the cause. By making the opening line feel universal rather than partisan, Paine ensures that even reluctant readers cannot dismiss the argument as mere propaganda."

**Answer Key:**
- **Paragraph A:** Summary
- **Paragraph B:** Analysis
- **Explanation:** Paragraph A names the quote and mentions devices like "strong language" but never explains HOW or WHY they work -- it stays at the Surface level. Paragraph B moves through all three S-F-E levels: it identifies the word choice (Surface), explains the double meaning of "try" and how it reframes the Revolution (Function), and connects this to the pressure it places on the audience to act (Effect).
- **Key Difference:** Paragraph A says Paine "uses strong language to make his point" without ever explaining what the strong language accomplishes or how it works. Paragraph B unpacks the mechanics of a single word and traces its impact on the audience.

### PAIR 2 | Passage: SP-030 | Moment: "Summer soldier" and "sunshine patriot" metaphors

**Paragraph A:**
"By labeling fair-weather supporters as 'the summer soldier and the sunshine patriot,' Paine weaponizes the natural world against cowardice, associating unreliable allies with seasons of comfort rather than seasons of hardship. The paired metaphors function through contrast: summer and sunshine connote ease, while the implied winter of Valley Forge connotes suffering, so the labels carry built-in shame for anyone who identifies with them. This shame operates as a rhetorical trap -- no reader wants to see themselves as the person who 'shrinks from the service of their country,' which means the metaphor doesn't just describe deserters but actively pressures the audience to prove they are not among them. Paine thus transforms a simple name-calling moment into a self-sorting mechanism that recruits commitment from his readers."

**Paragraph B:**
"In this section of the pamphlet, Paine introduces the metaphors of the 'summer soldier' and the 'sunshine patriot' to describe people who do not fully support the Revolution. These metaphors compare uncommitted colonists to soldiers who only fight when conditions are good. Paine is trying to say that real patriots need to stay loyal even during hard times, not just when things are going well. He uses these metaphors effectively because they create a vivid image that the reader can easily understand, and they help strengthen his overall argument about the importance of commitment to the cause."

**Answer Key:**
- **Paragraph A:** Analysis
- **Paragraph B:** Summary
- **Explanation:** Paragraph A traces the metaphors through all three levels: naming them (Surface), explaining the summer/winter contrast and the shame mechanism (Function), and showing how they pressure the audience into commitment (Effect). Paragraph B correctly identifies the metaphors and their general meaning but then says they work "because they create a vivid image" -- this is a generic claim that could apply to any metaphor and does not explain the specific function or effect of THESE metaphors.
- **Key Difference:** Paragraph B uses the empty phrase "effectively because they create a vivid image," which sounds analytical but is actually a placeholder -- it never explains what makes this particular image effective or what specific effect it has on the reader.

### PAIR 3 | Passage: SP-030 | Moment: Analogy of a thief breaking into a house

**Paragraph A:**
"Paine discusses the analogy of a thief breaking into someone's house to make a point about the British. He compares the British government to a burglar and suggests that the colonists should defend themselves just as a homeowner would defend their property. This analogy is important because it helps the reader understand the situation in simpler terms. Paine mentions that no reasonable person would just let a thief take their belongings, and he uses this idea to argue that the colonists should not let Britain continue to control them. The analogy makes the argument more relatable to ordinary people."

**Paragraph B:**
"When Paine asks his reader to imagine 'a thief' breaking into their home, he is not merely simplifying the political conflict but strategically reframing it as a domestic violation that demands immediate, physical response. The analogy strips away the abstractions of governance and sovereignty and replaces them with the visceral image of a stranger in your house, which bypasses the reader's political hesitations and activates a more primal sense of self-defense. This reframing is particularly cunning because it makes pacifism seem absurd -- no one would 'argue' with a burglar, so why would anyone negotiate with Britain? By anchoring the Revolution in the logic of home defense, Paine transforms a controversial political rebellion into what feels like common-sense self-preservation, making it far harder for moderates to justify inaction."

**Answer Key:**
- **Paragraph A:** Summary
- **Paragraph B:** Analysis
- **Explanation:** Paragraph A identifies the analogy and says it "helps the reader understand the situation in simpler terms" and is "more relatable," but these are Surface-level observations that never reach Function or Effect. Paragraph B explains the specific mechanism of the analogy (replacing political abstraction with domestic violation), its function (bypassing political hesitation, activating self-defense instinct), and its effect (making pacifism seem absurd, transforming rebellion into common sense).
- **Key Difference:** Paragraph A says the analogy "makes the argument more relatable" -- a vague, generic claim. Paragraph B explains precisely HOW and WHY the analogy changes the way the reader thinks, tracing the logical chain from image to emotional response to political commitment.

### PAIR 4 | Passage: SP-030 | Moment: Paine's direct address and tone of urgency

**Paragraph A:**
"Throughout 'The Crisis, No. 1,' Paine shifts between speaking to the reader as 'you' and speaking as 'we,' and this oscillation is not accidental but rhetorically calculated. When he uses 'you,' as in his challenge to imagine the thief in your home, he isolates the reader and forces individual accountability -- you cannot hide behind the crowd. When he shifts to 'we,' as in 'we have this consolation with us,' he absorbs the reader into a collective identity that has already decided to fight. The alternation between isolation and inclusion mimics the very choice Paine is presenting: you are either alone in your cowardice or together in your courage. This pronoun strategy transforms a pamphlet into a loyalty test, compelling readers to place themselves on one side or the other."

**Paragraph B:**
"Paine creates a strong sense of urgency in 'The Crisis, No. 1' through his use of direct address and emotional language. He speaks directly to the reader using words like 'you' and 'we,' which makes the writing feel personal and immediate. Paine also uses an urgent tone throughout the passage to convey the seriousness of the situation facing the colonies. His choice to address the audience directly is an effective rhetorical strategy because it pulls the reader into the argument and makes them feel like they are part of the conversation. Overall, Paine's tone and direct address work together to create a sense of urgency that supports his argument for independence."

**Answer Key:**
- **Paragraph A:** Analysis
- **Paragraph B:** Summary
- **Explanation:** Paragraph A identifies a specific pattern (the pronoun shifts between "you" and "we"), explains the distinct function of each pronoun, and connects the alternation to Paine's larger rhetorical strategy of forcing a binary choice. Paragraph B notices that Paine uses "you" and "we" but lumps them together as "direct address" and only says it "makes the writing feel personal" -- a Surface-level observation dressed up with words like "effective" and "rhetorical strategy."
- **Key Difference:** Paragraph A distinguishes between what "you" does and what "we" does, showing the specific mechanism. Paragraph B treats them as interchangeable and never explains the difference, relying on the generic claim that direct address "pulls the reader into the argument."

### PAIR 5 | Passage: ES-014 | Moment: Mrs. Auld teaching Douglass to read, then stopping

**Paragraph A:**
"The transformation of Mrs. Auld from willing teacher to hostile enforcer is one of the most striking moments in Chapter 7 because Douglass uses it to argue that slavery corrupts everyone it touches, not just the enslaved. When Douglass describes her initial 'kindly aid' followed by her 'tiger-like ferocity' in snatching newspapers from his hands, the contrast functions as a before-and-after portrait of what slaveholding does to a person's moral character. Mrs. Auld does not simply stop teaching -- she becomes surveillance itself, and Douglass's careful documentation of this change shifts blame from the individual woman to the institution that deformed her. This move is essential to Douglass's larger argument because it preempts the defense that slavery is maintained by 'good' masters, showing that the system makes goodness impossible."

**Paragraph B:**
"In Chapter 7 of his Narrative, Douglass describes how Mrs. Auld began teaching him the alphabet but then stopped after Mr. Auld told her it was dangerous to educate a slave. Douglass mentions that Mrs. Auld changed dramatically and became angry whenever she saw him reading. He discusses how this experience was an important turning point in his life because it showed him the connection between literacy and freedom. The incident with Mrs. Auld is significant because it reveals the harsh realities of slavery and how it affected everyone involved, including the slaveholders themselves."

**Answer Key:**
- **Paragraph A:** Analysis
- **Paragraph B:** Summary
- **Explanation:** Paragraph A identifies the contrast between Mrs. Auld's two states (Surface), explains how Douglass uses the transformation to shift blame from individual to institution (Function), and connects it to his larger argument against the "good master" defense (Effect). Paragraph B retells the events accurately and even gestures toward a deeper point ("how it affected everyone involved"), but it never explains HOW Douglass constructs this argument -- it simply reports THAT the incident "is significant."
- **Key Difference:** Paragraph B says the incident "reveals the harsh realities of slavery" but never explains the mechanism -- how does Douglass make it reveal those realities? Paragraph A explains the specific rhetorical moves Douglass makes to build his argument.

### PAIR 6 | Passage: ES-014 | Moment: Douglass learning to read from white boys on the street

**Paragraph A:**
"Douglass talks about how he learned to read by interacting with white boys he met on the street. He would trade bread for reading lessons, using food from the Auld household to convince the boys to help him. Douglass mentions that these boys were poor and that he sometimes felt sorry for them even though he was enslaved. This section shows Douglass's determination and resourcefulness in pursuing literacy despite the obstacles he faced. The fact that he had to learn in secret from children demonstrates how the institution of slavery tried to keep enslaved people uneducated and powerless."

**Paragraph B:**
"When Douglass describes trading bread for reading lessons from poor white boys, he constructs an ironic economy that quietly dismantles the racial hierarchy slavery depends on. The bread comes from the Auld household -- the very institution trying to keep Douglass illiterate -- meaning the slaveholder's own resources fund the slave's liberation, an irony Douglass lets the reader notice rather than stating outright. Meanwhile, the white boys are hungry and Douglass has food, temporarily inverting the power dynamic that supposedly justifies slavery's existence. By underplaying this moment rather than announcing its significance, Douglass mirrors the stealth of his own education: the argument, like the learning, operates beneath the surface, reaching the reader before they have a chance to resist it."

**Answer Key:**
- **Paragraph A:** Summary
- **Paragraph B:** Analysis
- **Explanation:** Paragraph A retells the events and makes a general thematic claim ("demonstrates how slavery tried to keep enslaved people uneducated") but does not examine how Douglass constructs the scene to make that point. Paragraph B identifies the ironic exchange (Surface), explains the inversion of the power dynamic and the source of the bread (Function), and connects Douglass's understated narration to a deliberate rhetorical strategy of stealth persuasion (Effect).
- **Key Difference:** Paragraph A states what the scene "demonstrates" as if the meaning is self-evident. Paragraph B explains the specific literary and rhetorical mechanisms Douglass uses to create that meaning.

### PAIR 7 | Passage: ES-014 | Moment: Reading as both awakening and torment

**Paragraph A:**
"Douglass's description of literacy as a 'curse rather than a blessing' is not a contradiction but a deliberate paradox that deepens his indictment of slavery. Once he can read, Douglass gains the intellectual framework to understand his condition -- but understanding without the power to change it produces what he calls 'torment.' The paradox functions as a trap for pro-slavery readers: if they argue that slaves are content, Douglass's anguish disproves it; if they argue that education causes suffering, they inadvertently admit that slavery can only survive by enforcing ignorance. Either way, the reader is forced to condemn the institution. Douglass thus transforms his personal pain into a logical weapon, making his suffering itself an argument against the system that produced it."

**Paragraph B:**
"In this part of Chapter 7, Douglass discusses how learning to read was both a positive and negative experience for him. On one hand, reading opened his eyes to the injustice of slavery and gave him a new understanding of the world. On the other hand, this knowledge caused him great pain because he now fully understood his situation but could not change it. Douglass describes reading as something that was both a blessing and a curse, showing the complex relationship between knowledge and freedom. This moment is important in the Narrative because it reveals Douglass's inner conflict and the emotional toll that slavery took on him."

**Answer Key:**
- **Paragraph A:** Analysis
- **Paragraph B:** Summary
- **Explanation:** Paragraph A names the paradox (Surface), explains how it creates a logical trap for pro-slavery readers (Function), and shows how Douglass transforms personal pain into a structural argument against slavery (Effect). Paragraph B accurately paraphrases the paradox and calls it "complex," but it merely restates what Douglass says rather than explaining how the paradox works as an argument -- it tells us the moment "reveals inner conflict" without explaining the rhetorical machinery beneath it.
- **Key Difference:** Paragraph B restates the paradox ("both a blessing and a curse") but treats it as a biographical fact rather than a rhetorical strategy. Paragraph A shows how the paradox functions as a logical trap that forces readers into condemning slavery regardless of their starting position.

### PAIR 8 | Passage: SP-025 | Moment: Red Cloud's factual, restrained tone

**Paragraph A:**
"In his Cooper Union speech, Red Cloud recounts the broken treaties and lost lands of the Lakota people using a tone of measured restraint, and this restraint is itself a rhetorical act. By presenting facts -- names, dates, agreements violated -- without the anger his white audience likely expected from a Native leader, Red Cloud denies them the comfort of dismissing him as emotional or 'savage.' His calm recitation of betrayal after betrayal forces the audience to sit with the accumulating weight of the evidence rather than reacting to the speaker's emotion. The restraint thus functions as a form of cross-cultural code-switching: Red Cloud adopts the evidentiary style his audience would associate with credibility, turning their own rhetorical standards against them to expose the government's dishonesty."

**Paragraph B:**
"Red Cloud's speech at Cooper Union is notable for its calm and restrained tone. Rather than expressing anger about the injustices done to his people, Red Cloud calmly presents the facts of what happened, including broken treaties and lost territory. He discusses the various promises made by the U.S. government and how those promises were not kept. This approach is effective because it allows the audience to focus on the facts rather than getting distracted by emotion. Red Cloud's restraint makes his speech more powerful and convincing to his audience at Cooper Union."

**Answer Key:**
- **Paragraph A:** Analysis
- **Paragraph B:** Summary
- **Explanation:** Paragraph A identifies the restrained tone (Surface), explains how it operates as cross-cultural code-switching that denies the audience a reason to dismiss Red Cloud (Function), and connects it to the larger strategy of turning the audience's own credibility standards against the government (Effect). Paragraph B names the restraint and says it is "effective" and "powerful" but never explains the mechanism -- WHY does calm make the speech more convincing? What specific audience expectations does it subvert?
- **Key Difference:** Paragraph B relies on empty evaluative words ("effective," "powerful," "convincing") without explaining what makes the restraint work. Paragraph A explains the specific cultural and rhetorical dynamics that give the restraint its power.

### PAIR 9 | Passage: SP-025 | Moment: Red Cloud's use of understatement and contrast with expected anger

**Paragraph A:**
"Red Cloud mentions several injustices committed against his people, including broken promises and the seizure of Lakota lands. Instead of speaking with rage or hostility, he uses a surprisingly understated approach, simply listing the wrongs that were done. He talks about how the government failed to honor its agreements and how his people suffered as a result. The understatement in Red Cloud's speech is important because it contrasts with what the audience probably expected to hear from a Native American leader. This contrast makes the speech stand out and helps Red Cloud get his message across to the audience more effectively."

**Paragraph B:**
"Red Cloud's most devastating rhetorical move may be what he refuses to say. When describing the systematic dispossession of the Lakota, he does not rage or accuse -- he simply states, in plain language, what was promised and what was taken. This understatement creates an unbridgeable gap between the enormity of the injustice and the quietness of the telling, and the audience's own moral imagination is forced to fill that gap. The effect is more damning than any direct accusation could be: by not telling the audience how to feel, Red Cloud makes them arrive at outrage on their own, which means they own that outrage rather than resisting it as someone else's emotion. The understatement thus transforms the audience from passive listeners into active moral agents who must confront their own government's record."

**Answer Key:**
- **Paragraph A:** Summary
- **Paragraph B:** Analysis
- **Explanation:** Paragraph A identifies the understatement and the contrast with audience expectations but stops at naming these features -- it says the contrast "makes the speech stand out" without explaining the psychological or rhetorical mechanism. Paragraph B identifies the understatement (Surface), explains the gap it creates between event and telling that the audience must fill (Function), and shows how this transforms the audience into active moral participants who own their outrage (Effect).
- **Key Difference:** Paragraph A says the understatement "helps Red Cloud get his message across more effectively" -- a claim so vague it could describe any successful speech. Paragraph B explains the precise psychological mechanism: the audience must fill the emotional gap themselves, which makes the resulting outrage more powerful because it feels self-generated.

### PAIR 10 | Passage: SP-025 | Moment: Red Cloud's cross-cultural audience awareness

**Paragraph A:**
"Red Cloud demonstrates awareness of his audience throughout the Cooper Union speech by adjusting his language and approach to suit a predominantly white, Eastern audience. He discusses the history of treaties between the U.S. government and the Lakota, presenting events in a chronological and logical manner that his audience would find familiar. Red Cloud also mentions specific names and places, which adds credibility to his account. His awareness of the audience is an important rhetorical strategy because it helps him connect with people who may not have been sympathetic to Native American concerns. By presenting himself as reasonable and knowledgeable, Red Cloud makes his arguments more persuasive."

**Paragraph B:**
"What makes Red Cloud's audience awareness so sophisticated is that it operates on two levels simultaneously. On the surface, he adopts the conventions of Western oratory -- chronological structure, specific evidence, measured tone -- signaling to his Cooper Union audience that he speaks their rhetorical language. But beneath this accommodation lies a subtle subversion: by meeting every standard of 'civilized' discourse that white America claims to value, Red Cloud implicitly exposes the fact that the government, not the Lakota, has failed to behave 'civilly.' Each calmly presented broken treaty becomes an indictment not just of policy but of the audience's own cultural hypocrisy. Red Cloud's code-switching thus serves a double function -- it earns credibility with his immediate audience while quietly dismantling the moral framework they use to justify westward expansion."

**Answer Key:**
- **Paragraph A:** Summary
- **Paragraph B:** Analysis
- **Explanation:** Paragraph A accurately describes Red Cloud's audience awareness and says it "helps him connect" and makes arguments "more persuasive," but it never moves past the Surface observation that he adjusts his approach. Paragraph B identifies the code-switching (Surface), explains how it operates on two levels -- earning credibility while exposing hypocrisy (Function), and shows how it dismantles the moral framework justifying expansion (Effect).
- **Key Difference:** Paragraph A treats audience awareness as a one-directional strategy (Red Cloud adapts to fit his audience). Paragraph B reveals it as a two-directional strategy (Red Cloud adapts to his audience AND uses that adaptation to expose their contradictions), which is the critical analytical insight.
