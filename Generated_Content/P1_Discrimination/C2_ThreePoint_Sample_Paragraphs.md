# C2 Three-Point Sample Paragraphs -- Score-Then-Compare Exercise (C.10.12)

**Instructions:** Each set below contains three paragraphs (A, B, C) arguing about the same topic. Read all three, then score each as **Weak**, **Adequate**, or **Strong** using the 3-point rubric. Each set contains exactly one of each. After scoring, check the Answer Key at the bottom of each set to calibrate your judgment.

**Rubric Reminder:**
- **Weak:** Binary thinking, no counterargument, evidence dropped without analysis, no source integration, possible logical fallacies.
- **Adequate:** Specific position with evidence and partial analysis, attempts counterargument but doesn't develop it, sources present but not synthesized.
- **Strong:** Nuanced (non-binary) position, full S-F-E analysis reaching Effect level, genuine counterargument with concession + rebuttal, sources synthesized using They Say / I Say moves.

### SET 1 | Topic: SYN-01 | Claim Area: Whether social media causes depression in teenagers

**Paragraph A:**
"While many commentators frame social media as either a harmless pastime or a mental health crisis, the reality is more conditional: platforms like Instagram and TikTok appear to amplify existing vulnerabilities in adolescents rather than create problems from scratch. The CDC's 2023 Youth Risk Behavior Survey found that nearly 60% of teen girls reported persistent sadness or hopelessness, a figure that has climbed steadily alongside smartphone adoption. However, as researchers writing in The Conversation have pointed out, correlation does not establish causation, and teens who already struggle with anxiety may simply gravitate toward screen time as a coping mechanism. This counterargument has real merit -- self-selection bias is notoriously difficult to control for in observational studies -- yet it does not fully account for experimental findings showing that even brief Instagram exposure increases body-image dissatisfaction among young women. What the evidence suggests, then, is not that social media single-handedly causes depression but that it functions as a risk multiplier, turning low-level insecurities into clinical-level distress for the most vulnerable users. Policymakers who ignore this conditional relationship risk crafting interventions that are either too sweeping or too lax to protect the teens who need it most."

**Paragraph B:**
"Social media is terrible for teenagers' mental health and everyone knows it. The CDC reported that teen sadness is at an all-time high and it's obvious that phones are the reason. Every day kids are scrolling through Instagram seeing perfect lives and feeling bad about themselves. Some people say social media isn't that bad but they're wrong because the statistics clearly show teens are more depressed than ever. If we just got rid of social media or at least banned it for kids under 16, teen depression rates would probably go back to normal. The evidence is overwhelming that social media is destroying a generation of young people."

**Paragraph C:**
"Social media has a significant negative impact on adolescent mental health, particularly for teen girls. According to the CDC's Youth Risk Behavior Survey, rates of persistent sadness among teen girls have reached nearly 60%, which is alarming. Research discussed in The Conversation suggests that platforms like Instagram contribute to body-image issues through constant exposure to idealized content. Some argue that social media is not the main driver of teen depression and that other factors are at play, but the timing of the mental health decline lines up too closely with smartphone adoption to ignore. The data from the CDC makes it clear that something changed in the mid-2010s when social media became ubiquitous. While more research is needed, the current evidence strongly suggests that social media is a major factor in declining teen mental health."

**Answer Key:**
- **A:** Strong -- Presents a nuanced, conditional claim ("risk multiplier"), synthesizes CDC data with Conversation research, engages the self-selection counterargument with genuine concession before rebutting with experimental evidence, and reaches Effect-level analysis on policy implications.
- **B:** Weak -- Binary framing ("terrible," "destroying"), no real counterargument engagement ("they're wrong"), evidence cited without analysis, and the proposed solution (banning social media) is presented without reasoning. Relies on "everyone knows it" as a warrant.
- **C:** Adequate -- Has a specific claim with real sources (CDC, The Conversation), but analysis stays at the Function level (what the data shows) without reaching Effect (what it means for policy or individuals). The counterargument is acknowledged but dismissed with correlation logic ("timing lines up") rather than genuinely engaged.
- **Key Progression:** From binary claim to specific claim to conditional/nuanced claim -- the strongest writer resists the urge to say social media simply "is" or "isn't" harmful.

### SET 2 | Topic: SYN-02 | Claim Area: Whether standardized tests accurately measure student learning

**Paragraph A:**
"Standardized tests are the worst thing in education today. They don't measure how smart you are, they just measure how good you are at taking tests. The NCES has tons of data showing that some groups score lower than others and that just shows the tests are unfair. Teachers hate them too because they have to spend all their time on test prep instead of real teaching. Some people think we need standardized tests but I disagree because there are way better ways to assess students. If schools got rid of standardized tests, students would actually enjoy learning again and be less stressed. Basically standardized testing needs to go."

**Paragraph B:**
"Standardized testing provides a useful but incomplete snapshot of student learning, and the key question is not whether to test but what we do with the scores. Data from the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES) shows that NAEP scores enable comparisons across states and demographic groups, a function no classroom-based assessment can replicate at scale. Critics rightly point out, as several education researchers in The Conversation have argued, that standardized tests reward test-taking strategy over deep understanding and disproportionately penalize students from under-resourced schools. This critique deserves serious weight: when a student's zip code predicts her score more reliably than her effort, the test is partly measuring privilege rather than learning. Still, abandoning standardized measures entirely would leave policymakers without the very data needed to identify and address those inequities. The most productive path forward treats test scores not as verdicts on individual students but as diagnostic signals that, when combined with portfolio assessments and teacher evaluations, reveal where systemic support is lacking. In this framing, the test itself is not the problem -- the problem is a system that treats a single number as a complete story."

**Paragraph C:**
"Standardized tests like the SAT and state assessments are not a fair way to measure what students actually know. NCES data shows that test scores vary widely based on income and race, which proves the tests are biased. Students from wealthy neighborhoods have access to test prep courses and tutors while students from poorer areas do not, so the tests are really just measuring wealth. Some people argue that standardized tests are necessary for accountability, but accountability should not come at the expense of fairness. Research from The Conversation highlights that many teachers end up teaching to the test instead of actually educating students, which defeats the whole purpose. If we really cared about measuring learning, we would use projects and portfolios instead of bubble sheets. Standardized testing does more harm than good and should be significantly reduced."

**Answer Key:**
- **A:** Weak -- Hyperbolic framing ("worst thing in education"), no source integration beyond a vague NCES mention, counterargument dismissed in one sentence, and the conclusion ("students would actually enjoy learning") is speculative without evidence. Reads as opinion rather than argument.
- **B:** Strong -- Nuanced framing ("useful but incomplete"), synthesizes NCES data with Conversation critiques, concedes the equity argument genuinely before rebutting with a diagnostic-use reframing, and reaches Effect level on systemic policy implications.
- **C:** Adequate -- Specific claim supported by NCES and Conversation sources, identifies the wealth-score correlation, and raises a counterargument about accountability. However, the counterargument is dismissed rather than engaged, and the analysis stays at Function level without exploring what "significantly reduced" testing would look like in practice.
- **Key Progression:** From emotional opinion to evidence-backed position to a reframed argument that changes the terms of the debate -- the strongest writer moves beyond "for or against" testing.

### SET 3 | Topic: SYN-01 | Claim Area: Whether parents should limit teens' screen time to protect mental health

**Paragraph A:**
"Parents should definitely limit how much time their kids spend on social media. The CDC found that teen depression and sadness are higher than ever, and social media is clearly a big part of the problem. If parents just set rules about screen time, like no phones at dinner or after 9 PM, their kids would be happier and healthier. Some parents think their kids should learn to manage technology on their own, but teens' brains aren't fully developed so they can't make good decisions about this. Countries like China have already put screen time limits in place for minors, which shows that this is a serious issue worldwide. Parents need to step up and take control of their children's screen time before things get even worse."

**Paragraph B:**
"There is a strong case for parents setting reasonable limits on their teenagers' social media use, given the growing body of evidence linking heavy screen time to mental health challenges. The CDC's Youth Risk Behavior Survey data shows persistent increases in reported sadness and hopelessness among teens, especially girls, over the past decade. Articles in The Conversation have explored how algorithmic feeds can create echo chambers of negative content, keeping vulnerable teens locked in cycles of comparison and self-criticism. While some argue that restricting screen time infringes on teens' autonomy and that learning to self-regulate is an important developmental skill, the scale of the current mental health crisis suggests that waiting for teens to figure it out on their own is too risky. Setting limits doesn't have to mean banning social media entirely; parents can establish phone-free zones and times while still allowing teens some access. Overall, the evidence supports a proactive parental role in managing teen screen time."

**Paragraph C:**
"Parents who set firm boundaries around screen time may see short-term behavioral improvements, but unless those limits are paired with open conversation about online experiences, the underlying mental health risks remain. The CDC's data on rising teen sadness suggests that mere hours of exposure matter less than the nature of the content consumed and the social dynamics it triggers -- a teen spending two hours in a supportive group chat faces different risks than one spending thirty minutes on appearance-focused feeds. Researchers in The Conversation have noted that heavy-handed restrictions can backfire, pushing adolescents toward secretive device use and eroding the trust that makes parental guidance effective. This is a legitimate concern: developmental psychology consistently shows that autonomy-supportive parenting outperforms controlling parenting for adolescent wellbeing. However, this does not mean a laissez-faire approach is wise, since younger adolescents in particular lack the self-regulation to manage infinite scroll without guardrails. The most effective strategy, then, is a collaborative one in which parents and teens negotiate boundaries together, focusing less on total minutes and more on which platforms and behaviors carry the most risk. This approach treats teens as developing agents rather than either helpless victims or fully autonomous adults."

**Answer Key:**
- **A:** Weak -- Oversimplified solution ("just set rules"), evidence dropped without analysis (CDC data mentioned but not examined), counterargument dismissed with a brain-development cliche rather than engaged, and the China reference is unexplained. Lacks source integration.
- **B:** Adequate -- Uses CDC and Conversation sources with some analysis, offers a moderate position (limits without banning), and acknowledges the autonomy counterargument. But the counterargument is quickly overridden ("too risky") without engaging with the developmental reasoning, and the analysis doesn't reach Effect level on what kind of limits would be most effective.
- **C:** Strong -- Reframes the debate from "how much" to "what kind," synthesizes CDC data with Conversation research on parenting styles, concedes the autonomy concern with psychological evidence before proposing a collaborative model, and reaches Effect level on developmental outcomes.
- **Key Progression:** From a simple rule (limit screen time) to a moderate position (set reasonable limits) to a reframed argument (focus on content type and collaborative negotiation) -- the strongest writer questions the assumption behind the debate itself.

### SET 4 | Topic: SYN-02 | Claim Area: Whether test scores should be used to evaluate teacher effectiveness

**Paragraph A:**
"Using standardized test scores to evaluate teachers makes sense because it provides an objective measure of how well they are doing their job. NCES data shows that student performance varies significantly between schools and districts, and test scores help identify which teachers are getting results and which are not. According to research discussed in The Conversation, value-added models that track student growth over time are considered more fair than raw score comparisons because they account for where students started. However, some teachers argue that test scores do not capture everything they do in the classroom. While this may be true to some extent, we still need some way to hold teachers accountable, and test scores are the most readily available data point. Districts that ignore test performance entirely risk allowing ineffective teaching to continue unchecked. Test scores should remain a component of teacher evaluation, even if other measures are added alongside them."

**Paragraph B:**
"Although test-based accountability systems were designed to ensure educational quality, using student scores as a primary measure of teacher effectiveness introduces perverse incentives that can undermine the very learning those systems aim to promote. NCES longitudinal data reveals that teacher impact on standardized scores accounts for roughly 1% to 14% of total score variance, with out-of-school factors like family income exerting far greater influence. Education researchers writing in The Conversation have documented how high-stakes score-based evaluations push teachers toward curriculum narrowing, drilling tested subjects at the expense of critical thinking, art, and social-emotional learning. Proponents of value-added models argue, with some justification, that tracking individual student growth rather than raw scores controls for demographic differences and isolates the teacher's contribution more fairly. That methodological improvement is real, yet even value-added scores fluctuate so dramatically from year to year for the same teacher that researchers have called their reliability into question. What this instability reveals is not that measurement is impossible but that a single metric, however sophisticated, cannot capture the complex, long-term work of teaching. A credible evaluation system would weight classroom observations, student feedback, and portfolio evidence alongside growth data, treating scores as one input in a triangulated judgment rather than the bottom line."

**Paragraph C:**
"Test scores should never be used to evaluate teachers because it is totally unfair. Teachers can't control what their students do at home or whether they even try on the test. The NCES has data showing that schools in poor areas always score lower, so using those scores to judge teachers is basically punishing them for working with disadvantaged kids. Some people think test scores are objective, but they're not because standardized tests are biased in the first place. If a teacher is creative and inspiring but her students don't test well, does that make her a bad teacher? Obviously not. We should evaluate teachers based on how much their students enjoy the class and how much effort the teacher puts in, not on some random test score."

**Answer Key:**
- **A:** Adequate -- Cites NCES data and value-added models from The Conversation, takes a measured position, and acknowledges the counterargument about test scores not capturing everything. However, the counterargument is brushed aside ("this may be true to some extent"), and the analysis doesn't reach Effect level on what happens when test scores dominate evaluation systems.
- **B:** Strong -- Nuanced claim about perverse incentives, synthesizes NCES variance data with Conversation research on curriculum narrowing, concedes the value-added improvement genuinely before countering with reliability evidence, and reaches Effect level by proposing a triangulated evaluation framework.
- **C:** Weak -- Binary framing ("never," "totally unfair"), rhetorical question substitutes for analysis, NCES data mentioned but not integrated, counterargument dismissed without engagement, and the proposed alternative (enjoyment, effort) is vague and unsupported.
- **Key Progression:** From rejecting test scores outright to accepting them with caveats to reframing the role scores should play within a larger system -- the strongest writer moves from "should we or shouldn't we" to "how and in what proportion."

### SET 5 | Topic: SYN-01 | Claim Area: Whether schools should ban phones to improve student wellbeing

**Paragraph A:**
"There is growing support for banning phones in schools as a way to protect student wellbeing and improve the learning environment. CDC data on declining teen mental health has prompted many educators and parents to look for concrete solutions, and phone bans represent one actionable step schools can take. Research covered in The Conversation suggests that the constant presence of phones, even when silenced, can reduce cognitive capacity and increase anxiety about missing notifications. While opponents argue that phone bans are difficult to enforce and may not address the root causes of teen mental health struggles, the potential benefits for focus and in-person social interaction seem worth the effort. Some schools that have piloted phone-free policies report positive results, including more engaged students and fewer disciplinary issues related to social media conflicts. Phone bans alone will not solve the teen mental health crisis, but they appear to be a reasonable and evidence-informed component of a larger strategy."

**Paragraph B:**
"Schools should definitely ban phones during the school day. The CDC's data proves that social media is hurting teens' mental health, and the easiest way to reduce exposure is to take phones away during school hours. Students can't focus when they're getting notifications every five minutes, and teachers are tired of competing with TikTok for attention. Some people say students need their phones for emergencies, but schools have office phones and students survived without cell phones for decades. Other countries like France have already banned phones in schools, so there's no reason American schools can't do the same. Banning phones would improve both mental health and academic performance, and schools that refuse to act are failing their students."

**Paragraph C:**
"Schools across the country are considering phone bans as a response to the adolescent mental health crisis, and the impulse is understandable given CDC data showing dramatic increases in teen sadness and hopelessness. Several Conversation articles have reported on early results from schools that implemented phone-free policies, with teachers noting improved focus and more face-to-face social interaction during breaks. These are promising signals, but it would be premature to treat phone bans as a mental health intervention rather than a classroom management tool. The wellbeing gains documented so far are largely self-reported and short-term, and they may reflect the novelty of the policy rather than a durable shift. Critics who worry that bans leave students unable to reach parents during emergencies raise a practical concern that schools must address through clear communication protocols rather than dismiss. Ultimately, phone bans may be a necessary first step, but they function more like removing a distraction than treating a condition -- if the underlying drivers of teen distress, from academic pressure to social comparison, remain unaddressed, the mental health needle is unlikely to move in any lasting way."

**Answer Key:**
- **A:** Adequate -- Cites CDC and Conversation sources, offers a balanced position (bans as one component), and notes enforcement challenges. However, the counterargument about root causes is acknowledged but not engaged in depth, and the analysis stays at Function level without examining what conditions would make bans effective versus symbolic.
- **B:** Weak -- Binary framing ("definitely ban"), CDC data used as a blanket proof without analysis, counterargument dismissed flippantly ("students survived without cell phones"), France reference dropped without context, and the conclusion overstates what a phone ban could achieve.
- **C:** Strong -- Distinguishes between classroom management and mental health intervention, synthesizes CDC data with Conversation reporting on pilot results, concedes early positive findings while questioning their durability, engages the emergency counterargument constructively, and reaches Effect level on why bans alone are insufficient.
- **Key Progression:** From "ban phones, problem solved" to "bans are one component" to "bans address symptoms, not causes" -- the strongest writer distinguishes between surface-level action and structural change.

### SET 6 | Topic: SYN-02 | Claim Area: Whether standardized tests worsen educational inequality

**Paragraph A:**
"Standardized tests have a complicated relationship with educational inequality: they simultaneously reveal gaps and, depending on how results are used, can either narrow or widen them. NCES data consistently shows achievement gaps along racial and socioeconomic lines on assessments like the NAEP, and this transparency has historically been essential for directing Title I funding and triggering intervention in underperforming districts. Yet as education scholars in The Conversation have argued, the same data can be weaponized when real estate markets use school ratings, largely derived from test scores, to sort families into stratified neighborhoods, reinforcing the very segregation that produced the gaps. Those who contend that eliminating tests would level the playing field have a point about reducing one source of stigma, but they overlook the political reality that without hard data, legislatures are far less likely to fund equity initiatives. The deeper problem is not the existence of tests but the policy architecture surrounding them: when scores trigger punishment rather than support, when test prep replaces enrichment in low-scoring schools, the assessment becomes part of the inequality machine it was meant to diagnose. Reforming the incentive structures around testing, rather than abolishing the tests themselves, offers the most credible path toward using measurement in the service of justice."

**Paragraph B:**
"Standardized testing contributes to educational inequality in several important ways. NCES data reveals persistent achievement gaps between white students and students of color, as well as between students from high-income and low-income families. Research discussed in The Conversation points out that affluent families can afford test preparation services, private tutoring, and enrichment activities that give their children an advantage on standardized assessments. Some defenders of standardized testing argue that the tests themselves are not biased and that they simply reflect existing inequalities in the education system. While this distinction is technically valid, it sidesteps the practical reality that test results are used to make high-stakes decisions about school funding, student placement, and college admissions. The net effect is that standardized testing tends to reinforce existing advantages rather than create opportunities for disadvantaged students. Addressing this problem will require either significant reforms to how test results are used or a move toward alternative assessment methods."

**Paragraph C:**
"Standardized tests definitely make inequality worse and it's not even close. NCES data shows that rich kids score way higher than poor kids every single year, which proves the tests are designed to favor wealthy students. Kids in underfunded schools don't have access to good test prep so they're set up to fail from the start. Some people claim the tests are fair and just measure knowledge, but that's ridiculous when some students have every advantage and others have none. Schools in poor neighborhoods end up getting labeled as failures because of low test scores, which just leads to less funding and more problems. If we actually cared about equality, we'd get rid of standardized tests and focus on giving every student the resources they need to succeed."

**Answer Key:**
- **A:** Strong -- Frames tests as both diagnostic tools and potential drivers of inequality, synthesizes NCES funding data with Conversation analysis of real estate sorting, concedes the stigma argument before rebutting with political reality, and reaches Effect level on policy architecture and incentive reform.
- **B:** Adequate -- Cites NCES and Conversation sources with relevant evidence on test prep access, acknowledges the "tests reflect inequality" counterargument with partial engagement ("technically valid" but then pivots). Analysis identifies the reinforcement mechanism but doesn't reach Effect level on what specific reforms would break the cycle.
- **C:** Weak -- Hyperbolic framing ("not even close"), claims tests are "designed to favor" wealthy students without evidence, counterargument dismissed ("that's ridiculous"), and the proposed solution (get rid of tests) is asserted without analyzing consequences. Conflates correlation with intentional design.
- **Key Progression:** From "tests cause inequality" to "tests reinforce inequality" to "tests can either diagnose or deepen inequality depending on the policy surrounding them" -- the strongest writer identifies the mechanism, not just the outcome.

### SET 7 | Topic: SYN-01 | Claim Area: Whether social media companies bear responsibility for teen mental health outcomes

**Paragraph A:**
"Social media companies bear significant responsibility for adolescent mental health outcomes because they design and control the platforms that teens use daily. Leaked internal research, as reported in The Conversation, revealed that Meta's own researchers found Instagram had negative effects on teen girls' body image and mental health. CDC data on rising rates of teen sadness and hopelessness adds urgency to the argument that platforms must be held accountable for their products' effects. Some argue that responsibility lies primarily with parents who should monitor and restrict their children's social media use, and it is true that parental involvement plays an important role. However, the addictive design features built into these platforms, such as infinite scroll and push notifications, make it unreasonably difficult for parents or teens to self-regulate. While the exact extent of social media's causal role in the teen mental health crisis remains debated, the companies that profit from teen engagement have an ethical obligation to invest in safety measures and transparency."

**Paragraph B:**
"The question of corporate responsibility becomes unavoidable once we recognize that social media platforms are not neutral utilities but attention-harvesting systems whose revenue model depends on maximizing engagement, including among adolescents. Internal documents discussed in The Conversation and leaked during congressional hearings show that at least one major platform's own researchers found that Instagram worsened body-image concerns for roughly one in three teen girls, yet the company shelved the findings rather than redesigning the product. Critics of regulation argue, not unreasonably, that parents bear primary responsibility for their children's media consumption and that government intervention risks chilling innovation and free expression. This concern has genuine weight in a democratic society, and any regulatory framework must be carefully scoped to avoid overreach. However, the parental-responsibility argument loses force when platforms are deliberately engineered, through autoplay, infinite scroll, and algorithmically curated feeds, to override the very self-regulation that parents are trying to teach. What the internal research reveals is not that companies failed to foresee harm but that they identified it and chose growth over safety, a pattern that shifts the moral calculus decisively toward accountability. Meaningful reform would require transparency mandates for algorithmic impact on minors, not a ban on social media itself."

**Paragraph C:**
"Social media companies should be held responsible for the damage they've done to teen mental health, including rising rates of depression, anxiety, and even suicide. The CDC's data shows that the mental health of teenagers has gotten dramatically worse since social media became popular, and it's no coincidence. Leaked documents have shown that companies like Meta knew their platforms were harmful to teens and did nothing about it, which is basically like a cigarette company hiding evidence that smoking causes cancer. Some people think it's the parents' job to monitor what their kids do online, but that's unrealistic when the apps are designed to be addictive. Social media companies have billions of dollars and armies of engineers specifically making their products harder to put down, so blaming parents is just letting corporations off the hook. These companies need to be regulated immediately before more teens are harmed."

However, the counterargument engagement is shallow ("it is true" followed by a quick pivot), and the conclusion ("ethical obligation") stays at Function level without specifying what accountability would look like in practice.
- **B:** Strong -- Frames the issue around business models and engineered attention, synthesizes leaked internal research with The Conversation reporting, concedes the parental-responsibility and free-expression arguments genuinely before rebutting with platform-design evidence, and reaches Effect level by proposing specific transparency mandates.
- **C:** Weak -- Uses an analogy (cigarette companies) in place of analysis, CDC data cited without examination, the parental-responsibility counterargument is dismissed ("unrealistic") rather than engaged, and the call for immediate regulation is asserted without considering trade-offs. The emotional urgency substitutes for reasoning.
- **Key Progression:** From "regulate them now" to "they have an ethical obligation" to "the revenue model itself creates the harm, so here is what accountability requires" -- the strongest writer traces the problem to its structural source and proposes a proportionate response.

### SET 8 | Topic: SYN-02 | Claim Area: Whether colleges should adopt test-optional admissions permanently

**Paragraph A:**
"Colleges adopting test-optional admissions policies is a positive development that should continue beyond the pandemic era. NCES data has shown that standardized test scores correlate strongly with family income, meaning they often reflect socioeconomic status rather than academic potential. Research in The Conversation indicates that several universities that went test-optional during COVID saw increases in applications from underrepresented minorities without drops in academic quality. Opponents counter that without test scores, colleges lose a standardized metric for comparing applicants from different high schools with different grading standards. This is a fair point, since GPA can vary widely in meaning depending on the school. Still, the benefits of increased access and diversity seem to outweigh the drawbacks of losing one data point, especially when colleges have other tools like essays, recommendations, and interviews to evaluate applicants. Test-optional admissions represent a meaningful step toward a more equitable college admissions process."

**Paragraph B:**
"Colleges should go test-optional permanently because standardized tests are unfair and don't actually predict who will succeed in college. The NCES has data showing that wealthy students score way higher on the SAT, so really these tests just measure how much money your family has. During COVID, colleges had to go test-optional and guess what, the sky didn't fall. Students were just fine without submitting test scores. People who want to keep requiring tests are basically saying they want to keep a system that benefits rich kids at the expense of everyone else. If standardized tests disappeared from admissions tomorrow, college would be more accessible and more diverse, and that's something everyone should want. It's time to stop pretending these tests serve any useful purpose."

**Paragraph C:**
"The COVID-era shift to test-optional admissions gave colleges an unplanned experiment, and the early data suggests that the relationship between test scores and college success is weaker than the testing industry has long claimed. NCES data shows that high school GPA is a stronger predictor of first-year college performance than SAT or ACT scores across most institutional types. Researchers writing in The Conversation have reported that many test-optional schools saw more diverse applicant pools without a measurable decline in academic quality, a finding that challenges the assumption that scores are essential gatekeepers. Defenders of standardized admissions testing argue that GPA is inflated and inconsistent across high schools, making test scores the only common yardstick available. This is a serious methodological concern: a 4.0 at a school with rampant grade inflation means something very different from a 4.0 at a school with rigorous standards. Yet the solution to GPA inconsistency need not be a return to tests that, as NCES data confirms, correlate as strongly with family income as with academic ability. Colleges could instead invest in transcript contextualization tools and holistic review processes that weigh course rigor, school profile, and extracurricular depth. Permanentizing test-optional policies is not a concession to lower standards but a recognition that the blunt instrument of a composite score obscures more than it reveals about a student's readiness for college-level work."

**Answer Key:**
- **A:** Adequate -- Cites NCES and Conversation sources with relevant data, takes a clear position, and engages the GPA-comparability counterargument ("this is a fair point"). However, the rebuttal ("benefits outweigh drawbacks") is asserted rather than demonstrated, and the analysis doesn't reach Effect level on how holistic review would work at scale.
- **B:** Weak -- Binary framing ("don't actually predict," "no useful purpose"), dismissive tone ("guess what, the sky didn't fall"), counterargument strawmanned ("basically saying they want to keep a system that benefits rich kids"), and no analysis of how admissions would function without any standardized measure. Treats a complex policy question as if it has an obvious answer.
- **C:** Strong -- Frames the debate around predictive validity evidence, synthesizes NCES GPA data with Conversation reporting on test-optional outcomes, concedes the GPA-inconsistency counterargument with genuine weight before proposing transcript contextualization as an alternative, and reaches Effect level on what "readiness" actually means.
- **Key Progression:** From "tests are useless, drop them" to "tests are flawed, the benefits of dropping them outweigh the costs" to "the predictive data is mixed, and here are specific alternatives that address the legitimate concerns on both sides" -- the strongest writer replaces the dropped tool with a better one rather than leaving a gap.
