# Thesis Triage Exercise (C.9.07)

## Activity Instructions

Sort each thesis statement into one of three categories: **Strong**, **Weak**, or **Fixable**. Use the shared vocabulary (defensible, arguable, line of reasoning, "So What?" test, S-F-E) to justify your sorting decision.

---

## Thesis Statements

---

### TH-01 | Passage: SP-001

**Thesis:** "In 'What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?' Frederick Douglass deliberately opens with restrained, respectful praise of the Founders before pivoting to scorching denunciation of American hypocrisy, a structural shift designed to first earn the goodwill of his white audience so that his subsequent indictment of their complicity in slavery lands with inescapable moral force."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Strong
- **Explanation:** This thesis is defensible, specific, and arguable. It identifies a concrete authorial choice (the structural shift from praise to denunciation), names the rhetorical purpose (earning goodwill before indicting the audience), and provides a clear line of reasoning that connects technique to effect. It passes the "So What?" test because it explains *why* the structural choice matters -- not just *what* Douglass does, but *how* and *to what end*.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** N/A

### TH-02 | Passage: SP-002

**Thesis:** "Sojourner Truth uses repetition in her speech 'Ain't I a Woman?'"

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Weak
- **Explanation:** This thesis is **too vague** and functions as an observation rather than an argument. It identifies a technique (repetition) but says nothing about its purpose, effect, or significance. It fails the "So What?" test entirely -- a reader would respond, "So what? What does the repetition *do*?" There is no line of reasoning, no claim about why the repetition matters, and nothing arguable; no reasonable person would disagree that Truth uses repetition.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** N/A

---

### TH-03 | Passage: ES-002

**Thesis:** "Emerson's aphoristic style in 'Self-Reliance' makes his argument about individualism more persuasive and memorable."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Fixable
- **Explanation:** This thesis has the seed of a strong argument -- it connects a specific technique (aphoristic style) to an effect (persuasion, memorability). However, it remains partially vague: *how* does the aphoristic style make the argument more persuasive? What is it about short, self-contained maxims that serves his argument about individualism specifically? The line of reasoning is gestured at but not developed. It needs sharpening to explain the mechanism by which style serves purpose.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** "In 'Self-Reliance,' Emerson's reliance on pithy, self-contained aphorisms -- such as 'Trust thyself' and 'A foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds' -- structurally enacts his argument for individualism by presenting each idea as a freestanding truth that requires no external authority, modeling for the reader the very intellectual independence the essay champions."

### TH-04 | Passage: SP-002

**Thesis:** "Through the repeated refrain 'Ain't I a Woman?' Sojourner Truth systematically dismantles each argument used to justify denying women's rights -- physical weakness, intellectual inferiority, and theological subordination -- by positioning her own lived experience as irrefutable counterevidence, forcing her audience to confront the contradiction between their justifications and the reality standing before them."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Strong
- **Explanation:** This thesis is defensible and highly specific. It identifies the authorial choice (the repeated refrain used as a refutation device), names the specific arguments Truth targets, explains her method (lived experience as counterevidence), and articulates the effect on the audience (confronting contradiction). The line of reasoning is fully developed: technique leads to method leads to audience impact. It is arguable because another reader might claim Truth's rhetorical power comes from a different source.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** N/A

### TH-05 | Passage: SP-001

**Thesis:** "Frederick Douglass gave his Fourth of July speech in 1852 to a crowd in Rochester, New York, where he argued that the holiday was hypocritical because enslaved people were not free."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Weak
- **Explanation:** This thesis is **summary-based** rather than analytical. It recounts biographical facts and paraphrases Douglass's argument without making any claim about *how* he constructs that argument or *why* his rhetorical choices matter. There is no analysis of authorial choices, no line of reasoning connecting technique to purpose, and nothing arguable -- it is a factual report that could appear in an encyclopedia entry.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** N/A

### TH-06 | Passage: SP-001

**Thesis:** "Douglass uses irony in 'What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?' to show his audience that slavery is wrong and that the celebration is meaningless to enslaved people."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Fixable
- **Explanation:** This thesis identifies a real technique (irony) and gestures toward its purpose, but it lacks specificity and analytical depth. "To show his audience that slavery is wrong" is too broad -- Douglass's audience already included many abolitionists. The thesis does not explain *how* the irony functions, what specific forms it takes, or what rhetorical effect it achieves beyond stating the obvious. The line of reasoning needs to move from naming the tool to explaining the mechanism.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** "In 'What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?' Douglass weaponizes irony by lavishing praise on the Declaration of Independence's ideals of liberty and equality only to reveal, through biting juxtaposition, that his audience's nation systematically violates every principle it celebrates -- a strategy that transforms the Fourth of July from a source of national pride into evidence of national guilt."

### TH-07 | Passage: ES-002

**Thesis:** "In 'Self-Reliance,' Emerson constructs his argument not through conventional logical progression but through an accumulation of bold, declarative aphorisms that function as rhetorical ultimatums, a structure that mirrors his central claim by refusing to justify individualism through external reasoning and instead asserting it as self-evident truth."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Strong
- **Explanation:** This thesis is defensible, specific, and deeply arguable. It makes a claim about how the essay's *form* embodies its *content* -- the structure (aphoristic, non-linear) mirrors the philosophy (self-reliance, rejection of external authority). The line of reasoning is sophisticated: Emerson's refusal to argue conventionally *is itself* the argument for individualism. It passes the "So What?" test because it reveals something genuinely insightful about the relationship between style and meaning. Another reader could reasonably argue that Emerson *does* employ logical reasoning, making this defensible but debatable.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** N/A

### TH-08 | Passage: ES-002

**Thesis:** "Emerson's 'Self-Reliance' is a very important essay that has influenced many people throughout history."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Weak
- **Explanation:** This thesis is **not arguable** -- it makes a vague evaluative claim ("very important") supported by an equally vague assertion ("influenced many people") that no one would dispute and no one could meaningfully defend in an analytical essay. It says nothing about the text itself, identifies no authorial choices, and offers no line of reasoning. It reads as a book-jacket blurb rather than an analytical claim.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** N/A

---

### TH-09 | Passage: SP-002

**Thesis:** "Sojourner Truth's 'Ain't I a Woman?' is effective because she connects with her audience by using emotional appeals and logical reasoning to argue for women's rights."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Fixable
- **Explanation:** This thesis is on the right track -- it tries to connect technique to purpose and mentions audience awareness. However, it remains generic: "emotional appeals and logical reasoning" could describe almost any persuasive speech. It does not identify *which* emotional appeals, *what kind* of logical reasoning, or *how* these work together in Truth's specific rhetorical context. The word "effective" also begs the question: effective at what, specifically? The core idea (Truth's dual use of emotion and logic) is worth developing with concrete specificity.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** "In 'Ain't I a Woman?' Sojourner Truth pairs the raw emotional weight of her personal testimony -- bearing thirteen children, enduring backbreaking labor -- with precise logical refutation of each anti-women's-rights argument, a dual strategy that leaves her audience unable to dismiss her claims as either sentimental or abstract."

### TH-10 | Passage: SP-002

**Thesis:** "Sojourner Truth's 'Ain't I a Woman?' speech is inspiring and powerful."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Weak
- **Explanation:** This thesis is **too obvious** and amounts to a personal reaction rather than an analytical claim. Calling the speech "inspiring and powerful" is a value judgment that most readers would share, but it provides no analytical foothold -- it identifies no authorial choices, offers no line of reasoning, and cannot be developed into a sustained argument. An analytical thesis must move beyond "I liked it" to "here is how and why it works."
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** N/A

### TH-11 | Passage: SP-001

**Thesis:** "By addressing his Rochester audience with the pointed second-person pronoun 'your' rather than the inclusive 'our' when referring to the Fourth of July, Douglass performs a rhetorical act of exclusion that forces his listeners to experience, if only momentarily, the alienation that enslaved Americans endure permanently -- transforming a grammatical choice into an instrument of moral confrontation."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Strong
- **Explanation:** This thesis is defensible, specific, and passes the "So What?" test with force. It identifies a precise authorial choice (the pronoun shift from "our" to "your"), explains its rhetorical function (performing exclusion), and articulates its deeper significance (making the audience feel alienation as a route to moral confrontation). The line of reasoning moves from a small textual detail to a large rhetorical effect, modeling strong S-F-E thinking. It is arguable because a reader could interpret the pronoun choice differently -- as distancing rather than confrontational, for instance.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** N/A

### TH-12 | Passage: ES-002

**Thesis:** "Emerson uses a lot of interesting literary devices in 'Self-Reliance' to get his point across about being yourself."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Fixable
- **Explanation:** This thesis has a recoverable core idea -- Emerson's stylistic choices serve his argument for individualism -- but it is currently too vague to be useful. "A lot of interesting literary devices" names nothing specific; "get his point across" is imprecise; "being yourself" is a casual reduction of Emerson's philosophy. However, a student who wrote this clearly understands that technique serves purpose. The fix requires naming specific devices, specifying the argument, and articulating the connection.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** "In 'Self-Reliance,' Emerson employs paradox and metaphor -- comparing society to a 'joint-stock company' and nonconformity to divine self-trust -- to reframe his audience's understanding of individualism not as selfishness but as a moral obligation, challenging the assumption that conformity equals virtue."

### TH-13 | Passage: SP-002

**Thesis:** "Sojourner Truth structures 'Ain't I a Woman?' as a series of direct refutations aimed at specific male arguments against women's equality, a debate-like framework that transforms her speech from a plea for sympathy into an intellectual confrontation, positioning Truth not as a supplicant requesting rights but as a logician exposing the incoherence of her opponents' claims."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Strong
- **Explanation:** This thesis is defensible, arguable, and rich with a clear line of reasoning. It identifies the structural choice (series of direct refutations), names its effect (transforming plea into confrontation), and makes a sophisticated claim about how this repositions Truth's ethos (from supplicant to logician). It passes the "So What?" test because it reveals how structure shapes the audience's perception of the speaker's authority. It is arguable because another reader might contend that Truth's emotional testimony is equally central to her authority.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** N/A

### TH-14 | Passage: SP-001

**Thesis:** "I think Frederick Douglass's speech is the best piece of American oratory ever written because it is so well argued."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Weak
- **Explanation:** This thesis commits the flaw of being **a statement of personal taste disguised as analysis**. The superlative ranking ("best piece of American oratory ever written") is neither defensible nor productive for an analytical essay -- it invites a ranking debate rather than textual analysis. The justification ("so well argued") is circular and vague. An analytical thesis must move beyond evaluation to explanation: not *whether* the speech is good, but *how* specific choices achieve specific effects.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** N/A

### TH-15 | Passage: SP-001

**Thesis:** "Douglass manipulates his audience in 'What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?' by making them feel guilty about slavery."

**Answer Key:**
- **Rating:** Fixable
- **Explanation:** This thesis identifies something real and analytically productive -- Douglass does strategically target his audience's emotions, and guilt is a central part of his rhetorical design. However, "manipulates" is imprecise (it implies deception rather than persuasion), and the thesis does not explain *how* Douglass generates that guilt or *why* guilt specifically serves his rhetorical purpose. The line of reasoning stops at the effect without tracing the mechanism. With specificity about technique and a clearer articulation of purpose, this becomes a strong thesis.
- **If Fixable, Model Fix:** "In 'What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?' Douglass strategically cultivates guilt in his white audience by first validating their patriotic pride in the Founders and then systematically demonstrating that their continued tolerance of slavery betrays those very ideals, a technique that makes his listeners' own values the standard by which they must condemn themselves."
