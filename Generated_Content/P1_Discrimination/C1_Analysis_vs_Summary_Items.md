## Pair 1 -- Paine's Opening Line

### AS-01 | Passage: SP-030

**Passage Moment:** The opening sentence, "These are the times that try men's souls."

**Sentence:** "Paine uses personification in his opening line when he says that the times 'try men's souls.'"

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Summary
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure only
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies a device (personification) and locates it in the text, but it stops there. It does not explain what the personification accomplishes or how it affects the reader. Naming a technique without explaining its function or effect is a hallmark of summary-level writing.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** "Paine's personification in 'These are the times that try men's souls' serves to transform an abstract political crisis into a visceral, personal ordeal, thereby positioning each colonist's inner resolve -- not military strategy -- as the true battleground of the revolution."

---

### AS-02 | Passage: SP-030

**Passage Moment:** The opening sentence, "These are the times that try men's souls."

**Sentence:** "By personifying the era itself as an adversary that 'tries' the soul, Paine reframes the Revolutionary War not as a distant political conflict but as an intimate spiritual test, thereby compelling his audience to see inaction as a failure of personal character rather than mere political disagreement."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Analysis
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function + Effect
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies the device (personification -- Structure), explains what it does rhetorically (reframes war as spiritual test -- Function), and articulates the impact on the audience (compels them to equate inaction with personal failure -- Effect). The verb "compelling" and the connector "thereby" signal the causal chain characteristic of analysis.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** N/A

## Pair 2 -- Paine's Emotional-Logical Blend

### AS-03 | Passage: SP-030

**Passage Moment:** Paine's combination of emotional appeals with logical argument in the opening paragraph

**Sentence:** "In the opening of *The Crisis*, Paine discusses both the emotional and logical sides of the conflict, talking about how the soldiers feel and also giving reasons why the cause is just."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Summary
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure only
- **Explanation:** The sentence recognizes that emotion and logic coexist in the passage but merely retells this observation. The verbs "discusses" and "talking about" are summary signals. There is no explanation of why Paine blends these appeals or what effect the blend creates for the audience.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** "Paine's deliberate interweaving of visceral emotional language with calm logical reasoning serves to disarm skeptics who might dismiss pure passion as hysteria, thereby constructing an argument that validates the reader's fear while simultaneously demonstrating that courage is the only rational response."

---

### AS-04 | Passage: SP-030

**Passage Moment:** Paine's combination of emotional appeals with logical argument in the opening paragraph

**Sentence:** "Paine strategically alternates between emotional urgency and measured reasoning, a structure that serves to preempt the objection that revolutionary fervor is mere recklessness; by grounding each passionate appeal in a logical warrant, he positions the patriot cause as both morally felt and intellectually defensible, reinforcing the reader's confidence that action is not impulse but duty."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Analysis
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function + Effect
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies the structural pattern (alternation of emotion and logic), explains the rhetorical function (preempts accusations of recklessness), and traces the effect on the reader (reinforces confidence that action equals duty). The verbs "serves to," "positions," and "reinforcing" all signal analytical reasoning.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** N/A

## Pair 3 -- Paine's Urgency and Diction

### AS-05 | Passage: SP-030

**Passage Moment:** Paine's use of urgent, present-tense diction ("these are the times")

**Sentence:** "Paine uses present tense and urgent words at the beginning of his pamphlet to grab the reader's attention and make the crisis feel immediate."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Summary
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function (partial)
- **Explanation:** This is a "tricky" near-analysis sentence. It identifies the device (present tense, urgent diction) and gestures toward a function ("grab attention," "feel immediate"), but it stops at a generic, surface-level function without reaching a specific effect. "Grab attention" could describe any hook in any text; it does not explain what Paine's specific urgency accomplishes within his argument about the Revolution.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** "Paine's insistence on the present tense -- 'these ARE the times' -- collapses any comfortable temporal distance between the reader and the crisis, thereby undermining the impulse to defer action and positioning hesitation itself as a form of complicity with tyranny."

---

### AS-06 | Passage: SP-030

**Passage Moment:** Paine's use of urgent, present-tense diction ("these are the times")

**Sentence:** "Paine's demonstrative 'these' and present-tense 'are' collapse the distance between the reader and the Revolutionary crisis, a move that serves to eliminate the psychological refuge of 'someday'; by forcing the audience into an inescapable present, Paine undermines any rationalization for delay and positions the pamphlet itself as a call to arms that demands an immediate verdict from every reader's conscience."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Analysis
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function + Effect
- **Explanation:** The sentence pinpoints specific structural choices (demonstrative pronoun, present tense), explains the rhetorical function (eliminates temporal distance and the refuge of delay), and traces the audience-level effect (demands an immediate moral verdict). The verbs "undermines" and "positions" drive the causal reasoning.

## Pair 4 -- Bacon's Parallel Structure

### AS-07 | Passage: ES-006

**Passage Moment:** Bacon's use of triadic parallel structure ("Studies serve for delight, for ornament, and for ability")

**Sentence:** "Bacon uses parallel structure when he lists the three purposes of studies: 'for delight, for ornament, and for ability.'"

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Summary
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure only
- **Explanation:** The sentence correctly identifies parallel structure and quotes the relevant passage, but it only names the device and locates it. There is no explanation of why Bacon chose parallelism or what effect the triadic rhythm has on the reader's understanding of his argument. The verb "uses" is a summary-level verb.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** "Bacon's triadic parallelism in 'for delight, for ornament, and for ability' imposes a sense of logical completeness on the purposes of study, thereby positioning his taxonomy as exhaustive and self-evident rather than debatable, which reinforces the essay's authoritative, aphoristic tone."

---

### AS-08 | Passage: ES-006

**Passage Moment:** Bacon's use of triadic parallel structure ("Studies serve for delight, for ornament, and for ability")

**Sentence:** "Bacon's triadic construction -- 'for delight, for ornament, and for ability' -- mirrors the rhetorical pattern of a logical proof, lending his classification an air of mathematical completeness that serves to foreclose debate; the reader is positioned not as a critic evaluating a claim but as a student receiving settled wisdom, which reinforces the essay's broader strategy of presenting opinion as axiom."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Analysis
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function + Effect
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies the triadic pattern (Structure), explains how it mimics logical proof to shut down counterargument (Function), and articulates the reader's repositioned role and the essay-level effect of presenting opinion as axiom (Effect). Multiple analytical verbs -- "serves to," "positioned," "reinforces" -- chain the reasoning together.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** N/A

## Pair 5 -- Bacon's Concision

### AS-09 | Passage: ES-006

**Passage Moment:** Bacon's extreme brevity and aphoristic sentence style

**Sentence:** "Bacon's sentences in 'Of Studies' are very short and concise, and he mentions many different ideas in just a few words, which makes the essay feel dense and packed with meaning."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Summary
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function (partial)
- **Explanation:** Another "tricky" near-analysis sentence. It identifies the structural feature (short, concise sentences) and offers a vague functional observation ("dense and packed with meaning"), but it never explains why Bacon's brevity matters argumentatively or what effect it has on the reader's relationship to the claims. "Packed with meaning" describes a quality without analyzing its rhetorical purpose.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** "Bacon's aphoristic brevity -- packing entire arguments into single clauses -- denies the reader the elaboration that would invite scrutiny, thereby positioning each assertion as a self-evident truth and reinforcing a power dynamic in which the essayist dispenses wisdom and the reader simply receives it."

---

### AS-10 | Passage: ES-006

**Passage Moment:** Bacon's extreme brevity and aphoristic sentence style

**Sentence:** "Bacon's refusal to elaborate -- delivering claims in compressed, aphoristic bursts rather than extended argument -- serves to deny the reader the discursive space in which objections could form; each sentence lands as a finished verdict rather than an opening proposition, thereby reinforcing Bacon's self-positioning as an authority who catalogues truths rather than one who argues for them."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Analysis
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function + Effect
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies the compressed style (Structure), explains how brevity suppresses counterargument (Function), and traces the effect on the reader-author dynamic and Bacon's ethos (Effect). The phrase "serves to deny" and the connector "thereby reinforcing" demonstrate the causal chain that distinguishes analysis from summary.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** N/A

## Pair 6 -- Bacon's Prescriptive Tone

### AS-11 | Passage: ES-006

**Passage Moment:** Bacon's prescriptive, instructional voice ("Read not to contradict and confute...")

**Sentence:** "In 'Of Studies,' Bacon tells the reader how they should approach reading, saying they should not read just to argue with others but to think carefully about what they read."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Summary
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure only
- **Explanation:** The sentence paraphrases Bacon's content -- it retells what he says rather than analyzing how or why he says it. The verb "tells" and the paraphrase structure are classic summary indicators. There is no attention to the rhetorical effect of Bacon's prescriptive mode.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** "Bacon's use of the imperative mood -- 'Read not to contradict and confute' -- positions the reader as a subordinate receiving instruction rather than a peer engaging in dialogue, a move that serves to enact the very hierarchy of knowledge the essay argues for, thereby reinforcing the claim that studies confer 'ability' on those who approach them with proper discipline."

---

### AS-12 | Passage: ES-006

**Passage Moment:** Bacon's prescriptive, instructional voice ("Read not to contradict and confute...")

**Sentence:** "Bacon's shift into the imperative mood -- 'Read not to contradict and confute' -- enacts the essay's argument at the level of syntax: the reader is commanded rather than persuaded, a structural choice that serves to model the very intellectual discipline Bacon advocates, thereby positioning the essay itself as the first 'study' that the audience must learn to receive properly."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Analysis
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function + Effect
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies the imperative mood (Structure), explains how the command models the discipline the essay advocates (Function), and traces the meta-rhetorical effect of turning the essay into its own example (Effect). The analysis connects form to argument in a way that reveals something non-obvious about the text.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** N/A

## Pair 7 -- Truth's Repetition of "Ain't I a Woman?"

### AS-13 | Passage: SP-002

**Passage Moment:** The repeated refrain "Ain't I a woman?"

**Sentence:** "Sojourner Truth uses repetition throughout her speech by asking 'Ain't I a woman?' several times to emphasize her point about women's rights."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Summary
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure only
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies repetition and quotes the refrain, but "to emphasize her point" is a generic placeholder that could apply to any use of repetition in any speech. It does not explain what specific argumentative work the refrain performs or how it dismantles the opposition's logic. The verb "uses" is a summary-level verb.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** "Truth's repeated refrain 'Ain't I a woman?' serves to turn each preceding example of her physical endurance into a logical premise in an accumulating syllogism: if womanhood supposedly requires delicacy, and Truth has demonstrably survived brutality, then the audience must either redefine womanhood or admit that their definition was designed to exclude, thereby exposing the category itself as a tool of oppression rather than a biological fact."

---

### AS-14 | Passage: SP-002

**Passage Moment:** The repeated refrain "Ain't I a woman?"

**Sentence:** "Each iteration of 'Ain't I a woman?' transforms the preceding autobiographical detail into a logical premise, so that by the speech's climax the refrain functions not as a plea for inclusion but as an indictment; Truth's repetition thereby dismantles the nineteenth-century cult of femininity by forcing the audience to confront the contradiction between their definition of womanhood and the undeniable evidence of her lived experience."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Analysis
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function + Effect
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies the repeated refrain (Structure), explains how each repetition converts narrative into logical proof (Function), and traces the audience-level effect of exposing a cultural contradiction (Effect). The shift from "plea" to "indictment" demonstrates the kind of interpretive claim that separates analysis from summary.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** N/A

## Pair 8 -- Truth's Rhetorical Questions

### AS-15 | Passage: SP-002

**Passage Moment:** Truth's use of rhetorical questions to challenge her opponents

**Sentence:** "Truth asks rhetorical questions in her speech to make her audience think about whether women really are as weak as men claim they are."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Summary
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function (partial)
- **Explanation:** This is a "tricky" near-analysis sentence. It identifies rhetorical questions (Structure) and offers a vague function ("make her audience think"), but "make the audience think" is too generic to count as genuine functional analysis. It does not explain the specific argumentative mechanism by which the questions dismantle the opposition's logic, nor does it reach a concrete effect.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** "Truth's rhetorical questions do not merely invite reflection; they weaponize the interrogative form by embedding their own answers, so that each question serves to corner the listener into an admission -- either women are not inherently weak, or the category of 'woman' has been fraudulently constructed -- thereby forcing the audience to dismantle its own premises rather than passively receive Truth's counterargument."

---

### AS-16 | Passage: SP-002

**Passage Moment:** Truth's use of rhetorical questions to challenge her opponents

**Sentence:** "Truth's rhetorical questions function as logical traps rather than genuine inquiries: each question embeds its own irrefutable answer, which serves to shift the burden of proof onto her opponents; the audience is not asked to consider whether Truth deserves equality but is instead cornered into explaining why she does not, thereby repositioning the defenders of inequality as the ones whose claims require justification."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Analysis
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function + Effect
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies rhetorical questions (Structure), explains how they embed answers and shift the burden of proof (Function), and traces the effect of repositioning the opposition as the party that must justify itself (Effect). The verbs "serves to shift" and "repositioning" signal causal analytical reasoning.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** N/A

## Pair 9 -- Truth's Refutation of the "Intellect" Argument

### AS-17 | Passage: SP-002

**Passage Moment:** Truth's response to the claim that women cannot have equal rights because they are less intelligent

**Sentence:** "Truth also addresses the argument that women are not as smart as men, and she mentions that even if that were true, it would not be a good reason to deny women their rights."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Summary
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure only
- **Explanation:** The sentence paraphrases Truth's counterargument without analyzing her rhetorical strategy. "Addresses" and "mentions" are summary-level verbs. The sentence retells what Truth says but does not examine how her refutation works as a piece of argumentative architecture or what effect her concession-then-pivot strategy has on the audience.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** "Truth's strategic concession -- momentarily granting the premise that women may have less 'intellect' -- serves to reframe the debate by separating the question of capacity from the question of rights; this move undermines the opposition's strongest-seeming argument by revealing it as irrelevant, thereby positioning her adversaries as people who confuse justification with rationalization."

---

### AS-18 | Passage: SP-002

**Passage Moment:** Truth's response to the claim that women cannot have equal rights because they are less intelligent

**Sentence:** "Rather than directly denying the claim that women possess inferior intellect, Truth deploys a strategic concession that serves to detach the question of intelligence from the question of rights entirely; by granting her opponent's premise and then demonstrating its irrelevance, she undermines the logical foundation of the argument more effectively than a direct rebuttal would, thereby exposing the intellect claim as a distraction designed to avoid the moral core of the debate."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Analysis
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function + Effect
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies the concession strategy (Structure), explains how conceding the premise detaches intelligence from rights (Function), and traces the effect of exposing the opposition's argument as a deliberate distraction from the moral question (Effect). The comparative note -- "more effectively than a direct rebuttal would" -- adds evaluative depth that is characteristic of strong analysis.

## Pair 10 -- Truth's Autobiographical Evidence

### AS-19 | Passage: SP-002

**Passage Moment:** Truth's references to her own physical labor and suffering as evidence

**Sentence:** "In her speech, Truth talks about how she has worked in the fields, carried heavy loads, and endured suffering, and she uses these personal experiences to support her argument that women are strong."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Summary
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure only
- **Explanation:** The sentence catalogs Truth's autobiographical examples and states their general purpose ("to support her argument"), but it does not analyze the rhetorical mechanism by which personal testimony functions as proof in this specific argumentative context. "Talks about" and "uses" are summary-level verbs. The observation that personal experience "supports" an argument is too generic to qualify as analysis.
- **If Summary, Model Revision:** "Truth's catalog of physical endurance -- plowing, planting, bearing the lash -- serves to transform her own body into irrefutable counter-evidence, an argumentative strategy that bypasses abstract debate entirely; because her suffering is undeniable and publicly known, each autobiographical detail positions her opponents as people who must deny observable reality in order to maintain their claims, thereby rendering their position not merely wrong but absurd."

---

### AS-20 | Passage: SP-002

**Passage Moment:** Truth's references to her own physical labor and suffering as evidence

**Sentence:** "Truth's accumulation of autobiographical detail -- plowing, lifting, enduring the lash -- serves to convert her lived body into a form of evidence that cannot be dismissed as theoretical or abstract; each physical fact functions as a premise in an embodied syllogism whose conclusion the audience has already been forced to accept by the time the refrain arrives, thereby rendering any further defense of female frailty not merely unconvincing but visibly dishonest in the presence of the speaker herself."

**Answer Key:**
- **Classification:** Analysis
- **S-F-E Level Reached:** Structure + Function + Effect
- **Explanation:** The sentence identifies the pattern of accumulated autobiographical detail (Structure), explains how the body becomes undeniable evidence that bypasses abstraction (Function), and traces the effect on the audience -- the defense of female frailty becomes dishonest in Truth's physical presence (Effect). The phrase "serves to convert" and the connector "thereby rendering" build the causal chain. The concept of an "embodied syllogism" demonstrates interpretive depth.
