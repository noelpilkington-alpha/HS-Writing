# A1 L02: Taking a Position -- Content Items

## Coping Model: Thesis Sharpening

**Topic:** Social media and teenagers

Below is a step-by-step revision showing how a writer moves from a weak position to a strong one. Notice the false starts and self-talk -- this is what real revision sounds like.

---

**Step 1 (Weak):** "Social media is bad."

- *Debatable?* Barely -- it's so broad that most people would just shrug.
- *Specific?* No -- bad for whom? Bad how? Which platforms?
- *Supportable?* Too vague to even know what evidence to look for.

*Writer's self-talk:* "Okay, that's basically just a feeling. I need to say WHO it's bad for and WHAT kind of bad I mean."

---

**Step 2 (Better but still weak):** "Social media is bad for teenagers."

- *Debatable?* Somewhat -- some people would push back, but it's still a blanket statement.
- *Specific?* Getting there -- at least we know the audience is teenagers.
- *Supportable?* Still vague -- bad how? Grades? Mental health? Sleep? All of the above?

*Writer's self-talk:* "Better, but 'bad' is doing too much work. I need to pick ONE specific problem and say what should actually happen."

---

**Step 3 (Strong):** "Social media platforms should be required to disable algorithmic feeds for users under 16."

- *Debatable?* Yes -- reasonable people disagree. Some say algorithms are fine; others say parents, not platforms, should decide.
- *Specific?* Yes -- names the mechanism (algorithmic feeds) and the age (under 16).
- *Supportable?* Yes -- can point to research on how algorithm-driven content affects adolescent mental health, plus real legislative proposals already in play.

*Writer's self-talk:* "Now THAT is something I can actually argue. Someone could write a solid counter-argument against this, and I can defend it with real evidence."

---

### The 3-Test Checklist

Use this checklist every time you draft a position statement.

1. **Debatable** -- Could a reasonable person disagree? If everyone would agree, it's a fact, not a position.
2. **Specific** -- Does it name what, who, and how? Vague = weak.
3. **Supportable** -- Can you give at least 2 reasons with evidence? If not, narrow or change it.

## Weak/Strong Position Pairs

Directions: For each pair, decide which position is stronger and which is weaker. Use the 3-Test Checklist to explain your reasoning.

---

### WS-01 | School Policies

**Position A:** "Schools should replace traditional letter grades with mastery-based progress reports that show exactly which skills a student has and hasn't demonstrated."

**Position B:** "The grading system needs to change."

**Stronger Position:** A

**Why the other is weak:** Position B fails the Specific test -- it doesn't say who should change grading, what the replacement would look like, or how the change would work. You can't argue for "change" without saying what that change actually is.

**Weakness Type:** Too vague

---

### WS-02 | Technology and AI

**Position A:** "Smartphones use electricity and connect to the internet."

**Position B:** "High schools should allow students to use AI writing tools for brainstorming and outlining but prohibit AI-generated text in final submitted drafts."

**Stronger Position:** B

**Why the other is weak:** Position A fails the Debatable test -- it's a simple factual statement that no reasonable person would argue against. There's nothing to defend because there's nothing to dispute.

**Weakness Type:** Obvious fact

---

### WS-03 | Environment

**Position A:** "Every fossil fuel power plant in the country should be shut down within the next two years to stop climate change completely."

**Position B:** "Local governments should offer tax rebates to homeowners who install solar panels, making renewable energy accessible to middle-income families."

**Stronger Position:** B

**Why the other is weak:** Position A fails the Supportable test -- shutting down all fossil fuel plants in two years is logistically impossible, and no credible evidence suggests climate change can be "completely stopped." The extreme timeline and absolute promise make it indefensible.

**Weakness Type:** Too extreme

---

### WS-04 | Health and Sports

**Position A:** "Physical fitness is important for students."

**Position B:** "Public high schools should require at least 30 minutes of physical activity daily, integrated into the school schedule rather than replaced by study halls during exam weeks."

**Stronger Position:** B

**Why the other is weak:** Position A fails both the Debatable and Specific tests. Almost no one would argue that fitness is unimportant, and the statement doesn't say what should happen, for whom, or how. "Important" is a vague value judgment that doesn't lead anywhere.

**Weakness Type:** Vague value judgment

## Practice Prompts

Directions: For each broad topic below, narrow it down to a specific, debatable, supportable position statement. Use the 3-Test Checklist to verify your position before you finalize it.

---

### PP-01 | Homework

**Broad topic:** Homework

**Your task:** Narrow this to a specific, debatable, supportable position statement.

**Hint:** Think about WHO gets homework, WHAT kind of homework is the problem (or solution), and HOW MUCH is too much or too little. Consider whether all homework is the same or whether some types matter more than others.

**Sample strong position (reveal after writing yours):** "Teachers should eliminate homework that only requires copying or memorization and instead assign no more than 30 minutes of practice that applies skills taught in class that day."

---

### PP-02 | School Start Times

**Broad topic:** School start times

**Your task:** Narrow this to a specific, debatable, supportable position statement.

**Hint:** Think about WHO is affected most, WHAT time would be better and why, and WHAT evidence you could use about sleep and teen health. Consider the trade-offs -- what would have to change if start times shifted?

**Sample strong position (reveal after writing yours):** "High schools should start no earlier than 8:30 a.m. because adolescent sleep cycles make it nearly impossible for teenagers to be alert and learning-ready before that time."

---

### PP-03 | AI in Schools

**Broad topic:** Artificial intelligence in schools

**Your task:** Narrow this to a specific, debatable, supportable position statement.

**Hint:** Think about WHICH use of AI you want to address (grading? tutoring? writing? test prep?), WHO benefits or loses, and WHAT guardrails or rules should exist. Avoid an all-or-nothing claim.

**Sample strong position (reveal after writing yours):** "Schools should use AI-powered tutoring programs for math practice but require that all writing assignments be completed without generative AI so students develop their own voice and reasoning skills."
