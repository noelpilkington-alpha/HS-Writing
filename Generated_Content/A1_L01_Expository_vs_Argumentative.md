# A1 L01: From Expository to Argumentative -- Content Items

---

## Model Paragraphs

### Model A -- Expository (School Lunch)

**[Topic Sentence]** Most American public schools operate a lunch program that follows guidelines set by the United States Department of Agriculture. **[Supporting Sentence 1]** These programs offer students a meal that must include a protein, a grain, a fruit, a vegetable, and milk. **[Supporting Sentence 2]** Schools receive federal funding to help cover the cost of food, and families can apply for free or reduced-price meals based on household income. **[Supporting Sentence 3]** Cafeteria staff prepare hundreds of meals each day, balancing nutrition standards with student preferences. **[Concluding Sentence]** Through this system, school lunch programs provide millions of students with a midday meal during the academic year.

*(~79 words)*

---

### Model B -- Argumentative (School Lunch)

**[Topic Sentence]** Every student in America should receive free school lunch, regardless of family income. **[Supporting Sentence 1]** When meals are free for everyone, no student has to feel embarrassed about qualifying for reduced-price lunch or worry about unpaid meal debt. **[Supporting Sentence 2]** Research from the National Bureau of Economic Research shows that universal free lunch programs improve test scores and reduce disciplinary incidents. **[Supporting Sentence 3]** The cost of expanding these programs is small compared to the academic and social benefits students gain. **[Concluding Sentence]** Schools that truly want every student to succeed should start by making sure no one learns on an empty stomach.

*(~80 words)*

---

### Key Differences

- Model A topic sentence = **FACT** (no one would disagree that USDA guidelines exist)
- Model B topic sentence = **CLAIM** (someone could disagree -- they might argue families who can afford lunch should pay)
- Model A supporting sentences = **information** (how the program works, what meals include, how funding operates)
- Model B supporting sentences = **reasons + evidence** (stigma reduction, research findings, cost-benefit argument)
- Model A concluding sentence = **summary** (restates what the program does)
- Model B concluding sentence = **call to action / reinforcement** (urges schools to act)

## Sort Items

### Item 1

Social media platforms use algorithms to decide which posts appear in a user's feed. These algorithms track what a person likes, shares, and watches, then prioritize similar content. As a result, two people following the same accounts may see very different posts. This process, sometimes called a "filter bubble," shapes the online experience of billions of users every day.

- **Classification:** Expository
- **Topic Sentence:** "Social media platforms use algorithms to decide which posts appear in a user's feed."
- **Signal:** States a fact about how algorithms work. No position is taken on whether this is good or bad.

---

### Item 2

High schools should require every student to participate in at least one season of a team sport before graduation. Playing on a team teaches discipline, time management, and how to handle both winning and losing. Students who compete in athletics also report stronger connections with their classmates and higher motivation in the classroom. A one-season requirement would give every student access to these benefits without an overwhelming time commitment.

- **Classification:** Argumentative
- **Topic Sentence:** "High schools should require every student to participate in at least one season of a team sport before graduation."
- **Signal:** Takes a position someone could disagree with. The word "should" signals a claim, and opponents might argue that forced participation is unfair to students with other interests or physical limitations.

---

### Item 3

Electric vehicles run on rechargeable battery packs instead of gasoline engines. When plugged into a charging station, the battery stores electricity that powers an electric motor. Most modern electric vehicles can travel between 200 and 300 miles on a single charge. Charging stations are becoming more common in cities, shopping centers, and highway rest stops across the country.

- **Classification:** Expository
- **Topic Sentence:** "Electric vehicles run on rechargeable battery packs instead of gasoline engines."
- **Signal:** States a factual observation about how electric vehicles operate. The paragraph explains technology without arguing for or against it.

---

### Item 4

Schools need to stop assigning homework on weekends and holidays. Students already spend roughly seven hours a day in class, and many participate in sports, jobs, or family responsibilities after school. Weekend homework cuts into the limited free time young people have to rest, socialize, and explore personal interests. If learning cannot be accomplished during the school week, the problem is the schedule, not the student's spare time.

- **Classification:** Argumentative
- **Topic Sentence:** "Schools need to stop assigning homework on weekends and holidays."
- **Signal:** Takes a strong position someone could disagree with. "Need to stop" signals a demand for change. Teachers and parents might argue that weekend practice is essential for retention.

---

### Item 5

Screen time among teenagers has increased significantly over the past decade. According to a 2023 Gallup survey, the average American teen spends over four hours per day on screens outside of schoolwork. Health experts note that excessive screen use is linked to poor sleep quality and increased anxiety. Understanding these patterns can help families make more informed decisions about technology use at home.

- **Classification:** Expository (with argumentative undertones)
- **Topic Sentence:** "Screen time among teenagers has increased significantly over the past decade."
- **Signal:** The topic sentence states a factual trend. The paragraph presents data and expert findings, which keeps it mostly informative.
- **Discussion Note:** This item is tricky because the phrase "excessive screen use" carries a negative judgment, and the concluding sentence ("make more informed decisions") gently nudges the reader toward reducing screen time. However, the paragraph never makes an outright claim that teens *should* use screens less. It stays closer to expository because it reports what experts have found rather than openly arguing a position. In class discussion, reasonable students could defend either label -- and that ambiguity is exactly the point. Recognizing when writing *leans* toward a position without fully committing is an important skill.

## Quick Reference

| | Expository | Argumentative |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Explain or inform | Persuade or convince |
| **Topic Sentence** | States a fact or observation | Makes a claim someone could disagree with |
| **Support** | Information, details, examples | Reasons, evidence, logical connections |
| **Tone** | Neutral, objective | Committed, persuasive |
