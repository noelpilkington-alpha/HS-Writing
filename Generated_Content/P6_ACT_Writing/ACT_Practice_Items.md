### ACT-PD-01: Social Media Age Restrictions

**Issue:** Many lawmakers have proposed raising the minimum age for social media accounts from 13 to 16, arguing that early exposure harms adolescent mental health. Others believe such restrictions are impractical and infringe on young people's ability to connect and learn in a digital world. The question of how society should balance youth safety with digital access continues to provoke debate.

**Perspective 1:** Social media platforms should be restricted for children under 16 because adolescent brains are not equipped to handle the psychological manipulation built into these platforms, and protecting minors must take priority over corporate profits.

**Perspective 2:** Age restrictions are an ineffective approach because teens will find workarounds, and the real solution is teaching digital literacy so young people can navigate online spaces responsibly.

**Perspective 3:** Rather than restricting access by age, the government should regulate the platforms themselves, banning manipulative design features like infinite scroll and algorithmic feeds so that social media is safer for users of all ages.

**Task:** Complete a Perspective Map for all three perspectives. For each perspective, identify: (a) the core claim, (b) the underlying assumption, (c) what the perspective values most, and (d) what it overlooks.

**Model Answer:**

| Element | Perspective 1 | Perspective 2 | Perspective 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core Claim** | Minors under 16 should be banned from social media. | Age restrictions won't work; digital literacy education is the real answer. | Regulate platform design rather than restricting user access by age. |
| **Underlying Assumption** | Adolescent brains are uniquely vulnerable to social media's manipulative design, and removal is the most reliable form of protection. | Young people will circumvent bans, so empowerment through education is more practical than prohibition. | The danger lies in how platforms are built, not in who uses them; fix the product and the age question becomes irrelevant. |
| **Values Most** | Child safety and developmental well-being. | Individual agency, practicality, and education as empowerment. | Systemic reform and universal consumer protection. |
| **Overlooks** | The social and educational benefits teens gain from online communities, and the difficulty of enforcing age verification without invasive data collection. | The fact that digital literacy programs take years to develop and implement, leaving current adolescents unprotected in the meantime. | The political difficulty of regulating powerful tech companies and the likelihood that platforms will lobby against or find loopholes in design regulations. |

### ACT-PD-02: Standardized Testing in College Admissions

**Issue:** In recent years, many colleges have adopted test-optional admissions policies, while others insist that standardized tests like the SAT and ACT remain essential for evaluating applicants fairly. As institutions weigh equity against consistency, the role of standardized testing in higher education remains contested.

**Perspective 1:** Standardized tests should remain a central part of college admissions because they provide an objective, comparable measure of academic readiness that grades alone cannot offer, since grading standards vary widely across schools.

**Perspective 2:** Standardized testing should be eliminated from admissions because it systematically advantages wealthy students who can afford test prep, reinforcing socioeconomic inequality rather than measuring true ability.

**Perspective 3:** Rather than eliminating tests or keeping them as-is, colleges should redesign assessments to measure skills like critical thinking and problem-solving in contextualized tasks, moving beyond the narrow format of current standardized exams.

**Task:** Complete a Perspective Map for all three perspectives. For each perspective, identify: (a) the core claim, (b) the underlying assumption, (c) what the perspective values most, and (d) what it overlooks.

**Model Answer:**

| Element | Perspective 1 | Perspective 2 | Perspective 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core Claim** | Standardized tests should remain central to admissions as an objective benchmark. | Standardized tests should be eliminated because they perpetuate inequality. | Tests should be redesigned into contextualized skill-based assessments. |
| **Underlying Assumption** | A single uniform metric is necessary to compare students fairly across different educational contexts. | Test scores reflect access to resources more than they reflect ability, making them inherently unfair. | The problem is not measurement itself but the narrow format of current tests; better-designed assessments can be both fair and informative. |
| **Values Most** | Objectivity, comparability, and merit measured by consistent standards. | Equity, access, and dismantling systemic barriers. | Innovation, holistic measurement, and the alignment of assessment with meaningful skills. |
| **Overlooks** | The well-documented score gaps correlated with income and race, which undercut the claim of objectivity, as well as the narrow range of skills these tests actually measure. | The possibility that without any standardized measure, admissions may rely more heavily on subjective factors like recommendation letters and extracurriculars, which also favor privileged students. | The enormous cost and logistical difficulty of developing, validating, and scaling new assessment formats across thousands of institutions, and the time such a transition would require. |

### ACT-PD-03: Remote Work vs. Office Work

**Issue:** Since the rise of remote work, companies have debated whether to require employees to return to the office, allow fully remote arrangements, or adopt a hybrid model. Proponents of each approach cite productivity, culture, and employee well-being as key concerns. The future of the workplace remains a subject of sharp disagreement.

**Perspective 1:** Companies should require employees to return to the office because in-person collaboration fosters innovation, strengthens workplace culture, and allows managers to support employee development more effectively.

**Perspective 2:** Remote work should be the default for any job that can be done from home because it improves work-life balance, reduces commuting costs and emissions, and allows companies to hire talent from anywhere.

**Perspective 3:** A structured hybrid model, with set in-office days and remote days, offers the best of both worlds by preserving collaboration opportunities while still giving employees flexibility and autonomy.

**Task:** Complete a Perspective Map for all three perspectives. For each perspective, identify: (a) the core claim, (b) the underlying assumption, (c) what the perspective values most, and (d) what it overlooks.

**Model Answer:**

| Element | Perspective 1 | Perspective 2 | Perspective 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core Claim** | Employees should return to the office for the sake of collaboration, culture, and development. | Remote work should be the default because it benefits employees, companies, and the environment. | A structured hybrid model balances collaboration with flexibility. |
| **Underlying Assumption** | Physical proximity is essential for the spontaneous interactions and mentorship that drive innovation and career growth. | Technology has advanced enough to make remote collaboration fully effective, and the benefits of eliminating commutes outweigh any losses. | Neither extreme is optimal; a scheduled blend can capture the advantages of both while minimizing the downsides of each. |
| **Values Most** | Organizational cohesion, mentorship, and innovation through face-to-face interaction. | Individual autonomy, quality of life, environmental sustainability, and access to a global talent pool. | Compromise, structure, and pragmatic balance. |
| **Overlooks** | That many employees are equally or more productive at home, that forced office returns can drive attrition, and that "culture" often reflects management preference rather than employee experience. | That some roles and some employees genuinely benefit from in-person interaction, that remote work can increase isolation and blur work-life boundaries, and that not all home environments are conducive to focused work. | That "hybrid" can become the worst of both worlds if poorly implemented—employees may commute to the office only to sit on video calls, and scheduling set days can reduce the flexibility that makes remote work appealing. |

### ACT-SL-01: Social Media Age Restrictions

*(Uses the same issue and perspectives as ACT-PD-01.)*

**Task:** For each of the three perspectives, write one sentence explaining when the perspective is most compelling and one sentence explaining where it breaks down.

**Model Answer:**

**Perspective 1** (Ban social media for under-16s)
- **Most compelling when:** Research on adolescent mental health shows rising rates of anxiety, depression, and self-harm correlated with early social media use, making a strong case that the most direct protection is to delay access until brains are more developed.
- **Breaks down when:** Enforcement requires invasive age-verification systems such as government ID uploads, which raise serious privacy concerns, and determined teens routinely bypass such measures, rendering the ban symbolic rather than functional.

**Perspective 2** (Focus on digital literacy instead)
- **Most compelling when:** Schools that have implemented comprehensive digital literacy curricula report that students make more thoughtful choices online, suggesting that education can build durable skills that outlast any age-based restriction.
- **Breaks down when:** Digital literacy programs take years to design, fund, and roll out at scale, leaving millions of currently vulnerable adolescents with no near-term protection while the educational infrastructure catches up.

**Perspective 3** (Regulate platform design for all ages)
- **Most compelling when:** Evidence shows that features like autoplay, push notifications, and algorithmic amplification of extreme content are harmful to users across all age groups, which means targeting the design rather than the user addresses the root cause.
- **Breaks down when:** Technology companies wield enormous lobbying power and can redesign features faster than regulators can update rules, creating a regulatory cat-and-mouse game that may never achieve stable consumer protection.

### ACT-SL-02: Standardized Testing in College Admissions

*(Uses the same issue and perspectives as ACT-PD-02.)*

**Model Answer:**

- **Most compelling when:** Admissions officers must compare applicants from thousands of different high schools with wildly different grading practices, making a uniform benchmark genuinely useful for distinguishing academic preparation.

**Perspective 3** (Redesign assessments)
- **Most compelling when:** Pilot programs using portfolio-based or performance-based assessments have shown they can predict college success while also revealing skills—like sustained research and collaborative problem-solving—that traditional tests ignore.
- **Breaks down when:** Developing and norming new assessments across a nation of diverse school systems requires enormous investment, years of validation research, and broad institutional buy-in, none of which can be achieved quickly enough to help the students currently navigating admissions.

### ACT-SL-03: Remote Work vs. Office Work

*(Uses the same issue and perspectives as ACT-PD-03.)*

**Task:** For each of the three perspectives, write one sentence explaining when the perspective is most compelling and one sentence explaining where it breaks down.

**Model Answer:**

**Perspective 1** (Return to office)
- **Most compelling when:** Teams engaged in early-stage creative projects or complex problem-solving benefit measurably from the spontaneous whiteboard conversations and rapid feedback loops that happen naturally in a shared physical space.
- **Breaks down when:** Companies mandate full-time office returns primarily to justify real-estate costs or to exert managerial control, leading to talent attrition as skilled employees leave for competitors that offer flexibility.

**Perspective 2** (Default to remote)
- **Most compelling when:** Employees with long commutes, caregiving responsibilities, or disabilities report dramatically higher job satisfaction and productivity when freed from the requirement to be physically present five days a week.
- **Breaks down when:** Fully remote environments make it difficult for new hires and junior employees to absorb tacit organizational knowledge, build mentor relationships, and develop the professional networks that in-person proximity naturally supports.

**Perspective 3** (Structured hybrid)
- **Most compelling when:** Organizations that designate specific in-office collaboration days and specific remote deep-work days report both high employee satisfaction and strong team cohesion, suggesting the model can deliver measurable gains on both fronts.
- **Breaks down when:** The "structured" part of hybrid often devolves into rigid scheduling that pleases no one—commuters resent traveling to offices where half the team is still on Zoom, while remote-first employees feel their autonomy has been reduced to a half-measure.

### ACT-RM-01 | Affordable Housing

**Issue:** Cities across the country are debating how to address a growing affordable housing shortage. As rents rise and homelessness increases, leaders disagree on the most effective response.

**Perspective 1:** The government should fund the construction of large-scale public housing so that every resident has access to a safe, affordable place to live regardless of income.

**Perspective 2:** Cities should offer tax incentives and streamlined permits to private developers who include affordable units in their projects, using market forces to expand the housing supply.

**Perspective 3:** The housing crisis is ultimately a wages crisis; rather than building more units, policymakers should raise the minimum wage and strengthen labor protections so workers can afford existing housing.

**Which of the following best describes the relationship among these three perspectives?**

**(A)** All three perspectives share the assumption that government intervention is needed but disagree on the form it should take.

**(B)** Perspectives 1 and 2 share the assumption that the housing supply must increase, while Perspective 3 rejects that assumption and reframes the problem as one of income rather than inventory.

**(C)** Perspectives 1 and 3 focus on government spending, while Perspective 2 rejects government involvement entirely.

**(D)** Perspective 3 is a compromise between Perspectives 1 and 2, blending elements of both.

**Correct Answer: (B)**

**Rationale:** Perspectives 1 and 2 both take it as given that the fundamental problem is insufficient housing supply—one wants the government to build it, the other wants to incentivize the private sector to build it. Perspective 3 challenges this shared assumption entirely, arguing that the real issue is low wages, not low inventory. This is the classic "shared assumption rejected by a third" structure.

**Distractor Rationales:**
- **(A)** is partially true—all three do involve some government action—but it misses the deeper structural difference: Perspective 3 redefines the problem itself, not just the form of intervention. This option flattens a fundamental disagreement into a surface-level similarity.
- **(C)** mischaracterizes Perspective 2. Tax incentives and streamlined permits are forms of government involvement, not the absence of it. This option creates a false binary.
- **(D)** is incorrect because Perspective 3 does not blend supply-side strategies; it abandons the supply frame altogether in favor of a demand-side analysis. A compromise would still accept the shared premise.

### ACT-RM-02 | Campus Mental Health

**Issue:** Student mental health on college campuses has reached what many administrators call a crisis point. Universities are debating how to respond as demand for counseling services outpaces capacity.

**Perspective 1:** Universities must dramatically expand their counseling centers by hiring more therapists and reducing wait times, because students in crisis need immediate professional support.

**Perspective 2:** The focus should be on prevention rather than treatment: universities should redesign academic culture—reducing course loads, reforming grading, and eliminating unnecessary stressors—so fewer students reach the crisis point in the first place.

**Perspective 3:** The real gap is social connection; universities should invest in peer support programs, community-building initiatives, and residential life reforms that combat the isolation driving much of the mental health decline.

**Which of the following best describes the relationship among these three perspectives?**

**(A)** Perspectives 1 and 3 both address treatment, while Perspective 2 addresses prevention.

**(B)** Each perspective addresses a different aspect of the same problem—Perspective 1 targets the clinical response, Perspective 2 targets the academic causes, and Perspective 3 targets the social causes—meaning they are more complementary than contradictory.

**(C)** Perspective 2 is the most radical because it challenges the structure of the university, while Perspectives 1 and 3 accept the status quo.

**(D)** All three perspectives assume the university is responsible for student mental health, making them essentially identical in their reasoning.

**Correct Answer: (B)**

**Rationale:** Rather than directly contradicting one another, these three perspectives each zoom in on a different dimension of the campus mental health crisis: clinical infrastructure (Perspective 1), academic pressure (Perspective 2), and social isolation (Perspective 3). A comprehensive strategy could plausibly incorporate all three. The key relationship is that they address different facets of one problem rather than offering mutually exclusive solutions.

**Distractor Rationales:**
- **(A)** misclassifies Perspective 3. Peer support and community-building are preventive strategies, not clinical treatment. This option confuses "non-clinical" with "treatment."
- **(C)** overstates the radicalism of Perspective 2 and understates Perspective 3. Reforming residential life and investing in community infrastructure can also challenge institutional norms. More importantly, this option misses the complementary structure that defines the set.
- **(D)** identifies one shared element (institutional responsibility) but then leaps to "essentially identical," ignoring the substantive differences in diagnosis and proposed action. Sharing one assumption does not make arguments identical.

### ACT-RM-03 | Obesity and Public Health

**Issue:** Obesity rates have risen steadily for decades, prompting debates about who or what is responsible and how society should respond.

**Perspective 1:** Individuals must take personal responsibility for their dietary and exercise choices; the government should not intervene in what people eat, because freedom of choice is a fundamental right.

**Perspective 2:** Schools and community organizations should promote nutrition education and expand access to recreational facilities, empowering people to make better choices within a supportive local environment.

**Perspective 3:** The obesity epidemic is driven by corporate food systems that engineer addictive products and target vulnerable communities with aggressive marketing; the government must regulate food industry practices, including advertising, labeling, and ingredient standards.

**Which of the following best describes the relationship among these three perspectives?**

**(A)** Perspectives 1 and 3 are opposites, and Perspective 2 is irrelevant to the debate between them.

**(B)** All three perspectives blame corporations but propose different levels of regulation.

**(C)** The three perspectives form a spectrum from individual responsibility (Perspective 1) to community-level support (Perspective 2) to systemic regulation of industry (Perspective 3), progressively shifting the locus of responsibility from the person to society.

**(D)** Perspectives 2 and 3 are essentially the same because both involve government action, while Perspective 1 stands alone.

**Correct Answer: (C)**

**Rationale:** The defining structure of this set is a graduated shift in where responsibility is located. Perspective 1 places it squarely on the individual. Perspective 2 moves it outward to the community level—still involving personal choice, but acknowledging that environment matters. Perspective 3 pushes responsibility to the systemic level, arguing that corporate practices must be regulated. Together they form a coherent spectrum from individual to collective responsibility.

**Distractor Rationales:**
- **(A)** correctly notes tension between Perspectives 1 and 3 but wrongly dismisses Perspective 2 as irrelevant. Perspective 2 is the crucial middle position that reveals the spectrum structure.
- **(B)** is factually incorrect. Perspective 1 does not blame corporations at all; it explicitly locates responsibility with the individual. This option misreads the most distinctive perspective in the set.
- **(D)** collapses an important distinction. Perspective 2 focuses on education and community resources with no regulatory enforcement, while Perspective 3 calls for binding industry regulation. Grouping them simply as "government action" erases the meaningful difference in scope and mechanism.

### ACT-RM-04 | Carbon Neutrality

**Issue:** Many cities have set ambitious targets to become carbon-neutral within the next two decades. The debate centers on how—or whether—such targets should be pursued.

**Perspective 1:** Cities should invest heavily in renewable energy infrastructure—solar, wind, and battery storage—because transitioning away from fossil fuels is the most direct path to carbon neutrality and will create clean-energy jobs in the process.

**Perspective 2:** The fastest route to carbon neutrality is to redesign urban spaces around public transit, cycling, and walkability, drastically reducing the emissions generated by car-dependent development patterns.

**Perspective 3:** Carbon-neutrality targets are a distraction from more pressing concerns like poverty, housing, and unemployment; cities should not divert scarce public funds toward climate goals that primarily benefit affluent residents and future generations at the expense of struggling communities today.

**Which of the following best describes the relationship among these three perspectives?**

**(A)** Perspectives 1 and 2 agree on the goal of carbon neutrality but disagree on the best strategy to achieve it, while Perspective 3 questions whether carbon neutrality should be the goal at all.

**(B)** All three perspectives agree that climate change is a serious problem but propose different timelines for addressing it.

**(C)** Perspective 3 is a compromise between Perspectives 1 and 2 because it introduces a social-equity consideration missing from both.

**(D)** Perspectives 2 and 3 both challenge Perspective 1's reliance on technology, forming a united critique of techno-optimism.

**Correct Answer: (A)**

**Rationale:** Perspectives 1 and 2 both accept carbon neutrality as a worthy and urgent municipal goal; their disagreement is purely about means (energy infrastructure vs. urban redesign). Perspective 3 stands apart by challenging the goal itself, arguing that the pursuit of carbon neutrality misallocates resources that should address immediate human needs. This is the "two disagree on means, share a goal; third questions the goal" structure.

**Distractor Rationales:**
- **(B)** misrepresents Perspective 3. It does not agree that climate change should be the priority and propose a slower timeline; it argues the goal should be replaced with different priorities entirely. This option softens a fundamental challenge into a minor scheduling difference.
- **(C)** misidentifies Perspective 3's role. A compromise would accept carbon neutrality and add equity considerations. Perspective 3 rejects the carbon-neutrality framework, which is not a middle-ground position but a departure from the shared premise of the other two.
- **(D)** forces an alliance that does not exist. Perspective 3 does not critique technology specifically; it critiques the entire carbon-neutrality agenda regardless of strategy. And Perspective 2's urban-redesign approach is not primarily a critique of technology—it is an alternative means to the same end.

### ACT-RM-05 | School Discipline

**Issue:** Schools nationwide are reconsidering their approaches to student discipline, particularly as data reveal that suspensions and expulsions disproportionately affect students of color and students with disabilities.

**Perspective 1:** Schools should replace punitive discipline with restorative justice practices—such as mediation circles, community service, and guided dialogue—that address the root causes of misbehavior and keep students in the classroom.

**Perspective 2:** Restorative justice should be one component of a broader transformation that also includes trauma-informed counseling, culturally responsive teaching, bias training for staff, and reformed school policies, because no single practice can fix a system shaped by structural inequity.

**Perspective 3:** Schools should maintain clear consequences including suspension for serious infractions, because removing disruptive students protects the learning environment for the majority and teaches accountability.

**Which of the following best describes the relationship among these three perspectives?**

**(A)** Perspectives 1 and 2 are identical, and both oppose Perspective 3.

**(B)** Perspective 1 is essentially a subset of Perspective 2—Perspective 2 accepts restorative justice but embeds it within a larger systemic framework—while Perspective 3 challenges both by defending traditional punitive discipline.

**(C)** Perspective 2 is a compromise between Perspectives 1 and 3 because it combines restorative practices with continued consequences.

**(D)** All three perspectives agree that discipline reform is necessary but differ only on the speed of implementation.

**Correct Answer: (B)**

**Rationale:** Perspective 1 advocates restorative justice as the primary solution. Perspective 2 does not reject restorative justice—it explicitly includes it—but argues it is insufficient on its own, wrapping it into a wider package of structural reforms. This makes Perspective 1 a subset of Perspective 2: everything Perspective 1 wants is contained within Perspective 2's broader vision. Perspective 3 operates from an entirely different framework, defending the punitive approach that both 1 and 2 seek to move away from.

**Distractor Rationales:**
- **(A)** misses the key structural nuance. Perspectives 1 and 2 are not identical; Perspective 2 is significantly broader in scope. Calling them identical erases the subset relationship that defines this item.
- **(C)** misreads Perspective 2. It does not incorporate punitive discipline alongside restorative practices; it calls for systemic reforms like bias training and trauma-informed care, which are not concessions to Perspective 3's position. A true compromise would blend restorative and punitive elements.
- **(D)** is incorrect because Perspective 3 does not agree that reform is necessary. It defends the current punitive system, which is a fundamental disagreement about values and approach, not a question of pacing.

### ACT-POS-01: Gig Economy Regulation

**Issue:**
The growth of the gig economy -- ride-sharing, freelance platforms, and on-demand delivery services -- has transformed how millions of people earn a living. As gig work expands, debate intensifies over whether and how governments should regulate these arrangements.

**Perspective One:** Gig workers are independent entrepreneurs who freely choose flexibility over traditional employment benefits. Government regulation would destroy the very appeal of gig work and raise costs for consumers.

**Perspective Two:** Without regulation, gig companies exploit workers by denying them basic protections like minimum wage guarantees, health insurance, and retirement contributions. Workers deserve the same safeguards as traditional employees.

**Perspective Three:** Rather than forcing gig work into old regulatory categories, governments should create an entirely new classification for gig workers -- one that provides a portable safety net without eliminating the flexibility that makes gig work attractive.

**Task:** Develop three distinct positions on this issue:

(a) Choose one perspective you find most compelling and extend it with additional reasoning and evidence.
(b) Synthesize two of the perspectives into a unified position.
(c) Reframe the issue in a way that none of the three perspectives fully captures.

Then select which of your three positions is the strongest and justify your choice.

**Model Answer:**

**(a) Extend Perspective Three:**
Perspective Three is the most forward-thinking because it recognizes that the nature of work itself has fundamentally changed and that our regulatory frameworks must evolve accordingly. The traditional binary of "employee" versus "independent contractor" was designed for a mid-twentieth-century economy in which most workers held a single, long-term job. Today, a freelance graphic designer might work with five clients simultaneously, a rideshare driver might split time between two platforms, and a software developer might combine part-time employment with freelance projects. A new classification -- sometimes called a "dependent contractor" or "independent worker" category -- could require platforms to contribute to a portable benefits fund tied to the worker rather than to any single employer. This model already has precedent: several European nations have introduced intermediate worker categories, and pilot programs in the United States have tested portable benefit pools. Extending this perspective, we should also mandate that gig platforms provide transparent data on average earnings so workers can make genuinely informed choices. The key insight is that flexibility and security are not inherently opposed -- they only seem that way when we insist on using outdated categories.

**(b) Synthesize Perspectives One and Two:**
Perspectives One and Two appear to be in direct conflict, but their core concerns are compatible. Perspective One is correct that flexibility is a genuine and valuable feature of gig work -- many workers actively prefer setting their own schedules and choosing their projects. Perspective Two is correct that the absence of any safety net leaves workers dangerously exposed to income volatility, illness, and economic downturns. A synthesized position holds that regulation should protect workers without mandating the rigid structures of traditional employment. For example, rather than requiring gig companies to offer full employee benefits, legislation could require platforms to contribute a percentage of each transaction to a worker-controlled benefits account. Workers would retain the freedom to choose when and how much they work, while accumulating protections proportional to their labor. This approach honors the entrepreneurial spirit that Perspective One champions while addressing the exploitation that Perspective Two rightly condemns. The goal is not to choose between freedom and security but to design systems that deliver both.

**(c) Reframe the Issue:**
All three perspectives treat gig economy regulation as primarily a labor policy question -- a matter of classifying workers and distributing benefits. But the deeper issue is one of economic power and market structure. The real question is not whether gig workers should receive benefits, but why a handful of massive platform companies have accumulated enough power to set the terms of work for millions of people with virtually no negotiation. When a single ride-sharing app controls the majority of the market, individual drivers have no meaningful ability to negotiate pay rates, regardless of whether they are classified as employees or contractors. Reframing the issue around market concentration suggests that the most impactful intervention is not reclassifying workers but breaking up monopolistic platforms, enforcing interoperability standards so workers can move freely between competing services, and supporting worker-owned cooperative platforms. If gig workers had genuine market alternatives and collective bargaining power, many of the problems that Perspectives One, Two, and Three debate would diminish on their own. Regulation of benefits matters, but it treats symptoms while ignoring the structural cause.

**Strongest Position and Justification:**
Position (c), the reframe, is the strongest for an ACT essay because it demonstrates the highest level of critical thinking. It does not simply agree or disagree with the given perspectives -- it identifies an assumption shared by all three (that the debate is fundamentally about worker classification) and challenges it. This move shows the scorer that the writer can analyze the issue at a deeper level than the prompt itself invites. The reframe also naturally incorporates and addresses the other perspectives rather than ignoring them, which satisfies the ACT rubric's emphasis on engaging multiple viewpoints. Finally, it offers a concrete and original policy direction (addressing market concentration), which gives the essay a purposeful argument rather than a vague both-sides compromise.

### ACT-POS-02: School Start Times

**Issue:**
Sleep research consistently shows that adolescents' biological clocks shift later during puberty, making early wake times especially difficult for teenagers. Yet most high schools in the United States begin classes before 8:00 a.m. Communities are debating whether to push start times later.

**Perspective One:** Later start times are supported by overwhelming scientific evidence linking adequate sleep to better academic performance, improved mental health, and fewer car accidents among teen drivers. Schools have a responsibility to align schedules with student well-being.

**Perspective Two:** Changing school start times would create serious logistical problems for families and communities. Parents' work schedules, after-school activities, bus routes, and childcare arrangements all depend on current start times, and the disruption would outweigh the benefits.

**Perspective Three:** The real problem is not when school starts but how much we demand of teenagers. Between homework, extracurriculars, part-time jobs, and screen time, adolescents are chronically overscheduled. Changing the clock without changing the culture will not solve sleep deprivation.

**Task:** Develop three distinct positions on this issue:

(a) Choose one perspective you find most compelling and extend it with additional reasoning and evidence.
(b) Synthesize two of the perspectives into a unified position.
(c) Reframe the issue in a way that none of the three perspectives fully captures.

Then select which of your three positions is the strongest and justify your choice.

**Model Answer:**

**(a) Extend Perspective One:**
The scientific case for later start times is not merely strong -- it is essentially settled. The American Academy of Pediatrics, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, and the American Medical Association have all recommended that middle and high schools start no earlier than 8:30 a.m. Studies from districts that have already made the switch -- including Seattle, Minneapolis, and several California districts following the state's 2019 law -- show measurable gains in attendance, graduation rates, and standardized test scores, along with reductions in tardiness, disciplinary incidents, and teen car accidents. Extending this perspective, we should recognize that the costs of inaction are not merely academic but a matter of public health and equity. Sleep-deprived teenagers are more likely to experience depression and anxiety, and low-income students who rely on public transportation to reach early-starting schools are disproportionately affected. Furthermore, the logistical objections raised by opponents, while real, have been successfully addressed by every district that has committed to the change. Seattle, for example, restructured its bus routes and staggered elementary and high school schedules with only modest additional cost. When the science is this clear and the solutions are this tested, continued inaction is a choice to prioritize adult convenience over adolescent health.

**(b) Synthesize Perspectives One and Three:**
Perspective One is right that start times matter, and Perspective Three is right that the problem is bigger than the clock. A comprehensive approach requires both structural and cultural change. Pushing start times to 8:30 a.m. or later addresses the biological reality that adolescents cannot simply "choose" to fall asleep earlier -- their circadian rhythms genuinely shift during puberty. But if schools merely shift the schedule forward by an hour while maintaining the same volume of homework, the same pressure to pad college applications with extracurriculars, and the same tolerance for late-night screen use, students will simply stay up an hour later and end up just as sleep-deprived. The synthesized position is that later start times are a necessary first step -- they set the floor -- but they must be paired with a deliberate reduction in academic overload and a school culture that treats sleep as a health priority rather than a luxury. Concretely, this means later start times combined with homework caps, scheduling policies that protect a minimum rest period, and education for families about sleep hygiene. Neither the schedule change nor the cultural change is sufficient alone; together, they address both the biology and the behavior.

**(c) Reframe the Issue:**
The debate over school start times assumes that all students in a school must start at the same time. But advances in educational technology and instructional design make that assumption increasingly outdated. The deeper question is whether the industrial-era model of a fixed school day -- with every student in a seat at the same hour -- is still the best way to educate adolescents. A reframed approach would explore flexible scheduling: core instructional blocks in the late morning and early afternoon (when adolescent alertness peaks), with optional early-morning and late-afternoon periods for electives, labs, or independent study. Some students might begin at 7:30; others at 9:00, depending on their needs, course loads, and family circumstances. This model, already used in some college and vocational settings, sidesteps the all-or-nothing debate between Perspectives One and Two. It respects the science of sleep without requiring every family to reorganize around a single new schedule. It also prepares students for the increasingly flexible work environments they will encounter after graduation. The real opportunity is not to shift the rigid schedule but to question whether a rigid schedule is necessary at all.

**Strongest Position and Justification:**
Position (b), the synthesis, is the strongest because it addresses the issue most realistically and comprehensively. The ACT rubric rewards writers who engage with complexity, and this position does so by acknowledging that both the biological evidence for later start times and the cultural problem of overscheduling are legitimate concerns that reinforce each other. Unlike position (a), which could be dismissed as ignoring the broader lifestyle factors that contribute to sleep deprivation, and unlike position (c), which proposes a model that most school districts lack the resources to implement in the near term, the synthesis offers a position that is both ambitious and actionable. It gives the writer a clear, defensible thesis -- "later start times are necessary but not sufficient" -- that naturally generates a well-structured essay with distinct body paragraphs addressing each component.

### ACT-POS-03: Public Surveillance Cameras

**Issue:**
Cities around the world are expanding networks of public surveillance cameras equipped with facial recognition and artificial intelligence capabilities. Supporters argue these systems deter crime and help solve cases; critics worry about the erosion of privacy and the potential for misuse.

**Perspective One:** Public surveillance cameras make communities safer by deterring criminal activity and providing crucial evidence for law enforcement. In an era of rising security concerns, the benefits to public safety far outweigh minor privacy trade-offs in already-public spaces.

**Perspective Two:** Mass surveillance fundamentally changes the relationship between citizens and the state. When people know they are constantly watched, they alter their behavior in ways that chill free expression, discourage protest, and erode the open society that democracies depend on.

**Perspective Three:** The technology itself is neutral; the real issue is governance. With proper oversight -- clear rules about data storage, access, and use, along with independent auditing -- surveillance cameras can enhance safety without threatening civil liberties.

**Task:** Develop three distinct positions on this issue:

(a) Choose one perspective you find most compelling and extend it with additional reasoning and evidence.
(b) Synthesize two of the perspectives into a unified position.
(c) Reframe the issue in a way that none of the three perspectives fully captures.

Then select which of your three positions is the strongest and justify your choice.

**Model Answer:**

**(a) Extend Perspective Two:**
Perspective Two identifies the most profound risk of public surveillance: not any single abuse, but the gradual, almost invisible transformation of public life. The concept of the "chilling effect" is well documented in legal scholarship -- when people believe they may be watched, they avoid lawful but controversial behavior. They skip the protest, leave the political meeting early, avoid the neighborhood associated with dissent. This effect is not hypothetical. Studies of surveillance expansion in London, where one of the world's densest camera networks has operated for decades, show that cameras have had only modest effects on crime rates but have significantly increased self-reported feelings of being monitored, particularly among minority communities. Extending this perspective, the addition of AI-powered facial recognition magnifies the threat exponentially. Traditional cameras recorded anonymous footage reviewed only when a crime occurred. Modern systems can identify individuals in real time, track their movements across a city, and build comprehensive profiles of daily routines -- capabilities that no democratic society has ever granted to law enforcement without a warrant. Even if current officials use these tools responsibly, the infrastructure, once built, is available to every future administration. History teaches that surveillance powers, once established, are rarely rolled back and are inevitably expanded. The question is not whether today's cameras are being misused but whether we are comfortable building a system whose potential for misuse is essentially unlimited.

**(b) Synthesize Perspectives Two and Three:**
Perspective Two is correct that surveillance poses genuine dangers to democratic life, and Perspective Three is correct that governance frameworks can mitigate those dangers. The synthesized position is that public surveillance should be permitted only under strict, enforceable conditions that directly address the chilling effects Perspective Two warns about. This means, first, that facial recognition should be banned from general public surveillance and permitted only under specific judicial warrants for serious crimes -- treating it like a wiretap, not a traffic camera. Second, footage retention should be limited to a short window (no more than 72 hours) unless flagged in connection with an active investigation. Third, an independent civilian oversight board -- not the police department itself -- should audit camera usage, access logs, and algorithmic outputs on a quarterly basis, with public reporting. Fourth, communities should have a direct vote on whether to install or expand surveillance networks in their neighborhoods. This framework takes Perspective Three's faith in governance seriously while insisting, with Perspective Two, that the default must be restriction, not permission. Governance is not a magic word; it only works when the rules are specific, the enforcement is independent, and the public has meaningful control.

**(c) Reframe the Issue:**
All three perspectives focus on government surveillance of public spaces, but the most consequential surveillance of daily life is conducted not by the state but by private corporations. Smartphones, social media platforms, retail loyalty programs, and internet service providers already track individuals with a precision and persistence that no city camera network can match. The debate over public cameras, while important, distracts from the larger reality that most people have already surrendered far more intimate data to private companies than any streetcorner camera could ever capture. A reframed position argues that the real issue is not "Should the government watch us in public?" but "Who controls the total surveillance infrastructure -- public and private -- and what rights do individuals have over their own data?" This reframing suggests that the most urgent policy need is comprehensive data privacy legislation that governs both public and private surveillance, gives individuals the right to know when they are being tracked and by whom, and establishes meaningful penalties for misuse regardless of whether the watcher is a police department or an advertising network. Debating public cameras in isolation is like arguing about the lock on the front door while the back wall of the house is missing.

**Strongest Position and Justification:**
Position (b), the synthesis, is the strongest for an ACT essay because it produces the most structured and persuasive argument. It acknowledges the legitimate concerns of both sides -- the real dangers of unchecked surveillance and the real possibility of effective governance -- and resolves the tension with specific, concrete policy proposals. This gives the writer four distinct body paragraph topics (facial recognition restrictions, retention limits, independent oversight, community consent), making the essay naturally well organized. The position also demonstrates the nuanced thinking the ACT rubric rewards at the highest score levels: it avoids the extremes of "ban all cameras" and "trust the government," instead articulating a precise set of conditions under which surveillance is acceptable. Finally, it engages all three perspectives directly, showing the scorer that the writer has carefully considered the full range of viewpoints before arriving at a considered position.

### ACT-CS-01

**Claim:** "Schools should prioritize teaching digital literacy over traditional subjects."

**Task:** Generate four types of support for this claim:

1. A real-world example
2. A logical reasoning chain
3. A hypothetical scenario
4. An analogy

Then rank all four by persuasiveness and explain your ranking.

**Model Answer:**

**1. Real-World Example:**
In 2016, a wave of fabricated news stories spread across social media during the U.S. presidential election. A Stanford University study later found that the vast majority of middle school, high school, and college students could not reliably distinguish between credible news sources and sponsored content or misinformation. Students evaluated the trustworthiness of articles based on superficial features -- the amount of detail, the presence of a large photo -- rather than checking sources, examining evidence, or considering the publisher's credibility. In response, the Stanford History Education Group developed a "Civic Online Reasoning" curriculum, and early evaluations suggest it improves students' ability to evaluate online sources. This example demonstrates that digital literacy is not an abstract future need but an urgent present one, and that schools that teach it produce measurably more capable citizens.

**2. Logical Reasoning Chain:**
The purpose of education is to prepare students for the world they will actually inhabit. The world today's students will inhabit is one in which virtually every profession -- from medicine to agriculture to the arts -- requires the ability to navigate digital systems, evaluate online information, manage data, and communicate through digital platforms. Traditional subjects like history and literature remain valuable, but their value is diminished if students lack the digital skills to access, assess, and apply knowledge in real-world contexts. A student who has memorized historical dates but cannot identify a manipulated photograph or a fabricated statistic is poorly equipped for modern citizenship. Therefore, if schools must make choices about how to allocate limited instructional time, digital literacy should take priority because it is the foundational skill that makes all other knowledge usable in a digital world.

**3. Hypothetical Scenario:**
Imagine two students graduating from high school in the same year. Student A attended a school with a traditional curriculum heavy on classic literature, advanced algebra, and memorization-based history courses, but with no formal instruction in digital literacy. Student B attended a school that reduced time spent on some traditional content to make room for courses in media literacy, data analysis, online research methods, and digital communication. Ten years later, Student A struggles to evaluate the credibility of health information online, falls for a phishing scam that compromises personal financial data, and finds it difficult to adapt when their employer transitions to a new digital workflow system. Student B navigates all of these challenges with confidence -- not because they are more intelligent, but because their education equipped them with the specific skills that daily life demands. This scenario illustrates that the gap between digitally literate and digitally illiterate graduates will only widen as technology becomes more deeply embedded in every aspect of life.

**4. Analogy:**
Prioritizing traditional subjects over digital literacy in today's schools is like a driving school in the 1920s that insisted on teaching students how to care for horses because equestrian knowledge had been important for centuries. The skills are not worthless -- understanding horses still has value -- but the world has moved on, and the primary mode of transportation now requires an entirely different set of competencies. A responsible driving school would have recognized the shift and restructured its curriculum around the automobile. Similarly, a responsible education system should recognize that the primary environment in which students will work, communicate, and participate as citizens is now digital, and it should restructure accordingly. Clinging to a traditional curriculum simply because it has historical prestige is as shortsighted as teaching horse care in the age of the automobile.

**Ranking (Most to Least Persuasive):**

1. **Real-World Example** -- This is the most persuasive because it provides concrete, verifiable evidence. The Stanford study gives the claim empirical weight, and the specific detail about students' inability to evaluate online information makes the need for digital literacy vivid and undeniable. ACT scorers value evidence that grounds abstract claims in observable reality.

2. **Logical Reasoning Chain** -- This is the second most persuasive because it builds a step-by-step argument from a premise most readers accept (education should prepare students for the real world) to a conclusion that follows naturally. Its strength is its logical inevitability; each step is hard to dispute individually.

3. **Analogy** -- The horse-and-automobile comparison is memorable and intuitively compelling, making it effective for an essay's opening or closing. However, analogies can always be challenged on the grounds that the comparison is imperfect (schools are not driving schools; traditional subjects are not "horses"), so it is less airtight than direct evidence or formal reasoning.

4. **Hypothetical Scenario** -- While the scenario effectively illustrates the claim's real-world implications, it is the least persuasive because it is entirely invented. A skeptical reader can dismiss it by arguing that Student A might have learned digital skills on their own, or that Student B might have suffered from gaps in traditional knowledge. Hypotheticals are useful for illustration but weak as standalone proof.

### ACT-CS-02

**Claim:** "Cities should invest in public transportation rather than expanding roads."

**Task:** Generate four types of support for this claim:

1. A real-world example
2. A logical reasoning chain
3. A hypothetical scenario
4. An analogy

Then rank all four by persuasiveness and explain your ranking.

**Model Answer:**

**1. Real-World Example:**
In the 1990s and 2000s, the city of Seoul, South Korea, demolished the Cheonggyecheon Expressway -- a major elevated highway running through the city center -- and replaced it with a restored urban stream, parks, and expanded public transit options including bus rapid transit lanes. Rather than increasing traffic congestion, as critics had predicted, the project reduced traffic in the area by diverting commuters to public transit, decreased air pollution, lowered urban heat island temperatures, and revitalized surrounding neighborhoods economically. Property values in the area rose, new businesses opened, and the restored stream became a cultural landmark. Seoul's experience demonstrates that investing in public transit and green infrastructure, even at the expense of existing road capacity, can produce better outcomes for mobility, health, and economic vitality than road expansion ever could.

**2. Logical Reasoning Chain:**
Road expansion is subject to a well-documented phenomenon called "induced demand": when road capacity increases, driving becomes temporarily easier, which attracts more drivers, which restores the original level of congestion -- often within just a few years. This means that building wider roads does not solve traffic; it generates more traffic. Public transportation, by contrast, moves more people per unit of infrastructure. A single bus lane can carry as many commuters per hour as multiple lanes of car traffic, and a rail line can carry many times more. Therefore, every dollar spent expanding roads produces a temporary and self-defeating improvement, while every dollar spent on public transit produces a permanent increase in the system's capacity to move people. If the goal is to reduce congestion and improve mobility, the logical conclusion is that investment should flow toward the option that actually achieves that goal in the long term: public transportation.

**3. Hypothetical Scenario:**
Consider two mid-sized cities, each facing the same problem: growing populations and worsening rush-hour traffic. City A responds by widening its main highways and adding new interchanges, spending billions over a decade. City B invests the same amount in a light rail system, protected bus lanes, and pedestrian-friendly transit hubs. Fifteen years later, City A's highways are just as congested as before -- the wider roads attracted more suburban development and more drivers -- and the city faces pressure to widen them again. Meanwhile, City B's transit ridership has grown steadily, its downtown has become a walkable destination attracting young professionals and new businesses, its per-capita carbon emissions have fallen, and low-income residents who cannot afford cars have reliable access to jobs across the metro area. City A spent the same money and got a treadmill; City B got transformation.

**4. Analogy:**
Expanding roads to solve traffic congestion is like loosening your belt to solve weight gain. It provides immediate, visible relief -- your pants feel more comfortable -- but it does nothing to address the underlying cause. In fact, it may make the problem worse by removing the discomfort that might have motivated a change in behavior. A tighter belt (congestion) is unpleasant, but it signals that something needs to change. Public transportation is the equivalent of a healthier diet: it addresses the root cause by reducing the number of cars generating the congestion in the first place. The belt-loosening approach can continue indefinitely -- always adding another notch, always widening another lane -- but the problem never gets smaller. Only a change in the system itself (diet or transit) produces a lasting solution.

**Ranking (Most to Least Persuasive):**

1. **Logical Reasoning Chain** -- The concept of induced demand is the single most powerful argument against road expansion because it turns the opposition's own logic against itself. If wider roads attract more drivers and restore congestion, then road expansion literally cannot achieve what its proponents promise. This reasoning is airtight and applies universally, not just to one city. For an ACT essay, this chain gives the writer an argument that is nearly impossible to refute without disputing well-established transportation research.

2. **Real-World Example** -- Seoul's Cheonggyecheon project is a vivid, counterintuitive example that challenges the assumption that removing road capacity worsens traffic. Its strength is its specificity: real city, real highway, real measurable outcomes. It is ranked second because, while powerful, a single example from a different country can be dismissed as non-transferable.

3. **Analogy** -- The belt-loosening comparison is intuitive and memorable, and it captures the concept of induced demand in everyday terms that any reader can grasp immediately. It is ranked third because, like all analogies, it simplifies a complex system and can be challenged on the grounds that cities are not bodies and roads are not belts.

4. **Hypothetical Scenario** -- The two-city comparison effectively illustrates the long-term divergence between the two investment strategies, but because both cities are invented, a skeptic can argue that the outcomes are rigged in favor of the writer's conclusion. Hypotheticals are most effective when supported by the stronger forms of evidence that precede them in this ranking.

### ACT-CS-03

**Claim:** "Volunteer service requirements for graduation do more harm than good."

**Task:** Generate four types of support for this claim:

1. A real-world example
2. A logical reasoning chain
3. A hypothetical scenario
4. An analogy

Then rank all four by persuasiveness and explain your ranking.

**Model Answer:**

**1. Real-World Example:**
In Ontario, Canada, all high school students have been required to complete 40 hours of community service to graduate since 1999. A study published in the *Canadian Journal of Education* found that the mandate did not increase students' likelihood of volunteering after graduation and, in some cases, actually decreased positive attitudes toward community service compared to students who had volunteered without being required to do so. Students frequently reported treating the requirement as a checkbox -- completing hours at the most convenient location possible rather than engaging meaningfully with a cause. Some students from low-income families faced additional hardship because they needed that time for paid work to support their households but were forced to volunteer instead. Ontario's experience suggests that mandatory service can undermine the intrinsic motivation that makes volunteering genuinely beneficial for both the volunteer and the community.

**2. Logical Reasoning Chain:**
The value of volunteer service depends on the volunteer's genuine engagement and willingness. When service is compelled rather than chosen, it ceases to be volunteering in any meaningful sense -- it becomes an obligation, no different from a homework assignment or a test requirement. Obligations are completed with minimum effort and maximum efficiency; they do not cultivate the empathy, civic commitment, or personal growth that proponents of service requirements hope to foster. Furthermore, mandatory service sends students a contradictory message: "Caring about your community is so important that we have to force you to do it." This undermines the very ethic it claims to promote by framing service as a burden rather than a privilege. If schools want to inspire a culture of service, they should model it, celebrate it, and create opportunities for it -- but the moment they mandate it, they transform an act of generosity into an act of compliance, and compliance does not build character.

**3. Hypothetical Scenario:**
Imagine a high school junior named Maria who is passionate about environmental conservation and already spends her weekends helping restore a local wetland with a nonprofit group. Under her school's mandatory service requirement, however, Maria's wetland work does not count because it is not on the school's approved list of partner organizations. She is forced to spend 40 hours shelving books at the public library -- a perfectly fine activity, but one she has no connection to and no interest in. Meanwhile, her classmate Jake, who has never volunteered and resents the requirement, signs his mother's friend's name on a form claiming he completed 40 hours at her church, when he actually showed up for three. Maria loses time she could have spent on work she cares about; Jake learns that service requirements are something to game. The school counts both as successes. This scenario illustrates how mandatory service can simultaneously punish the already-motivated and fail to reach the disengaged -- the worst of both worlds.

**4. Analogy:**
Requiring students to volunteer in order to graduate is like requiring someone to smile in order to prove they are happy. The outward behavior -- smiling or volunteering -- can be performed without the inner state it is supposed to represent. A forced smile does not create happiness; it creates a performance. Similarly, forced service does not create civic-mindedness; it creates compliance. And just as we would find it strange and slightly disturbing if someone were punished for not smiling enough, there is something troubling about a system that withholds a diploma from a student who has completed all academic requirements but failed to perform a mandated number of hours of unpaid labor. The analogy reveals the fundamental flaw in service requirements: they confuse the visible behavior with the invisible value that behavior is supposed to reflect, and in doing so, they devalue both.

**Ranking (Most to Least Persuasive):**

1. **Logical Reasoning Chain** -- This is the most persuasive because it identifies a fundamental contradiction at the heart of mandatory service: compulsion destroys the very quality (genuine willingness) that makes volunteering valuable. This argument applies to all mandatory service programs, not just one, and it is difficult to refute without arguing that forced generosity is still generosity -- a hard case to make. For an ACT essay, this reasoning would form the strongest thesis paragraph.

2. **Real-World Example** -- Ontario's program provides empirical evidence that mandatory service does not achieve its stated goals: it fails to increase future volunteering and can decrease positive attitudes toward service. This is powerful because it counters the intuitive assumption that "more service hours must be good" with actual data. It is ranked second because a single study from one Canadian province, while suggestive, can be questioned on grounds of generalizability.

3. **Hypothetical Scenario** -- Maria and Jake's stories are vivid and emotionally resonant, capturing both how requirements can harm motivated students and how they can be gamed by unmotivated ones. The scenario makes the abstract argument concrete and human. It is ranked third because, as a hypothetical, it can be dismissed as a carefully constructed worst case that does not represent typical experiences.

4. **Analogy** -- The forced-smile comparison is memorable and captures the philosophical absurdity of mandating an activity whose value depends on being freely chosen. However, it is ranked last because the comparison between smiling and volunteering is imperfect: volunteering, even reluctantly, can produce tangible benefits for the community that a forced smile does not produce for anyone. A careful reader could use that gap to weaken the argument.
