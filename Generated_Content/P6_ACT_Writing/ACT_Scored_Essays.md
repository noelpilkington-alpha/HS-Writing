# ACT Writing Scored Essays -- Course A2: Calibration Exercises

## Part 1: Score-Level Exemplars (ACT-P01: Social Media Age Restrictions)

---

### ACT-SE-01 | Score: 2-3 | Prompt: ACT-P01

**Student Essay:**

Social media is a big problem for kids today. I think that kids shouldnt be on social media because it is bad for them. There are alot of problems with it like cyberbullying and stuff like that.

Some people think the age should be raised to 16 which I think is a good idea because younger kids dont know what their doing on there. My little cousin is on tiktok all the time and my aunt doesnt even know about it. Thats not good.

Other people say parents should handle it but parents cant watch there kids all the time. The government should do something about it because its getting out of hand. Social media companies just want money and they dont care about kids.

In conclusion social media is bad for kids and something needs to be done about it. We need to protect kids from the dangers of social media.

**Scoring Breakdown:**

| Domain | Score (1-6) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Ideas & Analysis | 1 | The essay restates the general topic but does not meaningfully engage with any of the three perspectives. There is no analysis -- only broad assertions ("it is bad for them") with no attempt to examine why or how the perspectives relate to each other. |
| Development & Support | 1 | The only piece of support is a personal anecdote about a cousin on TikTok, which is undeveloped. Claims like "parents cant watch there kids all the time" receive no elaboration or reasoning. |
| Organization | 1 | There is a loose intro-body-conclusion shape, but paragraphs do not develop distinct ideas. The essay jumps between perspectives without transition or logic. The conclusion merely repeats the introduction. |
| Language Use | 2 | Frequent errors in spelling ("alot," "shouldnt," "there/their"), grammar, and punctuation. Sentences are simple and repetitive. Word choice is vague ("stuff like that," "something needs to be done"). |

**Calibration Notes:** Students often score this higher than it deserves because it "has an opinion" and "mentions the perspectives." Remind scorers that mentioning a topic is not the same as engaging with a perspective. The essay never explains *why* any perspective is right or wrong -- it just agrees or disagrees in passing. Also, having a five-paragraph shape does not automatically earn a strong Organization score if the paragraphs lack internal coherence.

### ACT-SE-02 | Score: 4-5 | Prompt: ACT-P01

**Student Essay:**

The question of whether social media should have stricter age restrictions is an important topic today. There are three perspectives on this issue, and I will discuss them in this essay.

Perspective One says that social media companies should verify ages and raise the minimum to 16. I agree with this because social media really does cause problems for young people. Studies have shown that social media is linked to depression and anxiety. If companies made it harder for kids to sign up, that would help protect them.

Perspective Two says we should teach digital literacy instead of banning kids. This has some good points because education is important. However, I don't think education alone is enough. Kids are still going to see harmful content even if they know about digital literacy.

Perspective Three says parents should be responsible. I think parents do have a responsibility, but not all parents are good at monitoring technology. Some parents don't understand social media themselves, so they can't really guide their children through it.

In my opinion, I think we need a combination of all three. Companies should do their part, parents should be involved, and schools should teach digital literacy. That way everyone is working together to keep kids safe online.

**Scoring Breakdown:**

| Domain | Score (1-6) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Ideas & Analysis | 2 | The essay acknowledges all three perspectives but engages with them only at a surface level. Each perspective is summarized and given a brief reaction, but no perspective is genuinely analyzed. The "combination of all three" thesis is stated but not explored. |
| Development & Support | 2 | Support is thin and generic. The reference to "studies" linking social media to depression is vague and unelaborated. Counterarguments are raised ("not all parents are good at monitoring") but never developed with reasoning or examples. |
| Organization | 3 | The essay follows a clear perspective-by-perspective structure with an introduction and conclusion. However, this structure is mechanical -- it reads as a list of reactions rather than a building argument. Transitions are basic ("Perspective Two says..."). |
| Language Use | 2 | Language is clear and generally correct, with few mechanical errors. However, sentence structures are repetitive (many begin with "I think" or "I agree"), and word choice is plain. The writing is competent but unvaried. |

**Calibration Notes:** Students frequently overscore this essay because it "addresses all three perspectives" and "has a clear structure." Remind them that the ACT rubric distinguishes between *acknowledging* perspectives and *analyzing* them. This essay summarizes each perspective and offers a quick reaction but never digs into assumptions, implications, or tensions. The "combination" thesis is a common strategy that can work well at higher score levels, but here it is asserted in one sentence rather than developed into a real argument.

### ACT-SE-03 | Score: 6-7 | Prompt: ACT-P01

**Student Essay:**

In an era where children can access social media with a few taps on a screen, the debate over age restrictions has become unavoidable. While all three perspectives raise valid concerns, I believe that a combination of corporate responsibility and digital literacy education offers the strongest path forward.

Perspective One argues that companies should enforce age limits and raise the minimum age to 16. There is real merit to this argument. Research from organizations like the American Psychological Association has highlighted the connection between heavy social media use and increased rates of anxiety among teenagers. If these platforms are genuinely harmful to developing minds, then the companies profiting from young users' engagement should bear some responsibility for limiting that harm. However, Perspective One overlooks a practical problem: age verification systems are difficult to implement without collecting sensitive personal data, which raises its own set of risks.

This is where Perspective Two becomes relevant. Rather than relying solely on restrictions that may be hard to enforce, investing in digital literacy gives young people tools they can carry with them throughout their lives. A student who learns to recognize misinformation or manage screen time is better equipped than one who was simply blocked from accessing a platform until an arbitrary age. That said, digital literacy alone places a heavy burden on schools and assumes that education can fully counteract the addictive design features that social media companies deliberately build into their products.

Perspective Three's emphasis on parental responsibility is understandable, but it assumes a level of technological knowledge and available time that not all families have. A single parent working multiple jobs may not have the ability to closely monitor a child's online activity, no matter how much they care.

Ultimately, the most realistic solution involves shared responsibility. Companies should be transparent about how their algorithms target young users and should invest in meaningful safety features. Schools should integrate digital literacy into their curricula. And families should be supported -- not blamed -- as they navigate a digital landscape that changes faster than any policy can keep up with.

**Scoring Breakdown:**

| Domain | Score (1-6) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Ideas & Analysis | 3 | The essay engages with all three perspectives and identifies strengths and limitations in each. The analysis goes beyond summary -- for example, noting that age verification raises data privacy concerns, or that digital literacy cannot fully counteract addictive design. However, the analysis remains somewhat predictable and does not explore deeper tensions. |
| Development & Support | 3 | The essay offers reasonable support (APA research, the example of a single parent), but development is uneven. Some claims are asserted more than argued. The final paragraph's "shared responsibility" conclusion is sensible but could be more specifically developed. |
| Organization | 4 | The essay has a clear and logical structure. The introduction establishes a thesis, body paragraphs address perspectives in a sequence that builds toward the conclusion, and transitions ("This is where Perspective Two becomes relevant") connect ideas effectively. |
| Language Use | 3 | Language is competent and occasionally strong ("a digital landscape that changes faster than any policy can keep up with"). Sentences vary in structure, and word choice is precise. Minor issues -- some phrasing is slightly stiff -- keep this from the highest tier. |

**Calibration Notes:** This is the score range where calibration disagreements are most common. Students tend to either underscore this essay (because it uses the "combination" approach they associate with the 4-5 range) or overscore it (because the language sounds polished). The key distinction from a 4-5 essay is that this one *analyzes* perspectives rather than merely reacting to them -- it identifies specific weaknesses in each argument. The key distinction from an 8-9 is that the analysis, while competent, does not push into genuinely insightful territory or develop its own perspective with real depth.

### ACT-SE-04 | Score: 8-9 | Prompt: ACT-P01

**Student Essay:**

The impulse to protect children from social media is understandable -- and the evidence of harm is mounting. But the way we frame the problem determines the solutions we consider, and too often this debate gets reduced to a single question: who should be in charge? A more productive approach recognizes that the issue is not really about control. It is about designing systems -- technological, educational, and familial -- that account for the reality of how young people actually live.

Perspective One is grounded in legitimate concern. The adolescent brain is uniquely vulnerable to the dopamine-driven feedback loops that social media platforms are engineered to create. Raising the age limit to 16 and requiring verification would send a clear signal that society takes this harm seriously. Yet this perspective contains a tension it does not resolve. The same companies being asked to verify ages have a financial incentive to maximize their user base. Trusting them to effectively limit their own market is like asking a candy company to enforce nutrition standards -- possible, but only with significant external pressure.

Perspective Two correctly identifies the long-term value of education over restriction. A sixteen-year-old who turns sixteen and suddenly gains access to social media with no preparation is arguably more vulnerable than a fourteen-year-old who has spent two years learning to think critically about what they see online. The skills-based approach respects young people's agency in a way that blanket bans do not. However, Perspective Two underestimates the asymmetry of the situation. Digital literacy curricula are developed by educators with limited budgets; the platforms they are teaching students to navigate are designed by engineers with billions of dollars and sophisticated behavioral research at their disposal. Education is necessary, but framing it as sufficient ignores this power imbalance.

Perspective Three appeals to a principle most people share -- that families know their children best. But principles operate within contexts, and the context here matters enormously. Social media is not like deciding when a child is ready to ride a bike. These platforms are specifically designed to override rational decision-making through variable reward schedules and social pressure mechanics. Expecting individual parents to outmaneuver systems built by teams of behavioral psychologists is unrealistic, not because parents are failing, but because the challenge is structural.

What this debate needs is honesty about scale. Individual solutions -- whether parental or educational -- are important but insufficient against an industry-level problem. Meaningful regulation, combined with genuine investment in digital literacy and support for families, is not a compromise position. It is the only approach that matches the scope of the challenge.

**Scoring Breakdown:**

| Domain | Score (1-6) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Ideas & Analysis | 4 | The essay demonstrates genuine analytical insight. It reframes the debate ("the issue is not really about control"), identifies internal tensions within perspectives (the conflict of interest in Perspective One), and introduces the concept of structural asymmetry. Each perspective is evaluated on its own terms before being critiqued. |
| Development & Support | 4 | Ideas are developed with specific and effective reasoning. The candy company analogy, the scenario of an unprepared sixteen-year-old, and the reference to variable reward schedules all serve as concrete support. Development is thorough, though a few points could be pushed further. |
| Organization | 5 | The essay builds a clear argument through a strategic sequence. The opening reframes the debate, each body paragraph both credits and critiques a perspective, and the conclusion synthesizes without merely repeating. Transitions are organic rather than mechanical. |
| Language Use | 4 | Prose is confident and varied. Sentences range from short and declarative ("It is about designing systems") to complex and layered. Word choice is precise ("dopamine-driven feedback loops," "variable reward schedules") without being showy. The writing has a distinctive voice. |

**Calibration Notes:** Students sometimes underscore this essay because it does not use the phrase "I agree with Perspective..." or state a thesis in the first paragraph in a traditional way. Remind them that the ACT rubric rewards productive engagement and effective argumentation, not a specific essay formula. The reframing move in the introduction is a sign of strong analytical thinking, not a structural flaw. Conversely, some students may want to give this a 10+ because of the strong language; note that while the analysis is insightful, it could be even more nuanced in its treatment of counterarguments and implications.

### ACT-SE-05 | Score: 10-11 | Prompt: ACT-P01

**Student Essay:**

Debates about children and social media tend to assume that the problem is straightforward: young people are being harmed, and the question is simply who should stop it. But this framing obscures a more fundamental issue. The reason this problem is so difficult is not that we lack the will to act -- it is that the proposed solutions each contain contradictions that, if left unexamined, undermine the very goals they claim to serve. A serious response to this challenge requires acknowledging those contradictions rather than pretending any single approach resolves them.

Perspective One offers the most immediately satisfying answer: make the companies responsible, raise the age, verify identities. The appeal is obvious. If social media harms young people, and the evidence increasingly suggests that it does, then the entities designing and profiting from these harmful experiences should be compelled to limit them. But Perspective One's logic quietly depends on a premise it never defends -- that age is a reliable proxy for readiness. A sixteen-year-old with severe anxiety and a history of online harassment is not inherently better equipped to handle Instagram than a mature thirteen-year-old with strong critical thinking skills and involved parents. Age-based regulation is administratively convenient, but it substitutes a bureaucratic category for the nuanced judgment the situation actually demands. More troublingly, requiring platforms to verify ages means requiring them to collect verified identity data from every user, including adults. In the name of protecting children's wellbeing, we would be handing surveillance infrastructure to the very corporations whose data practices sparked concern in the first place.

Perspective Two avoids these pitfalls by focusing on capability rather than age. Teaching digital literacy addresses the root vulnerability -- not the platform, but the user's ability to engage with it thoughtfully. This is genuinely compelling, and any serious policy framework should include it. Yet Perspective Two carries its own quiet contradiction. It frames the relationship between users and platforms as if it were a fair contest that skills can equalize. It is not. Social media companies employ thousands of engineers whose explicit purpose is to maximize engagement -- a goal that often conflicts directly with user wellbeing. Teaching a thirteen-year-old to recognize manipulative design is valuable, but it is not a symmetrical contest. We do not teach children to resist the persuasive techniques of tobacco advertising and then conclude that cigarette regulation is unnecessary.

Perspective Three is the most philosophically coherent of the three -- families should make decisions about their own children -- but it is also the most disconnected from material reality. Parental oversight was a plausible framework when "media" meant a television in the living room. Today, social media is accessible on devices that fit in a pocket, through apps that update constantly, within social dynamics that shift daily. The parental responsibility argument asks individual families to solve a collective action problem. Even a parent who successfully limits their own child's access cannot control the social environment that child inhabits, where peers who do have access set the norms.

The honest answer is uncomfortable: no single mechanism is adequate, and the mechanisms we have each carry real costs. Regulation is blunt and creates surveillance risks. Education is essential but outmatched. Parental authority is legitimate but structurally overwhelmed. Progress requires pursuing all three simultaneously while remaining honest about what each one cannot do -- and resisting the political temptation to declare any one of them a solution.

**Scoring Breakdown:**

| Domain | Score (1-6) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Ideas & Analysis | 6 | The essay demonstrates sophisticated analytical thinking throughout. It identifies internal contradictions within each perspective (age as a flawed proxy, the asymmetry of the literacy approach, the collective action problem in parental responsibility). The framing in the introduction -- that the difficulty lies in the contradictions within proposed solutions -- is genuinely insightful and governs the entire essay. |
| Development & Support | 5 | Development is thorough and specific. The tobacco advertising analogy effectively crystallizes the asymmetry argument. The observation about surveillance infrastructure emerging from age verification is a concrete and original point. The shift from televisions to pocket devices grounds the parental responsibility critique in material terms. Occasionally, a point could be developed with one more layer of specificity. |
| Organization | 6 | The essay is strategically organized around its central claim about contradictions. Each body paragraph follows a consistent and effective pattern: credit the perspective, identify its internal contradiction, explain the consequences. The conclusion does not merely summarize but advances the argument by naming the discomfort of the honest answer. Transitions are seamless. |
| Language Use | 5 | The prose is consistently strong and controlled. Sentences are varied and purposeful. Phrases like "substitutes a bureaucratic category for the nuanced judgment the situation actually demands" demonstrate precise, confident writing. The voice is mature without being pretentious. Occasional moments of slightly dense phrasing prevent a perfect score. |

**Calibration Notes:** This is a very strong essay, and students may struggle to distinguish it from a 12. The key difference is in degree rather than kind. While this essay identifies contradictions within each perspective (which is excellent), a 12-level essay would also more fully develop the *relationships* between those contradictions and push toward a more original or unexpected synthesis. The conclusion here -- pursue all three, be honest about limits -- is sophisticated but ultimately a more refined version of a common position. Students should also note that strong language alone does not make a 12; the highest scores require the ideas themselves to be exceptional.

### ACT-SE-06 | Score: 12 | Prompt: ACT-P01

**Student Essay:**

There is a recurring pattern in American policy debates: a genuine problem emerges, competing factions claim ownership of the solution, and the public is asked to choose between them as though the answers were mutually exclusive. The debate over children and social media follows this script precisely -- and in doing so, it misses what may be the most important question. The issue is not simply whether to restrict, educate, or defer to families. It is whether our current frameworks for thinking about childhood, technology, and responsibility are even adequate to the situation we face.

Perspective One locates the problem in access and proposes a structural fix: raise the age, verify identities, hold companies accountable. This approach has the virtue of treating social media's harms as systemic rather than individual -- a crucial insight, because the harms genuinely are systemic. Platforms are not neutral tools that some children happen to misuse. They are attention economies whose profitability depends on behavioral patterns that are developmentally harmful. Perspective One correctly identifies this. But it falters by proposing a remedy that mirrors the very logic it critiques. Age verification requires centralized identity data, which means entrusting the same profit-driven companies with even more personal information about young users. The solution to corporate overreach, in other words, becomes a mechanism for deeper corporate entrenchment. This is not a reason to abandon regulation, but it reveals that regulation without structural reform of the platforms' business model is treating a symptom while feeding the disease.

Perspective Two shifts from restriction to empowerment, and there is something genuinely democratic about this instinct. Rather than deciding for young people, it proposes to equip them. In an ideal world, this is clearly the superior approach -- a society of critically literate digital citizens would need far less regulation. But we do not educate in a vacuum. Digital literacy programs operate within school systems that are underfunded, understaffed, and slow to adapt, while the platforms students are learning to navigate are redesigned quarterly by teams with resources that dwarf entire state education budgets. More fundamentally, Perspective Two rests on an Enlightenment-era assumption that informed individuals can reliably act in their own interest when given adequate knowledge. A generation of behavioral science research complicates this. Social media platforms are specifically engineered to exploit cognitive vulnerabilities -- not ignorance, but the architecture of human attention itself. Literacy is necessary. But positioning it as sufficient asks education to do what it has never done: consistently override neurological wiring that billions of dollars have been spent learning to exploit.

Perspective Three raises perhaps the most foundational question of the three, even if it does not realize it. When it argues that parents, not governments, should decide, it is really making a claim about where authority over children's development should reside. This is worth taking seriously. Centralized regulation of young people's access to communication platforms does carry genuine risks for free expression and informational autonomy -- risks that deserve more attention than they typically receive in this debate. But Perspective Three's defense of parental authority inadvertently reveals its limits. A parent can set rules within their household, but they cannot reshape the social ecosystem their child inhabits. When most of a child's peers are on social media, opting out carries significant social costs that fall entirely on the child. Parental authority, exercised in isolation, becomes a form of imposed disadvantage. This is the structure of a collective action problem, and collective action problems are precisely the situations where individual choice, however well-intentioned, is insufficient.

What emerges from examining these perspectives together is not that one is right and the others wrong, but that each correctly diagnoses a piece of the problem while remaining blind to its own assumptions. The real challenge is not choosing between regulation, education, and family autonomy. It is recognizing that all three operate within an economic system that treats children's attention as a commodity -- and that until we confront that underlying reality, every proposed solution will be working against a current stronger than itself. The most honest position is not a tidy synthesis but an acknowledgment that this problem is genuinely harder than any of the three perspectives admits, and that the discomfort of that uncertainty is preferable to the false confidence of a premature answer.

**Scoring Breakdown:**

| Domain | Score (1-6) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Ideas & Analysis | 6 | The essay operates at a level of analytical sophistication rarely seen in timed student writing. It reframes the entire debate by questioning whether existing frameworks are adequate, identifies how Perspective One's solution replicates the problem it critiques, challenges the Enlightenment assumptions underlying Perspective Two, and reveals the collective action structure hidden in Perspective Three. The analysis builds toward a genuinely original insight about the attention economy as the underlying driver. |
| Development & Support | 6 | Every claim is thoroughly and specifically developed. The argument that regulation without business model reform "treats a symptom while feeding the disease" is compelling and concrete. The contrast between school system resources and platform engineering budgets makes the asymmetry vivid. The observation that opting out of social media imposes social costs on the child -- reframing parental authority as potential imposed disadvantage -- is a striking and well-supported move. |
| Organization | 6 | The essay is masterfully structured. The introduction establishes a meta-analytical frame (the pattern of American policy debates) that governs the entire piece. Each body paragraph follows a sophisticated rhythm: engage the perspective seriously, identify its blind spot, show how that blind spot connects to a larger structural issue. The conclusion does not merely synthesize but advances the argument to a new level by naming the underlying economic reality. Each paragraph builds on the last. |
| Language Use | 6 | The prose is polished, precise, and stylistically confident. Complex ideas are expressed with clarity ("The solution to corporate overreach becomes a mechanism for deeper corporate entrenchment"). Sentence variety is exceptional -- long, multi-clause analytical sentences are balanced by short, forceful declarations. The voice is authoritative without being arrogant, and the vocabulary is sophisticated without being obscure. The writing sustains this quality throughout. |

**Calibration Notes:** Students often react to this essay in one of two ways: they either recognize it as clearly the strongest piece and score it appropriately, or they resist giving it the highest score because it "doesn't clearly state which perspective it agrees with." This is a crucial teaching moment. The ACT rubric does not require the writer to pick a winner among the perspectives; it rewards "generating productive discussion of the issue." This essay generates more productive discussion than any of the others precisely because it refuses to reduce the issue to a simple choice. Also note that this essay earns its 12 not through length or vocabulary alone but through the quality and originality of its thinking -- every paragraph contains at least one insight that the other essays do not reach.

## Part 2: Fine-Line Pairs

### Prompts Used in Part 2

**ACT-P02 | Homework in Public Schools**
Issue: The role of homework in education has become increasingly controversial. Some schools have dramatically reduced or eliminated homework, while others have doubled down on it as essential practice.
P1: Homework is essential -- it builds discipline, reinforces classroom instruction, and prepares students for the demands of college and careers.
P2: Homework should be significantly reduced -- it disproportionately harms students who lack resources, quiet spaces, or parental support at home, widening existing inequities.
P3: The real problem is homework quality, not quantity -- schools should replace rote busywork with meaningful, project-based assignments that deepen understanding.

**ACT-P03 | Renewable Energy Transition**
Issue: As climate change accelerates, governments worldwide face pressure to transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy sources. The pace and method of this transition remain fiercely debated.
P1: The transition to renewable energy must happen as rapidly as possible -- the scientific consensus on climate change demands immediate, aggressive action regardless of short-term economic disruption.
P2: A gradual transition is more responsible -- moving too quickly risks devastating fossil fuel-dependent communities, destabilizing energy grids, and triggering economic crises that ultimately undermine public support for clean energy.
P3: The debate over speed is premature -- the real barrier is technological readiness. Massive investment in research and infrastructure (energy storage, grid modernization, nuclear innovation) must come before large-scale mandates can succeed.

### ACT-SE-07 | Score: 5 | Prompt: ACT-P02 | Fine-Line Pair 1

**Student Essay:**

Homework has been a part of school for as long as anyone can remember, but recently people have started to question whether it is actually helpful. There are different perspectives on this issue, and each one makes some good points.

The first perspective says that homework is essential because it builds discipline and reinforces what students learn in class. I think this is mostly true. When I have math homework, it helps me practice the formulas we learned that day so I don't forget them. Without that practice, I would probably do worse on tests. Homework also teaches you to manage your time and be responsible, which are skills you need in college and in life.

The second perspective argues that homework should be reduced because it hurts students who don't have good conditions at home. This is a fair point. Not every student has a quiet room or parents who can help them. Some students have to work after school or take care of younger siblings. If teachers assigned projects that were actually interesting and helped students think deeper about the material, students would be more motivated to do them. Busywork like copying vocabulary words ten times doesn't really help anyone learn.

In conclusion, I believe that homework can be valuable, but only if it is well-designed and if teachers are aware of the challenges some students face at home. Schools should focus on making homework meaningful rather than just assigning it out of habit.

### ACT-SE-08 | Score: 6 | Prompt: ACT-P02 | Fine-Line Pair 1

**Student Essay:**

The homework debate is more complicated than it first appears. On the surface, it seems like a simple question of more or less, but the three perspectives reveal that the real disagreements are about what homework is supposed to accomplish and who it is really serving.

Perspective One defends homework as a builder of discipline and a reinforcer of learning. There is truth to this -- repetition does help cement skills, especially in subjects like math and foreign languages where practice matters. But this perspective tends to treat all homework as equally valuable, which it clearly is not. A student who spends thirty minutes solving challenging problems is having a very different experience from one who spends thirty minutes filling in a worksheet that requires no real thought. By defending homework in general terms, Perspective One avoids the harder question of what kinds of practice actually lead to learning.

Students without those advantages may struggle with the same assignments, not because they are less capable but because they are working under fundamentally different conditions. This means that homework, as it is typically assigned, can actually widen the gap between advantaged and disadvantaged students rather than closing it.

Perspective Three tries to resolve this tension by focusing on quality over quantity. Meaningful projects and thoughtful assignments could, in theory, engage all students more effectively. However, this perspective assumes that all teachers have the time and training to design such assignments, which is not always the case -- especially in underfunded schools where teachers are already stretched thin.

My own view is closest to Perspective Three, but with an important caveat. Improving homework quality is the right goal, but it requires investing in teachers and schools, not just telling teachers to "assign better work." Without that investment, the quality argument becomes another way of blaming educators for systemic problems.

### ACT-SE-09 | Score: 8 | Prompt: ACT-P02 | Fine-Line Pair 2

**Student Essay:**

When schools debate homework policy, they tend to treat it as an isolated pedagogical question: how much practice do students need? But homework is never just about practice. It is a policy that follows students home, entering living rooms and kitchen tables and bedrooms, and its effects are shaped by the vastly different conditions it finds there. Any serious position on homework must account for that reality.

Perspective One's defense of homework rests on a model of learning that is individually focused: students receive instruction, practice at home, and return prepared. This model works reasonably well for students whose home environments support it. A student with a dedicated study space, a parent who can clarify a confusing concept, and enough free time to engage thoughtfully with assignments will almost certainly benefit from homework. The problem is that Perspective One universalizes this experience. It treats the ideal conditions as the default, when for millions of students they are the exception. A policy justified by its benefits under favorable conditions is not a good policy if it produces harm under common ones.

Perspective Two names this inequity directly and argues for reduction. The logic is sound: if homework advantages those who already have advantages, then reducing it narrows the gap. But Perspective Two's solution is blunt. Reducing homework across the board means that students who do benefit from practice lose something real. The line between genuine concern for workers and regulatory capture by incumbent firms is not always easy to locate, and Perspective Two tends to blur it.
