## Part 1: Analytical Essay (40 minutes)

### Passage: SP-031 — Abigail Adams, "Remember the Ladies" Letter (1776)

**Source:** Letter from Abigail Adams to John Adams, March 31, 1776. Public domain.

---

I long to hear that you have declared an independency. And, by the way, in the new code of laws which I suppose it will be necessary for you to make, I desire you would remember the ladies and be more generous and favorable to them than your ancestors. Do not put such unlimited power into the hands of the husbands. Remember, all men would be tyrants if they could. If particular care and attention is not paid to the ladies, we are determined to foment a rebellion, and will not hold ourselves bound by any laws in which we have no voice or representation.

That your sex are naturally tyrannical is a truth so thoroughly established as to admit of no dispute; but such of you as wish to be happy willingly give up the harsh title of master for the more tender and endearing one of friend. Why, then, not put it out of the power of the vicious and the lawless to use us with cruelty and indignity with impunity? Men of sense in all ages abhor those customs which treat us only as the vassals of your sex; regard us then as beings placed by Providence under your protection, and in imitation of the Supreme Being make use of that power only for our happiness.

I have sometimes been ready to think that the passion for liberty cannot be equally strong in the breasts of those who have been accustomed to deprive their fellow creatures of theirs. Of this I am certain, that it is not founded upon that generous and Christian principle of doing to others as we would that others should do unto us.

I feel very differently at the approach of spring from what I did a month ago. We knew not then whether we could plant or sow with safety, whether when we had tilled we could reap the fruits of our own industry, whether we could rest in our own cottages or whether we should be driven from the seacoast to seek shelter in the wilderness; but now we feel as if we might sit under our own vine and eat the good of the land.

Though we felicitate ourselves, we sympathize with those who are trembling lest the lot of Boston should be theirs. But they cannot be in similar circumstances unless pusillanimity and cowardice should take possession of them. They have time and warning given them to see the evil and shun it.

*[~390 words]*

---

### Essay Prompt

In her March 31, 1776 letter to John Adams, Abigail Adams makes a case for women's rights during the founding of a new nation. Read the letter carefully. Then write an essay in which you analyze how Adams uses rhetorical choices — including diction, tone, structure, and audience awareness — to advance her argument.

Your essay should:

- Present a clear, arguable thesis about Adams's rhetorical strategy
- Support your thesis with specific evidence from the text
- Analyze how Adams's choices function and what effects they create (use S-F-E)
- Demonstrate organized, logical essay structure

### Model Essay (Scorer Calibration Only — NOT Shown to Students)

**Target Score: 5/6**

---

Abigail Adams writes to her husband not as a petitioner begging for mercy but as a political strategist who understands that the most persuasive arguments wear the clothing of shared values. In her March 31, 1776 letter, Adams employs a deliberate blend of affectionate intimacy, Enlightenment reasoning, and veiled threat to argue that the new nation's laws must protect women — and she does so by turning the Revolution's own logic against those who would exclude half the population from its promises.

Adams's most striking rhetorical choice is her diction of tyranny, which she borrows directly from the patriots' vocabulary to reframe gender relations. She writes that "all men would be tyrants if they could" and declares "your sex are naturally tyrannical" as "a truth so thoroughly established as to admit of no dispute." The strategy here is unmistakable: she selects the very word — *tyrant* — that the Continental Congress uses to denounce King George, and she applies it to husbands. This functions as a mirror, forcing John Adams and his colleagues to confront the uncomfortable parallel between the British tyranny they oppose and the domestic tyranny they perpetuate. The effect is to make any dismissal of her argument intellectually dishonest; if tyranny justifies revolution against a king, it must at least justify legal reform for wives.

Equally deliberate is Adams's structural choice to pair threat with tenderness. She warns that women "are determined to foment a rebellion, and will not hold ourselves bound by any laws in which we have no voice or representation," echoing the colonists' own rallying cry. Yet she immediately softens this warning by appealing to men who "wish to be happy" and who would "willingly give up the harsh title of master for the more tender and endearing one of friend." This juxtaposition functions as a classic persuasive structure: she raises the cost of refusal (rebellion) while simultaneously lowering the cost of agreement (friendship rather than mastery). The effect is to make her demand feel reasonable and the alternative feel foolish, nudging her audience toward compliance rather than resistance.

Finally, Adams deploys audience awareness through her appeal to religious and moral authority. She invokes "that generous and Christian principle of doing to others as we would that others should do unto us" and asks men to act "in imitation of the Supreme Being." These references function as an ethical frame that elevates her argument beyond personal complaint; she is not merely a wife asking her husband for a favor but a moral voice appealing to universal principles that John Adams, as a man of faith and philosophy, cannot easily reject. The effect is to place the burden of justification on those who would deny women's rights, rather than on those who claim them.

Adams's letter succeeds because she refuses to argue from weakness. By co-opting the Revolution's language, balancing threat with warmth, and grounding her claim in shared moral authority, she constructs an argument that her audience can only dismiss by abandoning the very principles they claim to fight for.

## Part 2: Conventions (20 minutes, 15 items)

---

### Inline-Choice Items (CONV-01 through CONV-05)

---

### CONV-01 | Subject-verb agreement

**Sentence:** The series of arguments Adams presents in her letter _____ that she understood political rhetoric.

**(A)** demonstrate
**(B)** demonstrates
**(C)** are demonstrating

**Correct:** B

**Rationale:** The subject is "series" (singular), not "arguments," so the verb must be singular: "demonstrates."

---

### CONV-02 | Verb tense consistency

**Sentence:** In the opening paragraph, Adams appeals to shared values and then _____ the consequences of ignoring her request.

**(A)** outlined
**(B)** outlines
**(C)** will outline

**Correct:** B

**Rationale:** The sentence establishes present-tense literary analysis ("appeals"), so the parallel verb must also be present tense: "outlines."

---

### CONV-03 | Comma usage — introductory phrase

**Sentence:** _____ Adams shifts to a tone of moral authority.

**(A)** After warning of rebellion
**(B)** After warning of rebellion,
**(C)** After, warning of rebellion

**Correct:** B

**Rationale:** An introductory adverbial phrase should be followed by a comma before the main clause.

---

### CONV-04 | Semicolon usage

**Sentence:** Adams's letter balances warmth with firmness _____ this combination makes her argument difficult to dismiss.

**(A)** , and
**(B)** ;
**(C)** ,
**(D)** and

**Correct:** B

**Rationale:** Two independent clauses without a coordinating conjunction require a semicolon; option C creates a comma splice, and option D is a run-on.

---

### CONV-05 | Parallel structure

**Sentence:** Adams's rhetorical strategy involves _____ the language of revolution, appealing to moral principle, and warning of consequences.

**(A)** to co-opt
**(B)** co-opting
**(C)** she co-opts
**(D)** the co-opting of

**Correct:** B

**Rationale:** The series uses gerund phrases ("appealing," "warning"), so the first item must also be a gerund: "co-opting."

---

### Hottext / Error ID Items (CONV-06 through CONV-10)

---

### CONV-06 | Subject-verb agreement

**Sentence:** (A) Each of the rhetorical strategies (B) that Adams employs in her letter (C) serve a distinct persuasive purpose. (D) No error.

**Correct:** C

**Rationale:** "Each" is singular, so the verb should be "serves," not "serve."

---

### CONV-07 | Pronoun-antecedent agreement

**Sentence:** (A) When a reader encounters Adams's argument, (B) they must consider the historical context (C) in which the letter was written. (D) No error.

**Correct:** D

**Rationale:** Singular "they" is accepted as a gender-neutral pronoun for the indefinite "a reader"; the sentence contains no error.

---

### CONV-08 | Comma usage — restrictive vs. nonrestrictive

**Sentence:** (A) The phrase "remember the ladies" (B) which has become Adams's most famous line, (C) functions as both a personal plea and a political demand. (D) No error.

**Correct:** A

**Rationale:** The nonrestrictive clause "which has become Adams's most famous line" requires a comma before "which"; the comma should appear after "ladies" — i.e., the area around (A)/(B) is missing a comma.

---

### CONV-09 | Verb tense consistency

**Sentence:** (A) Adams wrote the letter in 1776, (B) and she appeals to Enlightenment values (C) that resonated with her husband's beliefs. (D) No error.

**Correct:** B

**Rationale:** The sentence begins in past tense ("wrote") describing a historical action, but "appeals" shifts to present tense inconsistently; it should read "appealed" to match the past-tense frame.

---

### CONV-10 | Sentence fragment

**Sentence:** (A) Adams's argument is compelling. (B) Especially when she invokes the Golden Rule. (C) This appeal strengthens her moral authority. (D) No error.

**Correct:** B

**Rationale:** "Especially when she invokes the Golden Rule" is a dependent clause fragment; it should be attached to the preceding sentence.

### Revision / Concision Items (CONV-11 through CONV-15)

---

### CONV-11 | Concision / wordiness

**Original sentence:** It is important to note that the fact that Adams uses the word "tyrant" is significant because of the reason that it mirrors the colonists' language about the king.

**(A)** Adams's use of "tyrant" is significant because it mirrors the colonists' language about the king.
**(B)** It is notable that Adams's word "tyrant" mirrors the language the colonists used about the king, which is significant.
**(C)** The fact that Adams uses "tyrant" is important to note because it is the same word used about the king.
**(D)** Adams uses the significant word "tyrant," and this is important because the colonists also used it.

**Correct:** A

**Rationale:** Option A eliminates the redundant phrasing ("it is important to note that the fact that") while preserving the original meaning clearly and concisely.

---

### CONV-12 | Concision / wordiness

**Original sentence:** Due to the fact that Adams was writing to her husband, she was able to make use of an intimate tone in order to be more persuasive in her argument.

**(A)** Because Adams was writing to her husband, she could use an intimate tone to strengthen her argument.
**(B)** Adams, due to the fact she wrote to her husband, made use of intimacy for persuasion in her argument.
**(C)** Writing to her husband was the reason Adams was able to make use of an intimate and persuasive tone.
**(D)** Since she wrote to her husband, Adams was in a position to be more persuasive by making use of intimacy.

**Correct:** A

**Rationale:** Option A replaces wordy constructions ("due to the fact that," "was able to make use of," "in order to") with concise equivalents without losing meaning.

---

### CONV-13 | Run-on sentence

**Original sentence:** Adams warns of rebellion she also appeals to shared values this dual approach strengthens her argument.

**(A)** Adams warns of rebellion, she also appeals to shared values, this dual approach strengthens her argument.
**(B)** Adams warns of rebellion and also appeals to shared values. This dual approach strengthens her argument.
**(C)** Adams warns of rebellion; she also appeals to shared values; this dual approach strengthens her argument.
**(D)** Adams warns of rebellion she also appeals to shared values, and this dual approach strengthens her argument.

**Correct:** B

**Rationale:** Option B correctly separates the fused sentence into two clear sentences; option A is a double comma splice, C over-uses semicolons awkwardly, and D retains the initial run-on.

---

### CONV-14 | Comma usage — comma splice

**Original sentence:** Adams's letter is a masterwork of persuasion, few political documents from the era match its rhetorical sophistication.

**(A)** Adams's letter is a masterwork of persuasion few political documents from the era match its rhetorical sophistication.
**(B)** Adams's letter is a masterwork of persuasion; few political documents from the era match its rhetorical sophistication.
**(C)** Adams's letter is a masterwork of persuasion, and, few political documents from the era match its rhetorical sophistication.
**(D)** Adams's letter, is a masterwork of persuasion, few political documents from the era match its rhetorical sophistication.

**Correct:** B

**Rationale:** A semicolon correctly joins two independent clauses without a coordinating conjunction, fixing the comma splice.

---

### CONV-15 | Modifier placement

**Original sentence:** Carefully choosing her words, the argument Adams constructs becomes impossible to dismiss on logical grounds.

**(A)** Carefully choosing her words, Adams constructs an argument that becomes impossible to dismiss on logical grounds.
**(B)** The argument Adams constructs, carefully choosing her words, becomes impossible to dismiss on logical grounds.
**(C)** Adams constructs an argument, carefully choosing her words, becoming impossible to dismiss on logical grounds.
**(D)** On logical grounds, carefully choosing her words, the argument becomes impossible to dismiss.

**Correct:** A

**Rationale:** The participial phrase "Carefully choosing her words" must modify "Adams" (the person doing the choosing), not "the argument"; only option A places the correct subject immediately after the modifier.
