# AP Language Scored Essays for Rubric Calibration

**Prompt:** "In his 1852 speech 'What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?' Frederick Douglass addresses the Rochester Ladies' Anti-Slavery Society. Read the passage carefully. Then write an essay that analyzes the rhetorical strategies Douglass uses to convey his message about American hypocrisy regarding slavery."

**AP Rubric:** Row A (Thesis, 0-1), Row B (Evidence & Commentary, 0-4), Row C (Sophistication, 0-1). Total: 0-6.

### LANG-05 | Score: 3 | Type: Rhetorical Analysis

In "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" Frederick Douglass strategically employs bitter irony and direct confrontation to expose the contradiction between America's celebration of liberty and its continued practice of slavery, forcing his audience to reckon with their own complicity in this national hypocrisy.

By acknowledging the greatness of American ideals first, he makes it harder for the audience to dismiss him when he then argues that those very ideals condemn the practice of slavery. He deliberately separates himself from the celebration, reminding the audience that the freedoms they enjoy are denied to millions. He goes on to call the holiday a "sham" and "mere bombast," using harsh diction to strip away any comfortable patriotism the audience might feel. He compares slavery to sins condemned in the Bible, which would resonate strongly with the Rochester Ladies' Anti-Slavery Society, a group motivated by moral and religious conviction. By framing slavery as not just politically wrong but spiritually condemned, Douglass elevates his argument beyond policy debate.

### LANG-06 | Score: 3 | Type: Rhetorical Analysis

Frederick Douglass uses pointed rhetorical questions and stark juxtaposition in his 1852 speech to confront his audience with the moral failure of a nation that proclaims liberty while enslaving millions.

One of Douglass's most prominent strategies is his use of juxtaposition to highlight American hypocrisy. He places images of Fourth of July celebration directly alongside the reality of slavery. While Americans enjoy "shouts of liberty and equality," enslaved people suffer in chains. By putting these two realities side by side, Douglass makes it impossible for the audience to hold both ideas comfortably. The contrast is not subtle, and it is not meant to be -- Douglass wants the dissonance to be jarring. This juxtaposition functions as an argument in itself: the two realities cannot coexist, so either the celebration is hollow or the nation must change.

Douglass also uses second-person address strategically. He repeatedly says "your" rather than "our" when referring to national celebrations and ideals. When he says the Fourth of July is "yours, not mine," he is performing an act of rhetorical exclusion. He positions himself outside the national community, which forces the audience to see liberty as a possession they hold while others are denied it. This is particularly effective because the audience likely expected a celebratory speech, and Douglass's refusal to include himself in the celebration disrupts that expectation.

Finally, Douglass employs increasingly intense diction as the speech progresses. Early in the speech he is measured and respectful, but he builds toward language like "revolting barbarity and shameless hypocrisy." This escalation mirrors the structure of his argument: he begins by granting America its ideals, then systematically dismantles the nation's claim to those ideals. The building intensity is meant to leave the audience with no comfortable retreat.

In conclusion, Douglass's rhetorical strategies all work toward the same goal of stripping away the audience's complacency and making them see their celebration of freedom as an act of hypocrisy while slavery persists.

## Key Calibration Patterns

**Score 1 vs. Score 2:** The dividing line is whether the student attempts analysis or merely summarizes. Score 1 essays talk about what Douglass says; Score 2 essays try to talk about how he says it, even if that analysis is shallow. The presence of device names alone does not guarantee a 2 -- there must be some attempt to connect device to text.

**Score 2 vs. Score 3:** The dividing line is the thesis. Score 2 essays typically have a "list thesis" that names devices without making an argument. Score 3 essays make a defensible claim about how and why Douglass's strategies function. In Row B, both score levels may earn a 2, but Score 3 essays tend to show moments of genuine S-F-E analysis even if those moments are inconsistent.

**Common Scoring Errors:**
- Giving credit for device identification without analysis (inflates Score 1 essays to 2)
- Awarding thesis points for restating the prompt with device names inserted (inflates Score 2 essays to 3)
- Scoring based on the best paragraph rather than the whole essay (inflates Row B scores)
- Confusing content knowledge about the speech with rhetorical analysis of the speech (inflates all score levels)

### LANG-07 | Score: 4 | Type: Rhetorical Analysis

In his 1852 speech "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" Frederick Douglass uses several rhetorical strategies to convey his message about American hypocrisy regarding slavery. Through the use of rhetorical questions, irony, and strong diction, Douglass effectively argues that the Fourth of July is meaningless to enslaved people and that America's celebration of freedom is deeply hypocritical.

One rhetorical strategy Douglass uses is rhetorical questions. He asks, "What, to the American slave, is your Fourth of July?" This question forces the audience to consider a perspective they have never had to think about. By posing this as a question rather than a statement, Douglass makes his audience feel the weight of the contradiction themselves. He does not simply tell them that the holiday is meaningless to slaves; he makes them arrive at this conclusion on their own. This is an effective strategy because it creates discomfort in the audience, which is exactly what Douglass wants.

Another strategy Douglass employs is irony. He describes the Fourth of July as a day that reveals "the gross injustice and cruelty to which he is the constant victim." The irony is that a celebration of liberty actually highlights the absence of liberty for millions of Americans. Douglass uses this irony to show that the holiday is not just irrelevant to enslaved people but is actually an insult. By pointing out this contradiction, Douglass strengthens his argument that America is being hypocritical.

Douglass also uses strong, emotional diction to convey his message. Words like "fraud," "deception," and "hollow mockery" paint the Fourth of July in a very negative light. These word choices make the audience feel the anger and frustration that Douglass and enslaved people experience. The strong diction helps Douglass communicate not just an intellectual argument but an emotional one as well.

In conclusion, Frederick Douglass uses rhetorical questions, irony, and powerful diction to expose American hypocrisy. These strategies work together to make his audience understand that the Fourth of July means something very different to those who are enslaved. His speech remains a powerful example of how rhetoric can challenge injustice.

Overall, Douglass uses contrast, pathos, and allusion to build a convincing argument about American hypocrisy. Each strategy works to expose the distance between what America says it believes and what it actually practices. His speech is a masterful example of persuasion.

### LANG-09 | Score: 5 | Type: Rhetorical Analysis

When Frederick Douglass stood before the Rochester Ladies' Anti-Slavery Society in 1852, he faced a peculiar rhetorical challenge: he was asked to celebrate a holiday that excluded him. His speech, "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" transforms this impossible position into a devastating argument, using irony, second-person address, and shifting tone to force his white audience to confront the moral chasm between their ideals and their actions.

Douglass's most striking strategy is his weaponization of the pronoun "your." Throughout the speech, he refers to "your Fourth of July," "your celebration," and "your national independence." This deliberate use of second-person address draws a line between himself and his audience, reminding them that the freedoms they celebrate are not his to share. The word "your" does double work: it is polite — he is, after all, their invited guest — but it is also an accusation. Every time he says "your," he is really saying "not mine," and by extension, "not ours." This subtle distancing forces the audience to own America's contradictions rather than hiding behind vague national pride.

The speech also builds its power through a dramatic tonal shift. Douglass begins with apparent deference, praising the founding fathers and acknowledging the greatness of the Declaration of Independence. He seems, for a moment, to be delivering the patriotic address his audience expects. But this praise is a rhetorical trap. When Douglass pivots to ask what this "great" document means to the enslaved, the contrast between his earlier praise and his present condemnation makes the hypocrisy feel even more extreme. The audience has nodded along to his compliments; now they must face the fact that those very ideals condemn them. Douglass essentially uses America's own mythology as evidence against it.

Finally, Douglass deploys biblical language to reframe slavery as not just a political failure but a sin. He invokes the prophetic tradition of calling a nation to account, positioning himself not as a radical but as a moral voice in the tradition of Isaiah or Jeremiah. For his audience — largely religious abolitionists — this framing makes his argument nearly impossible to dismiss without also dismissing their own faith.

Douglass's speech succeeds because it does not simply argue against slavery; it turns America's proudest symbols into proof of its greatest shame. The Fourth of July, the Declaration, the Bible — all become evidence for the prosecution.

### LANG-10 | Score: 5 | Type: Rhetorical Analysis

Frederick Douglass's "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" is not simply an antislavery speech — it is a rhetorical performance that uses the occasion itself as a weapon. By exploiting the expectations of his audience, employing scorching irony, and marshaling the language of American patriotism against itself, Douglass constructs an argument that is nearly impossible to refute without abandoning the principles America claims to hold dear.

The foundation of Douglass's rhetorical power is irony, and it begins before he speaks a single word. He has been invited to address a Fourth of July celebration — as a formerly enslaved man. The occasion itself is ironic, and Douglass makes sure his audience feels it. When he states that "this Fourth of July is yours, not mine," he is not simply excluding himself from the holiday; he is exposing the fact that the invitation itself was an act of unconscious hypocrisy. His audience invited him to celebrate freedom while his people remain in chains. By naming this contradiction directly, Douglass transforms what could have been an awkward undercurrent into the central argument of his speech.

Douglass further strengthens his case by turning the founders' own words into ammunition. He quotes and praises the Declaration of Independence, calling its principles "great" and "saving," only to ask whether those principles apply to the enslaved. This is not mere contrast; it is a logical trap. If the audience agrees that the Declaration is great, they must also agree that slavery violates it. Douglass does not need to introduce outside moral authority — he simply holds America to the standard it set for itself. This strategy is particularly effective because it makes his audience complicit in his argument: their own patriotism becomes the evidence against them.

Douglass also modulates his tone with precision. He moves from measured respect to biting sarcasm to righteous fury, and each shift serves a purpose. The early restraint earns him credibility; the sarcasm destabilizes his audience's comfort; the fury demands action. When he declares that "for revolting barbarity and shameless hypocrisy, America reigns without a rival," the escalation feels earned precisely because he began with such control. The audience cannot dismiss him as merely angry — they have already agreed with his premises.

In the end, Douglass does not ask his audience to pity the enslaved. He asks them to be consistent. That is what makes the speech so difficult to argue against, and what makes it resonate far beyond 1852.
