## Prompt 1: The Nature of Progress

### For Student

In his essay "The Cost of Change," a contemporary social critic writes: "All genuine progress is built upon the ruins of what came before. We cannot create the new without destroying the old; meaningful change always requires sacrifice and disruption."

This perspective suggests that progress and destruction are inseparable—that society cannot move forward without leaving something valuable behind. Yet others argue that the most enduring advances come through careful preservation and gradual evolution, not through radical upheaval.

**Write an essay that argues your position on whether significant progress requires sacrifice or disruption.**

In your response you should do the following:
- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument

### Designer Notes

**Topic:** Whether progress inherently requires destruction/sacrifice vs. gradual/preservative change

**Core Claim:** The quotation argues that meaningful progress necessarily involves sacrifice and disruption; you cannot advance without destroying what existed before.

**Complexity:** This is genuinely debatable because history shows both revolutionary breakthroughs (often destructive) and evolutionary progress (preserving while improving). The tension lies in defining "genuine progress" and determining whether disruption is a *necessary condition* or merely a *frequent accompaniment*.

**Three Strong Positions:**
1. **Agree:** Industrial Revolution, civil rights movements, scientific paradigm shifts all required breaking old systems; progress means fundamental transformation
2. **Disagree:** Medical advances, legal reforms, educational improvements often build incrementally; destruction creates chaos that slows real change
3. **Complicate:** Depends on what's being changed (social structures vs. technology), or progress requires disrupting *ideas* not necessarily *institutions*, or small destructions enable larger preservation

**Evidence Students Might Use:**
- Historical: Industrial Revolution, Civil Rights Movement, Renaissance, French/American Revolutions, fall of colonialism
- Literary: *Brave New World*, *The Crucible*, *Things Fall Apart*, Romantic poets vs. industrialization
- Scientific: Paradigm shifts (Copernicus, Darwin, Einstein), medical breakthroughs, environmental movements
- Contemporary: Social media disruption, renewable energy transition, education reform debates

**Row C Opportunities:**
- Distinguishing between types of progress (technological vs. moral vs. social)
- Exploring who defines what is "sacrificed" and who benefits from disruption
- Examining whether perceived destruction is actually loss or transformation
- Questioning the frame: perhaps "progress" itself is the wrong goal

**Common Student Pitfalls:**
- Listing examples of change without analyzing whether disruption was *necessary*
- Assuming all change is progress or all disruption is bad
- Failing to define "genuine progress" or engage with what counts as meaningful
- Simply stating "balance is needed" without defending a position

## Prompt 2: Knowledge and Certainty

### For Student

The philosopher Bertrand Russell observed: "The whole problem with the world is that fools and fanatics are always so certain of themselves, and wiser people so full of doubts."

Russell's observation suggests that intellectual humility and doubt are marks of true wisdom, while certainty often accompanies shallow thinking. Yet others contend that doubt can paralyze action, and that some degree of certainty is necessary for moral courage, scientific progress, and effective leadership.

**Write an essay that argues your position on the relationship between knowledge and certainty.**

In your response you should do the following:
- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument

### Designer Notes

**Topic:** Whether true knowledge requires doubt/uncertainty vs. whether certainty is necessary for wisdom and action

**Core Claim:** Russell argues that certainty correlates with foolishness while doubt indicates wisdom; the most dangerous people are those who lack intellectual humility.

**Complexity:** Both extremes are problematic: absolute certainty can lead to dogmatism and cruelty, but paralyzing doubt prevents action. The tension involves distinguishing between *justified confidence* and *arrogant certainty*, and determining whether uncertainty is a bug or a feature of genuine understanding.

**Three Strong Positions:**
1. **Agree with Russell:** History's atrocities often stem from ideological certainty (totalitarianism, religious persecution); scientific method requires questioning; Socratic wisdom is knowing you don't know
2. **Disagree:** Moral progress requires conviction (abolitionists, civil rights leaders had certainty about justice); scientists must have confidence in established findings to build knowledge; doubt enables inaction
3. **Complicate:** Domain-specific (doubt in science, certainty in ethics?), or process vs. conclusion (doubt while investigating, certainty when deciding), or distinguishing confidence from closed-mindedness

**Evidence Students Might Use:**
- Historical: WWII totalitarianism, Inquisition, McCarthyism, civil rights leaders' moral certainty, scientific revolutions
- Literary: *Oedipus*, *Othello*, *1984*, *The Crucible*, *Hamlet* (paralysis through doubt)
- Scientific: Paradigm shifts requiring doubt of established theory, but also need for reliable knowledge to make decisions
- Contemporary: Conspiracy theories, expert dismissal, climate science, social justice movements

**Row C Opportunities:**
- Distinguishing between epistemic humility (about what we *know*) and moral confidence (about what we *value*)
- Exploring how language of "certainty" vs. "confidence" frames the issue
- Examining power dynamics: who is allowed to express doubt vs. who must project certainty
- Questioning whether "knowledge" is the right framework—perhaps wisdom is about judgment, not certainty or doubt

**Common Student Pitfalls:**
- Treating this as "moderation" argument (balance between extremes) without defending why that's wisdom
- Confusing doubt with ignorance or certainty with knowledge
- Using examples of people who were wrong without analyzing their certainty vs. their reasoning
- Failing to distinguish between different domains (science, ethics, politics) where certainty functions differently

## Prompt 3: Individual Freedom and Community Obligation

### For Student

In an influential essay on democratic society, a political theorist argues: "The individual who demands absolute personal freedom while contributing nothing to the common good is not a citizen but a parasite. True liberty exists only within a framework of mutual obligation and shared sacrifice."

This view suggests that individual freedom must be balanced against—or perhaps even depends upon—responsibility to community. Yet others maintain that individual rights are fundamental and must be protected from collective demands, arguing that society flourishes most when individuals are free to pursue their own interests.

**Write an essay that argues your position on the tension between individual freedom and community obligation.**

In your response you should do the following:
- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument

### Designer Notes

**Topic:** Relationship between individual liberty and community responsibility; whether freedom requires obligation or is threatened by it

**Core Claim:** The quotation argues that unfettered individualism is parasitic; genuine freedom exists only through mutual obligation and collective sacrifice.

**Complexity:** This is a foundational political/philosophical tension. Both extremes fail: pure individualism undermines the social infrastructure that enables freedom, but excessive collectivism crushes individual autonomy. The debate involves defining "freedom" (negative rights vs. positive liberty) and determining whether obligation *limits* freedom or *creates conditions for* freedom.

**Three Strong Positions:**
1. **Agree with quotation:** Lockean social contract, public health (vaccination), taxation for public goods, military service; individual rights meaningless without collective structures (courts, security, education)
2. **Disagree:** Mill's harm principle, free speech absolutism, entrepreneurship and innovation require freedom from collective demands; history shows collective obligation often masks tyranny
3. **Complicate:** Depends on type of freedom (political vs. economic), or distinguishes voluntary mutual aid from coerced obligation, or argues the dichotomy is false (individual flourishing and community health are interdependent)

**Evidence Students Might Use:**
- Historical: Founding debates (Federalist vs. Anti-Federalist), New Deal, Great Society programs, draft resistance, taxation battles, pandemic response
- Literary: *Walden*, *Civil Disobedience*, *Atlas Shrugged*, *The Grapes of Wrath*, *Beloved*, *The Jungle*
- Philosophical: Locke, Mill, Rawls, Nozick, communitarian critiques
- Contemporary: Healthcare debates, climate change collective action, social media responsibility, vaccine mandates, wealth inequality

**Row C Opportunities:**
- Examining how power structures determine who is called "parasite" vs. "free rider" (welfare recipients vs. tax avoiders?)
- Exploring whether "obligation" must be coerced or can emerge from enlightened self-interest
- Distinguishing between formal liberty (legal rights) and substantive freedom (actual capability)
- Questioning the frame: perhaps freedom/obligation binary obscures more than it reveals; maybe we need to redefine "community"

**Common Student Pitfalls:**
- Generic "balance" argument without defending a priority when they conflict
- Treating this as capitalism vs. socialism without engaging the philosophical tension
- Using examples of bad government to argue against all collective obligation
- Failing to define what "freedom" means (freedom from vs. freedom to)
- Assuming community = government (ignoring voluntary associations, families, etc.)

## Prompt 4: The Value of Difficulty (RESERVED FOR L20 GATE)

### For Student

In her memoir of scientific discovery, a Nobel Prize-winning physicist writes: "Every breakthrough I achieved came through struggle. Easy problems taught me nothing; only when I was forced to confront challenges that seemed insurmountable did I develop the tools to think in genuinely new ways. We do students no favors when we smooth their path."

This perspective suggests that difficulty and struggle are not merely obstacles to overcome but essential components of meaningful learning and achievement. However, others argue that unnecessary difficulty is simply inefficient, that removing barriers allows more people to achieve their potential, and that suffering is not a prerequisite for excellence.

**Write an essay that argues your position on whether difficulty and struggle are essential to meaningful achievement.**

In your response you should do the following:
- Respond to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position
- Provide evidence to support your line of reasoning
- Explain how the evidence supports your line of reasoning
- Use appropriate grammar and punctuation in communicating your argument

### Designer Notes

**Topic:** Whether difficulty/struggle is essential to meaningful achievement vs. whether removing obstacles is always preferable

**Core Claim:** The physicist argues that struggle is not just unavoidable but *pedagogically necessary*—that challenges create capacity, and making things easier prevents real growth.

**Complexity:** This cuts across education, equity, and achievement. There's an important distinction between *productive struggle* (challenging but achievable with effort) vs. *arbitrary barriers* (hazing, systemic obstacles). The tension involves determining when difficulty builds capacity vs. when it merely excludes people, and whether "meaningful achievement" requires personal adversity or just high standards.

**Three Strong Positions:**
1. **Agree:** Growth mindset research, athletic training principles, artistic mastery requiring practice through failure, resilience developed through adversity; "desirable difficulties" in learning science
2. **Disagree:** Accessibility accommodations enable achievement without unnecessary suffering; systemic barriers (poverty, discrimination) don't build character but crush potential; genius often emerges when barriers are *removed*
3. **Complicate:** Distinguish productive struggle from systemic obstacles, or argue difficulty must be *chosen* not imposed, or claim struggle matters for some domains (skill development) but not others (access to opportunity), or examine who romanticizes struggle and who benefits from that narrative

**Evidence Students Might Use:**
- Educational: Growth mindset (Dweck), deliberate practice, scaffolding debates, accessibility and accommodations, tracking and AP access
- Historical: Immigrant narratives, civil rights struggle, scientists overcoming obstacles (also: privilege enabling achievement)
- Literary: *Educated*, *The Glass Castle*, Horatio Alger myths, *Hidden Figures*, bildungsroman tradition
- Athletic/Artistic: Training principles, 10,000-hour rule, stories of perseverance
- Contemporary: Debate over college admissions essays about adversity, grade inflation, student debt, disability rights

**Row C Opportunities:**
- Examining who gets to narrate their struggle as achievement vs. who is simply labeled as deficient
- Distinguishing between internal challenges (intellectual difficulty) and external barriers (discrimination, poverty)
- Exploring survivorship bias: we hear from those who succeeded through struggle, not those crushed by it
- Questioning "meaningful achievement" itself: meaningful to whom? Achievement defined how? Is the physicist's frame itself privileged?
- Analyzing how difficulty-as-virtue narratives can justify systemic inequality

**Common Student Pitfalls:**
- Uncritically celebrating all forms of struggle without distinguishing productive from destructive difficulty
- Using personal anecdote about overcoming challenge without analyzing whether the challenge was necessary
- Treating this as purely individual psychology question (ignoring systemic barriers)
- Assuming that because *some* great achievers struggled, struggle *causes* achievement
- Failing to question who benefits from narratives that valorize difficulty
