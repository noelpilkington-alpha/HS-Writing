## What Is Voice in Academic Writing?

**Voice** = the consistent set of choices a writer makes in diction, syntax, tone, and rhythm that creates a recognizable personality on the page.

**Key insight:** Voice is NOT informality. It is not about being casual or breaking rules. It is about making CONSISTENT, DELIBERATE choices within the constraints of academic writing. Two scholars can analyze the same text and both be rigorous -- but sound completely different.

## Two Analytical Excerpts: Same Passage, Different Voices

Both excerpts analyze the same rhetorical choice in Thomas Paine's *The Crisis* (SP-030) -- specifically, Paine's use of the paired epithets "summer soldier" and "sunshine patriot" in the opening paragraph.

### Excerpt A: Spare and Direct

Paine does not ease into his argument. He attacks. "The summer soldier and the sunshine patriot" -- the phrase lands like an accusation. Notice the structure: two compound nouns, both yoked to weather. That is deliberate. Paine links loyalty to comfort. A "summer soldier" fights only when fighting is easy. A "sunshine patriot" believes only when belief costs nothing. The effect is immediate and brutal. Paine forces every reader to ask a single uncomfortable question: which one am I? No qualifications. No escape. Choose.

(103 words)

### Excerpt B: Elaborate and Layered

In what is perhaps the most memorable phrase of the entire pamphlet, Paine constructs a pair of compound epithets -- "the summer soldier" and "the sunshine patriot" -- that function as a kind of rhetorical trap, one designed to make neutrality itself feel shameful. By linking military and civic identity to seasonal metaphors (summer, sunshine), Paine implies that fair-weather commitment is not merely insufficient but fundamentally dishonest, a performance of loyalty rather than its substance. The parallelism of the two phrases reinforces this point through rhythm: the reader encounters not one accusation but two, each building on the other, so that by the sentence's end, the cumulative weight of Paine's contempt has made fence-sitting feel untenable.

(115 words)

---

### Voice Comparison

| Feature | Excerpt A (Spare) | Excerpt B (Layered) |
|---|---|---|
| Average sentence length | ~10 words | ~29 words |
| Dominant sentence type | Simple/compound; fragments for emphasis | Complex with subordination and parenthetical asides |
| Diction register | Direct, everyday verbs ("attacks," "lands," "fights") | Precise, Latinate vocabulary ("epithets," "untenable," "cumulative") |
| Hedging/qualification | Almost none -- declarative throughout | Frequent ("perhaps," "a kind of," "not merely... but") |
| Effect on reader | Urgency, certainty, confrontation | Depth, nuance, intellectual richness |

**Key point:** Both excerpts are equally valid analytical voices. Neither is "better." Both use S-F-E correctly. Both embed evidence. Both make defensible claims about how Paine's language works. The difference is not rigor -- it is personality. Your job across the rest of this course and beyond is to develop YOUR voice -- the one that feels natural to you while maintaining analytical rigor.

## Supported Practice: Write the Same Claim in Two Voices

**Task:** Write the same analytical claim in two different voices (spare/direct vs. elaborate/layered).

**The claim to express:** Chief Joseph's decision to end his surrender speech with the simple declaration "I will fight no more forever" (SP-010) creates a more powerful emotional effect than any elaborate closing could have achieved.

### Model Answer A (Spare)

Chief Joseph ends with five words: "I will fight no more forever." No ornamentation. No appeal to the audience's sympathy. Just a flat statement of surrender. That flatness IS the power. Elaborate rhetoric would have given his listeners distance. This gives them nothing to hide behind.

(44 words)

### Model Answer B (Layered)

By choosing to conclude with the starkly unadorned declaration "I will fight no more forever," Chief Joseph employs a rhetorical strategy that derives its emotional force precisely from what it refuses to do -- it refuses to elaborate, to qualify, to soften. The sentence's monosyllabic simplicity, set against the more complex syntax of the preceding lines, creates a tonal rupture that leaves the audience with no interpretive cushion, compelling them to sit with the raw finality of defeat.

(77 words)

## Independent Practice: Strengthen Your Voice

**Task:** Take one paragraph from your L18 practice essay. Revise it to strengthen your voice -- whichever voice feels more natural to you.

**Self-check questions:**
- Can a reader tell this paragraph was written by ME, not just any student?
- Are my sentence lengths varied, or do they all sound the same?
- Did I use at least one strong verb instead of a linking verb?
- Did I cut any phrases that sound like filler rather than MY thinking?
