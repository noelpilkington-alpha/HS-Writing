## Model Essay Plan (Completed)

**Prompt:** "Should schools replace traditional letter grades with a pass/fail system?"

**My Position:** Schools should keep traditional letter grades rather than switching to a pass/fail system because grades give students specific feedback, motivate improvement, and prepare them for college applications.

**Body Paragraph 1:**
- Sub-claim: Letter grades give students specific feedback about how well they understand a subject.
- Evidence (from knowledge): A student who earns a C+ in biology knows she is passing but has clear room to grow, while a student who earns an A- knows she has strong mastery with minor gaps. Under pass/fail, both students would simply receive "Pass," and neither would know where she actually stands.
- Reasoning (WHY this supports the claim): Without that level of detail, students lose the information they need to figure out what to study harder, what to maintain, and where to ask for help. Grades act like a GPS — they show not just "you arrived" but "here is exactly where you are on the route."

**Body Paragraph 2:**
- Sub-claim: The possibility of earning a higher grade motivates many students to push beyond the minimum.
- Evidence (from knowledge): In classes where effort is reflected in the grade — moving from a B to an A by revising an essay or doing extra practice problems — students often choose to put in that work. In a pass/fail system, once a student crosses the passing threshold, there is no visible reward for continuing to improve.
- Reasoning (WHY this supports the claim): This matters because motivation is not just about fear of failing; it is also about the pull of achievement. Remove the spectrum of achievement, and many students will settle for "good enough," which over four years of high school adds up to a significant loss of learning.

**Body Paragraph 3 (optional):**
- *(Not used in this model — demonstrating a strong 4-paragraph essay.)*

**Counterargument:**
- Strongest opposing position: Pass/fail grading reduces student stress and anxiety, which improves mental health and may actually help students learn better.
- Acknowledge: It is true that grade-related stress is a real problem; many students experience anxiety over GPAs, and some focus more on the grade than on actual learning.
- Concede: In certain situations — such as elective courses or exploratory classes — pass/fail grading can free students to take risks without fear.
- Respond: However, the solution to grade-related stress is not to remove all grades; it is to teach students healthier ways to respond to feedback. Eliminating letter grades removes valuable information to solve a problem that has better solutions, like emphasizing growth over perfection.

**Conclusion:**
- Restate position (different words): Keeping letter grades preserves the detailed feedback loop that helps students understand their progress, stay motivated, and build the academic record they need.
- "So what?" — why does this matter? High school is the last stage before students enter college or careers where performance is constantly measured. Learning to interpret and respond to graded feedback now prepares them for a world that will not hand them a simple "pass" or "fail."

## Model Essay

*(~420 words, written from the completed plan above)*

### [Introduction]

Imagine training for a race but never being told your time — just whether you finished or not. You would have no idea if you were getting faster, slower, or staying the same. That is essentially what a pass/fail grading system does to students: it tells them they crossed the finish line but hides everything else. Schools should keep traditional letter grades rather than switching to pass/fail because grades give students specific feedback, motivate genuine improvement, and prepare them for the demands of college admissions.

### [Body 1]

Letter grades provide students with specific feedback about how well they understand a subject. A student who earns a C+ in biology knows she is passing but has clear room to grow, while a student who earns an A- knows she has strong mastery with only minor gaps to close. Under a pass/fail system, both students would simply receive "Pass," and neither would know where she actually stands relative to the material. Without that level of detail, students lose the information they need to figure out what to study harder, where to ask for help, and what habits are working. Grades function like a GPS for learning — they do not just confirm "you arrived" but show exactly where you are on the route.

### [Body 2]

Beyond feedback, the possibility of earning a higher grade motivates many students to push past the minimum. In classes where effort is visibly reflected — moving from a B to an A by revising an essay or completing extra practice sets — students often choose to put in that additional work. In a pass/fail system, once a student crosses the passing threshold, there is no visible reward for continuing to improve. This matters because motivation is not only about avoiding failure; it is also about the pull of achievement. Remove that spectrum, and many students will settle for "good enough," which over four years of high school adds up to a significant amount of lost learning.

### [Counterargument]

Supporters of pass/fail grading argue that it reduces student stress and anxiety, which may actually help students learn better. It is true that grade-related stress is a real problem, and some students focus more on chasing a number than on genuine understanding. In certain contexts, like elective courses, pass/fail can free students to take creative risks. However, the solution to grade anxiety is not to strip away all grades; it is to teach students healthier ways to respond to feedback. Eliminating letter grades removes valuable information to fix a problem that has better remedies, such as emphasizing growth and revision over perfection.

### [Conclusion]

Keeping letter grades preserves the detailed feedback loop that helps students track progress, stay motivated, and build a meaningful academic record. High school is the last stretch before students enter college or careers where performance is constantly evaluated. Learning to interpret and respond to honest, specific feedback now prepares them for a world that will never simply tell them "pass" or "fail."

## Debatable Prompts

### DP-01 | Homework Policies
**Prompt:** Many teachers assign daily homework to reinforce classroom lessons, but some educators argue that homework creates unnecessary pressure and cuts into time students need for rest, family, and personal interests. Should teachers be allowed to assign graded homework that counts toward a student's final course grade?
**Why this works:** Students have direct, daily experience with homework, and there are strong arguments on both sides regarding learning, fairness, and student well-being.

### DP-02 | Public Surveillance Cameras
**Prompt:** Cities across the country have installed security cameras in parks, intersections, and public transit stations, arguing that the cameras deter crime and help police solve cases faster. Others say constant surveillance invades privacy and disproportionately targets certain communities. Should local governments expand the use of surveillance cameras in public spaces?
**Why this works:** Students encounter public cameras regularly and can draw on their own observations about safety and privacy without needing research data.

### DP-03 | Youth Sports Specialization
**Prompt:** Some coaches and parents encourage young athletes to focus on a single sport year-round to maximize skill development and scholarship opportunities. Others warn that early specialization leads to burnout, overuse injuries, and a narrower social experience. Should young athletes specialize in one sport before high school, or should they be encouraged to play multiple sports?
**Why this works:** Most ninth graders have personal experience with sports or know peers who specialize, making the topic accessible while genuinely dividing opinion among athletes, parents, and coaches.
