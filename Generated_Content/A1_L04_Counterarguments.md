# A1 L04: Addressing Counterarguments — Content Items

---

## The Three Moves

1. **Acknowledge** — Show you understand the other side. ("Some argue that...", "Critics point out that...", "It's understandable that some believe...")
2. **Concede** — Admit what's valid in the opposing view. ("While it's true that...", "Granted,", "There is some merit to the idea that...")
3. **Respond** — Explain why your position still stands despite the concession. ("However, this overlooks...", "Nevertheless,", "But this argument fails to account for...", "The more significant issue is...")

**Why this matters:** Ignoring the other side makes your argument WEAKER, not stronger. Acknowledging and responding to it shows confidence and earns credibility.

---

## Coping Model: Counterargument Paragraph

**Topic:** Social media platforms should disable algorithmic feeds for users under 16 (from L02).

### False Start (what NOT to do):

"Some people think algorithmic feeds are fine for teenagers, but they're wrong. Algorithms are manipulative and harmful, and anyone who disagrees doesn't understand technology."

**Annotation:** This dismisses the opposition without engaging with it. "They're wrong" is not a response — it's a refusal to respond. "Anyone who disagrees doesn't understand" is an insult, not an argument. This WEAKENS the writer's credibility.

### Revised (Three Moves):

"[ACKNOWLEDGE] Critics of algorithmic restrictions argue that feeds personalized by algorithms help teenagers discover content aligned with their interests, from educational videos to communities that share their hobbies. [CONCEDE] There is some truth to this — algorithmic curation can surface genuinely valuable content that a teenager might never find through manual searching. [RESPOND] However, the same algorithms that recommend helpful content also optimize for engagement at any cost, which means they systematically promote sensational, emotionally provocative material because it keeps users scrolling longer. The occasional useful recommendation does not outweigh a system designed to exploit adolescent psychology for profit, which is precisely why disabling these feeds for minors is a necessary safeguard."

**Annotation:** Each move is labeled. The Acknowledge shows the writer understands the opposition. The Concede admits something real — algorithmic curation CAN be useful. The Respond doesn't dismiss the concession but explains why the bigger picture still supports the original position. This is genuine intellectual engagement.

## Practice Items: Position + Counterargument Pairs

Each item gives you a position and its strongest counterargument. Write a response paragraph using all three moves: Acknowledge, Concede, Respond.

### CA-01 | School Start Times

**Your Position:** "High schools should start no earlier than 8:30 AM to align with adolescent sleep biology."

**Strongest Counterargument:** Starting later would shorten the afternoon, reducing time for after-school jobs, sports practice, and extracurricular activities that many students depend on. Some families also rely on older siblings being home early to care for younger children.

**Task:** Write a counterargument response paragraph using all three moves: Acknowledge, Concede, Respond.

**Model Answer:**
Those who oppose later start times argue that pushing the school day forward would compress the afternoon window students need for jobs, athletics, and family responsibilities such as caring for younger siblings. This is a legitimate concern — many students depend on after-school hours for income or caregiving, and dismissing those realities would be unfair. However, chronic sleep deprivation among adolescents forced into early start times leads to measurably worse academic performance, higher rates of depression, and increased car accidents among teen drivers. Scheduling inconveniences can be solved by adjusting practice times and activity hours, but the cognitive and health damage caused by fighting adolescent biology cannot be solved by willpower alone.

### CA-02 | AI Tools in School

**Your Position:** "Schools should teach students to use AI tools like ChatGPT as part of the curriculum rather than banning them."

**Strongest Counterargument:** If students rely on AI to generate ideas and draft writing, they will never develop their own critical thinking and writing skills. The purpose of school assignments is to practice thinking, not to practice prompting a machine.

**Task:** Write a counterargument response paragraph using all three moves: Acknowledge, Concede, Respond.

**Model Answer:**
Critics of integrating AI into classrooms contend that allowing students to use tools like ChatGPT will erode the very thinking skills that education is supposed to build, turning assignments into exercises in prompting rather than reasoning. This concern has real weight — if students outsource their drafting and idea generation entirely to a machine, they lose the struggle that builds intellectual muscle. Nevertheless, teaching students to use AI within structured guidelines — such as brainstorming with AI but writing independently, or drafting first and then using AI to critique their logic — actually cultivates higher-order skills like evaluating source quality, detecting weak reasoning, and distinguishing useful output from plausible nonsense. These are precisely the competencies students will need in every workplace they enter.

### CA-03 | Dress Codes

**Your Position:** "Public schools should eliminate strict dress codes because they disproportionately target female students and students of color."

**Strongest Counterargument:** Dress codes create a professional learning environment, reduce distractions, and minimize socioeconomic differences in appearance. Without them, schools risk becoming more chaotic and less equitable.

**Task:** Write a counterargument response paragraph using all three moves: Acknowledge, Concede, Respond.

**Model Answer:**
Supporters of dress codes argue that standardized clothing expectations foster professionalism, reduce social comparison based on wealth, and keep the focus on learning rather than appearance. There is genuine value in this goal — a shared learning environment benefits when students are not pressured to compete over brands or trends, and some baseline standards of dress in a communal space are reasonable. Yet research consistently shows that strict dress codes are enforced inequitably: girls and students of color receive violations at significantly higher rates than their peers for comparable clothing choices. A policy that claims to promote fairness but is applied through biased enforcement does not reduce inequity — it institutionalizes it under the cover of neutrality.

### CA-04 | Paying College Athletes

**Your Position:** "College athletes in revenue-generating sports should receive a share of the profits their labor produces."

**Strongest Counterargument:** College athletes already receive scholarships worth tens of thousands of dollars, and paying them would create inequality between athletes in popular sports and those in less profitable programs. It would also blur the line between amateur and professional athletics.

**Task:** Write a counterargument response paragraph using all three moves: Acknowledge, Concede, Respond.

**Model Answer:**
Opponents of paying college athletes point out that full athletic scholarships — often covering tuition, housing, and meals — represent substantial compensation, and that direct payment would widen the gap between athletes in marquee sports and those competing in programs that generate little revenue. These are fair points: a scholarship valued at tens of thousands of dollars is not nothing, and pay disparities across sports could breed resentment within athletic departments. But universities collectively earn billions of dollars from football and basketball broadcasting rights, merchandise, and ticket sales, while many of the athletes generating that revenue cannot afford basic living expenses beyond what their scholarships cover. The label "amateur" has become a legal fiction that protects institutional profit, not student welfare, and sharing revenue with the workers who create it is not a radical idea — it is a basic principle of fairness.
