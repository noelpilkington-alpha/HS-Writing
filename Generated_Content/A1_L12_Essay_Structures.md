# A1 L12: Essay Structures Beyond the 5-Paragraph -- Content Items

---

## Five Essay Structures Overview

| Structure | Definition | When to Use | Basic Shape |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Classical (Aristotelian)** | Thesis → evidence → counterargument → conclusion | When your audience is neutral and you need a clear, logical progression | Intro (thesis) → Body 1-3 (evidence) → Counter → Conclusion |
| **Rogerian** | Shared ground → opposing view → your view → compromise/synthesis | When your audience is hostile and you need to build trust first | Common ground → Their view (fair summary) → Your view → Synthesis |
| **Problem-Solution** | Establish the problem → propose and defend a solution | When the audience agrees a problem exists but not on what to do | Problem (scope + stakes) → Solution → Defense of solution → Call to action |
| **Comparison** | Analyze two texts, ideas, or approaches side by side | When you need to show how two things relate, contrast, or complicate each other | Point-by-point OR subject-by-subject organization |
| **Cause-Effect** | Trace causes that lead to effects, or effects back to causes | When you need to explain WHY something happened or what the CONSEQUENCES are | Cause(s) → Effect(s) OR Effect → Traced back to cause(s) |

**Key insight:** The 5-paragraph essay you learned in G8 is a simplified version of Classical structure. It works, but it's not the only option — and choosing a structure is itself a rhetorical choice (M1).

## Structure Sort Items

### SS-01
**Excerpt:** Schools across the country have adopted later start times for high school students, yet the debate over whether this policy genuinely improves academic outcomes remains unresolved. While some researchers point to higher test scores and improved attendance, the evidence is more complicated than advocates suggest. A closer look at the data reveals that later start times alone do not account for the gains — and that other variables deserve equal scrutiny.

**Structure:** Classical (Aristotelian)

**Signal:** The opening establishes a clear thesis ("later start times alone do not account for the gains") and previews a logical, evidence-driven argument. The phrase "a closer look at the data reveals" signals that the essay will march through evidence toward a conclusion, with counterargument embedded along the way.

---

### SS-02
**Excerpt:** Many parents and educators believe that assigning literary classics — works like *The Odyssey* and *Romeo and Juliet* — is essential to a well-rounded education. Their concern is understandable: these texts have shaped how we think about storytelling, morality, and human nature for centuries. At the same time, there is a strong case that curriculum space should also include contemporary authors whose perspectives reflect the lived experiences of today's students.

**Structure:** Rogerian

**Signal:** The opening leads with the opposing view and treats it with genuine respect ("their concern is understandable"). Rather than immediately countering, it validates the other side before introducing an alternative. The phrase "at the same time" signals a move toward synthesis rather than refutation.

---

### SS-03
**Excerpt:** In most American high schools, students write dozens of essays but receive substantive feedback on fewer than half of them. The rest come back with a grade and little else. This gap between effort and response leaves students repeating the same mistakes across assignments, unable to identify what they need to improve. Without a systemic change in how writing feedback is delivered, the cycle will continue.

**Structure:** Problem-Solution

**Signal:** The opening defines a concrete problem (lack of substantive feedback), establishes its scope ("most American high schools"), and names the stakes ("repeating the same mistakes"). The final sentence — "without a systemic change" — signals that a proposed solution is coming next.

---

### SS-04
**Excerpt:** Frederick Douglass's 1852 speech "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" and Abraham Lincoln's Second Inaugural Address both confront the moral failure of slavery in America, yet they do so with strikingly different rhetorical strategies. Where Douglass uses direct accusation and bitter irony to indict his audience, Lincoln adopts a tone of collective sorrow and theological reasoning. Examining these two approaches side by side reveals how audience and purpose shape an author's choices.

**Structure:** Comparison

**Signal:** The opening names two texts and immediately establishes the framework for comparing them ("strikingly different rhetorical strategies"). The "where X does this, Y does that" sentence sets up a point-by-point contrast, and the final sentence confirms the essay will analyze the two approaches in relation to each other.

---

### SS-05
**Excerpt:** When the College Board redesigned the SAT in 2016, removing the mandatory essay and shifting toward evidence-based reading, the change was intended to make the test more equitable. But the redesign set off a chain of consequences that few predicted. High schools reduced emphasis on timed analytical writing, test-prep companies pivoted to new formats, and a generation of students arrived at college with less practice constructing sustained arguments under pressure.

**Structure:** Cause-Effect

**Signal:** The opening identifies a specific cause (the SAT redesign) and then traces a chain of effects ("set off a chain of consequences"). The list of outcomes — reduced writing emphasis, industry pivots, underprepared students — signals that the essay will explore how one decision cascaded into multiple results.

## Purpose-Structure Match Items

### PP-01
**Prompt:** Your English teacher asks the class to write an essay arguing whether the author of a short story you read in class uses symbolism effectively. Your audience is your teacher, who has not taken a position and wants to see your reasoning. Your purpose is to present a clear, evidence-backed interpretation.

**Best structure:** Classical (Aristotelian)

**Why:** The audience is neutral and intellectually open — your teacher wants to evaluate the quality of your reasoning, not be persuaded away from a strongly held belief. Classical structure lets you state your thesis up front, build your case with textual evidence, address an alternative reading, and close with a strong conclusion. There is no need to build trust or find common ground because there is no hostility to navigate.

---

### PP-02
**Prompt:** Your school's parent-teacher organization is debating whether to ban phones during the school day. You are writing an op-ed for the school newsletter arguing that a modified phone policy (phones allowed during passing periods and lunch) would be more effective than a total ban. Most of the parents reading the newsletter strongly support a complete ban.

**Best structure:** Rogerian

**Why:** The audience is hostile to your position — most parents already favor a total ban and may dismiss any argument that sounds like a defense of phone use. Rogerian structure lets you open by acknowledging their concerns (distraction, screen addiction, classroom disruption) before presenting your alternative. By demonstrating that you take their worries seriously, you lower resistance and make it possible for them to consider a compromise they might otherwise reject outright.

---

### PP-03
**Prompt:** You are writing a research-based essay for a regional writing competition. The topic is: "How has social media changed the way young people engage with news?" The judges are college writing instructors who value analytical depth. You may approach the topic however you wish.

**Best structure:** Could be Cause-Effect or Comparison

**Why Cause-Effect works:** The prompt's phrasing — "how has social media *changed*" — naturally invites a causal analysis. You could trace how specific features of social media platforms (algorithmic feeds, shareability, brevity) have led to measurable shifts in how teens consume, evaluate, and share news. This structure would let you build a compelling chain of reasoning that shows analytical depth.

**Why Comparison works:** You could frame the essay around a before-and-after comparison (news engagement pre-social-media vs. now) or compare two platforms' effects on news consumption. This structure would let you develop nuanced, side-by-side analysis that highlights complexity — a quality competition judges reward.

**The choice depends on:** Whether you want to emphasize *why* the change happened (Cause-Effect) or *what the change looks like* when examined from multiple angles (Comparison).

## Model Comparison: Same Thesis, Two Structures

**Thesis:** In "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" Frederick Douglass deliberately shifts between celebration and condemnation to force his audience to confront the hypocrisy between America's founding ideals and the reality of slavery.

### Classical Plan (~110 words)

- **Introduction:** Open with context (Douglass invited to speak at a Fourth of July celebration in 1852). State the thesis: Douglass's structural shift from praise to condemnation is a deliberate rhetorical strategy to expose hypocrisy.
- **Body 1:** Analyze the opening section where Douglass praises the Founders. Show how this builds credibility and lulls the audience into comfort.
- **Body 2:** Analyze the pivot — the moment Douglass turns to bitter irony and direct accusation. Trace how the tonal shift creates shock precisely because of the praise that preceded it.
- **Counterargument:** Address the reading that Douglass's praise is sincere patriotism rather than setup. Show why the textual evidence contradicts this.
- **Conclusion:** Reassert that the structural shift is the speech's central rhetorical engine. Connect to the broader idea that structure itself carries meaning.

### Rogerian Plan (~110 words)

- **Introduction:** Acknowledge that many readers and scholars see Douglass's speech primarily as a passionate expression of outrage — a reading that captures something real about the text's emotional power.
- **Their view:** Present the interpretation that the speech's force comes from Douglass's moral anger and personal experience as an enslaved person, not from calculated structural choices.
- **Common ground:** Agree that the speech is genuinely emotional and that Douglass's lived experience gives it authenticity no purely technical analysis can explain.
- **Your view:** Argue that the deliberate structural shift — praise first, then condemnation — is what *makes* the emotion land. Without the setup, the outrage loses its rhetorical force.
- **Synthesis:** Propose that the speech works because Douglass channels genuine emotion *through* a carefully chosen structure. The two readings are not opposed — they are complementary.

### Key Difference
The Classical plan works best when writing for an audience that has no strong prior interpretation — you lead with your thesis and prove it. The Rogerian plan works best when writing for an audience that already holds a different reading of the text and might resist yours. By validating their interpretation first, you create space for them to consider your structural analysis without feeling their own reading has been dismissed.
