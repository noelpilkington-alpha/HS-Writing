# Adversarial Verification — Model B Restructure of English I Unit 1

**Date:** 2026-06-18
**Verifier stance:** Adversarial. The goal was to break Model B, not to ratify it. Sources are quoted verbatim with file + locator. What could not be verified is flagged.

**The decision under test:** shift English I Unit 1 from **Model A** (5 discrete lessons, one isolated writing move each — the team-locked C1 "one new move per lesson" structure) to **Model B** (one recurring activity = write a whole FACT paragraph every rep; the FACT fields *are* the activity; scaffold fades Heavy→Medium→Light→None; the teaching spotlight rotates to the frontier field; early reps pre-scaffold F/A/T so the budget concentrates on the C/Clarify frontier).

---

## Overall verdict: **ADOPT-WITH-MODIFICATION**

Model B is, in substance, *already the team's adopted direction* — not a fresh proposal that wins or loses on its own merits. `C1_Amendments_PostVerification.md` Amendment 1 (dated 2026-06-18, the same day) already binds the FACT mnemonic and the SRSD Activity-Specs 4-phase fade into English I Unit 1 1.1–1.4 and calls it "an INTEGRATION, not a new design" (Amend 1, lines 16, 19). So the real question is not "A or B" but "is the *whole-FACT-paragraph-every-rep* form of the integration faithful and sound, or does it overreach what the evidence and the specs actually support?"

It is sound in its core mechanism (Memorize-It, plan-to-draft, mnemonic-as-activity, gated fade are all genuinely evidenced or house-locked) but it **overstates its evidentiary warrant on two of five claims**, **diverges from the Activity Specs in one structurally important way** (the "frontier-field rotation + pre-fill F/A/T" is an *invention* not in the specs), and **sacrifices a real diagnostic capability that Model A has**. None of those is fatal; all are fixable. Hence ADOPT-WITH-MODIFICATION, not a clean ADOPT.

A caveat that cuts in Model B's favor and must be stated up front so the rest reads fairly: **Model A and Model B are NOT as opposed as the framing implies.** C1 Unit 1's five lessons each already run the *full* AlphaWrite cycle on one piece of writing (`C1_English_I_II_Lesson_Map.md` line 38: "the AI-Tutor DI sequence ... Only the prompt, stimulus, and scaffold level change rep to rep"; the Arch column shows 1.1–1.4 are all "Para" reps). C1's "isolate one move" refers to which move carries the *heaviest instruction* (the ★ Frontier column), not to writing a fragment. So C1 is closer to "whole-task with a rotating instructional spotlight" than to true part-task. That makes Model B's claimed contrast with Model A partly a strawman — see Steelman section.

## Each claimed advantage: SUPPORTED / OVERSTATED / UNSUPPORTED

### Claim 1 — "The SRSD effect size (d≈1.14) is for Model B (the studied form), NOT Model A's isolated-move form." → **UNSUPPORTED (the number) + OVERSTATED (the A-vs-B attribution)**

Two distinct problems.

**(a) The number d=1.14 is not in the SRSD research corpus at all.** I searched the full corpus. The figure traces only to the *internal MVP brainlift's* own summary line — `LearningSci_and_House_Docs_Mining.md` line 179: "cornerstone = Writing Next, SRSD d = 1.14." That is a house citation, not a finding extracted from a paper. The actual numbers the corpus reports verbatim are different:
- "writing strategy instruction has a strong positive effect on writing quality (Effect Size = 0.82, confirmed verbatim in the Writing Next report)" — `SRSD Integration Brainlift.md` line 50. (0.82 is the *Writing Next strategy-instruction* category figure, which is broader than SRSD.)
- "Graham et al. (2013) reported a weighted effect size of 1.75 based on 28 studies" — `SRSD_Mining.md` line 162.
- Adolescents w/ LD: essay-quality effect sizes "1.26 ... to ... 1.35" (line 166); "the average effect size was 1.21" (line 168).

So 1.14 sits *between* plausible SRSD quality effects but is not sourced to any paper in what we hold. **Flag: the specific "1.14" should be re-sourced or replaced with a corpus-backed figure (0.82 Writing Next strategy category, or 1.75 Graham 2013 weighted).** Do not present 1.14 as a verified SRSD effect size.

**(b) Whatever the number, it does NOT distinguish Model B from Model A.** Every effect size in the corpus is **SRSD-vs-control** (vs. no-strategy or business-as-usual instruction), not whole-mnemonic-vs-isolated-component. Examples: "essay writing performance was significantly higher for the SRSD group than the control group (... d = 0.826)" (`SRSD_Mining.md` line 175); the ACT study is SRSD-with-HIT-SONGS3 vs. ACT-math-prep controls (`SRSD Integration Brainlift.md` line 69). None of these compares "teach the whole FACT paragraph each rep" against "isolate one move per lesson." **The effect sizes say SRSD works; they are silent on A-vs-B.** Using d≈1.14 to argue *for B over A* is a non-sequitur.

**The ONE study in the corpus that actually does component comparison cuts AGAINST the strong version of the claim.** Geres-Smith et al. (2019) is "A Preliminary Component Analysis of SRSD" — and its finding is *narrow*: "these results suggest that explicit instruction on the use of self-statements may not be an essential component" (`SRSD_Mining.md` line 152). It dismantles the *self-statements* component, not the structure question. The corpus's own gloss: "The strategy + memorization + graphing components are the load-bearing core" (line 152). That supports *having a memorized strategy* (good for B) but says nothing about whole-paragraph-vs-isolated-move staging.

**Verdict on Claim 1: UNSUPPORTED as stated.** The honest claim is: "SRSD as a whole strategy (memorized mnemonic + plan-to-draft + faded support) is large-effect vs. no-strategy instruction; this favors *adding the FACT mnemonic and Memorize-It*, which Model A as locked does not install (`C1_Amendments` Amend 1, line 17: 'no lesson installs a strategy to retrieval criterion'). It does not, by itself, establish whole-FACT-every-rep over a rotating-spotlight structure."

### Claim 2 — "Model B delivers Memorize-It / mnemonic-to-automaticity (the #1 architecture gap) that Model A doesn't." → **SUPPORTED (gap is real) but the credit is MISATTRIBUTED**

The gap is real and independently confirmed. `C1_Amendments` Amend 1 (line 17): C1 "gates on a cold, timed, no-reference task ... but only fades the *visible* scaffold — it never installs the *internal* one ... no lesson installs a strategy to retrieval criterion." The research warrant is strong and exam-relevant: "You can't use it if you can't remember it!" (`SRSD_Mining.md` line 110); "they cannot bring physical reminders ... with them when taking the ACT test" (line 113); "The goal is for students to identify the steps of the strategy and use them automatically" (line 111).

**But two things break the clean attribution to Model B:**

1. **Memorize-It is a property of *adding a carried mnemonic + a retrieval-criterion gate*, not of *whole-paragraph staging*.** You could bolt Memorize-It onto Model A unchanged: each of 1.1–1.4 opens with a FACT retrieval quiz, the cue card fades across lessons, and a recall-to-criterion checkpoint sits before the gate. The `SRSD in Practice` digital mapping makes Stage 4 a discrete "mastery gate (retrieval quiz): student must pass before unlocking Stage 5" (line 185) — that is independent of whether reps are whole or partial. **So Model A *as currently locked* lacks Memorize-It, but Model A *is not architecturally incompatible with it*.** Model B's advantage here is "B includes Memorize-It and the locked A doesn't," which is true but is a comparison against an un-amended A, not against A+Memorize-It.

2. **Filling labeled fields ≠ memorizing — the corpus is explicit that this is a failure mode.** "memorizing a strategy goes well beyond parroting the steps of the strategy. Students need to know and understand what is involved with each step" (`SRSD_Mining.md` line 111). And the documented fade-failure: students "merely wrote the mnemonic at the top of their paper, using it as a prompt to remember the parts of a story" (line 201). A student who fills C/A/S/E fields every rep with the labels visible is doing exactly the "mnemonic at the top of the page" crutch the literature warns about. **Filling fields builds the *procedural motor pattern* (which `SRSD Integration Brainlift` Insight 4, line 602, openly concedes: "procedural memory ... rather than declarative memory") — but procedural field-filling with the scaffold present is not the same as recall-to-criterion with the scaffold absent.** Memorize-It is delivered by the *fade-to-no-fields gate*, not by the field-filling. That gate is in both the Activity Specs ("planning optional → mastery gate (no fields)") and is exactly what C1 already gates on. So Model B installs Memorize-It *only if the fade actually reaches "no fields, recall the meaning"* — and that part is shared with an amended Model A.

**Verdict: SUPPORTED that the gap is real and that B closes it; OVERSTATED that B *uniquely* closes it or that field-filling = memorizing.** Scrutiny target #6 confirmed: field-filling is not memorization; the memorization comes from the criterion-gated fade, which both models can carry.

### Claim 3 — "Model B gives plan-to-draft integration (write a whole piece from a plan) that Model A's isolated moves don't." → **OVERSTATED (rests on a strawman of A)**

Plan-to-draft is genuinely the highest-confidence design principle in the SRSD docs: "Planning Must Be Connected to Production — They Are Not Separate Skills" (`SRSD Integration Brainlift` Truth 2, line 155); "a student who has done 20 plan-to-draft activities will plan on a test. A student who has done 20 planning activities and 20 separate writing activities will not" (Insight 2, line 582). If Model A were "plan in lesson X, draft in lesson Y," B would clearly win.

**But Model A as locked is NOT plan/draft-separated.** `C1_English_I_II_Lesson_Map.md` is explicit: "**every lesson is one piece of writing run through the AlphaWrite cycle (Setup → Outline → Draft → Revise → Polish → Submit)**" (line 5–6, restated lines 38, 71). Unit 1's 1.1, 1.3 are "Para" reps — write the paragraph — and 1.5 is a full essay. The Outline-then-Draft is *inside each lesson*. So C1 already does plan-to-draft within the rep; it does not have the separated-skills disease the SRSD docs diagnose (that disease is the *current AlphaWrite* G3-5 build, per Truth 2, not the C1 HS map).

Where Model A *is* weaker: its plan-to-draft uses a generic Outline form (controlling idea + evidence slots + explain sub-prompts, per `LearningSci_Mining` line 79), not a *named carried mnemonic* the student internalizes. That is a real but *smaller* gap than "no plan-to-draft" — and again it is closed by adding FACT (Amendment 1), not by whole-paragraph staging per se.

**Verdict: OVERSTATED.** The principle is sound and B honors it, but B does not deliver plan-to-draft "that Model A doesn't" — A already has plan-to-draft inside each rep. B's true delta is "plan-to-draft *anchored on a memorized mnemonic*," which is the FACT addition, available to either model.

### Claim 4 — "Make the mnemonic the activity (instruction in the fields) beats tutorial-based instruction for struggling students who skip tutorials." → **SUPPORTED as a design principle, but it is a HOUSE SPOV, not a cited study**

Distinguishing house-assertion from research is exactly scrutiny target #3, and here is the honest line.

**It is a house SPOV.** `SRSD Integration Brainlift` Truth 4 ("The Mnemonic Structure Is the Instruction — Not a Tutorial Students Can Skip," line 163) and Insight 4 ("The Students Who Skip Tutorials Are the Students Who Most Need the Strategy," line 598) carry **no citation** for the core "make-the-mnemonic-the-activity" mechanism. Insight 4 even concedes it is a fidelity compromise: "This is less elegant than full SRSD fidelity, but it reaches the population that fidelity misses" (line 602). That is an admission it is a *design adaptation*, not a research finding.

**What IS research-backed nearby (and which the claim can legitimately lean on):**
- *Discovery/minimal-guidance fails novices* — Kirschner, Sweller & Clark (2006): "minimally guided instruction is less effective and less efficient" (`LearningSci_Mining` line 55). This supports "don't make struggling students *discover* the strategy" — but a *tutorial* is explicit guidance, not minimal guidance, so this cuts only weakly toward "skip the tutorial."
- *Generative/forced engagement beats passive consumption* — Fiorella & Mayer: "Students don't carry out the activities by themselves. Left to their own devices ... they'll revert to rereading ... they must be told to do the activities" (`LearningSci_Mining` line 81). This is the strongest real warrant: a *required* field-filling act is a generative act; passive tutorial-reading is not. So "the structure forces engagement" has genuine support.
- *Worked-example / completion-task ladder* — Atkinson/Rosenshine: "Start with worked-out examples ... Then move to completion assignments ... remove — one by one — the presented steps" (`LearningSci_Mining` line 22, 77). **This actually argues for a SCAFFOLDED PROGRESSION INTO the fields, not for jumping straight to "fill all four fields cold," and it argues that worked examples / the tutorial content matter for the bridge.** It is double-edged for Model B.

**The genuine adversarial catch:** "make the mnemonic the activity" was written for the **G3-8 AlphaWrite context where students skip tutorials and lack paragraph fluency** (`SRSD Integration Brainlift` Out-of-Scope line 24-29 and population line 76). C1 Unit 1 is **G9-10, entry = "a typical on-grade G8 writer ... can write expository paragraphs/essays and basic argument from knowledge"** (`C1_Lesson_Map` line 18-21). For a more capable, more expertise-bearing learner, the **expertise-reversal effect** (`LearningSci_Mining` line 26-27: "A teaching approach that works well with an expert will most probably not work well with a beginner and can even be detrimental") warns that heavy field-scaffolding that helps a struggling G4 can *bore or hinder* a fluent G9. So the SPOV transfers to HS only with the "recall-or-learn refresh, not slow first-teach" delta that Amendment 1 already specifies (Amend 1, line 30). **Without that delta, Claim 4 is weaker at G9-10 than the brainlift's G3-8 framing implies.**

**Verdict: SUPPORTED as a defensible design principle (and consistent with Fiorella & Mayer + Kirschner), but it is a HOUSE SPOV without a dedicated study, and it was authored for a younger/weaker population — its strength is reduced for the G9-10 target unless delivered as a brisk refresh.**

### Claim 5 — "It dissolves a forward-reference problem (no 'teach FACT then reference it') and removes passive read-it coping models." → **PARTIALLY SUPPORTED / OVERSTATED on the coping-model half**

**Forward-reference half: SUPPORTED and minor.** Putting the mnemonic's structure into the activity from rep 1 does avoid "lesson 1 teaches the word, lessons 2-4 reference it." Real, low-stakes benefit.

**"Removes passive read-it coping models" half: this is where Model B risks *destroying value*, not adding it.** The coping model is not decorative — it is doctrinally load-bearing in *every* governing doc:
- C1 itself locks it: "Model-with-coping-false-start" is part of the fixed wrapper (`C1_Lesson_Map` line 38, 71); the Generate column repeatedly specifies "coping model" (rows 1.1, 1.3).
- The SRSD digital doc treats it as a *design requirement*, not a tutorial students skip: "Coping models are especially effective for students with low self-efficacy" and asynchronous platforms "should include both" expert and coping models (`SRSD in Practice` line 171); Tension 3 explicitly warns that *passive video viewing reduces the collaborative element* and prescribes pairing the model with **interactive decision-point tasks** (line 246) — i.e. the fix for passivity is interactivity *within the model*, NOT deleting the model.
- García-Sánchez & Fidalgo (2006) finding, per the brainlift source table: "Coping modeling builds self-efficacy; mastery modeling alone does not" (`SRSD Integration Brainlift` line 543).

So "passive read-it" is a real anti-pattern, but the evidenced remedy is **make the coping model interactive** (`SRSD in Practice` Tension 3) — not **remove it**. If Model B is read as "the fields replace the coping model," that **loses the self-efficacy mechanism** the corpus says mastery-modeling alone cannot supply. **This is a concrete way Model B could be WRONG: a student filling fields never sees the expert hesitate, false-start, and recover, which is the documented engine of writing self-efficacy for low-confidence writers.**

**Verdict: forward-reference dissolution SUPPORTED (minor); "remove passive coping models" OVERSTATED and potentially harmful — the evidence says make the coping model interactive, keep it.** Model B must retain a coping model (interactive, not passive) at least on the frontier-field teach.

## Steelman for Model A — what it does better that B sacrifices

Model A deserves a real defense; here is the strongest one, grounded in Engelmann.

1. **Faultless single-dimension focus + clean diagnosis of WHICH move failed.** Engelmann's core construct is that a faultless communication "convey[s] only one interpretation" and that the diagnostic logic depends on isolating one varying feature: "To show differences ... juxtapose examples that are minimally different and treat them differently" (`Engelmann_DI_Mining` line 51, p.65). When a lesson isolates the instructional spotlight on ONE move (1.1 reasoning, 1.3 cite+explain, 1.4 controlling idea), a failure is *attributable* — the grader knows the student can't do the reasoning link specifically. **In a whole-FACT-every-rep design, a weak paragraph is a blend; you cannot cleanly tell whether F, A, C, or T failed** unless the verify-it step is engineered to isolate each — and even then the production is confounded. This matters because the platform has *no teacher* to disambiguate; the corpus stresses "diagnose the precise gap and prescribe the exact next action" (`LearningSci_Mining` line 94, Bloom). **Model A's per-move isolation is a diagnosis affordance Model B blurs.** (Model B's mitigation — the targeted verification questions and the frontier-spotlight — partly recovers this, but only for the spotlighted field.)

2. **Cognitive-load fit for the genuinely weak entrant.** "Always offer small amounts of information, then help students practice it and only go to the next step if the previous one is mastered" (`LearningSci_Mining` line 15, Sweller/Rosenshine). "Isolate ONE sub-skill per lesson screen ... gate progression" (line 16, design implication; and the explicit note "Unit 1's one-KC-per-lesson ... already enact this," line 18). The lowest-prepared G9 entrant (the one Model B's pre-scaffold is supposed to protect) is precisely the one for whom holding four fields + reading a passage + drafting at once may exceed the 4-7 element working-memory limit (line 15). **Engelmann pre-teaches components before the routine: "the component discriminations involved in a cognitive routine should be pretaught before the routine is introduced" (`Engelmann_DI_Mining` line 162, p.339).** That is a direct DI argument *for* teaching the moves before assembling the whole — i.e. *for* Model A's structure.

3. **Engelmann supports building the routine only when one rule holds for all examples — and warns routines are *less rigorous* than component discriminations.** "Designing cognitive routines is difficult ... these general guidelines ... do not lead to the same degree of rigor that is possible with the component discriminations" (`Engelmann_DI_Mining` line 202, p.340). FACT-the-whole-paragraph is the *routine*; reasoning/cite-explain/controlling-idea are the *component discriminations*. Engelmann's rigor lives at the component level. **Model A keeps the rigorous, decidable component discriminations front-and-center; Model B subsumes them into the less-rigorous routine earlier.**

**What B legitimately answers back (so the steelman is fair):** Engelmann *also* says even generative/routine skills should be "demonstrated then released" with a fading routine (`Engelmann_DI_Mining` line 206, p.334), and discrimination-before-production is preserved inside the Activity-Specs Step-1 "See it" beat. So B is not anti-Engelmann — but it concedes the per-move diagnosis and the strict small-step load management that A maximizes.

**Net of steelman:** Model A's real, sacrificed advantages are **(i) clean per-move failure diagnosis for a no-teacher grader, and (ii) tightest cognitive-load management / component-before-routine for the weakest entrant.** Model B must actively engineer these back (frontier-spotlight + isolated verify-it questions + genuine F/A/T pre-scaffold) or it loses them.

## Part-task vs whole-task for novices: what the evidence actually says

The literature does NOT give a clean win to either side; it gives a *conditional* answer, and the condition is element interactivity + learner expertise.

- **Whole-task / plan-to-draft integration is favored** by the SRSD house docs (Truth 2, Insight 2) and by transfer logic — but those arguments are about *not separating planning from drafting into different activities*, which **Model A already avoids** (it drafts within each rep). They are not arguments for "all four fields at full load from rep 1."
- **Part-task / component-first is favored for novices facing high element-interactivity** by cognitive-load theory: small steps, master before advancing (`LearningSci_Mining` line 15), pre-teach components before the routine (Engelmann line 162), and the worked-example → completion-task → independent ladder where steps are removed "one by one" (line 22, 77). A four-field paragraph from a passage is *high* element-interactivity for a struggling G9.
- **The deciding variable is expertise (expertise-reversal):** scaffolding/structure that helps a novice "can even be detrimental" to a more expert learner (`LearningSci_Mining` line 27). C1's entrant is *not* a true novice at paragraph writing — "can write expository paragraphs/essays and basic argument from knowledge" (`C1_Lesson_Map` line 18). **For a student who already has paragraph fluency, the whole-task FACT paragraph is closer to the right grain size, and heavy part-task isolation could be the detrimental over-scaffolding.** This is the single best evidence-based argument *for* Model B at this specific grade — and it depends entirely on the entrant actually having paragraph fluency. For the sub-population that does NOT (the weak-tail G9), the part-task argument reasserts.

**So the honest synthesis:** *the right design is whole-task framing with part-task instructional emphasis* — which is what C1's "★ Frontier" column already is, and what a faithful Model B (frontier-spotlight + real F/A/T pre-scaffold) also is. **The two models converge here.** The evidence does not support a *pure* whole-task (everything at full load) nor a *pure* part-task (write fragments only) design. It supports: whole piece every rep, one field carrying the load, the rest genuinely off-loaded for the novice and progressively loaded as expertise grows. Either model is acceptable *if it implements that*; Model A as locked implements it via the spotlight column, and Model B implements it via pre-scaffold + rotation. The danger is a Model B that pre-scaffolds in name only and dumps four fields on the weak entrant.

## Is the frontier-pre-scaffold reconciliation sound or hand-wavy?

**Sound in principle, but it is an INVENTION relative to the Activity Specs, and it is hand-wavy on the one detail that determines whether it works.** Three findings:

1. **The "frontier-field rotation + pre-fill/heavily-prompt F/A/T" is NOT in the SRSD Activity Specs.** I checked Activity 5/6 (the FACT/CASE whole-paragraph activity) carefully. The specs' fade is **field-by-field uniform**: "Phase 1: Full CASE (all fields labeled with descriptions) → Phase 2: Labels only → Phase 3: Planning optional → Phase 4: Mastery gate" (`SRSD Activity Specs` lines 365-384). **There is no per-field rotation and no "pre-fill the non-frontier fields" mechanism anywhere in Activity 5/6.** The specs fade *all four letters together*, not "C stays heavy while F/A/T pre-fill." So Model B's frontier-pre-scaffold is a *new variant the team is inventing*, layering C1's "★ Frontier" idea onto the FACT activity. **Flag: this is a divergence from the spec, and should be labeled as such, not presented as "faithful to SRSD Activity Specs."** (It is, separately, faithful to C1's own Frontier column — so it is internally consistent with the locked C1 map, just not with the SRSD specs it cites.)

2. **It is well-motivated by real evidence.** Concentrating budget on one field while off-loading the rest is precisely the worked-example/completion-task logic ("give claim+evidence → student writes reasoning," `LearningSci_Mining` line 207, listed as a *currently-missing* research-backed idea) and the cognitive-load isolate-one-element principle. So the *idea* is sound and arguably an improvement on the uniform-fade spec.

3. **It is hand-wavy on the make-or-break detail: what "pre-scaffold F/A/T" concretely means, and whether pre-filling them defeats the plan-to-draft mechanism.** If F/A/T are *pre-filled* (given to the student), then the student is no longer planning F/A/T — and the corpus's plan-to-draft value comes from the student experiencing *their own* plan improving *their own* draft (`SRSD Integration Brainlift` Insight 2, line 576: "The plan to be visible during drafting, so the student experiences using it"). Hand a student three of four fields and the "I planned this and it worked" felt experience is diluted to one field. There is a real tension between "off-load F/A/T to protect the C budget" (cognitive load) and "the student must own the whole plan to value planning" (plan-to-draft). **The spec resolves nothing here because the spec doesn't do per-field pre-fill at all.** The reconciliation is therefore *plausible but unspecified* — it needs a concrete rule (e.g. F/A/T are *heavily prompted with sentence frames*, NOT pre-filled with content; the student still produces every field, but C is the only field without a frame early). That distinction — prompt vs. pre-fill — is exactly what determines whether the reconciliation preserves Model A's load benefit without killing B's plan-to-draft benefit, and it is currently glossed.

**Verdict: the reconciliation is sound as a direction, an invention relative to the cited specs (must be relabeled), and hand-wavy on the prompt-vs-prefill mechanic that decides whether it works. Required modification below.**

## Net recommendation + required modifications

**ADOPT-WITH-MODIFICATION.** Adopt the whole-FACT-paragraph-every-rep form, because (a) it is already the team's Amendment-1 direction, (b) the FACT-mnemonic + Memorize-It + plan-to-draft additions it carries are genuinely evidenced and close the confirmed #1 gap, and (c) for the *fluent* G9 entrant the whole-task grain size is defensible on expertise-reversal grounds. But the marketing of B over A is overstated and several real Model-A strengths must be engineered back. Required modifications:

1. **Drop / re-source the "d=1.14 for Model B" claim.** It is not in the corpus and it does not distinguish A from B regardless. Replace with the honest framing: "whole-strategy SRSD (memorized mnemonic + plan-to-draft + gated fade) shows large effects vs. no-strategy instruction (Writing Next strategy category 0.82; Graham 2013 weighted 1.75); the corpus does not compare whole-mnemonic vs. isolated-component staging."

2. **Stop claiming field-filling = memorizing.** Memorize-It is delivered by the *criterion-gated fade to no-fields-recall-the-meaning*, not by filling labeled fields. Keep an explicit retrieval-to-criterion checkpoint (meaning, not just letters — `SRSD_Mining` line 111) before the timed gate, exactly as `C1_Amendments` Amend 1 (line 27, 35) and `SRSD in Practice` Stage-4 mastery gate (line 185) specify. This is shared with an amended Model A; it is not a B-only win.

3. **Keep an interactive coping model on the frontier-field teach. Do NOT delete coping models.** The evidence remedy for "passive read-it" is interactivity (`SRSD in Practice` Tension 3, line 246), not removal; coping modeling is the documented self-efficacy engine (line 171; García-Sánchez line 543) and is locked in C1 (line 38). "Make the mnemonic the activity" can co-exist with an interactive coping model; it must not replace it.

4. **Specify the pre-scaffold as PROMPT, not PRE-FILL.** Early reps give F/A/T sentence frames / guiding sub-prompts while leaving C (Clarify / explain-how) frame-light — the student still produces all four fields (preserving plan-to-draft ownership) but only C demands generation from scratch. Do not hand the student finished F/A/T content. Document this; it is the make-or-break detail the current reconciliation glosses.

5. **Relabel the frontier-rotation + pre-scaffold as a C1-Frontier-derived variant, NOT as "faithful to the SRSD Activity Specs."** The specs fade all four letters uniformly (Activity 5/6, lines 365-384); per-field rotation/pre-scaffold is the team's own layer. Faithful to C1's ★ Frontier column — fine — but cite it honestly.

6. **Preserve Model A's per-move diagnosis.** Because a whole-paragraph rep blurs which field failed, the Verify-It step must isolate each field's quality with targeted questions (the Activity-Specs "targeted verification questions" mechanism, `SRSD Integration Brainlift` line 513-522) so the no-teacher grader can still prescribe the exact next move (`LearningSci_Mining` line 94). Without this, B sacrifices A's cleanest affordance.

7. **Honor the HS "recall-or-learn refresh, not slow first-teach" delta** (`C1_Amendments` Amend 1, line 30) and watch expertise-reversal: do not heavy-scaffold a fluent G9 the way the G3-8 specs heavy-scaffold a developing writer.

**Bottom line for the decision-maker:** The shift is acceptable and largely already-decided, but Model B is not the slam-dunk the five claims paint. Two of the five claims (1 and 3) are overstated, one (5) contains a genuinely *harmful* sub-claim (delete coping models), the headline effect size is unsourced, the signature "frontier-pre-scaffold" feature is an un-spec'd invention, and Model A retains a real diagnostic edge. Adopt the whole-FACT form with the seven modifications above — most of which simply re-import what Model A and the SRSD corpus already require.

## What I could not verify
