# Verify: C1 English I/II Architecture vs. Real HS Writing Curricula

**Audited file:** `c:/Users/noelp/HS Writing/ap-frq-mastery-v2/C1_English_I_II_Lesson_Map.md`
(read in full — ~40 lessons, 8 units, English I G9 + English II G10, source-bound, STAAR EI/EII + ACT + AP on-ramp).
**Auditor stance:** adversarial. **Date:** 2026-06-18.

---

## Overall verdict: **ALIGNED-WITH-DIVERGENCES**

C1's acquisition order (analytical/expository response-to-text → argument → synthesis → multi-perspective → timed), its source-bound-from-the-start design, and its STAAR-informational-primary mode bet are all **consistent with established G9-10 curricula and with the actual STAAR evidence**. The load-bearing STAAR claim **verified cleanly against TEA primary sources**. The real divergences from credible curricula are about **scope and pacing model**, not sequence: C1 omits several genuine CCSS 9-10 writing requirements (narrative, multi-day research/inquiry, extended-process writing volume), and its "one full piece per lesson, one new move, fade-then-cold-gate" pacing is a deliberately leaner, more exam-targeted machine than any of the comparison curricula. Those omissions are largely **defensible for a STAAR/ACT/AP-writing MVP**, but two of them (narrative; extended research) are real coverage gaps against the standards C1 cites, and should be named as scoped-out rather than left implicit.

What I could and couldn't verify is itemized below. The single most important claim — STAAR mode — is **VERIFIED** from TEA's own released-test answer keys, blueprints, and scoring documents.

## How real curricula sequence HS writing (per source, verbatim/cited)

### STAAR English I / II (TEA) — the assessment C1 targets — PRIMARY SOURCES READ
Agent read TEA blueprints, the assessed-curriculum TEKS PDF, the official "Scoring Process for STAAR Constructed Responses" doc, and the 2023/2024/2025 released answer keys + rationales for both EI and EII.

- The writing test is an **Extended Constructed Response (ECR)** that is **"a written response to a single or paired reading passage"** (STAAR English I blueprint). It is **source-bound by definition.**
- Eligible composition TEKS: **(5)(B)** analytical response, **(10)(B) informational**, **(10)(C) argumentative**, **(10)(D)** correspondence.
- **Every released ECR 2023-2025, both courses, was scored against 5.B (analytical) or 10.B (informational) — none against 10.C (argumentative).** Released ECR→TEKS map the agent traced from the official answer keys:

  | Form | ECR TEKS | Mode |
  |---|---|---|
  | 2023 EI | 10.B | Informational |
  | 2023 EII | 5.B | Analytical response |
  | 2024 EI | 5.B | Analytical response |
  | 2024 EII | 10.B | Informational |
  | 2025 EI | 10.B | Informational |
  | 2025 EII | 10.B | Informational |

- ECR rubric verbatim ("Scoring Process for STAAR Constructed Responses," TEA, Dec 2023): scored on an **"item-specific 5-point rubric ... two traits — development and organization of ideas (up to 3 points) and language conventions (up to 2 points)."** Nuance: the 5-point rubric is **scaled ×2 to 10 points** on the test.
- SCRs verbatim: **"The SCRs for the writing section ... are scored using a generic 0- or 1-point rubric. The SCRs for the reading section use an item-specific 0- to 2-point rubric."**

### CommonLit 360 (G9-10) — HTML blog/scope pages READ; scope PDF undecodable
Both grades **open with text-based literary/analytical response, push argument into the back half, and the year culminates in argument.** Synthesis appears *immediately* (G9 Unit 1 is a cross-textual literary-analysis essay synthesizing fiction + nonfiction). **Source-bound from day one** — no from-knowledge on-ramp.

- G9 arc (verbatim unit titles): U1 "Following the Crowd" (cross-textual literary analysis) → U2 *Animal Farm* (expository + a narrative arc) → U3 "Speak Up, Speak Out" (speech/argument) → U4 "Journalism and Society" (research/informational) → U5 *Antigone* (research-based argument) → U6 "Graffiti — Art or Argument?" (argument).
- G10 arc: U1 "Coming of Age" (literary analysis) → U2 *Fahrenheit 451* (expository) → U3 *A Raisin in the Sun* (argumentative letter) → U4 "Voting Rights" (informational research) → U5 *Things Fall Apart* (literary analysis) → U6 "Schools, Free Speech, and Social Media" (argument w/ counterclaims).
- Carries: **narrative** (G9 U2), **research-into-presentation/speech**, **dedicated vocabulary every unit**, **grammar lessons**, **full novels/dramas**.

### Odell Education G9 ("Developing Core Literacy Proficiencies") — toolbox page READ; unit PDFs undecodable
Published G9 arc, verbatim titles, in order: **(1) Reading Closely for Textual Details → (2) Making Evidence-Based Claims → (3) Researching to Deepen Understanding → (4) Building Evidence-Based Arguments**, plus a literary-technique EBC extension. **Comprehension/close reading is taught first; argument is the terminal unit.** Entirely **source-bound**; no free-form composition phase. ("Developing Evidence-Based Arguments" / DEBA is the historical name for the argument strand.)

### ACT Writing — act.org pages + official rubric PDF READ (PRIMARY)
- **4-domain analytic rubric**, domains: **Ideas and Analysis; Development and Support; Organization; Language Use (and Conventions)**. (Nuance: the official rubric table heads the 4th column "Language Use"; ACT's score-reporting pages call it "Language Use and Conventions." First three names match C1 exactly.)
- Scoring verbatim: **"Two trained readers will score your essay on a scale of 1-6 in each of the four writing domains"**; each domain summed to **2-12**; reported subject score is **"the rounded average of the four domain scores"** on a **2-12** range.
- Task verbatim: writer must **"clearly state your own perspective on the issue and analyze the relationship between your perspective and at least one other perspective,"** which **"may be in full agreement with any of those given, in partial agreement, or completely different."** The **three given perspectives** structure is confirmed (Wikipedia tertiary + the official "multiple perspectives" rubric language; the three labeled blocks weren't retrievable verbatim from act.org HTML).
- **Not source-bound** — write from general/constructed knowledge, no reading passage.

### CCSS Writing W.9-10 + Achieve the Core — structure confirmed; verbatim sub-text NOT retrievable this session
- corestandards.org returned **403** on every path; the official ELA PDF downloaded but **could not be decoded** (no `pdftotext`/poppler in this env; Bash denied; the Read tool's PDF renderer also requires poppler). State-DOE and education-mirror reprints were **denied at the permission layer or 403/404**. **I did not obtain verbatim W.9-10 sub-point text and will not reproduce it from memory.**

- **Order is NOT prescribed.** Verbatim (Wikipedia CCSSI): the standards **"do not dictate any particular pedagogy or what order topics should be taught."** The .1/.2/.3 numbering is a list, not a sequence. Achieve the Core prescribes a **balance/distribution across the three text types** and increasing sophistication *within* type across grades — **no genre order.**
- **Source-bound spine is real:** W.9-10.9 ("draw evidence from literary or informational texts") + the "Evidence" shift make text-based analytical writing central. achievethecore (verbatim, text-dependent-questions resource): questions that **"encourage students to use evidence from texts to present careful analyses and well-defended claims."**

## C1 vs real curricula: each divergence = IMPROVEMENT / NEUTRAL / MISTAKE

**1. Mode order — analytical/expository response-to-text BEFORE argument → IMPROVEMENT (and standard).**
C1 leads English I with expository response-to-text (U1-U2) then argument (U3). This is **exactly what CommonLit 360 (both grades open with literary/analytical, argument culminates) and Odell (close-reading → claims → research → argument-last) do**, and it is consistent with the STAAR reality that the released ECR is analytical/informational. CCSS does not mandate any order, so C1 is free here — and it chose the same order two credible curricula chose. Not idiosyncratic; well-supported.

**2. Source-bound from the start → NEUTRAL→IMPROVEMENT (for this target), with one honest caveat.**
C1 goes source-bound almost immediately (passage pre-read at 1.2). **CommonLit and Odell are both source-bound from day one with no from-knowledge phase**, and STAAR/ACT… wait — STAAR is source-bound, ACT is *not*. So C1's source-bound entry matches the established curricula and the STAAR target. The caveat: C1's own design rationale leans on the entry student being able to "write basic argument *from knowledge*" and bridges from there — but the very first passage rep (1.2) is already source-bound, so the "bridge from from-knowledge" framing is thinner than it reads. Real curricula don't bother with a from-knowledge on-ramp at all, so C1 going source-bound fast is the right call; just don't oversell the bridge. (Note: C1's own MEMORY notes the *A1 v2* design used argument-from-knowledge first; C1 here correctly does **not**, which is the better-aligned choice.)

**3. Reasoning-before-thesis (1.1 reasoning → 1.4 controlling idea) → NEUTRAL (defensible, not a known-curriculum pattern).**
No comparison curriculum sequences "explain-the-evidence" before "form-the-thesis" as an explicit ordering; Odell does the inverse-ish (claims before argument). C1's rationale (the explained-reasoning link is the STAAR Trait-1 lever from 1→3, so train it first and treat thesis as refinement) is internally coherent and matches STAAR's actual scoring emphasis. Not a mistake, but it's a C1-specific instructional bet, not borrowed from an established sequence — fine to keep, label as a design choice.

**4. Synthesis deferred to English II (U5) → NEUTRAL, lean toward IMPROVEMENT for STAAR.**
CommonLit puts cross-textual synthesis in **G9 Unit 1**; Odell has a mid-year G9 research/synthesis unit. C1 defers synthesis entirely to G10. Against CommonLit this looks *late*. But STAAR EI's ECR is single-or-paired passage and the top-band synthesis demand is heavier at EII, and C1 explicitly aligns synthesis to "STAAR EII top-band." Deferring the hardest orchestration to year 2 on an automatic year-1 spine is pedagogically sound. Acceptable; the only risk is G9 students never touching paired sources before the EI gate (EI gate is single-passage, so this is consistent).

**5. "One full piece per lesson, one new move, scaffold fade, cold gate" pacing → NEUTRAL with a real watch-item.**
This is C1's biggest *model* divergence. Established curricula (CommonLit, Odell, workshop models) teach **one move across multiple lessons** and build **one full essay over multiple class periods (2-6 weeks)**, with distributed practice and spiraling. C1 compresses "introduce one move + take a full piece through all 6 stages" into ~one lesson (~1.8 weeks of pace if 20/yr over 36 wks). Two consequences: (a) **finished-piece throughput is actually HIGHER than a conventional course** (~20 pieces/yr vs. the typical 4-8 formal essays) — so this is not "too thin" on volume; if anything it's ambitious; (b) **each move gets ~one exposure + one application**, with little of the repeated practice real curricula rely on. C1 partially mitigates with scaffold fade across a thread (Heavy→Med→Light→None) and reused spine, so a move does recur implicitly. Verdict: defensible *because* it's an adaptive AI-tutor cycle (not a 50-min class period) and the fade gives distributed practice within a thread — but flag **per-move repetition depth** as the thing to validate in C3 authoring. Not too granular and not too thin; just a different (leaner, exam-targeted) machine than a school curriculum.

**6. ACT U7 vs. the real ACT task → IMPROVEMENT (matches the real design closely).**
C1 U7: decompose 3 perspectives (7.1), stake your position relative to ≥1 given perspective (7.2), qualify + integrated reasoning (7.3), constructed support (no passage), timed, scored on the **ACT 4-domain rubric**. This maps **precisely** to the verified ACT task ("state your own perspective ... analyze the relationship between your perspective and at least one other perspective," three given perspectives, write from general knowledge) and the verified 4-domain rubric. The one tightening: C1's U7 rubric label should use ACT's exact domain names (Ideas & Analysis / Development & Support / Organization / Language Use & Conventions) — C1 references "Ideas & Analysis" and "Development & Support" correctly; ensure all four are named in C5. Strong alignment.

**7. EII Polish "diction/syntax → tone-for-effect" beat → NEUTRAL→IMPROVEMENT.**
C1 reintroduces a sliver of craft-for-effect at Polish (TEKS EII.8(F)) after deliberately stripping rhetorical-analysis. This is consistent with CCSS L.9-10 / W.9-10.4 (style appropriate to task) and with EII's TEKS. Sensible re-entry; correctly back-loaded to Polish after meaning is settled.

## STAAR mode claim: **VERIFIED**

C1's load-bearing claim — *"the actual STAAR ECR is informational/analytical-response (primary, TEKS 10.B/5.B); argumentative (10.C) has not appeared on a released EI/EII ECR but is eligible"* — is **VERIFIED** against TEA primary sources:

- **Mode:** All six released ECRs (EI & EII, 2023-2025) were scored against **5.B (analytical) or 10.B (informational)**; **none against 10.C (argumentative)**, traced from the official answer keys. Both 2025 exemplars are analytical/informational, and TEA's EII2025 rationale explicitly notes the source "does not offer any opinions."
- **Eligibility hedge is correct:** 10.C argumentative is a Readiness Standard and an eligible genre — so C1 keeping argument as the *secondary/eligible-alternate* mode (and for ACT readiness) is exactly the right risk posture. Argument is **not** dead weight; it's the eligible-mode hedge + the ACT bridge.
- **Rubric:** C1's "Trait 1 Org & Development 0-3, Trait 2 Conventions 0-2" is **VERIFIED** (TEA: two traits, "development and organization of ideas up to 3 points and language conventions up to 2 points"). **One correction to fold into C5:** the 5-point rubric is **scaled ×2 to a 10-point ECR** on the actual test. C1's rubric rows are right; the total-points framing should note the ×2.
- **SCRs:** "reading SCR 0-2, writing SCR 0-1" is **VERIFIED** verbatim.
- **Source-bound:** **VERIFIED** — the ECR is "a written response to a single or paired reading passage."

**Caveat (honest):** "has not appeared" is confirmed for **2023-2025 released forms only**; TEA does not publish every operational form, so "never" is unprovable. The released-form evidence fully supports C1's bet, and C1's hedge (keep argument eligible) correctly handles the residual risk. Also: the per-released-test "constructed response scoring guide" page (annotated student samples) could **not** be reached; rubric structure was confirmed from the scoring-process doc + answer keys instead.

## What C1 omits that real curricula include (ranked; defensible vs real gap)

1. **Narrative writing (W.9-10.3; CommonLit G9 U2) — REAL GAP, but defensible for the exam MVP.**
   Narrative is a co-equal CCSS 9-10 text type and appears in CommonLit. It is **not** assessed by STAAR EI/EII ECR or ACT Writing, so dropping it is fine for the *exam* mission. But C1 cites CCSS W.9-10 in its standards tags and the parent project positions Track A as "standalone, college-ready, not just AP-prep." **Recommendation:** explicitly mark narrative as scoped-out-of-MVP rather than tag CCSS W.9-10 broadly and silently skip .3.

2. **Multi-day research / inquiry projects (W.9-10.7, .8; Odell's whole "Researching" unit; CommonLit research units) — REAL GAP, defensible.**
   C1 has source-bound evidence use and synthesis-across-given-sources, but **no student-driven research** (find/evaluate/cite outside sources over days). STAAR/ACT don't assess this; the AP on-ramp doesn't need it. Defensible to omit — but it's a genuine CCSS 9-10 requirement and a real college-readiness skill. Name it as scoped-out.

3. **Extended-process / writing-volume range (W.9-10.10 "routine writing over extended ... and shorter time frames") — PARTIAL GAP, defensible.**
   C1 captures the short/on-demand end heavily (and actually has *high* finished-piece throughput), and includes process via the 6-stage cycle, but not multi-week extended pieces. Fine for the target; the volume is not thin.

4. **Vocabulary as a strand (CommonLit: every unit) — MINOR, defensible.**
   Not C1's job (the parent project has a separate Language course; EII craft beat is folded into Polish). Defensible omission.

5. **Grammar as its own strand (CommonLit grammar lessons) — COVERED ENOUGH.**
   C1 handles conventions functionally (U4 sentence boundaries, run-ons/fragments, combining, revising/editing) targeted at STAAR Trait 2 — the dominant fault. Not a standalone strand, but the assessed conventions skills have a home. Adequate.

6. **Peer review / collaborative revision (workshop models) — MINOR.**
   C1's revision is AI-tutor-mediated (meaning-before-surface gating, rescore-weakest-part). No peer review. Defensible in an individual AI-tutor product; note it's a deliberate substitution, not an oversight.

7. **Reading volume / full-length anchor texts (CommonLit novels/dramas; Odell text sets) — DEFENSIBLE.**
   C1 is passage-based by design (STAAR is passage-based). Whole-novel study is out of scope and correctly so for this mission, though it's the biggest experiential difference from a real ELA course.
