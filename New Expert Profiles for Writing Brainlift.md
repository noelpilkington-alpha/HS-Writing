## Expert 1: Gerald Graff & Cathy Birkenstein

### Who
- **Gerald Graff** - Professor Emeritus of English and Education at the University of Illinois at Chicago; former president of the Modern Language Association
- **Cathy Birkenstein** - Lecturer in English at the University of Illinois at Chicago

### Focus
Academic argument structure; rhetorical moves in persuasive writing; templates for entering scholarly conversations; demystifying academic discourse

### Main Views
1. **Writing is entering a conversation**: Effective academic writing requires engaging with what others say ("they say") before presenting your own view ("I say")
2. **Templates are legitimate tools**: Far from being "formulaic," templates represent the "stock-in-trade of sophisticated thinking" and help students make moves they might not otherwise make
3. **The "So What? Who Cares?" imperative**: Writers must explain why their argument matters
4. **Naysayer planting**: Anticipating and addressing counterarguments strengthens writing
5. **Reading and writing are reciprocal**: Students who learn to make rhetorical moves become better at identifying those moves in texts they read

### Why Follow
- Their book "They Say / I Say" has been used by millions of students and is considered the definitive guide to academic argument
- Provides explicit, learnable templates for argumentative writing (directly aligns with Andy's "templates are not cheating" SPOV)
- Essential for grades 9-12 argumentative and persuasive writing
- Bridges TWR's structural focus with the rhetorical sophistication needed for academic success

### Where to Find
- Book: [They Say / I Say: The Moves That Matter in Academic Writing](https://wwnorton.com/books/they-say-i-say) (6th Edition, 2024)
- Companion blog: [theysayiblog.com](https://theysayiblog.com)
- Book included in project files

### Key Frameworks

**The "They Say / I Say" Template Structure:**
- "In discussions of __________, a controversial issue has been __________"
- "On the one hand, __________ argues __________. On the other hand, __________ contends __________"
- "My own view is that __________. Though I concede that __________, I still maintain that __________"
- "Of course some might object that __________. Although I concede that __________, I still maintain that __________"

**Signal Verbs for Academic Writing:**
- Making claims: argue, assert, emphasize, insist, observe
- Agreeing: acknowledge, endorse, corroborate, support
- Disagreeing: complain, contend, refute, reject, question
- Recommending: advocate, call for, urge, warn

### Integration with TWR
| TWR Contribution | TSIS Contribution | Combined Outcome |
|------------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Sentence-level clarity | Rhetorical moves | Clear AND persuasive writing |
| Paragraph structure | Conversation framing | Structured arguments with context |
| Essay organization | "They say/I say" template | Essays that engage with others |

## Expert 2: Debra Myhill

### Who
Professor Emerita of Education at the University of Exeter; Director of the Centre for Research in Writing; Professorial Fellow at the University of Wollongong, Australia

### Focus
Grammar and writing; metalinguistic understanding; contextual grammar instruction; the writing process; language choice in composition

### Main Views
1. **Contextual grammar > isolated grammar**: Grammar taught in context of writing shows significant positive effects; isolated drill does not
2. **Grammar as meaning-making**: "Eschewing a view of grammar as solely concerned with error correction, and instead seeing 'language as a resource, a meaning-making system'"
3. **Metalinguistic talk is essential**: Students need opportunities to discuss language choices in their writing
4. **Teacher linguistic knowledge matters**: Teachers' linguistic subject knowledge (LSK) significantly mediates student outcomes

### Why Follow
- Conducted landmark RCT study (744 students, 31 schools) proving contextual grammar teaching improves writing by 11.52% vs 6.41% for comparison group
- Provides the research foundation for Andy's SPOV that "grammar instruction as a separate independent thread creates competence"
- Her approach reconciles the "grammar wars" with empirical evidence
- Essential for designing the grammar component of the K-12 curriculum

### Where to Find
- [University of Exeter Profile](http://education.exeter.ac.uk/people/profile/index.php?web_id=debra_myhill)
- [ResearchGate Profile](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Debra-Myhill)
- [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=MfNznzYAAAAJ) (9,281+ citations)

### Key Research Publications
1. Myhill, Jones, Lines, & Watson (2012). "[Re-thinking grammar: the impact of embedded grammar teaching on students' writing and students' metalinguistic understanding](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/02671522.2011.637640)." Research Papers in Education, 27(2), 139-166.
2. Myhill (2021). "[Grammar re-imagined: foregrounding understanding of language choice in writing](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/04250494.2021.1885975)." English in Education, 55(3), 265-278.
3. Myhill & Watson (2014). "[The role of grammar in the writing curriculum: A review of the literature](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0265659013514070)."

### Key Research Findings
| Finding | Effect Size | Implication |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Intervention group writing improvement | +11.52% | Contextual grammar works |
| Comparison group improvement | +6.41% | Isolated grammar is less effective |
| Teacher LSK impact | Significant | Teacher training essential |
| More able writers | Stronger benefit | Differentiation needed |

### LEAD Principles (Myhill's Pedagogical Approach)
At the heart of her approach is "the importance of making connections for learners between a grammatical choice and how it subtly shapes or shifts meaning in their own piece of writing."

## Expert 3: Virginia Berninger

### Who
Professor Emerita, Educational Psychology, University of Washington; leading researcher on writing development, learning disabilities, and the neuroscience of writing

### Focus
Simple View of Writing; Not-So-Simple View of Writing; transcription fluency; handwriting and spelling development; orthographic loop; writing disabilities

### Main Views
1. **Simple View of Writing**: Writing = Transcription x Text Generation. Both components are necessary; weakness in either constrains writing quality
2. **Transcription must be automatic**: Handwriting/keyboarding and spelling must be fluent before higher-order composition can flourish
3. **Working memory constraints**: When transcription is effortful, it consumes working memory that should be available for planning and idea generation
4. **Not-So-Simple View of Writing**: Expands the model to include executive function and self-regulatory processes (attention, goal setting, reviewing)

### Why Follow
- Foundational theorist for understanding why early writers struggle
- Her model explains the research basis for Datchuk's fluency work
- Essential for designing K-2 writing instruction that builds proper foundations
- Bridges neuroscience and classroom practice

### Where to Find
- [University of Washington Profile](https://education.uw.edu/about/directory/virginia-berninger)
- [Zaner-Bloser Research Summary](https://www.zaner-bloser.com/HW21Summit/research-berninger.html)
- Key publications on ResearchGate

### Key Frameworks

**Simple View of Writing (Berninger et al., 2002)**
```
Writing = Transcription x Text Generation

Where:
- Transcription = Handwriting/Keyboarding + Spelling
- Text Generation = Word-level + Sentence-level + Text-level composition
```

**Not-So-Simple View of Writing (Berninger & Winn, 2006)**
```
Writing = (Transcription + Text Generation) + Executive Functions

Where Executive Functions include:
- Planning
- Monitoring
- Reviewing/Revising
- Attention regulation
- Goal setting
```

### Key Research Publications
1. Berninger, Abbott, Abbott, Graham, & Richards (2002). "Writing and reading: Connections between language by hand and language by eye." Journal of Learning Disabilities.
2. Berninger & Winn (2006). "Implications of advancements in brain research and technology for writing development, writing instruction, and educational evolution."
3. Berninger & Swanson (1994). "Modifying Hayes and Flower's model of skilled writing."

### Implications for K-2 Curriculum
| Grade | Focus | Berninger's Research Support |
|-------|-------|------------------------------|
| K | Letter formation, phonemic awareness | Orthographic loop development |
| 1 | Handwriting fluency, basic spelling | Transcription automaticity |
| 2 | Spelling patterns, simple sentences | Freeing working memory for composition |

## Expert 4: Deborah McCutchen

### Who
Professor of Educational Psychology, University of Washington (PhD, University of Pittsburgh, 1985); former classroom writing teacher turned researcher

### Focus
Cognitive processes in reading and writing; working memory and composition; development of writing expertise; knowledge and language fluency in writing

### Main Views
1. **Capacity theory of writing**: Working memory constraints affect all three writing processes: planning, translating, and reviewing
2. **Fluent transcription is prerequisite**: "Lack of accuracy and fluency in transcription skills constrain writing by interfering with higher-order skills such as planning and content generation"
3. **From novice to expert**: Two necessary components for writing expertise: fluent language generation processes AND extensive domain knowledge
4. **Long-term working memory**: Skilled writers can access long-term memory resources during composition; novices are constrained to short-term working memory

### Why Follow
- Provides the cognitive psychology foundation for understanding WHY Datchuk's fluency interventions work
- Explains the developmental trajectory from struggling writer to expert
- Her "capacity theory" justifies TWR's sentence-first approach (reduce cognitive load)
- Essential for understanding differentiation for struggling writers

### Where to Find
- [University of Washington Profile](https://education.uw.edu/about/directory/deborah-mccutchen)
- [ResearchGate Profile](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Deborah-Mccutchen)
- [Academia.edu Profile](https://washington.academia.edu/DeborahMcCutchen)

### Key Research Publications
1. McCutchen (1996). "[A capacity theory of writing: Working memory in composition](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF01464076)." Educational Psychology Review, 8, 299-325.
2. McCutchen (2000). "[Knowledge, processing, and working memory: Implications for a theory of writing](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1207/S15326985EP3501_3)." Educational Psychologist, 35(1), 13-23.
3. McCutchen (2011). "From novice to expert: Implications of language skills and writing-relevant knowledge for memory during the development of writing skill."

### Key Theoretical Contributions

**Capacity Theory of Writing**
```
Writing Quality = f(Working Memory Capacity - Transcription Demands)

When transcription is automatic:
  → More WM available for planning, generating, reviewing
  → Higher quality composition

When transcription is effortful:
  → WM consumed by low-level processes
  → Constrained composition quality
```

**Novice vs. Expert Writer Differences**
| Dimension | Novice Writers | Expert Writers |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Memory access | Short-term WM only | Long-term WM available |
| Transcription | Effortful, slow | Automatic, fluent |
| Planning | Minimal | Extensive |
| Knowledge | Limited domain knowledge | Rich domain knowledge |
| Revision | Surface-level | Meaning-level |

## Expert 5: Pietro Boscolo & Suzanne Hidi

### Who
- **Pietro Boscolo** - Professor Emeritus of Educational Psychology, University of Padova, Italy (1975-2011); Department Director and Dean of Faculty
- **Suzanne Hidi** - Professor Emerita, Ontario Institute for Studies in Education (OISE), University of Toronto; leading researcher on interest and motivation

### Focus
Writing motivation; interest development; self-efficacy in writing; writing as a learning activity; the relationship between interest and writing quality

### Main Views
1. **Interest drives writing quality**: Students' interest in topics significantly affects their engagement and writing outcomes
2. **Multiple meanings of motivation**: Writing motivation encompasses goals, needs, values, interests, self-efficacy, and attitudes
3. **Writing fosters interest**: The act of writing about a topic can actually develop interest in that topic (bidirectional relationship)
4. **Motivational aspects are "partially still unexplored"**: The field needs more research on how to foster writing motivation

### Why Follow
- Their book "Writing and Motivation" is THE foundational text on this topic
- Directly supports Andy's SPOV that "motivation is 90%" and "kids love what they write because they write what they love"
- Provides research basis for interest-driven writing instruction
- Essential for designing motivating writing curricula

### Key Theoretical Framework

**Four-Phase Model of Interest Development (Hidi & Renninger)**
```
Phase 1: Triggered Situational Interest
  → External stimulus captures attention

Phase 2: Maintained Situational Interest
  → Engagement sustained through meaningfulness

Phase 3: Emerging Individual Interest
  → Student begins seeking engagement independently

Phase 4: Well-Developed Individual Interest
  → Deep, self-sustained engagement with domain
```

**Motivational Variables in Writing**
| Variable | Definition | Impact on Writing |
|----------|------------|-------------------|
| Self-efficacy | Belief in one's writing ability | Predicts persistence and quality |
| Interest | Affective response to topic/task | Drives engagement and effort |
| Goal orientation | Mastery vs. performance goals | Affects risk-taking and revision |
| Attitude | General disposition toward writing | Influences willingness to write |

## Expert 6: Jeff Anderson

### Who
Veteran middle school teacher (35+ years); author of 16 books on writing and grammar instruction; creator of the "Patterns of Power" approach; lives in San Antonio, Texas

### Focus
Grammar instruction within writer's workshop; mentor texts for grammar teaching; visual scaffolds; daily grammar routines; making grammar instruction joyful and effective

### Main Views
1. **Grammar belongs in writer's workshop**: Grammar should be taught in the context of real writing, not isolated drill
2. **Mentor texts are essential**: High-quality literature models effective grammar and mechanics in context
3. **Short, daily routines work best**: Brief, consistent grammar instruction beats lengthy, sporadic lessons
4. **Celebrate what's right**: Focus on effective grammar usage rather than error correction
5. **Visual scaffolds support learning**: Wall charts, writer's notebook cues, and visual references help students internalize conventions

### Why Follow
- Provides the PRACTICAL classroom implementation of Myhill's research
- His "Mechanically Inclined" approach has been tested in real classrooms for 20+ years
- Bridges research and practice in accessible, teacher-friendly ways
- Essential for training teachers to implement contextual grammar instruction

### Key Teaching Elements

**The "Mechanically Inclined" Approach**
1. **Short daily instruction**: 5-10 minutes of grammar/mechanics within writer's workshop
2. **Mentor texts**: Use high-quality literature to model conventions in context
3. **Visual scaffolds**: Wall charts and writer's notebook cues for reference
4. **Express-lane edits**: Quick routines to spot and correct errors automatically
5. **Invitation, not correction**: Invite students to notice and try techniques

**Patterns of Power Framework**
```
Day 1: Invitation to Notice
  → Present mentor sentence; students observe patterns

Day 2: Invitation to Compare and Contrast
  → Compare effective vs. ineffective examples

Day 3: Invitation to Imitate
  → Students write their own sentences using the pattern

Day 4: Invitation to Celebrate
  → Share and celebrate effective usage

Day 5: Invitation to Apply
  → Use pattern in authentic writing
```
