# Framework Audit — Validate-Then-Extend

Grades the five design frameworks already in use in these courses against the
research base, then says where the evidence points *beyond* them. This is the
"stance" you asked for: confirm what's backed, then find what's missing.

Verdict scale: **VALIDATED (A)** / **VALIDATED (B)** / **EXTRAPOLATED** (backed
outside writing, applied to writing on reasonable but unproven grounds) /
**UNVALIDATED** (no verified evidence for or against) / **CONTESTED** (active
counter-argument exists).

---

## 1. SRSD — VALIDATED (A). Keep it; sharpen the modeling and self-regulation.

**What's confirmed:**
- SRSD ES **1.14** vs 0.62 non-SRSD strategy instruction (p<.001); especially
  strong for struggling writers. WWC STRONG for the Model-Practice-Reflect cycle
  it embodies. [WritingNext; WWC-22]
- Our teach → model → guided → independent → transfer spine and our HIT/PROVE/S³
  mnemonics are textbook SRSD (the mnemonics are the "Memorize It" artifact).

**Where the research says to EXTEND:**
- **SRSD is not just the writing moves — it explicitly teaches self-regulation**
  (goal-setting, self-monitoring, self-instruction, self-reinforcement). Audit
  our lessons: do they build the *self-regulation* half, or only the writing
  strategy? The feedback loop (Layer 4) is where self-regulation gets trained —
  connect the mnemonic to a self-monitoring cue in every feedback message
  (Toolkit D1).
- **"Model It" must be a coping think-aloud, not a clean exemplar.** The
  worked-example effect + SRSD both require the expert to narrate messy
  decisions and false starts. This is our known "coping models required"
  principle — the research backs it hard; make sure it survives in the built
  lessons (worked-application in Toolkit F flags this as a common gap).
- **Struggling-writer priority:** SRSD's biggest edge is for struggling writers.
  A self-paced model that lets strong writers race ahead must not let the
  strategy scaffolding thin out for the students it helps most.

**Risk to watch:** SRSD's evidence is for *taught* SRSD with a human enacting the
stages. Our AI-tutor delivery is an adaptation; the stages must all be present
(especially live modeling and supported practice), or we're claiming SRSD's
evidence for something that isn't SRSD.

## 2. Cognitive Load Theory — VALIDATED (A) for the rules.

**What's confirmed:**
- Load additivity, worked-example, split-attention, redundancy, expertise
  reversal — all directly support our scaffold-heavy, worked-model, templates-
  reduce-load design. "Templates aren't cheating" is CLT-correct. [Paas2020;
  Kalyuga1998]

**Honest caveat (already in the KB):** the *internal theory* of germane load and
the three-types architecture is contested (de Jong). This does **not** weaken
the applied rules — but don't cite "germane load" as settled science; cite the
design rule (minimize extraneous load; match support to expertise).

**Where to EXTEND:**
- **Element-interactivity as a design metric.** Before building a task, estimate
  how many interacting elements it forces into working memory at once. High-
  interactivity tasks (compose an argued paragraph from a source) need more
  decomposition/scaffolding than we might guess. Use this to *decide* where to
  add scaffolds rather than adding them by feel.
- **Redundancy for advanced rungs:** we're good at adding support for novices;
  are we *removing* redundant support for advanced students? Redundancy actively
  harms experts. The audit skew (Rule 3 asymmetry) says under-support is worse,
  so this is second priority — but it's a real, unexploited lever for our
  strongest students.

## 3. Scaffold fading — VALIDATED (A) generally, EXTRAPOLATED for writing.

**What's confirmed:**
- Expertise reversal is robust (d=+0.505 novice-with-support, d=-0.428
  expert-with-support), asymmetric, and 4C/ID builds fading into its structure.
  [ExpertiseReversal2025; TenSteps]

**The caveat that matters most:** the 2025 meta-analysis **explicitly flags
weaker evidence for younger students and for humanities/language-learning** —
i.e., our exact domain. So scaffold fading in writing is a **well-grounded
extrapolation, not a proven writing result.**

**Where to EXTEND / what to DO about it:**
- **Fade on demonstrated performance, not on a lesson-number schedule.** This is
  the single most concrete upgrade the research demands. Define, per scaffold,
  the *observable performance* that triggers its removal.
- **Asymmetry rule:** when uncertain, keep the scaffold. Under-support hurts more
  than over-support.
- **Instrument it.** Because it's an extrapolation, we should watch our own data:
  do students who lose scaffold X at rung N actually outperform those who keep
  it? Self-paced delivery gives us the data to *locally validate* fading — turn
  the caveat into an experiment.

## 4. Discrimination-before-production — UNVALIDATED (and that's the finding).

**Status:** No verified claim in the research base supports OR refutes
discrimination-before-production as a writing-sequencing principle. It is
currently a **design heuristic, not an evidence-backed rule.** The honest KB
posture is to flag it as Grade C.

**Why it's plausible (the case for keeping it):**
- It aligns with the **contrasting-cases / example-based learning** literature
  (comparing good vs. flawed examples sharpens the schema before production).
- It's consistent with CLT: classifying reduces the generation load, letting WM
  focus on recognizing the target features first.
- *Writing to Read* shows write-about-text works for low achievers **only with
  explicit instruction** — discrimination is one form of that explicit
  instruction. That's indirect support, not proof.

**What to DO:**
- **Don't drop it, but stop treating it as settled.** Label it a hypothesis in
  our design docs.
- **Research it directly** (this is the top open question): contrasting-cases,
  example variability, and discrimination-learning literatures likely have a
  real evidence base we simply didn't surface in this pass. A targeted follow-up
  research run should either promote it to Grade A/B or replace it.
- **A/B it in the self-paced system.** We can test discrimination-first vs.
  model-then-produce directly on our own students.

## 5. Mastery / self-paced delivery — VALIDATED (B) as method, UNVALIDATED + CONTESTED for writing.

**What's confirmed (as a general method):**
- Bloom: mastery learning ≈ +1 sigma; feedback-corrective loop is a top
  alterable variable (1.00); formative assessment 0.4-0.7 and helps low achievers
  most. [Bloom1984; BlackWiliam1998] The 2 Hour Learning model is a serious
  attempt to operationalize Bloom's alterable variables via an AI tutor.

**Two hard caveats:**
1. **The "+2 sigma" headline is contested.** Later work rarely replicates 2
   sigma for tutoring; treat it as aspiration, not a bankable effect size. Don't
   promise 2 sigma.
2. **Nothing in the verified evidence supports mastery/self-paced delivery for
   WRITING specifically**, and there's an active counter-argument:
   [MasteryCritique] argues (a) "mastering writing" is a category error — writing
   is generative/ambiguous, not a discrete masterable skill; (b) fixed-benchmark
   pacing mislabels slower writers as deficient; (c) standards tight enough to
   "master" reward formula (so gameable that AI can reproduce them) over
   authentic expression. The same critics concede mastery/standards-based grading
   reduces bias, removes behavior from the grade, and can drive intrinsic
   motivation.

**Where to EXTEND / how to hold this responsibly:**
- **Gate on composition traits, not surface compliance** (Toolkit Rule 10). This
  directly answers the "rewards formula" critique — if the gate requires genuine
  reasoning/evidence integration, it can't be gamed by formula.
- **Treat "slow" as differently-paced.** Build the system so falling behind a
  benchmark triggers *support*, not a deficiency label. This answers critique
  (b).
- **Keep open/generative tasks in the mix.** The critique's strongest point is
  that pure mastery-of-standards squeezes out voice. Balance masterable
  sub-skills (claim structure, evidence integration, sentence combining) with
  open composition where "exceeds" is genuinely open (single-point rubrics
  help). This answers critique (a).
- **Reliability-aware gating.** Writing scores are rater-dependent; set gates
  inside the grader's reliable-discrimination band and validate AI-grader
  agreement with expert humans.
- **This is the #1 thing to research next for our specific model** — mastery
  learning *for writing* under AI-tutor delivery is genuinely unproven ground.
  We are somewhat ahead of the literature here, which is fine, but we should say
  so and measure it, not assume Bloom's general result transfers.

## The three highest-leverage moves this audit surfaces

1. **Fade scaffolds on demonstrated performance, not lesson number.** Concrete,
   research-mandated, and our self-paced system already has the data to do it.
   (Framework 3)
2. **Make every "Model It" a coping think-aloud, and connect every feedback
   message to a self-monitoring cue.** This is where SRSD's self-regulation half
   and Hattie's process-feedback level actually get built — and it's a common gap
   in clean, polished lessons. (Frameworks 1 + Layer 4)
3. **Gate mastery on genuine-composition traits and treat slow as
   differently-paced.** This is how we hold the self-paced-mastery model
   responsibly for writing given it's unproven and contested there.
   (Framework 5)

## The three biggest open questions (for the next research run)

1. Is **discrimination-before-production** supported by the contrasting-cases /
   discrimination-learning literature? (Promote or replace our heuristic.)
2. Does **scaffold fading / expertise reversal hold *within* writing**? (The 2025
   meta-analysis says the writing-domain evidence is thin — find or generate it.)
3. What does evidence say about **mastery learning for writing specifically**,
   and about **spaced/retrieval practice of composing *skills*** (not declarative
   content)? Both are extrapolations our model leans on heavily.
