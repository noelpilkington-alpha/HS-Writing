## A. The Lesson Spine — canonical sequence

Our teach → model → guided practice → independent → transfer spine IS the
WWC-Strong Model-Practice-Reflect cycle [WWC-22] and the SRSD six stages
[WritingNext]. Mapping:

| Our stage | SRSD stage | Gagné events | Cognitive job | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hook / activate** | Develop Background Knowledge | Gain attention; recall prior | Load prerequisites into WM; don't start cold | Ausubel prior-knowledge; [WWC-22] |
| **Teach** | Discuss It | Inform objective; present content | Name the move; give purpose + when/why | [WWC-22]; [WritingNext] |
| **Model** | Model It | Provide guidance | Worked example + expert think-aloud incl. self-talk & false starts | worked-example effect [Paas]; SRSD [WritingNext] |
| **Discriminate** *(our addition)* | (within Model/Support) | — | Classify strong vs. weak examples before producing | contrasting-cases; see Audit — Grade C |
| **Memorize** | Memorize It | — | Commit mnemonic to LTM so it doesn't eat WM during writing | CLT [Paas]; SRSD |
| **Guided practice** | Support It | Elicit performance; feedback | Practice with scaffolds; fade gradually | expertise reversal [ExpertiseReversal2025] |
| **Independent** | Independent Performance | Assess performance | Perform with scaffolds removed | SRSD; mastery gating |
| **Transfer / space** | (maintenance) | Enhance retention & transfer | Revisit across sessions; delayed, multi-round retrieval | spacing g=0.74 [Latimier2021]; retrieval [Corral-Carpenter2025] |

**The non-negotiables** (each is A-rated):
1. There is an explicit, named strategy. [WWC-22]
2. It is modeled by an expert think-aloud, not just described. [worked example]
3. Support is present for novices and faded as they progress. [expertise reversal]
4. It is revisited across time, not taught once. [spacing]
5. Feedback targets process/self-regulation, not the person. [Hattie2007]

## B. Ten Design Decision Rules

Each: **the rule → the evidence → the failure mode if you skip it.**

### Rule 1 — Every lesson teaches ONE named, explicit strategy.
- **Rule:** If you can't state the strategy in one sentence and its mnemonic,
  the lesson isn't ready. Teach it, model it, have students memorize it.
- **Evidence:** Strategy instruction is the #1 lever (ES 0.82; SRSD 1.14); WWC
  STRONG. [WritingNext; WWC-22]
- **Failure mode:** "Process writing" ambience with no explicit strategy —
  drops you to ES ~0.32 and abandons struggling writers.

### Rule 2 — Model before you ask them to produce; the model is a think-aloud with false starts.
- **Rule:** Show a completed, annotated example AND narrate the messy decision
  process (coping model), including a false start you fix. Then discriminate,
  then produce.
- **Evidence:** Worked-example effect [Paas]; SRSD "Model It"; write-about-text
  fails for low achievers *without* explicit instruction [WritingToRead].
- **Failure mode:** Blank-page tasks. Novices burn all working memory on "what
  do I even do," learn the least, and the weakest students gain ES ≈ 0.

### Rule 3 — Match support to expertise; fade gradually; when unsure, keep the scaffold.
- **Rule:** Novice rungs get high assistance (templates, sentence frames, worked
  models, integrated annotation). Advanced rungs strip them. Fade in principled
  steps tied to demonstrated performance — never all at once.
- **Evidence:** Expertise reversal: novices d=+0.505 with support, experts
  d=-0.428 with support; **asymmetric** — under-support hurts more than
  over-support. [ExpertiseReversal2025]
- **Failure mode:** One support level for everyone → bores/redundancy-loads the
  advanced, strands the novice. Or stripping scaffolds too early because the
  student "should" be ready.
- **Domain caveat:** evidence is weaker in humanities/language — so fade on
  *observed* performance data, not a fixed schedule, and watch our own numbers.

### Rule 4 — Give specific product goals, never "do your best."
- **Rule:** State the concrete target ("include 2 counterarguments and rebut
  one"; "3 pieces of textual evidence, each tied to your claim"). Goals are part
  of the strategy, not a grading afterthought.
- **Evidence:** Setting product goals ES 0.70 [WritingNext]; "do your best" is
  explicitly ineffective (self-regulation literature).
- **Failure mode:** Vague goals → students can't self-monitor → no self-regulation
  → feedback has no referent.

### Rule 5 — Build syntax through sentence combining, address conventions in context; never isolated grammar drill as a writing-improvement path.
- **Rule:** Keep a conventions/syntax thread, but drive it with sentence
  combining and expansion (because/but/so, appositives) and reconnect to real
  composing. Automaticity first, then it frees WM for ideas.
- **Evidence:** Sentence combining ES 0.50; isolated grammar **-0.32**;
  in-context grammar positive. [WritingNext]
- **Failure mode:** Grammar worksheets as the writing program → negative effect
  on writing quality; WM never freed for composition.

### Rule 6 — Space and retrieve: revisit each move across sessions with delayed, multi-round retrieval.
- **Rule:** No strategy is "done" after one lesson. Schedule it to reappear
  (uniform spacing is fine — don't overbuild expanding intervals). Use
  low-stakes retrieval; for transfer, use multiple rounds + a delay before the
  application item.
- **Evidence:** Spaced vs massed g=0.74; expanding≈uniform; transfer needs
  multi-round+delay. [Latimier2021; Corral-Carpenter2025]
- **Failure mode:** One-and-done teaching → performance looks fine immediately,
  learning doesn't stick, transfer never appears.
- **Domain caveat:** proven on facts/concepts; retrieval of composing *skills*
  is an extrapolation — treat as strong hypothesis, measure it.

### Rule 7 — Feedback targets the process and the next move, never the person.
- **Rule:** Write feedback as: (1) Where you're going [goal], (2) Where you are
  [status vs. goal], (3) Where to next [the specific process move]. Ban
  person-praise ("great job!") and grade-only feedback from the learning loop.
- **Evidence:** Feedback ES 0.79 but variable; process/self-reg levels win;
  self/person level ineffective (~0.12); grades-alone don't help. [Hattie2007;
  BlackWiliam1998]
- **Failure mode:** "B+. Nice work." → zero learning signal; can even reduce
  motivation.

### Rule 8 — Use analytic rubrics for growth, single-point for open tasks; never assume the rubric alone teaches self-assessment.
- **Rule:** Match rubric type to purpose (analytic = diagnostic feedback +
  calibration; single-point = open/creative + self-assessment; holistic = fast
  gate only). Always pair student self-scoring with expert feedback + repeated
  practice.
- **Evidence:** Self-assessment g=0.585 but rubrics alone did NOT improve
  calibration; feedback is the lever; calibration is trainable.
  [SelfPeerAssessment2022; SelfAssessmentAccuracy2023]
- **Failure mode:** "Here's the rubric, grade yourself" → students overestimate
  (bias g=0.206), no calibration gain.

### Rule 9 — Design backward from a transfer goal and an authentic whole task.
- **Rule:** Before building activities, write the Stage-1 transfer goal and the
  Stage-2 culminating performance task. Every activity must earn its place by
  building toward that task. Code activities T/M/A — make sure you're not 100%
  Acquisition.
- **Evidence:** UbD alignment [UbD]; rote acquisition doesn't transfer; 4C/ID
  whole-task backbone [TenSteps].
- **Failure mode:** Activity-first design → a pile of exercises that never
  composes into the target performance; students can do drills but not write.

### Rule 10 — Set mastery gates on genuine-composition traits, within the grader's reliability envelope.
- **Rule:** Gate advancement on demonstrated writing performance, but choose
  traits that require real composition (not surface features an AI can trivially
  detect/game), and don't gate on score differences finer than the grader can
  reliably distinguish. Treat a "slow" student as differently-paced until data
  says otherwise.
- **Evidence:** Bloom feedback-corrective loop [Bloom1984]; reliability/validity
  (Layer 4.5); mastery-for-writing critique [MasteryCritique].
- **Failure mode:** Gating on formula compliance → rewards gameable conformity,
  mislabels slower writers as deficient, measures the wrong thing.

## C. Build-Time Checklists

### C1. Per-lesson checklist (run before a lesson is "done")
- [ ] **Named strategy** stated in one sentence + mnemonic. [Rule 1]
- [ ] **Transfer/product goal** is specific and student-visible. [Rule 4]
- [ ] **Worked model** present: annotated exemplar + expert think-aloud with at
      least one coping-model false start. [Rule 2]
- [ ] **Discrimination step**: students classify strong vs. weak before
      producing. [Rule 2; Audit]
- [ ] **Scaffold level matches the rung**, and the fade step is defined (what
      support is removed at the next rung, on what evidence). [Rule 3]
- [ ] **Guided → independent** release is explicit (not teach-then-test).
- [ ] **Spacing hook**: where does this move reappear later? Named. [Rule 6]
- [ ] **Retrieval item**: a low-stakes retrieval of a *prior* move is included.
      [Rule 6]
- [ ] **Feedback spec** answers the 3 questions, targets process, no
      person-praise. [Rule 7]
- [ ] **Conventions handled in context** / via sentence combining, not isolated
      drill. [Rule 5]
- [ ] **CLT check**: is annotation integrated with the example (not split)? Is
      anything redundant for the intended expertise level? [split-attention;
      redundancy]
- [ ] **Provenance**: facts + teaching moves trace to a verified source (house
      rule) — cite or flag. [links to lesson-content-provenance-rule]

### C2. Per-unit checklist (run when sequencing a unit)
- [ ] **Backward-designed**: Stage-1 transfer goal + Stage-2 culminating task
      written before activities. [Rule 9]
- [ ] **Alignment**: every activity builds toward the culminating task; T/M/A
      coded; not all Acquisition. [Rule 9]
- [ ] **Whole-task presence**: students attempt authentic whole writing tasks
      (supported) early, not only after months of part-tasks. [4C/ID; Rule 9]
- [ ] **Progression**: rungs ordered by prerequisite; each rung is an observable
      writing performance; prerequisites pinned for remediation. [3.5]
- [ ] **Spacing across the unit**: earlier moves recur in later lessons; a
      distributed-practice map exists. [Rule 6]
- [ ] **Mastery gates**: set on composition traits, within grader reliability;
      "not yet" ≠ "deficient." [Rule 10]
- [ ] **Ranked-menu coverage**: the unit invests in high-ES elements (strategy,
      summarization, product goals, sentence combining) not low-ES filler. Check
      against the Writing Next table. [2.2]

### C3. Per-assessment checklist
- [ ] Rubric type matches purpose (analytic / single-point / holistic). [Rule 8]
- [ ] Rubric traits require genuine composition, not surface compliance. [Rule 10]
- [ ] Self-assessment step is paired with expert feedback + is part of a
      trained progression, not one-shot. [Rule 8]
- [ ] Feedback generated answers Where-going / Where-now / Where-next at the
      process level. [Rule 7]
- [ ] Gate threshold is inside the grader's reliable-discrimination band. [4.5]

## D. Feedback & Rubric Templates

### D1. The process-feedback template (Hattie 3-questions, process level)
```
GOAL:  You're aiming to [Stage-1/product goal in student words].
NOW:   Right now your [trait] does [X]; the target is [Y]. (Show, don't grade.)
NEXT:  Do this next: [one specific process move, tied to the named strategy].
       Check yourself with: [the self-monitoring cue from the mnemonic].
```
Rules baked in: no "good job"; names the process move; gives a self-regulation
cue (feeds the SRSD self-monitoring the course is trying to build).

### D2. Analytic rubric row shape (for growth/diagnostic use)
```
TRAIT: [e.g., Claim-Evidence link]
  Not yet | Approaching | Proficient | Exceeds
  [each level described by an observable writing behavior, not a quality adjective]
```

### D3. Single-point rubric shape (for open/creative tasks + self-assessment)
```
AREAS OF CONCERN  |  PROFICIENCY CRITERIA (the only column filled in advance)  |  EVIDENCE OF EXCEEDING
(open for feedback) | [what "meets the standard" looks like, per trait]        | (open — lets students exceed)
```
Best when students co-create it and self-assess against it — but STILL paired
with expert feedback (rubric alone doesn't calibrate).

### D4. Calibration progression (validated shape, extends our existing one)
`binary (meets/not) → 3-point → full analytic/AP → self-diagnosis`, and at every
stage the student self-scores THEN receives expert feedback on the gap, across
repeated tasks. Feedback + repetition is the calibrating agent, not the rubric.
[SelfAssessmentAccuracy2023]

## E. Anti-Pattern Kill-List (evidence-backed "don't")

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Isolated grammar drills to improve writing | Negative effect on writing quality | ES -0.32 [WritingNext] |
| Blank-page / unscaffolded "just write" for novices | Weakest students gain ~0; WM overload | [WritingToRead]; CLT |
| "Do your best" goals | No self-monitoring referent | product goals 0.70 vs vague |
| Grade-only or praise-only feedback | No learning signal; can demotivate | praise ~0.12; rewards negative [Hattie2007] |
| "Here's the rubric, self-grade" with no feedback | Overestimation, no calibration gain | rubric-alone n.s. [SelfAssessmentAccuracy2023] |
| One-and-done teaching of a move | No durability, no transfer | spacing g=0.74 [Latimier2021] |
| Same scaffold level for all/forever | Redundancy-loads advanced; can't strand novices both | expertise reversal |
| Designing learning-styles-matched tracks | Effect too small/inconsistent; single-session only | g=0.31, authors reject [LearningStyles2024] |
| Activity-first unit design | Drills that never compose into the target | rote ≠ transfer [UbD] |
| Mastery gates on formula compliance | Rewards gameable conformity; mislabels slow writers | [MasteryCritique]; validity |
| Expanding-interval spacing schedules (over-engineering) | No advantage over uniform; wasted build effort | expanding≈uniform [Latimier2021] |

## F. Worked Application — pressure-testing one lesson

Take a canonical Unit-1 argument lesson (claim + evidence + reasoning). Run the
C1 checklist:

- **Named strategy?** Yes — e.g., PROVE for the body paragraph, with mnemonic.
  ✔ [Rule 1]
- **Product goal?** "One claim, two pieces of evidence from the source, each
  followed by reasoning that ties it back." Specific ✔ [Rule 4]
- **Worked model with coping think-aloud?** Need an annotated exemplar AND a
  narrated "here's where I first picked weak evidence and swapped it" false
  start. If the lesson only shows a clean final model → **gap** [Rule 2].
- **Discrimination before production?** Students sort strong vs. weak
  evidence-reasoning pairs before writing their own ✔ (this is our design
  instinct; note it's Grade C evidentially — see Audit).
- **Scaffold + fade defined?** Sentence frames for the reasoning move at the
  novice rung; removed by the independent rung. Is the *trigger* for removal
  defined (a demonstrated performance, not "lesson 4")? If not → **gap** [Rule 3].
- **Spacing hook?** Does "reasoning that ties evidence to claim" reappear in a
  later lesson's retrieval? If the map doesn't show it → **gap** [Rule 6].
- **Feedback spec?** Uses D1 template at process level ✔ [Rule 7].
- **Conventions in context?** Any sentence-level work uses combining/expansion,
  not a detached comma worksheet ✔ [Rule 5].

Typical gaps this surfaces in our current lessons: (a) clean models without a
coping/false-start think-aloud; (b) fade steps defined by lesson number rather
than demonstrated performance; (c) no explicit spacing/retrieval of prior moves.
Those three are the highest-leverage, lowest-cost upgrades.
