# Thinking Like an AP Lang Student
## An Asynchronous Mental Models Course
## AP English Language and Composition

---

## Course Philosophy

Most students who struggle with AP Lang aren't missing knowledge -- they're missing **lenses**. They read a passage and see words. A strong AP Lang student reads the same passage and sees a writer making moves for reasons. The difference isn't intelligence or preparation -- it's that the strong student has internalized mental models that activate automatically when they encounter text.

This course teaches six mental models in sequence. Each one builds on the last. Together, they produce the analytical posture the AP Lang exam rewards.

---

## Course Design: Asynchronous Principles

This course is designed for independent completion without a live instructor present. Every element accounts for that constraint:

1. **Written think-alouds replace live modeling.** Where a tutor would talk through their reasoning, this course provides fully narrated expert analyses -- the internal monologue made visible on the page.
2. **Model answers replace tutor feedback.** Every practice exercise includes a detailed model response. Students compare their own reasoning to the model AFTER attempting independently.
3. **Self-assessment rubrics replace scoring.** Students evaluate their own work against specific criteria, not just answer keys. The criteria target reasoning quality, not just correctness.
4. **Checkpoints gate progression.** Students should not advance to the next module until they meet the checkpoint criteria. No external enforcer -- but the course is designed so that skipping ahead produces confusion, not shortcuts.
5. **Concreteness over abstraction.** Every concept is introduced through a specific passage example, never as a free-floating definition.

# MODULE 1: Everything Is a Choice

## The Mental Model

**Nothing in a published text is accidental.** Word choice, sentence length, paragraph breaks, what's included, what's omitted, the order of ideas, the tone of a single phrase -- all deliberate. All strategic. All chosen.

This sounds obvious. It isn't. Most students read text as information delivery: "The author is talking about climate change." An AP Lang student reads text as strategic performance: "The author OPENS with a personal anecdote about flooding BEFORE presenting data, because she wants the audience to feel the stakes before they evaluate the evidence."

The shift from "the author talks about X" to "the author CHOSE to do X for a reason" is the single most important cognitive move in AP Lang.

## Why This Model Matters for the Exam

Every AP Lang MCQ is, at its core, asking: **Why did the writer make this choice?** The question stems disguise this in different ways:

- "The author's use of the extended metaphor primarily serves to..." = Why did the author CHOOSE this metaphor?
- "The shift in tone in paragraph 4 has the effect of..." = Why did the author CHOOSE to shift tone here?
- "Which of the following best describes the function of the third paragraph?" = Why did the author CHOOSE to put this paragraph here?

If you don't believe the author made deliberate choices, these questions feel like guessing. If you do, they become solvable.

## Lesson: The Choice Audit

Read the following passage opening:

> *My grandmother kept a garden that no one else could see the point of. It was too far from the house to be convenient, too shaded to grow tomatoes reliably, and too small to feed anyone. She walked to it every morning anyway, her boots leaving the same ruts in the same mud, as if repetition were itself a form of argument.*

Now perform a **Choice Audit**. For every element, ask: *The author could have done something else. Why this?*

| Element | What the Author Chose | What They Could Have Done Instead | Why This Choice? |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------------------|-----------------|
| Subject | Grandmother's garden | Any family memory, any setting | Gardens = growth, patience, quiet persistence. Sets up a metaphor for the essay's argument. |
| Detail: "too far," "too shaded," "too small" | Three inadequacies in parallel | One inadequacy, or a positive description | The triple structure creates a rhythm of dismissal. The reader is meant to feel the garden's apparent pointlessness -- so the reversal ("She walked to it every morning anyway") lands harder. |
| Detail: "as if repetition were itself a form of argument" | An explicit claim embedded in a description | Could have left it as pure narrative | The author tips her hand: this essay will argue that persistence IS meaningful. The thesis is smuggled into the imagery. |
| Word: "ruts" | A word suggesting wear, habit, erosion | "tracks," "path," "footprints" | "Ruts" carries ambiguity -- it suggests both dedication and stuckness. The author wants the reader to hold both meanings. |

**The point:** Once you see every element as a choice, you can't unsee it. The passage stops being "about a grandmother's garden" and starts being a carefully constructed argument about persistence.

# MODULE 2: STRUCTURE --> FUNCTION --> EFFECT

## The Mental Model

Module 1 established that everything is a choice. Module 2 gives you a **procedure** for analyzing any choice:

> **STRUCTURE**: What is it? (Name the technique, device, or structural element.)
> **FUNCTION**: What does it do? (How does it serve the argument at this point in the text?)
> **EFFECT**: Why does it matter? (What impact does it have on the audience or the argument's persuasive power?)

This is the engine of AP Lang analysis. It converts "I notice things" into "I can explain why things work."

### The Three-Level Trap

Most students stop at Structure. They write: "The author uses a metaphor." That's identification, not analysis. Some reach Function: "The metaphor compares the economy to a sinking ship." That's paraphrase, not analysis.

AP Lang rewards Effect: "The metaphor frames economic decline as an emergency requiring immediate collective action, positioning the audience as passengers who must act NOW rather than passengers who can wait for port -- which serves the author's purpose of urging policy change before the next fiscal quarter."

The exam tests all three levels, but the points live in Function and Effect.

### How S-F-E Maps to the Exam

| Exam Skill Area | STRUCTURE Question | FUNCTION Question | EFFECT Question |
|----------------|-------------------|------------------|----------------|
| **Reasoning & Organization (Skills 5/6)** | "The author includes a counterargument in paragraph 4" | "The counterargument primarily serves to..." | "The concession strengthens the author's position by..." |
| **Claims & Evidence (Skills 3/4)** | "The author cites a government study" | "The study functions as evidence for the claim that..." | "Using institutional data rather than anecdotes persuades this audience because..." |
| **Style (Skills 7/8)** | "The author uses short, declarative sentences" | "The syntax creates a sense of urgency" | "The urgency reinforces the author's argument that delay is dangerous, making the audience feel the cost of inaction" |

## Lesson: The S-F-E Chain

Every analytical observation should be expressible as a chain:

> **[Element]** (Structure) **serves to** [role in the argument] (Function) **which** [impact on audience/persuasion] (Effect).

Examples:

> **The parallel structure in the opening three sentences** (S) **serves to establish a rhythm of accumulating evidence** (F) **which makes the audience feel the weight of the problem before the author proposes a solution** (E).

> **The shift from third person to second person in paragraph 5** (S) **serves to move from general observation to direct address** (F) **which implicates the reader personally in the issue, making it harder to remain a detached observer** (E).

> **The author's concession that "critics raise valid concerns"** (S) **serves to acknowledge opposing views before the rebuttal** (F) **which signals intellectual honesty and makes the subsequent dismissal of those concerns more credible to a skeptical audience** (E).

### The One-Level Test

After writing any analytical statement, count your levels. If you only have Structure, you've identified but not analyzed. If you have Structure + Function, you've explained but not evaluated. If you have all three, you've done what the AP exam rewards.

# MODULE 3: The Rhetorical Situation as a Force Field

## The Mental Model

Most students learn the rhetorical situation as a checklist: identify the speaker, occasion, audience, purpose, subject. Check the boxes, move on.

This is nearly useless for the exam. Knowing that "the speaker is a senator" doesn't help you answer a single MCQ.

The mental model that works: **The rhetorical situation is a set of forces that constrain and enable the writer's choices.** Every choice the author makes (Module 1) and every S-F-E chain you construct (Module 2) exists WITHIN a force field of pressures:

- **The audience** determines what evidence will persuade, what tone is appropriate, what can be assumed vs. what must be proven, and what objections must be addressed.
- **The exigence** (the urgent problem or gap that prompted the text) determines what the writer needs to accomplish -- not just "persuade" in the abstract, but persuade THIS audience about THIS issue at THIS moment.
- **The writer's position** (credentials, relationship to the topic, relationship to the audience) determines what moves are available. A doctor writing about healthcare can assert; a patient writing about healthcare must narrate.
- **The context** (historical moment, publication venue, cultural assumptions) determines what goes without saying and what must be made explicit.

These forces don't just exist as background. They EXPLAIN the choices. When you're stuck on an MCQ asking "why did the author do X?" -- the answer is almost always: "because the rhetorical situation demanded it."

### The Key Shift: From "Who" to "Why It Matters"

| Checklist Thinking (Useless) | Force Field Thinking (Useful) |
|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| "The audience is parents" | "The audience is parents who already believe education matters but doubt THIS reform -- so the author can skip the 'education is important' argument and go directly to addressing skepticism about implementation" |
| "The purpose is to persuade" | "The purpose is to move an audience from passive agreement to active support -- which is why the author ends with a specific call to action rather than a general conclusion" |
| "The exigence is a new policy" | "The exigence is a policy that passed last week over public objection -- the author writes from a position of having LOST the legislative fight, which explains the shift in strategy from 'stop the policy' to 'hold representatives accountable'" |

## Lesson: The Constraint Test

For any passage, ask: **What couldn't the author do?**

This question forces you out of checklist mode and into force-field mode. Examples:

- The author couldn't use technical jargon because the audience is general readers. (Audience constraint)
- The author couldn't ignore the counterargument because it was published in the same newspaper last week. (Context constraint)
- The author couldn't appeal to personal experience because she's writing as a policy analyst, not a patient. (Writer position constraint)
- The author couldn't wait to publish this next month because the vote is Friday. (Exigence constraint)

Every constraint EXPLAINS a choice. "The author uses accessible language" isn't just a style observation -- it's a strategic response to an audience constraint. S-F-E gets sharper when you know the forces behind the choices.

### Exercise 3.3: The Exigence Question

# MODULE 4: The Claim Hierarchy

## The Mental Model

Students see arguments as flat: "The author thinks X." Strong AP Lang readers see arguments as **nested structures** with distinct levels:

```
THESIS (the overarching position the entire text defends)
  |
  +-- CLAIM 1 (a supporting assertion -- one reason the thesis is true)
  |     |
  |     +-- EVIDENCE (specific support: data, anecdote, example, quotation)
  |     |
  |     +-- COMMENTARY (the writer's interpretation connecting evidence to claim)
  |
  +-- CLAIM 2 (another supporting assertion)
  |     |
  |     +-- EVIDENCE
  |     +-- COMMENTARY
  |
  +-- COUNTERCLAIM (an opposing assertion the writer addresses)
  |     |
  |     +-- CONCESSION (what the writer grants)
  |     +-- REBUTTAL (why the thesis still holds)
  |
  +-- CLAIM 3
        |
        +-- EVIDENCE
        +-- COMMENTARY
```

### Why the Hierarchy Matters

**For MCQs:** Questions like "Which of the following best describes the relationship between paragraphs 2 and 5?" are asking you to see the hierarchy. Paragraph 2 might state Claim 1; paragraph 5 might provide additional evidence for the SAME claim, or it might introduce Claim 3. You can only answer if you see the nesting.

**For the Synthesis Essay (FRQ 1):** You need to build a claim hierarchy yourself -- thesis, supporting claims, evidence drawn from sources, commentary connecting evidence to claims. The rubric explicitly rewards a "line of reasoning" (claims arranged in logical sequence) and penalizes "evidence without commentary" (evidence not connected to claims).

**For the Rhetorical Analysis Essay (FRQ 2):** You need to identify the ORIGINAL author's claim hierarchy AND build your own claim hierarchy about how it works.

**For the Argument Essay (FRQ 3):** You need to build a claim hierarchy from scratch using your own reasoning and evidence.

### The Most Common Confusion

Students confuse **claims** with **evidence** and **thesis** with **claims**:

| Level | What It Is | Test: Can It Stand Alone? |
|-------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Thesis** | The single overarching position | It's what the whole text exists to prove |
| **Claim** | A reason the thesis is true | It supports the thesis but needs its own support |
| **Evidence** | A specific fact, example, or source | It supports a claim but doesn't argue anything by itself |
| **Commentary** | The writer's explanation of why the evidence matters | It's the connective tissue -- without it, evidence just sits there |

## Lesson: Hierarchy Mapping

Take any passage and build the hierarchy. Start from the top:

1. **Find the thesis.** (Usually in the introduction or early body, but not always. In inductive arguments, it may appear at the end.)
2. **For each body paragraph, identify the claim.** What is this paragraph asserting? Express it as a complete sentence that could be argued against.
3. **Under each claim, identify the evidence.** What specific support does the author provide?
4. **Under each piece of evidence, find the commentary.** Does the author explain WHY this evidence supports the claim? Or is it left implicit?
5. **Identify the counterclaim.** Where does the author address opposition? Is there a concession? A rebuttal?

Draw it as an indented outline or a tree diagram. The visual structure reveals the argument's architecture.

## Guided Practice

### Exercise 4.1: Hierarchy Identification

Then answer: Which claim has the STRONGEST evidence-commentary connection? Which has the WEAKEST? Where does the author rely on the reader to supply the commentary? These gaps are where the argument is most vulnerable -- and where the exam often asks questions.

### Exercise 4.2: Claims vs. Evidence Sorting

### Exercise 4.3: Building Your Own Hierarchy

- 1 thesis
- 3 claims (each one a different REASON the thesis is true)
- 1 piece of evidence per claim
- 1 sentence of commentary per piece of evidence

(This exercise is especially valuable for students who write well but read poorly -- it bridges writing knowledge to reading, the same move used in Jeremy Wang's remediation Module 2.)

## Checkpoint

# MODULE 5: Reading as Reverse Engineering

## The Mental Model

Here is the bridge that connects everything:

> **Reading AP Lang is writing in reverse.** The writer started with a purpose, chose a structure, selected evidence, crafted style, and produced a text. Your job as a reader is to start with the finished text and **recover the decisions that built it.**

Every time you encounter a passage, you are reverse-engineering a set of choices. You're not reading for information. You're reading to reconstruct the writer's decision-making process.

This model is powerful because it makes reading ACTIVE. You're not absorbing -- you're investigating. Every paragraph is an answer to the question: "What problem was the writer solving at this point in the argument?"

### How Reverse Engineering Works

| What the Writer Did (Forward) | What You Do as Reader (Reverse) |
|-------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Identified an exigence | You ask: "What gap or problem prompted this text?" |
| Analyzed the audience | You ask: "Who is this written for, and what do they already believe?" |
| Chose a thesis | You ask: "What is the overarching claim, and where is it stated?" |
| Organized claims in sequence | You ask: "Why this order? What does each paragraph's position accomplish?" |
| Selected specific evidence | You ask: "Why THIS evidence? What alternatives existed and why were they rejected?" |
| Made style choices | You ask: "Why this word, this sentence length, this tone? What would change if the author chose differently?" |

Notice: every question in the right column uses a mental model from a previous module. Reverse engineering is the INTEGRATION model -- it deploys Everything Is a Choice (Module 1), S-F-E (Module 2), the Force Field (Module 3), and the Claim Hierarchy (Module 4) simultaneously.

### The "If I Were Writing This" Technique

When you're stuck on any MCQ, ask: **"If I were writing this essay, why would I have made this move at this point?"**

This converts reading comprehension into writing empathy. You step into the author's decision-making process and use YOUR knowledge of how arguments work to explain THEIR choices.

This technique is especially powerful for:
- Organization questions: "If I were building this argument, where would I put the counterargument? Why here and not earlier?"
- Evidence questions: "If I were trying to convince THIS audience, what kind of evidence would I reach for?"
- Style questions: "If I wanted to create urgency at this moment, what would I do with sentence length?"

## Guided Practice

### Exercise 5.1: Forward-Then-Reverse

**Part A (Forward -- Writing):** You're given a topic, an audience, and a purpose. In 10 minutes, outline an argument: thesis, paragraph functions, evidence types, where you'd concede to opposition, and how you'd conclude. Don't write the essay -- just the architectural plan.

**Part B (Reverse -- Reading):** Now read a published passage on the SAME topic. Map its architecture using the same categories: thesis, paragraph functions, evidence types, concession placement, conclusion strategy.

**Part C (Comparison):** Where did you and the published author make the same choices? Where did you differ? For every difference, explain WHY the author might have chosen differently. (The answer will almost always involve Module 3 -- different rhetorical situations produce different choices.)

### Exercise 5.2: The Decision Reconstruction

Read a passage. For each paragraph, write one sentence answering: **"The writer's problem at this point in the argument was ___, and they solved it by ___."**

Example:
- Paragraph 1: "The writer's problem was that the audience doesn't yet know what issue is at stake. They solved it by opening with a vivid anecdote that makes the problem tangible before naming it abstractly."
- Paragraph 4: "The writer's problem was that a strong counterargument exists and ignoring it would undermine credibility. They solved it by conceding the counterargument's partial validity before explaining why it doesn't defeat the thesis."

This exercise transforms passive reading into active problem-solving. Every paragraph becomes a puzzle: what challenge was the writer facing, and how did they address it?

### Exercise 5.3: MCQ Reverse Engineering

Take 5 MCQs you've gotten wrong on previous practice (from your remediation materials or review packets). For each one:

1. Identify what the question is really asking (which S-F-E level? which part of the hierarchy?)
2. Ask: "If I were writing this passage, why would I have made the choice the question asks about?"
3. Does the "If I Were Writing This" technique lead you to the correct answer?

Track your results. For most students, this technique corrects 60-70% of previously wrong answers.

## Checkpoint

You're ready for Module 6 when:

- [ ] Your first instinct when reading a passage is to reconstruct the writer's decisions, not summarize the content
- [ ] You can use "If I Were Writing This" to answer MCQs you'd otherwise miss
- [ ] You can map a published passage's architecture in under 4 minutes using the Decision Reconstruction format
- [ ] You see reading and writing as the same cognitive activity performed in opposite directions

# MODULE 6: Who Benefits?

## The Mental Model

This is the final lens -- the one that adds critical distance to everything you've built.

For any argument or rhetorical move, ask: **Who benefits from the audience accepting this claim? Whose interests are served by framing the issue this way? What is made visible by this framing -- and what is obscured?**

This model prevents you from taking texts at face value. It activates the skeptical, evaluative posture that distinguishes a 4-5 response from a 3.

### This Is Not Cynicism

"Who Benefits?" doesn't mean "every writer is lying." It means every writer has a perspective, and that perspective shapes which evidence gets included, which counterarguments get addressed, which audiences get centered, and which framings feel natural. Recognizing perspective isn't an accusation -- it's an analytical move.

### How "Who Benefits?" Sharpens Earlier Models

| Previous Model | Without "Who Benefits?" | With "Who Benefits?" |
|---------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| Everything Is a Choice (M1) | "The author chose to open with a statistic" | "The author chose to open with a statistic rather than a personal story -- foregrounding data over lived experience, which benefits an argument that wants to appear objective" |
| S-F-E (M2) | "The formal tone creates authority" | "The formal tone creates authority, but it also distances the author from the affected community -- a choice that benefits credibility with policymakers at the cost of solidarity with those experiencing the problem" |
| Force Field (M3) | "The audience is policymakers" | "The audience is policymakers, which explains why the human cost is translated into economic terms -- the framing that this audience responds to, even though it reduces people to budget lines" |
| Claim Hierarchy (M4) | "The author concedes that implementation is difficult" | "The author concedes implementation difficulty but frames it as a logistical problem rather than a structural one -- which benefits the argument by making the obstacle seem solvable within existing systems" |

### Where This Model Appears on the Exam

- **Synthesis Essay:** You must evaluate sources, not just cite them. "Who Benefits?" helps you assess source reliability, identify bias, and explain why you chose certain sources over others.
- **Rhetorical Analysis:** The sophistication point often requires situating the text within a broader context. "Who Benefits?" gives you the vocabulary to do this: "The author's framing benefits X while obscuring Y, a choice that reflects the constraints of writing for [publication/audience]."
- **Argument Essay:** The sophistication point rewards qualification and nuance. "Who Benefits?" prevents you from making absolute claims and pushes you toward "this is true, but only from this vantage point."

## Guided Practice

### Exercise 6.1: The Framing Analysis

Read a passage. Identify the author's central framing -- the metaphor, narrative, or conceptual lens through which they present the issue.

Then answer:
1. What does this framing make visible? (What aspects of the issue does it highlight?)
2. What does this framing obscure? (What aspects does it minimize or ignore?)
3. Who benefits from this framing? (Whose position is strengthened if the audience accepts it?)
4. What alternative framing could have been used? (What would change if the same issue were framed differently?)

### Exercise 6.2: Source Evaluation for Synthesis

Given 6 sources on a single topic (simulate a Synthesis Essay prompt):

For each source, identify:
- The source's perspective (not just "for" or "against" but WHOSE viewpoint it represents)
- Who benefits from the audience accepting this source's framing
- What this source reveals that other sources don't
- What this source obscures that other sources reveal

Then select 3 sources for a synthesis essay and write one sentence explaining WHY you chose each one. Your explanation should go beyond "it supports my thesis" to "it provides the [type of evidence] that my argument needs at [this point in the hierarchy] because my audience would find [this type of support] persuasive."

### Exercise 6.3: The Sophistication Move

Take an essay you've written (from remediation practice or a review packet exercise). Find one claim that could be qualified or complicated using "Who Benefits?" thinking. Rewrite that section to earn the sophistication point.

Before: "Social media has transformed political engagement by giving citizens a direct platform."

After: "Social media has transformed political engagement by giving citizens a direct platform -- though 'citizens' here means those with digital access, literacy, and time, a demographic that skews toward the already-engaged. The transformation is real, but its beneficiaries are not evenly distributed, which complicates any straightforward narrative of democratization."

The second version doesn't abandon the claim. It DEEPENS it by asking who benefits from the framing.

## Checkpoint

You've completed the course when:

- [ ] You automatically ask "Who benefits from this framing?" when reading any argumentative text
- [ ] You can identify what a text's framing makes visible AND what it obscures
- [ ] You evaluate sources by perspective and interest, not just agreement/disagreement
- [ ] Your essays include qualified claims that acknowledge complexity -- the sophistication move
- [ ] You integrate all six mental models fluidly: seeing choices (M1), analyzing them with S-F-E (M2), explaining them through the force field (M3), mapping them in the hierarchy (M4), reverse-engineering them (M5), and evaluating whose interests they serve (M6)

# APPENDIX B: Model Integration -- How They Work Together

On any AP Lang MCQ, you're typically deploying 2-3 models simultaneously. Here's how they combine:

**Example MCQ:** "The author's inclusion of the extended example in paragraph 4 primarily serves to..."

- **Module 1** (Everything Is a Choice): The example is deliberate. The author chose THIS example over others.
- **Module 2** (S-F-E): Structure = extended example; Function = ?; Effect = ?. The question asks for Function.
- **Module 3** (Force Field): Who is the audience? What kind of evidence persuades them? An extended example suggests the audience responds to narrative, not statistics.
- **Module 4** (Claim Hierarchy): Paragraph 4 supports Claim 2 in the argument. The example is Evidence for that claim.
- **Module 5** (Reverse Engineering): If I were writing this argument for this audience, I'd use an extended example here because I need the reader to FEEL the impact, not just know the numbers. The previous paragraph gave data; this one makes it human.
- **Module 6** (Who Benefits?): The extended example centers one person's experience. This framing benefits the argument by making the abstract concrete, but it also risks being dismissed as anecdotal.

Answer: The extended example serves to provide a concrete, humanizing illustration of the data presented in paragraph 3, making the argument's stakes tangible for an audience that might otherwise remain detached from statistical evidence.

That answer integrates multiple models into a single, fluid analytical move. That's what "thinking like an AP Lang student" means.
