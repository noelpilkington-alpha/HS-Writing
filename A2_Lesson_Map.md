# Course A2: G10 Sophisticated Argument, Synthesis & ACT Writing -- Lesson Map (v1)

## Course Summary
- **Track:** A (Core)
- **Grade:** 10
- **Calibration:** CCSS W.9-10 + ACT Writing Standards
- **Hours:** ~10-12
- **Prerequisites:** A1 Gate passed (4/6, >=1 on Commentary) or diagnostic entry
- **Mental Models Introduced:** M3 (Rhetorical Situation as Force Field), M4 (Claim Hierarchy), M5 (preview: "If I Were Writing This")
- **Mental Models Continued:** M1 (Everything Is a Choice), M2 (Structure -> Function -> Effect)
- **Gate:** Synthesis essay (40 min) + ACT Writing essay (40 min) + conventions (20 min), pass = 4/6 each essay + conventions
- **Completion Credential:** College-ready writer + ACT Writing-prepared

## Design Rationale

A1 completers can: take a position, support with evidence + reasoning, close-read with M1, produce S-F-E chains with M2, write analytical essays about nonfiction, self-assess on binary rubric, and revise with the Three-Pass Protocol.

**What they cannot yet do:**
1. Enter an academic conversation -- engage with existing positions (They Say/I Say)
2. Synthesize multiple sources into a unified argument
3. Construct support from knowledge (not just text evidence)
4. Analyze multiple perspectives on an issue (ACT Writing format)
5. Self-assess on a nuanced rubric (3-point scale)

**A2 sequence:**
1. Unit 1: Force Field & Claim Hierarchy (new analytical lenses)
2. Unit 2: Sophisticated Argument -- They Say/I Say moves
3. Unit 3: Synthesis -- integrating multiple sources
4. Unit 4: Calibration & Revision (3-point rubric)
5. Unit 5: Timed Writing Strategy
6. Unit 6: ACT Writing (perspectives, constructed support, timed performance)
7. Gate Assessment (synthesis essay + ACT essay + conventions)

**Key continuity from A1:**
- C.9.B04 (Counterarguments) -> C.10.03-C.10.05 (They Say/I Say) -- same moves, refined
- C.9.08 (Thesis) -> sophisticated thesis with They Say/I Say framing
- C.9.10 (Evidence + Commentary) -> source integration with attribution fluency
- C.9.11-C.9.12 (Binary self-assessment) -> 3-point calibration
- Three-Pass Revision Protocol continues as standard tool

## Unit 1: Force Field & Claim Hierarchy (~1.5 hrs, 3 lessons)

New analytical lenses that deepen M1 and M2. Force Field (M3) reveals WHY authors make choices. Claim Hierarchy (M4) reveals HOW arguments are structured.

### Lesson 01 | Rhetorical Force Field (M3 Introduction)
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.01 (Map Rhetorical Force Field)
- **Mental Model:** M3 introduced
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: M3 -- The rhetorical situation is not a checklist (speaker, audience, purpose). It is a set of FORCES that CONSTRAIN and ENABLE writer choices. Four forces: Audience (what evidence persuades?), Exigence (urgent gap prompting text), Writer's Position (what moves are available?), Context (what goes unsaid?). The question is not "Who is the audience?" but "What does the audience PREVENT the writer from doing?" (3 min)
  - Model: Coping model -- map the Force Field for Frederick Douglass's "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" (SP-001). False start: lists audience/purpose like a checklist. Self-correction: asks "What COULDN'T Douglass do?" -- realizes he can't directly insult his hosts, so he uses irony instead. THAT'S a force field insight. Strong version: completes the constraint map. (4 min)
  - Supported: Map the Force Field for Sojourner Truth's "Ain't I a Woman?" (SP-002). Complete the Force Field Map template for all 4 forces. For each force, answer: What does it constrain? What does it enable? (8 min)
  - Independent: Map the Force Field for Patrick Henry's "Give Me Liberty" (SP-014). No scaffold. Then write 2 sentences explaining how one force SHAPED a specific choice Henry made. (7 min)
  - Transfer: "Compare your A1 Choice Audits (M1) to this Force Field Map (M3). M1 asks WHAT the author chose. M3 asks WHY the situation forced that choice. How does M3 make your analysis deeper?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** SP-001 (Douglass), SP-002 (Sojourner Truth), SP-014 (Patrick Henry)
- **Content Files:** Force Field Map template (new), Coping model for Force Field
- **Notes:** M3 builds directly on M1. Students already identify choices (A1); now they explain the situational forces behind those choices. The Constraint Test ("What COULDN'T the author do?") is the key pedagogical move -- it forces students out of checklist mode.

### Lesson 02 | Claim Hierarchy (M4 Introduction)
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.02 (Build Claim Hierarchy Outline)
- **Mental Model:** M4 introduced
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: M4 -- Arguments are nested structures, not flat assertions. Thesis sits at the top; claims support it; evidence supports claims; commentary interprets evidence. Most students write FLAT arguments ("I think X. Here's some evidence. I think Y. Here's more evidence."). Strong writers build HIERARCHIES where every piece connects upward to the thesis. (3 min)
  - Model: Coping model -- map the Claim Hierarchy of Lincoln's Second Inaugural Address (SP-003). False start: writes a flat list of Lincoln's points. Self-correction: realizes Lincoln's concession ("Both read the same Bible...") is a SUBORDINATE move that strengthens the main claim, not a separate point. Strong version: tree diagram with thesis -> claims -> evidence -> commentary -> counterclaim. (4 min)
  - Supported: Map the Claim Hierarchy of Elizabeth Cady Stanton's "Declaration of Sentiments" (SP-004). Use the Claim Hierarchy Outline template. Identify: thesis, 2 main claims, evidence for each, one counterclaim addressed. (8 min)
  - Independent: Map the Claim Hierarchy of Thomas Paine's "Common Sense" excerpt (ES-009). No scaffold. Then evaluate: which claim has the strongest evidence? Which has the weakest? (7 min)
  - Transfer: "Take your A1 gate essay. Could you map its claim hierarchy? If your essay was flat, how would M4 help you restructure it?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** SP-003 (Lincoln, Second Inaugural), SP-009 (Stanton, Declaration of Sentiments), ES-009 (Paine, Common Sense)
- **Content Files:** Claim Hierarchy Outline template (new), Coping model for Hierarchy
- **Notes:** M4 gives students the vocabulary to SEE argument structure. This is prerequisite for They Say/I Say (Unit 2), synthesis (Unit 3), and ACT perspective analysis (Unit 6). The key insight: arguments have LEVELS, and strong writers make the connections between levels explicit.

### Lesson 03 | Force Field to Choices + Hierarchy to Plan
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.01 (applied), C.10.02 (applied)
- **Mental Model:** M3, M4 applied together
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Quick bridge -- M3 explains WHY choices were made; M4 shows HOW the argument is built. Together they give you complete analytical power: "The author chose X (M1) using S-F-E pattern Y (M2) BECAUSE force Z demanded it (M3), and it fits into the argument hierarchy at level W (M4)." (2 min)
  - Model: Demonstrate combined analysis on a passage from SP-001 (Douglass): pick one authorial choice, trace it through all 4 mental models. (3 min)
  - Supported: Force Field -> Choice Explanation -- pick 3 choices from SP-014 (Henry, "Give Me Liberty"). For each: "Author chose X BECAUSE [force] demanded it." Write as full sentences. (8 min)
  - Independent: Hierarchy to Plan -- given a prompt about Stanton's Declaration of Sentiments (SP-004), build your OWN claim hierarchy outline for an analytical essay. Must include: thesis, 2 claims, evidence for each, one counterargument. (8 min)
  - Transfer: "You just built an essay plan using M4. Compare it to the plans you made in A1 (L05, L13). What's different? What's better?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** SP-001, SP-014, SP-004
- **Content Files:** Combined Analysis Reference (new -- one-page showing how M1-M4 work together)
- **Notes:** Integration lesson. Students see all 4 mental models as a toolkit, not separate skills. The Hierarchy to Plan exercise bridges reading analysis to writing planning -- same skill used in both directions.

## Unit 2: Sophisticated Argument -- They Say/I Say (~2.5 hrs, 4 lessons)

The core A2 skill: entering an academic conversation. Students move from "I think X" to "While others argue Y, I contend Z because..."

### Lesson 04 | They Say/I Say -- Move Identification
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.03 (Agree with a Difference -- preview), C.10.04 (Disagree Diplomatically -- preview), C.10.05 (Complicate the Debate -- preview)
- **Mental Model:** M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: All academic writing is entering a conversation. You must represent what others say BEFORE presenting your own view. Three core moves: (1) Agree with a Difference ("While I agree that..., I want to add..."), (2) Disagree Diplomatically ("X's argument that... overlooks..."), (3) Complicate the Debate ("The debate typically focuses on..., but we should also consider..."). Connect to A1 L04 (counterarguments): "You already know move #2 -- acknowledging the other side. Now we name ALL THREE moves." (3 min)
  - Model: Show 3 published paragraph openings. Identify which move each uses. Map each onto the Claim Hierarchy (M4) -- where does the "They Say" fit in the hierarchy? (3 min)
  - Supported: Move Identification -- read 5 short argument openings. Classify each as Agree with Difference, Disagree Diplomatically, or Complicate. Explain your reasoning for each. (8 min)
  - Independent: Classify 5 more argument openings. Scored. (5 min)
  - Transfer: "Think of a recent debate you've had (with a friend, online, in class). Which move did you use? Which move would have been MORE effective?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** Published argument excerpts (to be generated -- editorial/essay openings that clearly demonstrate each move)
- **Content Files:** They Say/I Say Move Reference (new), 10 argument opening excerpts for classification
- **Notes:** Discrimination before production. Students IDENTIFY the moves before trying to USE them. The connection to A1 L04 is explicit -- they already know the counterargument move; this lesson names the full repertoire.

### Lesson 05 | They Say/I Say -- Template Practice
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.03 (Agree with a Difference), C.10.04 (Disagree Diplomatically), C.10.05 (Complicate the Debate)
- **Mental Model:** M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Templates are not formulas -- they are MOVES. Like a basketball player practices specific footwork patterns, a writer practices specific rhetorical patterns. The templates fade; the moves stay. (2 min)
  - Model: Coping model -- given the position "Schools should ban smartphones," write all 3 moves. False start: straw man ("People who disagree are just addicted to their phones"). Self-correction: "That's not genuine engagement -- I'm caricaturing the other side." Strong version: each move genuinely represents the opposing view before responding. (4 min)
  - Supported: Given 2 "they say" positions on debatable topics, write responses using each of the 3 moves (6 responses total). Templates visible as scaffolds. (10 min)
  - Independent: Given 1 new position, write all 3 moves with NO templates visible. (6 min)
  - Transfer: "Which move felt most natural? Which was hardest? The hard one is the one you need to practice most." (3 min)
- **Passages:** None (student-generated responses)
- **Content Files:** They Say/I Say templates (from Graff & Birkenstein framework), 3 debatable topic positions
- **Notes:** Production lesson. Students write using all 3 moves. The coping model's straw man false start is critical -- students must learn to distinguish genuine engagement from caricature. The templates are scaffolds that fade across Units 2-3.

### Lesson 06 | Straw Man Detection + Nuance Ladder
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.05 (Complicate the Debate), C.10.13 (Revise Flat Argument into Nuanced -- preview)
- **Mental Model:** M3, M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Two skills: (1) Straw Man Detection -- genuine engagement represents the STRONGEST version of the opposing view, not the weakest. If you can't state the other side's argument in a way THEY would accept, you haven't understood it. (2) Nuance Ladder -- most real issues aren't binary. Start with a flat position, revise through levels of nuance. (3 min)
  - Model: Show 2 argument openings side by side -- one genuine engagement, one straw man. "How can you tell the difference?" Then demonstrate the Nuance Ladder: "AI will take all jobs" (flat) -> "AI will displace many routine jobs" (better) -> "AI will displace routine cognitive jobs faster than policy can adapt, requiring proactive retraining" (nuanced). (3 min)
  - Supported: Straw Man Detection -- read 4 argument openings, classify each as genuine engagement or straw man. Fix the straw men. (8 min)
  - Independent: Nuance Ladder -- take 2 flat, binary positions. Revise each through 3 levels of nuance using M3 (audience awareness) and M4 (claim hierarchy). Write the final nuanced version as a full thesis statement. (8 min)
  - Transfer: "Read your L05 'Disagree Diplomatically' response. Did you commit a straw man? If so, revise it now." (3 min)
- **Passages:** None (generated items)
- **Content Files:** 4 straw man detection items, 2 flat position prompts, Nuance Ladder reference
- **Notes:** Straw man detection is discrimination-before-production. Nuance Ladder is the bridge from binary thinking (A1) to qualified, sophisticated argument. Both feed directly into the full argument essay (L07) and ACT perspective analysis (Unit 6).

### Lesson 07 | Full Argument Essay (Gateway: C.10.06)
- **Time:** ~50 min
- **KCs:** C.10.06 (Write Sophisticated Academic Argument) -- GATEWAY KC
- **Mental Model:** M3, M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Quick review -- the sophisticated argument essay uses They Say/I Say structure: (1) Introduce the conversation (They Say), (2) Present your nuanced position (I Say), (3) Build claims with evidence + commentary (Claim Hierarchy), (4) Address the strongest counterargument (not a straw man), (5) Conclude by explaining why this matters. (3 min)
  - Model: Show annotated model essay with each section labeled. Map it onto M4 hierarchy. (3 min)
  - Supported: Plan -- given a debatable prompt with a clearly stated "they say" position, build a Claim Hierarchy outline in 8 min. Must include: They Say, I Say (which move?), 2 claims with evidence, counterargument + response. (8 min)
  - Independent: Write the full essay from your plan. 25 minutes. Timed. (25 min)
  - Self-score: Score on the A2 argument rubric (6-point). Identify weakest row. (5 min)
  - Gateway check: Must score 4/6 with genuine counterargument engagement. (3 min)
- **Passages:** Debatable prompt with stated position (to be generated -- topic with clear "they say" framing)
- **Content Files:** A2 Argument Rubric (6-point), Gateway criteria, Model annotated essay
- **Notes:** First gateway assessment. The prompt includes a stated "they say" position so students MUST engage with it -- they can't default to a flat opinion essay. The rubric checks for genuine engagement (not straw man), nuanced position, and claim hierarchy structure.

## Unit 3: Synthesis Writing (~3 hrs, 5 lessons)

The hardest skill in A2: integrating multiple sources into a unified argument. Students learn to organize by ARGUMENT, not by source.

### Lesson 08 | Source Perspective Evaluation
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.07 (Evaluate Source Perspective)
- **Mental Model:** M3, M6 (preview)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Every source has a perspective -- a point of view shaped by WHO wrote it, WHEN, and WHY. M3 (Force Field) applies to sources too: What forces shaped this source? What does it reveal? What does it HIDE? M6 preview: "Who Benefits?" -- whose interests does this source serve? (3 min)
  - Model: Take a source set on education policy (Source Set A). Evaluate Source 1: identify author's position, what it reveals about the debate, what it obscures or downplays, whose perspective it represents. (3 min)
  - Supported: Evaluate Sources 2-3 from Source Set A using the same framework. Complete a Source Perspective Card for each. (8 min)
  - Independent: Evaluate 2 sources from Source Set B (new topic). No scaffold. Write one sentence per source: "This source represents [perspective] because [evidence], and it obscures [what's missing]." (8 min)
  - Transfer: "If you ONLY read Source 1 on this topic, what would you MISS? Why does synthesis require MULTIPLE sources?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** Source Set A (4 sources on education policy -- to be generated), Source Set B (4 sources on technology/society -- to be generated)
- **Content Files:** Source Perspective Card template, M6 "Who Benefits?" preview reference
- **Notes:** Source evaluation is the foundation of synthesis. Students must understand that sources have PERSPECTIVES before they can meaningfully combine them. The "Who Benefits?" question (M6 preview) plants a seed for Track B.

### Lesson 09 | Serial vs. Synthesis + Synthesis Matrix
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.08 (Source Mapping and Synthesis Matrix)
- **Mental Model:** M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The deadly pattern: "Source A says... Source B says... Source C says..." This is SERIAL SUMMARIZING, not synthesis. Synthesis organizes by THEME or ARGUMENT, not by source. Sources serve YOUR argument; you don't serve them. (2 min)
  - Model: Show two paragraphs on the same topic -- one serial, one synthesized. Highlight: in the synthesized version, multiple sources appear in the SAME sentence or paragraph, organized around a claim. Then demonstrate the Synthesis Matrix: rows = sources, columns = themes, cells = what each source says about each theme. (4 min)
  - Supported: Serial vs. Synthesis Detection -- classify 4 paragraphs as serial or synthesized. For each serial paragraph, identify how to reorganize by theme. (7 min)
  - Independent: Complete a Synthesis Matrix for Source Set A (from L08). 4 sources, 3 themes. Then write one synthesis sentence combining 2 sources around one theme. (8 min)
  - Transfer: "Why is the matrix organized by THEMES (columns), not by SOURCES (rows)? What does this force you to do differently?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** Source Set A (continued from L08)
- **Content Files:** Synthesis Matrix template, 4 serial/synthesis detection items
- **Notes:** The Serial vs. Synthesis detection is the discrimination move. The matrix is the planning tool. The key insight: synthesis requires you to break sources apart and recombine them by theme, not to present them whole.

### Lesson 10 | Attribution Fluency Drill
- **Time:** ~20 min
- **KCs:** C.10.09 (Attribution and Citation Fluency)
- **Mental Model:** --
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: 10+ ways to bring a source into your writing -- not just "According to..." Signal phrases carry ATTITUDE: "argues" (strong claim), "suggests" (tentative), "acknowledges" (concession), "contends" (debate). The signal phrase IS analysis. (2 min)
  - Model: Show the same evidence integrated 5 different ways, each with a different signal phrase. "Notice how each one frames the source differently -- the signal phrase tells the reader how to INTERPRET the evidence." (3 min)
  - Supported: Attribution Pattern Drill -- given one piece of evidence, write 5 different integrations using 5 different signal phrases from the Attribution Reference. (8 min)
  - Independent: Given 3 different pieces of evidence from Source Set A, integrate each using the most appropriate signal phrase. Explain your choice. (6 min)
  - Transfer: "Look at your L07 argument essay. How many different attribution patterns did you use? Most students use 1-2. Aim for 4+ in your next essay." (2 min)
- **Passages:** Source Set A (continued)
- **Content Files:** Attribution Reference (10+ signal phrases categorized by attitude: neutral, supportive, critical, tentative)
- **Notes:** Attribution fluency is a technical skill that makes synthesis possible. Without varied signal phrases, students default to "Source A says..." -- which produces serial summarizing. This is a drill lesson: high reps, concrete skill.

### Lesson 11 | Source Weaving Drill
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.10 (Weave Multiple Sources into Argument)
- **Mental Model:** M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Source weaving = making sources TALK TO EACH OTHER within your argument. Three patterns: (1) Agreement amplification ("Both X and Y confirm that..."), (2) Tension/contrast ("While X argues..., Y's data suggests..."), (3) Synthesis ("X's theory, combined with Y's evidence, reveals that..."). (2 min)
  - Model: Coping model -- write a paragraph integrating 3 sources. False start: serial summary ("Source 1 says... Source 2 says... Source 3 says..."). Self-correction: "I'm organizing by source, not by argument. My CLAIM should come first, then sources support it." Strong version: claim-first paragraph with sources woven in using varied attribution. (4 min)
  - Supported: Source Weaving Drill -- write 2 paragraphs from Source Set A, each integrating 2-3 sources around one claim from your Synthesis Matrix (L09). Scaffold: claim-first structure visible. (10 min)
  - Independent: Write 1 paragraph integrating 3 sources from Source Set B around a claim you generate. No scaffold. (6 min)
  - Transfer: "Read your paragraph aloud. Can you hear the CONVERSATION between sources? Or are they talking past each other?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** Source Set A, Source Set B
- **Content Files:** Source Weaving Patterns reference
- **Notes:** The hardest pre-essay skill. Students must hold multiple sources in mind while writing a paragraph organized by THEIR argument. The coping model's serial-to-synthesis transformation is the key learning moment.

### Lesson 12 | Full Synthesis Essay (Gateway: C.10.11)
- **Time:** ~55 min
- **KCs:** C.10.11 (Write Synthesis Essay) -- GATEWAY KC (gates Track B)
- **Mental Model:** M3, M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The synthesis essay plan: (1) Map source perspectives (Force Field for each), (2) Complete Synthesis Matrix by theme, (3) Build Claim Hierarchy with sources as evidence, (4) Write -- organize by argument, not by source. (2 min)
  - Model: Show annotated model synthesis essay. Highlight: thesis is the writer's OWN position; each body paragraph weaves 2-3 sources around a claim; counterargument uses a source the writer disagrees with. (3 min)
  - Planning: Given Source Set C (4 sources on a fresh topic -- NOT used in prior lessons), complete Synthesis Matrix in 10 min. Build Claim Hierarchy outline in 5 min. (15 min)
  - Independent: Write the full synthesis essay. 25 min timed. Must integrate at least 3 of 4 sources. (25 min)
  - Self-score: Score on the Synthesis Essay rubric (6-point). Identify weakest row. (5 min)
  - Gateway check: 4/6 with >=1 on Source Integration. (3 min)
- **Passages:** Source Set C (4 sources on fresh topic -- to be generated)
- **Content Files:** Synthesis Essay Rubric (6-point), Gateway criteria, Model annotated synthesis essay
- **Notes:** Second gateway. Source Set C must be on a topic NOT used in lessons 8-11 to test genuine synthesis ability. The rubric specifically checks for synthesis (not serial summarizing) and proper attribution. This KC gates Track B -- students who can't synthesize cannot do AP-level work.

## Unit 4: Self-Assessment & Revision (~1 hr, 2 lessons)

Calibration moves from binary (A1: analysis vs. summary) to 3-point (weak/adequate/strong).

### Lesson 13 | 3-Point Rubric Calibration
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.12 (Self-Assess on 3-Point Rubric)
- **Mental Model:** --
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: In A1, you scored paragraphs as "analysis or summary" (binary). Now: 3 levels -- Weak (binary position, minimal evidence, no counterargument), Adequate (clear position, adequate evidence, acknowledges other views), Strong (nuanced position, sophisticated evidence integration, engages opposition thoughtfully). The difference between Adequate and Strong is NUANCE -- the same skill you practiced in Unit 2. (3 min)
  - Model: Score-Then-Compare -- read one sample argument paragraph, score it, then see the model scoring with explanation. (3 min)
  - Supported: Score 3 sample paragraphs (from Calibration Bank -- to be generated). After each, compare to model answer. Track your accuracy. (8 min)
  - Independent: Score 3 more paragraphs. Scored on calibration accuracy (how close your score is to the model). (6 min)
  - Transfer: "Which level is hardest to identify? Most students confuse Adequate and Strong. The key marker: does the writer complicate the debate, or just state a position?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** None (sample paragraphs from Calibration Bank)
- **Content Files:** 3-Point Rubric Reference, 6 sample argument paragraphs with model scoring
- **Notes:** Discrimination before production. Students must SEE quality levels before they can produce or self-assess at those levels. This is the A2 step in the calibration progression: binary (A1) -> 3-point (A2) -> AP rubric (B1) -> self-diagnosis (B2).

### Lesson 14 | Nuance Revision + Self-Score
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.13 (Revise Flat Argument into Nuanced Argument), C.10.12 (applied)
- **Mental Model:** M3, M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Nuance Revision = taking a binary argument and making it sophisticated. Three moves: (1) Qualify the claim ("In most cases..." not "Always..."), (2) Acknowledge complexity ("While X is true in context Y, it fails in context Z"), (3) Reframe the debate ("The real question isn't X vs. Y, but Z"). These are the same They Say/I Say moves, applied to REVISION. (2 min)
  - Model: Take a flat student paragraph and revise it live. Show the before/after with annotations. (3 min)
  - Supported: Take 2 flat, binary argument paragraphs. Revise each into a nuanced version using the 3 moves. (8 min)
  - Independent: Self-Score Own Work -- take your L07 argument essay. Score it on the 3-point rubric. Identify one specific improvement and revise one paragraph to demonstrate that improvement. (8 min)
  - Transfer: "Compare your original L07 paragraph to your revision. What changed? Score the revision. Did the score improve?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** None (student work from L07)
- **Content Files:** Nuance Revision reference, 2 flat paragraphs for revision
- **Notes:** Production lesson that closes the calibration loop. Students don't just SCORE -- they REVISE using the scoring criteria. The self-score-then-revise cycle becomes standard practice.

## Unit 5: Timed Writing Strategy (~30 min, 1 lesson)

### Lesson 15 | Time Allocation + "Good Enough" Plan
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.14 (Timed Writing Strategy)
- **Mental Model:** --
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Three time profiles: (1) 40-min argument essay: 5 min plan, 30 min write, 5 min revise. (2) 55-min synthesis essay: 15 min read/plan, 35 min write, 5 min revise. (3) 40-min ACT essay: 5 min map perspectives/plan, 30 min write, 5 min revise. The key insight: a "good enough" plan written in 5 min beats a perfect plan written in 15 min, because you need WRITING TIME. (3 min)
  - Model: Demonstrate the 5-minute planning protocol: (1) Read prompt (30s), (2) Draft thesis (60s), (3) List 2-3 claims (60s), (4) Note key evidence for each (90s), (5) Choose structure (30s). Show what the plan looks like -- messy but SUFFICIENT. (3 min)
  - Supported: "Good Enough" Plan Drill #1 -- given a prompt, plan in exactly 5 min. Stop. Evaluate: Can you write from this plan? What's missing? What's good enough? (7 min)
  - Independent: "Good Enough" Plan Drill #2 -- new prompt, 5 min. Then write the FIRST PARAGRAPH from your plan in 5 min. (10 min)
  - Transfer: "Your enemy is not a bad plan. Your enemy is running out of time because you over-planned. What's the minimum you need in a plan to start writing?" (2 min)
- **Passages:** None (prompts only)
- **Content Files:** Time Allocation Reference (one-page for all 3 profiles), "Good Enough" Plan criteria
- **Notes:** Strategy lesson. Students practice the PLANNING phase under time pressure. This feeds directly into the ACT essay (Unit 6) and the gate assessment. The "good enough" concept combats perfectionism that causes students to spend 20 minutes planning and only 15 minutes writing.

## Unit 6: ACT Writing (~3 hrs, 4 lessons)

ACT Writing asks students to analyze 3 given perspectives and develop their own. This uses EVERYTHING from A2: Force Field (perspectives have situational logic), Claim Hierarchy (nested argument structure), They Say/I Say (engaging other perspectives), and constructed support (evidence from knowledge, not texts).

### Lesson 16 | ACT Perspective Analysis
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.15 (Analyze ACT Perspectives), C.10.16 (Evaluate Perspective Strengths & Limitations)
- **Mental Model:** M3, M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: ACT Writing gives you an issue and 3 perspectives. Each perspective is a MINI-ARGUMENT with a core claim, an underlying assumption, a value it prioritizes, and a gap it overlooks. Your job: decompose each perspective BEFORE forming your own position. Use M3 -- "Under what conditions is this perspective strongest? Weakest?" (3 min)
  - Model: Given ACT-style prompt (Prompt 1 from ACT Writing Bank), map all 3 perspectives using the Perspective Map template. Then evaluate: "Perspective 1 is strongest when [condition] but fails when [condition]." (4 min)
  - Supported: Perspective Decomposition -- given ACT Prompt 2, complete the Perspective Map for all 3 perspectives. Then write Strength/Limitation sentences for each. (10 min)
  - Independent: Given ACT Prompt 3, decompose all 3 perspectives and evaluate strengths/limitations. No scaffold. (7 min)
  - Transfer: "How is analyzing ACT perspectives similar to evaluating source perspectives (L08)? Both require you to see THROUGH the argument to its assumptions. The Force Field applies to perspectives, not just texts." (3 min)
- **Passages:** ACT Prompts 1-3 (from ACT Writing Bank -- B10 or newly generated)
- **Content Files:** ACT Perspective Map template, ACT Writing format overview
- **Notes:** The bridge from analytical reading (sources) to analytical thinking (perspectives). Students apply M3 (Constraint Test) to ACT perspectives: "Under what conditions is this perspective strongest?" This is the same analytical move they use for source evaluation, now applied to abstract positions.

### Lesson 17 | ACT Argument Development
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.10.17 (Develop & Position Own Perspective), C.10.18 (Generate Reasoning & Examples from Knowledge)
- **Mental Model:** M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Two new skills for ACT: (1) Position Development -- you can agree/extend one perspective, synthesize two, or reframe entirely. Your position must RELATE to the given perspectives (not ignore them). (2) Constructed Support -- ACT has NO source texts. You build evidence from: real-world examples, logical reasoning chains, hypothetical scenarios, and analogies. Not all support is equal -- rank by persuasiveness. (3 min)
  - Model: Given ACT Prompt 1, develop 3 possible positions (agree/extend, synthesize, reframe). Choose strongest. Then generate 4 types of support for one claim. Rank them. (4 min)
  - Supported: Position Development -- given ACT Prompt 2, develop 2 different positions using different strategies. Choose the stronger one and explain why using M4 (which has better claim hierarchy potential?). (8 min)
  - Independent: Constructed Support Drill -- given a claim from your chosen position, generate all 4 types of support (real-world example, logical reasoning, hypothetical scenario, analogy). Rank by persuasiveness. Then write an opposition paragraph: acknowledge the strongest point of an opposing perspective, explain why your position still holds. Uses They Say/I Say moves. (8 min)
  - Transfer: "How is constructed support different from textual evidence (A1)? In ACT, you are the evidence SOURCE. This means your general knowledge matters -- read widely." (3 min)
- **Passages:** ACT Prompts 1-2 (continued)
- **Content Files:** Constructed Support Types reference, Position Development strategies
- **Notes:** The critical difference between ACT Writing and text-based essays: students must BUILD their evidence, not find it in a text. The ranking exercise teaches that "my personal experience" is usually weaker than "historical example + logical reasoning." The opposition paragraph uses They Say/I Say moves from Unit 2.

### Lesson 18 | ACT Planning Sprint + Full ACT Essay #1
- **Time:** ~55 min
- **KCs:** C.10.19 (Plan ACT Essay Under Time Pressure), C.10.20 (Write ACT Essay -- first attempt)
- **Mental Model:** M3, M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The 5-minute ACT planning protocol: (1) Map 3 perspectives (90s), (2) Choose your position (60s), (3) Select 2 examples/reasons (60s), (4) Sketch paragraph sequence (30s). Your plan should fit on a half-page. (2 min)
  - Model: Demonstrate the 5-min protocol on ACT Prompt 1. Show the messy-but-sufficient plan. (3 min)
  - Supported: ACT Planning Sprint #1 -- complete the 5-min protocol on ACT Prompt 4. Evaluate: Is this plan sufficient? (7 min)
  - Independent: Full ACT Essay -- given ACT Prompt 5 (fresh), complete the 5-min plan, then write the full essay in 35 min. Timed. (40 min)
  - Self-score: Score on ACT Writing rubric (4 domains). Identify weakest domain. (5 min)
- **Passages:** ACT Prompts 4-5 (fresh)
- **Content Files:** ACT Essay Planner template, ACT Writing Rubric (4 domains, simplified for gate)
- **Notes:** First full timed ACT essay. The planning sprint gets 2 practice runs before the timed essay. Students who over-plan in the sprint learn their lesson before it costs them on the actual essay. Feedback targets the WEAKEST domain -- students improve fastest by focusing on their biggest gap.

### Lesson 19 | ACT Essay #2 + ACT Self-Assessment
- **Time:** ~55 min
- **KCs:** C.10.20 (Write ACT Essay -- second attempt), C.10.21 (Self-Assess Against ACT Rubric)
- **Mental Model:** M3, M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: ACT Rubric Calibration -- before writing your second essay, learn to SEE quality on the ACT rubric. Same principle as L13 (3-point calibration): discrimination before production. (2 min)
  - ACT Rubric Calibration: Score 2 sample ACT essays on all 4 domains. Compare to official ACT scoring. Discuss: "What separates a 4 from a 6 on Ideas & Analysis?" (10 min)
  - Planning Sprint: 5-min plan on ACT Prompt 6 (fresh). (5 min)
  - Independent: Full ACT Essay #2 -- write in 35 min. Timed. (35 min)
  - Self-score + Comparison: Score your essay on all 4 domains. Compare Essay #1 (L18) to Essay #2. Did your weakest domain improve? (5 min)
- **Passages:** ACT Prompt 6 (fresh), 2 sample ACT essays for calibration (from B12 or newly generated)
- **Content Files:** Sample scored ACT essays, ACT Rubric with scoring explanations
- **Notes:** The calibration-before-production sequence is maintained: students score sample essays THEN write their own. Having two timed essays (L18 + L19) gives students a growth trajectory. The self-comparison between Essay #1 and Essay #2 builds metacognitive awareness.

## Gate Assessment

### Lesson 20 | G10 Gate Assessment
- **Time:** ~100 min (can be split across 2 sessions)
- **KCs:** All A2 KCs assessed
- **Format:** Three-part timed assessment

**Part 1 -- Synthesis Essay (40 min):**
- Given 4 sources on a topic NOT used in any lesson, write an argument synthesizing material from at least 3 sources
- Rubric: Thesis (0-1), Source Integration (0-2), Argument Sophistication (0-2), Conventions (0-1) = 6 points
- Pass: 4/6, must earn >=1 on Source Integration

**Part 2 -- ACT Writing Essay (40 min):**
- Given issue and 3 perspectives NOT used in any lesson, write argument analyzing perspectives and developing own
- Rubric: Ideas & Analysis (0-2), Development & Support (0-2), Organization (0-1), Language Use (0-1) = 6 points
- Pass: 4/6, must earn >=1 on Ideas & Analysis

**Part 3 -- Conventions (20 min):**
- 22 items in SAT/ACT passage-based editing format
- Tests: subject-verb agreement, pronoun-antecedent, modifier placement, parallel structure, comma/colon/semicolon usage, verb tense/mood

**Completion:** Pass BOTH essays (4/6 each) + conventions. Students passing A2 are certified college-ready writers and ACT Writing-prepared. Students who also want AP preparation continue to Track B (B1).

- **Passages:** Source Set D (4 sources -- fresh), ACT Prompt 7 (fresh), 2 conventions passages
- **Content Files:** A2 Gate Synthesis Rubric, A2 Gate ACT Rubric, Conventions items

## QA Lessons Applied from A1

These standards apply to all A2 HTML lesson generation:

1. **Interactive elements:** Every `.check-question` must have `<ol type="A">` + `<details>` with `Answer: [letter]`. Reflection prompts use `.self-check` class (not `.check-question`).
2. **Sort/classify items:** `<details>` must be a direct child of `.sort-item` div. Classification answer (e.g., `Classification: X`) must be inside `<details>`.
3. **Coping models:** Must include all three spans: `.false-start`, `.correction`, `.strong`. No monospace font.
4. **Writing tasks:** Must contain trigger words (`write`, `draft`, `compose`) for engine auto-detection. OR use explicit `.writing-task` div.
5. **Rubric attributes:** Independent/assessment cards need `data-rubric="[rubric_id]"` for AI grading.
6. **Passage attribution:** Every `.passage-box` needs a `<p class="attribution">` element.
7. **Success criteria:** Use `.rubric-box` with `<ul>` (engine converts to interactive checkboxes).
8. **Phase gating:** Phases with interactive elements must have completable activities. Non-interactive phases get enabled buttons by default.
