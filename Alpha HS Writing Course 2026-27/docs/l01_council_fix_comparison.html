Cut: thesis-scoring aside, the argue-vs-explain habit paragraph (no task classifies verbs here), the 'is my reason strong enough' disclaimer. Kept: the one idea, fact/opinion/claim taxonomy, and now a real plain-words definition. 231 -> 123 words.
BEFORE (live)
The one idea An arguable claim takes a SIDE and gives a REASON . A fact or a bare opinion is not a claim. Three kinds of sentence look alike but do different jobs, so keep them apart: FACT : something you can check and nobody argues about ('Some districts have tried a four-day week'). OPINION : a bare preference with nothing behind it ('A four-day week sounds great'). Arguable claim : a sentence that takes a side someone could disagree with AND backs it with a reason ('Schools should switch to a four-day week, because a longer weekend gives students real time to rest'). That last one is the sentence an argument is built on. (Scoring sometimes calls this a thesis , but you do not need that word for today's task.) One habit first: when a task says 'argue' or 'should schools ... ?', it wants a side; 'explain' wants no side. Today's task asks you to argue. One thing this lesson does NOT ask yet: whether your reason is a strong one. Today a reason just has to be present and on-topic; judging a reason as strong or weak is its own skill you will build in later lessons. So if you finish and wonder 'is my reason good enough?', that is the right question at the wrong time: get the side plus a real reason down first.
