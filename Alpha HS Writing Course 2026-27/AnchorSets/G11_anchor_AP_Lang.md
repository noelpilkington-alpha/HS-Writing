### 2. What the exam / released form contains — VERIFIED (CED exam-structure table + exam page)
Total exam = **3 hours 15 minutes**, two sections:
- **Section I — Multiple Choice:** **45 questions, 60 minutes, 45% of the score.** Five sets: ~23–25 Reading questions (analyze nonfiction) + ~20–22 Writing questions ("read like a writer" — evaluate revisions/edits to a draft). Machine-scored.
- **Section II — Free Response:** **3 questions, 2 hours 15 minutes, 55% of the score.** The 2h15m **includes one 15-minute reading period** (used mainly to read the Q1 synthesis source packet). **College Board recommends ~40 minutes per essay.** Each FRQ is scored on the **same 6-point analytic rubric → 6 pts each, 18 pts total across the three.**

The three free-response task structures (own-words summary; stable prompt wording verified in CED):
- **Q1 SYNTHESIS (6 pts).** Students get **six sources** organized around one topic. **Two of the six are visual, including at least one quantitative source** (e.g., a graph or chart); the remaining ~four are text excerpts of **about 500 words each.** Task: write an essay that synthesizes material from **at least three** of the provided sources and develops the writer's own position on the topic. Sources must be clearly cited (Source A/B/… or by parenthetical description) via quotation, paraphrase, or summary. The reading period is designed around this packet.
- **Q2 RHETORICAL ANALYSIS (6 pts).** Students get **one nonfiction prose passage of ~600–800 words** (with a headnote establishing the rhetorical situation — author, occasion, audience). Task: analyze the rhetorical choices the writer makes to develop an argument / achieve a purpose / convey a message. Requires demonstrating understanding of the rhetorical situation. No source packet — one passage only.
- **Q3 ARGUMENT (6 pts).** Students get a **general prompt** — a quotation, statement, or idea plus brief background — and argue their own position on it. **No sources are provided; students supply evidence from their own knowledge, reading, observation, and experience.** Stable wording: "Write an essay that argues your position on [specific subject from the introduction]."

### 3. The official RUBRIC AS APPLIED — VERIFIED verbatim (2025 Scoring Guidelines, Set 1)
**This is the `rc.ap` asset.** ONE analytic rubric applies to all three FRQs, **6 points total = three rows.** Row descriptors below are College Board's published scoring-criteria language (2025 SG Set 1), lightly abridged and cited to that PDF.

**Row A — Thesis (0–1 point).**
- **0:** no defensible thesis; OR the intended thesis only restates the prompt; OR it summarizes the issue with no coherent claim; OR there is a thesis but it does not respond to the prompt.
- **1:** "Responds to the prompt with a thesis that presents a defensible position." (For Q2 rhetorical analysis, the 1-point criterion is: "Responds to the prompt with a defensible thesis that analyzes the writer's rhetorical choices.")
- Scoring notes (all three questions): the thesis may be more than one sentence (in close proximity) and may sit anywhere in the response; for it to be defensible the source(s)/passage must contain at least minimal supporting evidence (student need not cite it to earn the point); the thesis MAY but need not lay out a structuring line of reasoning; and it can earn the point even if the rest of the essay fails to support it. Equivocating / "there are pros and cons" / stating an obvious fact does NOT earn the point.

**Row B — Evidence AND Commentary (0–4 points).** This is the differentiator row. Each point stacks an EVIDENCE requirement with a COMMENTARY requirement.
- **0:** simply restates the thesis, repeats provided information, or references too little source material (for synthesis: fewer than two sources; for the single-passage questions: mostly irrelevant/absent references). Incoherent or off-prompt.
- **1:** EVIDENCE — provides evidence that is **mostly general** (for synthesis: references at least **two** sources). COMMENTARY — **summarizes** the evidence but does not explain how it supports the argument.
- **2:** EVIDENCE — provides **some specific, relevant** evidence (for synthesis: references at least **three** sources). COMMENTARY — explains how **some** evidence relates to the argument, **but no line of reasoning is established, or the line of reasoning is faulty.**
- **3:** EVIDENCE — provides **specific** evidence to support **all claims in a line of reasoning** (for synthesis: specific evidence from at least three sources). COMMENTARY — explains how **some** of the evidence supports a line of reasoning. (For Q2 rhetorical analysis, the 3-point row adds: explains how **at least one rhetorical choice** contributes to the writer's argument / purpose / message.)
- **4:** EVIDENCE — provides specific evidence to support all claims in a line of reasoning. COMMENTARY — **consistently** explains how the evidence supports a line of reasoning. (For Q2, the 4-point row adds: explains how **multiple rhetorical choices** contribute to the writer's argument / purpose / message.)
- Hard rule (verified, all three questions): "Writing that suffers from grammatical and/or mechanical errors that interfere with communication cannot earn the fourth point in this row." (This is the only place conventions gate the score — there is no separate conventions row.)

**Row C — Sophistication (0–1 point).**
- **0:** does not meet the criteria for the point (e.g., sweeping generalizations "In a world where…"/"Since the beginning of time…"; only hinting at counterarguments "Some people say…"; ineffective complex language; for Q2, examining choices in isolation or oversimplifying).
- **1:** "Demonstrates sophistication of thought and/or a complex understanding of the rhetorical situation." Earned (per SG) by, e.g., crafting a nuanced argument that explores tensions/complexities across sources; articulating implications or limitations of an argument by situating it in a broader context; making effective rhetorical choices that strengthen the argument's force throughout; or employing a consistently vivid and persuasive style. For Q2 the routes are worded around explaining the significance/function of the writer's choices and the passage's complexities.
- Scoring note: the point is awarded only when the sophistication is integral to the argument, "not merely a phrase or reference."

**`rc.ap` one-line contract:** Thesis (0–1) + Evidence & Commentary (0–4) + Sophistication (0–1) = **6 points**, applied identically to synthesis, rhetorical analysis, and argument (Row B carries the rhetorical-choice sub-criteria only for Q2). Cite: https://apcentral.collegeboard.org/media/pdf/ap25-sg-english-language-set-1.pdf

### 5. Modeling note (replicate WITHOUT copying)
- **Synthesis stimulus spec to match:** author a **6-source packet** per prompt — **~4 text sources of roughly 480–520 words each + 2 visual sources, at least one of which is quantitative** (graph/chart/table). Frame a single arguable topic with a short subject introduction, and require the writer to synthesize **≥3 sources** into an original position (not summarize the sources). Our Source Cache 480-word floor already aligns with the ~500-word text-source band — good fit; hold visuals to "≥1 quantitative."
- **Rhetorical-analysis stimulus spec to match:** ONE nonfiction prose passage, **~600–800 words**, with a headnote naming author + occasion + audience (the rhetorical situation). The prompt must direct analysis of the writer's *rhetorical choices* toward a stated argument/purpose/message — NOT a summary and NOT a source packet. (Copyright: our passages must be own-words / public-domain, since AP uses copyrighted excerpts we cannot reuse.)
- **Argument prompt spec to match:** a **general, source-free prompt** — a quotation or idea plus brief background — asking the writer to argue a position "on the extent to which [claim] is valid" or "on the value of [X]," drawing on their **own** knowledge and experience. No provided evidence. This is the one FRQ where our stimulus is just a prompt, not a text.
- **Scoring to match (`rc.ap`):** every AP prompt we author must be scorable on the SAME 6-point Thesis(0–1) / Evidence-&-Commentary(0–4) / Sophistication(0–1) rubric, with Q2 carrying the rhetorical-choice sub-criteria in Row B. Weight the rubric as AP does: Row B (evidence + explained line of reasoning) is 4 of 6 points and is the difficulty engine; thesis and sophistication are single binary points; conventions are NOT a separate row — they only gate the 4th Row B point when errors "interfere with communication." Mirror that weighting exactly.
- **Difficulty markers (from the SG decision rules):** the 1→4 climb in Row B is *specific evidence + an explicitly explained line of reasoning composed of multiple supporting claims* (2 = specific-but-no-line-of-reasoning; 3 = line of reasoning with *some* explained; 4 = *consistently* explained). Sophistication rewards nuance / tension / situating in a broader context, and explicitly penalizes "In a world where…" throat-clearing and "some people say" pseudo-counterargument — calibrate our top-band exemplars to these exact signals.
- **Reading-period design:** the 15-minute reading period exists for the synthesis packet — our AP synthesis items should be sized so the packet is readable in ~15 minutes and each essay is draftable in ~40 minutes.
