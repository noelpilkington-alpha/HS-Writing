# Course B1L: G11 AP Language Essay Writing -- Lesson Map (v1)

## Course Summary
- **Track:** B (AP)
- **Grade:** 11
- **Sub-track:** AP Language
- **Calibration:** AP Rubric (Rows A, B, C) -- target 4+
- **Hours:** ~10-12
- **Prerequisites:** A2 Gate passed (4/6 on both synthesis and ACT essays) or diagnostic entry
- **Mental Model Introduced:** M5 (Reading as Reverse Engineering -- "If I Were Writing This")
- **Mental Models Continued:** M1, M2, M3, M4; M6 preview
- **Gate:** Full AP Practice Exam -- Synthesis (55 min) + Rhetorical Analysis (40 min) + Argument (40 min), pass = 4+ on at least 2 of 3 essays
- **Completion Credential:** AP Language exam-ready writer (target score 4+)

## Design Rationale

A2 completers can: write sophisticated arguments with They Say/I Say framing, synthesize multiple sources, analyze perspectives (ACT format), construct support from knowledge, self-assess on 3-point rubric, and manage timed writing.

**What they cannot yet do:**
1. Analyze HOW a writer's rhetorical strategies serve a specific purpose (rhetorical analysis)
2. Write under AP rubric constraints (Row A thesis, Row B evidence + commentary, Row C sophistication)
3. Read AP-level passages as reverse engineering problems (M5)
4. Distinguish feature-spotting ("The author uses a metaphor") from analysis ("The metaphor functions to...")
5. Handle AP synthesis with 6-7 sources under 55-minute time pressure
6. Self-assess against the AP rubric (Rows A, B, C)

**B1L sequence:**
1. Unit 1: Rhetorical Analysis (~3.5 hrs, 7 lessons) -- M5 introduction, rhetorical strategies, S-F-E at AP level, thesis, body paragraphs, full essay
2. Unit 2: AP Argument Essay (~2.5 hrs, 5 lessons) -- prompt deconstruction, evidence from knowledge, line of reasoning, full essay
3. Unit 3: AP Synthesis Essay (~2.5 hrs, 4 lessons) -- rapid source analysis, strategic source selection, full essay
4. Unit 4: AP Calibration & Revision (~1 hr, 2 lessons) -- AP rubric calibration, weakest-row revision
5. Gate: AP Practice Exam (~2.25 hrs, 2 lessons) -- all 3 FRQs under timed conditions

**Key continuity from A2:**
- C.10.01 (Force Field) -> C.AP.L.01 (AP-level rhetorical situation mapping under time pressure)
- C.10.03-C.10.05 (They Say/I Say) -> AP Argument framing
- C.10.07-C.10.11 (Synthesis) -> C.AP.L.10-C.AP.L.12 (AP Synthesis with 6-7 sources)
- C.10.12 (3-point calibration) -> C.AP.L.13 (AP rubric calibration, Rows A/B/C)
- C.10.14 (Timed strategy) -> C.AP.L.15 (AP-specific timing)

## Unit 1: Rhetorical Analysis (~3.5 hrs, 7 lessons)

The core AP Lang skill. Students move from identifying rhetorical strategies to explaining HOW those strategies serve the writer's purpose within a specific rhetorical situation.

### Lesson 01 | Reverse Engineering (M5 Introduction)
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** M5 introduced, C.AP.L.01 (preview)
- **Mental Model:** M5 introduced
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: M5 -- Reading as Reverse Engineering. The writer started with a purpose, chose a structure, selected evidence, crafted style, and produced a text. Your job: start with the finished text and RECOVER the decisions that built it. The key question: "If I were writing this, why would I have made this move at this point?" This converts reading comprehension into writing empathy. (3 min)
  - Model: Decision Reconstruction on Frederick Douglass's "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" (SP-001). For 3 paragraphs, demonstrate: "The writer's problem at this point was ___, and they solved it by ___." Show how M5 deploys M1 (choices), M2 (S-F-E), M3 (force field), and M4 (hierarchy) simultaneously. False start: summarizes what Douglass says. Correction: "I'm describing WHAT he says, not WHY he chose to say it this way at this point." Strong version: reconstructs the decision behind each move. (5 min)
  - Supported: Decision Reconstruction on Sojourner Truth's "Ain't I a Woman?" (SP-002). For each of 4 sections, write: "The writer's problem was ___, and they solved it by ___." Template visible. (8 min)
  - Independent: Decision Reconstruction on Ida B. Wells's "Lynch Law in All Its Phases" excerpt (SP-013). No template. Write decision reconstructions for 3 key moments in the passage. (6 min)
  - Transfer: "Compare Decision Reconstruction (M5) to your A2 Force Field maps (M3). M3 identifies the forces. M5 asks what PROBLEMS those forces created and how the writer SOLVED them. M5 is M3 in motion." (3 min)
- **Passages:** SP-001 (Douglass), SP-002 (Sojourner Truth), SP-013 (Ida B. Wells)
- **Content Files:** M5 Decision Reconstruction template, Coping model for Decision Reconstruction
- **Notes:** M5 is the integration model. Students already have M1-M4 from Track A. M5 shows them how to deploy all four simultaneously on AP passages. The "If I Were Writing This" question is the key pedagogical move -- it forces empathy with the writer's choices.

### Lesson 02 | Rhetorical Situation Mapping (Timed)
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.01 (Map Rhetorical Situation for AP Passage)
- **Mental Model:** M3, M5
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP rhetorical situation mapping = Force Field (M3) under time pressure. You have 3 minutes maximum. The AP exam doesn't give you time for a full Force Field map -- you need the ESSENTIAL forces that explain the writer's key choices. Focus on: What is the exigence? Who is the audience, and what do they need/resist? What position does the writer occupy? What constraints does this create? (2 min)
  - Model: Timed (3 min) rhetorical situation map for Patrick Henry's "Give Me Liberty" (SP-008). Show the messy-but-sufficient output. Highlight: "I don't need to map every force -- just the ones that explain the BIG choices." (4 min)
  - Supported: Timed (3 min) rhetorical situation map for Susan B. Anthony's "On Women's Right to Vote" (SP-012). Use the AP Rhetorical Situation template. Self-check: "Did I identify the key constraint that explains Anthony's main strategic choice?" (6 min)
  - Supported: Timed (3 min) rhetorical situation map for Benjamin Franklin's "Speech at the Constitutional Convention" (ES-016). (6 min)
  - Independent: Timed (3 min) rhetorical situation map for Wendell Phillips's "The Philosophy of the Abolition Movement" excerpt (SP-027). No template. (5 min)
  - Transfer: "You now have 3 timed reps. Are you getting faster? What do you map FIRST? Most students find that identifying the exigence first makes everything else fall into place." (2 min)
- **Passages:** SP-008 (Patrick Henry), SP-012 (Susan B. Anthony), ES-016 (Benjamin Franklin), SP-027 (Wendell Phillips)
- **Content Files:** AP Rhetorical Situation template (streamlined for 3-min use)
- **Notes:** Three timed reps build automaticity. The shift from A2 Force Field (thorough, 8 min) to AP mapping (essential, 3 min) is critical. Students must learn to identify the forces that MATTER for the analysis, not map everything.

### Lesson 03 | Identifying Rhetorical Strategies
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.02 (Identify Rhetorical Strategies)
- **Mental Model:** M1
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP rhetorical strategies fall into 5 categories: Appeals (ethos, pathos, logos), Diction (connotation, tone, register), Syntax (parallelism, antithesis, anaphora, rhetorical questions), Figurative Language (metaphor, simile, irony, allusion), Structure (juxtaposition, shift, repetition, contrast). The goal is NOT to name every device -- it's to identify the strategies that MATTER MOST for the writer's purpose. (3 min)
  - Model: Read a passage from Emma Goldman's "The Tragedy of Woman's Emancipation" (SP-017). Identify 5 strategies. Show decision process: "I see parallelism, metaphor, rhetorical questions, formal diction, and a structural shift. Which 2-3 matter MOST for her purpose? The metaphor and the structural shift -- because they carry the argument's central tension." (4 min)
  - Supported: Read an excerpt from Frances Harper's "We Are All Bound Up Together" (SP-024). Identify strategies from each category. Then select the 2-3 that matter most for the writer's purpose. (8 min)
  - Independent: Read an excerpt from William Apess's "An Indian's Looking-Glass for the White Man" (ES-029). Identify and select key strategies. No scaffold. (7 min)
  - Transfer: "The AP exam doesn't reward listing devices. It rewards explaining how a FEW strategies work together. What criteria helped you decide which strategies 'matter most'?" (3 min)
- **Passages:** SP-017 (Emma Goldman), SP-024 (Frances Harper), ES-029 (William Apess)
- **Content Files:** AP Lang Rhetorical Device Reference (from blueprint), Strategy Selection criteria
- **Notes:** Discrimination lesson. Students must identify strategies before analyzing them. The key pedagogical move: selecting which strategies MATTER is itself an analytical act. Students who list 10 devices write weaker essays than students who deeply analyze 3.

### Lesson 04 | Feature-Spotting vs. Analysis
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.03 (Explain How Strategies Serve Purpose)
- **Mental Model:** M2, M3
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The #1 AP Lang mistake: feature-spotting. "The author uses a metaphor" = feature-spotting (names the device). "The author's metaphor of the cage reveals the protagonist's entrapment, suggesting that domestic life has become a prison she cannot name" = analysis (explains function and effect via S-F-E). The AP rubric (Row B) REQUIRES explanation of HOW strategies serve purpose. Naming is not enough. (3 min)
  - Model: Show 3 pairs of sentences about the same passage -- one feature-spotting, one analysis. Highlight the S-F-E structure in each analytical version. Then demonstrate: take a feature-spotting sentence, apply S-F-E, produce analysis. (4 min)
  - Supported: Feature-Spotting vs. Analysis Sort -- classify 5 sentences as feature-spotting or analysis. For each feature-spotting sentence, revise it into analysis using S-F-E. (8 min)
  - Independent: Analytical Sentence Drill -- given 3 strategies identified in an AP passage, write a full S-F-E analytical sentence for each: "[Author]'s use of [strategy] in [moment] (S) serves to [function] (F), thereby [effect on audience within rhetorical situation] (E)." (7 min)
  - Transfer: "Read your A2 L07 or L12 essay. How many of your analytical sentences were feature-spotting? How many reached the Effect level of S-F-E? The gap between those numbers is your growth edge." (3 min)
- **Passages:** Passage excerpts embedded (from SP-013 Ida B. Wells or similar)
- **Content Files:** Feature-Spotting vs. Analysis Sort items (5), Analytical Sentence Drill template
- **Notes:** The critical discrimination-before-production move. Students must SEE the difference between naming a device and analyzing its function before they can write AP-level body paragraphs. The sort exercise is the same type used in A1 L09 (analysis vs. summary) and A2 L09 (serial vs. synthesis).

### Lesson 05 | Rhetorical Analysis Thesis
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.04 (Write Rhetorical Analysis Thesis)
- **Mental Model:** M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The AP rhetorical analysis thesis (Row A) must: (1) Be defensible -- take a position about HOW the writer achieves their purpose, (2) Establish a line of reasoning -- preview the analytical path, (3) Connect strategies to purpose -- not just list strategies, name what they accomplish. Formula (to fade): "[Author] uses [strategy 1] and [strategy 2] to [purpose] by [how -- the line of reasoning]." The line of reasoning is what distinguishes a 1 from a 0 on Row A. (3 min)
  - Model: Show 3 thesis attempts for the same passage. Score each on Row A (0 or 1). Thesis 1: "Douglass uses many rhetorical strategies in his speech." (0 -- no strategies named, no purpose). Thesis 2: "Douglass uses irony and biblical allusion to criticize slavery." (0 -- names strategies and topic but no line of reasoning). Thesis 3: "Douglass deploys irony and biblical allusion to expose the hypocrisy of celebrating freedom while maintaining slavery, building from shared religious values to an indictment his audience cannot dismiss without rejecting their own principles." (1 -- strategies, purpose, and line of reasoning). (4 min)
  - Supported: Write 2 thesis statements for 2 different passages. Use the formula. Score your own on Row A. (8 min)
  - Independent: Write a thesis for a fresh passage with NO formula visible. Then score it on Row A and revise if needed. (7 min)
  - Transfer: "Compare your AP thesis to the theses you wrote in A1 and A2. What's different? The AP thesis must do three things in one sentence: name strategies, state purpose, and preview reasoning. That's the Row A bar." (3 min)
- **Passages:** SP-001 (Douglass, for model), SP-013 (Ida B. Wells, supported), SP-024 (Frances Harper, supported), SP-028 (William Lloyd Garrison, independent)
- **Content Files:** AP Row A scoring guide, Thesis formula reference (to fade)
- **Notes:** Thesis writing is where students first encounter the AP rubric directly. The 3-thesis model exercise (score 0, score 0, score 1) is a powerful discrimination move -- students see exactly what the bar is before writing their own.

### Lesson 06 | Body Paragraph Sprint
- **Time:** ~30 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.05 (Write Rhetorical Analysis Body Paragraph)
- **Mental Model:** M2
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The AP rhetorical analysis body paragraph structure: Claim (what the strategy does) -> Evidence (specific quote or paraphrase) -> S-F-E Analysis (Structure, Function, Effect) -> Connection to Purpose (link back to thesis). Row B scores 1-4 based on how many paragraphs reach genuine analysis. Feature-spotting with evidence = Row B 2. S-F-E analysis = Row B 3-4. (2 min)
  - Model: Coping model -- write one body paragraph about Douglass's use of irony (SP-001). False start: identifies irony and quotes an example but stops at naming ("This shows irony"). Correction: "I named the device and gave evidence, but I didn't explain HOW the irony works or what EFFECT it has. That's Row B 2, not 3." Strong version: full claim -> evidence -> S-F-E -> connection to purpose. (4 min)
  - Supported: Body Paragraph Sprint #1 -- write one rhetorical analysis paragraph in 7 minutes. Passage: Ida B. Wells (SP-013). Strategy: evidence-driven argument. Scaffold: paragraph structure visible. (9 min)
  - Independent: Body Paragraph Sprint #2 -- write one paragraph in 7 minutes. Passage: Carrie Chapman Catt's "The Crisis" (SP-015). No scaffold. (9 min)
  - Self-Score: Score both paragraphs on Row B (0-4). Which reached S-F-E? Which stopped at feature-spotting? (3 min)
  - Transfer: "The 7-minute paragraph is your building block. A full rhetorical analysis essay = thesis + 3 of these + conclusion. If you can write one strong paragraph in 7 minutes, you can write the essay in 40." (3 min)
- **Passages:** SP-001 (Douglass, model), SP-013 (Ida B. Wells, supported), SP-015 (Carrie Chapman Catt, independent)
- **Content Files:** AP Row B scoring guide (simplified), Body paragraph structure reference
- **Notes:** Timed production. The 7-minute constraint forces students to prioritize depth over breadth -- they can't list 5 devices in 7 minutes, but they CAN deeply analyze 1. This is exactly what the AP rubric rewards.

### Lesson 07 | Full Rhetorical Analysis Essay
- **Time:** ~55 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.06 (Write Complete Rhetorical Analysis Essay) -- GATEWAY KC
- **Mental Model:** M5
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Putting it together. The rhetorical analysis essay: (1) Read passage + map rhetorical situation (5 min), (2) Select 2-3 key strategies (2 min), (3) Write thesis with line of reasoning (3 min), (4) Write 2-3 body paragraphs with S-F-E analysis (25 min), (5) Write brief conclusion connecting to larger significance (3 min), (6) Review (2 min). Total: 40 min. (2 min)
  - Model: Show annotated model rhetorical analysis essay (~400 words) with Row A, Row B, and Row C annotations. Highlight thesis (Row A), evidence + S-F-E (Row B), and sophistication move (Row C preview). (3 min)
  - Planning: Given a fresh AP-level passage -- Frances Harper's "We Are All Bound Up Together" (SP-024). Map rhetorical situation (3 min). Select strategies (2 min). Write thesis (3 min). Outline body paragraphs (2 min). (10 min)
  - Independent: Write the full rhetorical analysis essay. 30 minutes timed. (30 min)
  - Self-Score: Score on AP rubric (Row A: 0-1, Row B: 0-4, Row C: 0-1). Identify weakest row. (5 min)
  - Gateway check: Must earn Row A = 1 and Row B >= 2. (2 min)
- **Passages:** SP-024 (Frances Harper, "We Are All Bound Up Together"), model essay passage TBD
- **Content Files:** AP Rhetorical Analysis Rubric (Rows A, B, C), Rhetorical Analysis Planner (from blueprint), Model annotated essay
- **Notes:** First full timed AP essay. SP-024 (Harper) is used here for the full essay; SP-034 (Cooper) is RESERVED exclusively for the L20 gate. The planning phase is scaffolded (10 min) but the writing phase is fully independent (30 min). Row C (Sophistication) is a preview here -- students see it on the rubric but aren't expected to earn it until B2. The gateway threshold (Row A = 1, Row B >= 2) ensures students can write a defensible thesis and provide some analysis before moving to argument.

## Unit 2: AP Argument Essay (~2.5 hrs, 5 lessons)

AP Argument builds directly on A2's sophisticated argument skills. The key new skill: generating evidence from knowledge (not sources) and building a line of reasoning under AP constraints.

### Lesson 08 | AP Argument Prompt Deconstruction
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.07 (Analyze an Argument Prompt)
- **Mental Model:** M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP Argument prompts have a specific structure: a quotation or claim, followed by a task. Your job BEFORE planning: (1) Identify the core claim, (2) Identify the complexity (what makes this debatable?), (3) Map 3 possible positions using M4 (agree, disagree, complicate). This is prompt deconstruction -- understanding the argument space before entering it. (3 min)
  - Model: Deconstruct an AP-style argument prompt. Show: core claim, complexity, 3 positions mapped on M4 hierarchy. "Notice: the strongest position is usually #3 (complicate) because it demonstrates the nuanced thinking Row C rewards." (4 min)
  - Supported: Deconstruct 2 AP-style argument prompts. For each: identify claim, complexity, map 3 positions. (8 min)
  - Independent: Deconstruct 1 fresh prompt. Map positions. Choose your strongest position and explain why using M4 (which position has the best hierarchy potential?). (7 min)
  - Transfer: "Compare AP prompt deconstruction to ACT perspective analysis (A2 L16). Both require you to map the argument space before writing. The difference: ACT gives you 3 perspectives; AP gives you one claim and expects you to generate your own perspectives." (3 min)
- **Passages:** 3 AP-style argument prompts (to be generated)
- **Content Files:** Prompt Deconstruction template, 3 AP-style argument prompts with designer notes
- **Notes:** Discrimination before production. Students decompose prompts before writing. The connection to A2 L16 (ACT perspective analysis) is explicit -- same analytical skill, different format.

### Lesson 09 | Evidence from Knowledge + Line of Reasoning
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.08 (Generate Evidence from Knowledge)
- **Mental Model:** M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP Argument has NO source texts. You build ALL evidence from knowledge: history, literature, current events, science, personal experience (weakest). Two skills: (1) Evidence Brainstorm Sprint -- generate 8+ pieces of evidence in 5 minutes, rank by strength using M3 (audience awareness). (2) Line of Reasoning -- thesis + 3 topic sentences that BUILD, not just list. Apply M4: each claim should advance the argument, not repeat it at a different angle. (3 min)
  - Model: Evidence Brainstorm Sprint -- given a prompt about the value of failure, generate 8 pieces of evidence in 5 minutes. Rank: historical example (strongest) > scientific research > literary reference > logical reasoning > personal anecdote (weakest for AP). Then build a line of reasoning: thesis + 3 topic sentences where each builds on the previous. (5 min)
  - Supported: Evidence Brainstorm Sprint -- fresh prompt, 5 minutes, generate 8+ pieces. Rank by strength. Then write thesis + 3 topic sentences (line of reasoning). (10 min)
  - Independent: Same drill, new prompt. No scaffold. Generate evidence, rank, build line of reasoning. (6 min)
  - Transfer: "Your evidence bank is your general knowledge. The wider you read -- history, science, literature, current events -- the stronger your AP Argument essays. Start a mental evidence bank now." (2 min)
- **Passages:** None (prompts only)
- **Content Files:** Evidence strength ranking reference (from A2 Constructed Support Types, adapted for AP), Line of Reasoning template
- **Notes:** Builds directly on A2 L17 (ACT Argument Development). The evidence ranking from A2 is refined for AP: historical/literary examples are stronger than personal anecdotes because the AP audience (readers) values breadth of knowledge. The line of reasoning concept is the M4 hierarchy applied to essay planning.

### Lesson 10 | AP Argument Body Paragraphs
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.08 (applied), C.AP.L.09 (preview)
- **Mental Model:** M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP Argument body paragraph structure: Claim (topic sentence that advances line of reasoning) -> Evidence (specific example from knowledge) -> Commentary (explain HOW the evidence supports the claim -- don't just drop it) -> Connection (link to thesis and to the NEXT paragraph's claim). The key difference from rhetorical analysis: you're building YOUR argument, not analyzing someone else's. (2 min)
  - Model: Coping model -- write one argument body paragraph. False start: makes claim, gives vague evidence ("Throughout history, many people have failed and succeeded"). Correction: "Vague evidence isn't evidence. I need a SPECIFIC example with details." Strong version: specific historical example with detailed commentary connecting to thesis. (4 min)
  - Supported: Write 2 body paragraphs for a prompt. First paragraph: use your strongest evidence. Second paragraph: address a counterargument (They Say/I Say from A2). (10 min)
  - Independent: Write 1 body paragraph + 1 counterargument paragraph for a new prompt in 12 minutes total. Timed. (12 min)
  - Transfer: "The counterargument paragraph is where you earn sophistication points. A2 taught you They Say/I Say. AP rewards you for using it." (2 min)
- **Passages:** None (student-generated arguments)
- **Content Files:** AP Argument body paragraph structure, Counterargument paragraph template (fading from A2)
- **Notes:** Production lesson bridging from evidence generation (L09) to full essay (L11). The counterargument paragraph explicitly connects to A2 L04-L06 (They Say/I Say). Students already know the moves; now they deploy them under AP constraints.

### Lesson 11 | Full AP Argument Essay
- **Time:** ~55 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.09 (Write AP Argument Essay) -- GATEWAY KC
- **Mental Model:** M4, M5
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP Argument essay timing: (1) Deconstruct prompt + map positions (3 min), (2) Brainstorm evidence + rank (4 min), (3) Write thesis + line of reasoning (3 min), (4) Write 3 body paragraphs including counterargument (25 min), (5) Conclusion (3 min), (6) Review (2 min). Total: 40 min. (2 min)
  - Model: Show annotated model AP argument essay with Row A, B, C labels. (3 min)
  - Planning: Fresh AP-style argument prompt. Deconstruct, brainstorm evidence, build line of reasoning. (10 min)
  - Independent: Write the full argument essay. 30 minutes timed. (30 min)
  - Self-Score: Score on AP rubric (Rows A, B, C). Identify weakest row. (5 min)
  - Gateway check: Row A = 1 and Row B >= 2. (2 min)
- **Passages:** AP-style argument prompt (to be generated)
- **Content Files:** AP Argument Rubric, Argument Essay Planner (from blueprint), Model annotated essay
- **Notes:** Second full timed AP essay. Students who passed A2's argument gateway should be able to earn Row A = 1 relatively easily. The challenge is Row B: using specific evidence from knowledge (not sources) with genuine commentary.

### Lesson 12 | Sophistication Moves (Row C Preview)
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.09 (deepened), Row C preview
- **Mental Model:** M4, M6 (preview)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Row C (Sophistication) is 0 or 1. It rewards: (1) situating the argument in a broader context, (2) acknowledging tension or complexity within your own position, (3) considering alternative interpretations of evidence, (4) using vivid, persuasive style. You don't bolt sophistication onto a finished essay -- you BUILD it into your line of reasoning from the start. M6 preview: "Who Benefits?" as a sophistication lens -- asking what your framing makes visible and what it obscures. (3 min)
  - Model: Take the model argument essay from L11. Identify where Row C moves already exist (counterargument nuance, broader context). Then show: one paragraph WITHOUT a sophistication move vs. the same paragraph WITH one. The difference: the second paragraph acknowledges a tension the first ignores. (4 min)
  - Supported: Given an argument paragraph, add a sophistication move: (1) broader context, (2) tension acknowledgment, or (3) alternative interpretation of the evidence. Write 2 revised paragraphs with sophistication moves. (8 min)
  - Independent: Return to your L11 essay. Identify your weakest paragraph. Revise it to include a Row C sophistication move. Then score the revised paragraph: does it now demonstrate complexity? (7 min)
  - Transfer: "Row C is the difference between a 5 and a 6. You already know They Say/I Say from A2. Sophistication is They Say/I Say PLUS -- acknowledging that even your own position has limits. That honesty is what earns the point." (3 min)
- **Passages:** Student's own L11 essay, model essay from L11
- **Content Files:** Row C Sophistication Moves reference (4 types with examples), M6 preview handout
- **Notes:** This lesson previews M6 ("Who Benefits?") which becomes central in B2. Here it appears as one of four sophistication strategies. The revision of the student's own L11 essay provides immediate, personal feedback on where sophistication was missing.

## Unit 3: AP Synthesis Essay (~2.5 hrs, 4 lessons)

AP Synthesis builds directly on A2's synthesis skills (L08-L12). The key new challenges: 6-7 sources instead of 4, 55-minute timing (15 reading + 40 writing), and AP rubric expectations for strategic source selection and integration.

### Lesson 13 | Rapid Source Analysis
- **Time:** ~30 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.10 (Rapid Source Analysis)
- **Mental Model:** M3, M5, M6 (preview)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP Synthesis gives you 6-7 sources and 15 minutes to read them. You CANNOT read every source carefully. Strategy: (1) Read the prompt FIRST (1 min), (2) Skim all sources for position and key evidence (8 min), (3) Build a rapid synthesis matrix: source, position (for/against/complicates), key evidence, credibility note (6 min). This is Source Perspective Evaluation (A2 L08) under extreme time pressure. Apply M6: note whose perspective each source represents. (3 min)
  - Model: Timed (15 min) rapid source analysis on SYN-07 (Free Speech on Campuses, 6 sources). Show the messy-but-functional matrix. Highlight: "I didn't read Source E fully -- I got the position and one key statistic. That's enough to decide whether to use it." (5 min)
  - Supported: Timed (15 min) rapid source analysis on SYN-04 (Space Exploration, 6 sources). Build synthesis matrix using template. Self-check: "Can I identify each source's position in one sentence?" (15 min)
  - Independent: Review your SYN-04 matrix. Choose 3-4 sources you would use for an argument. Justify each selection: why these sources and not the others? (5 min)
  - Transfer: "In A2, you had 4 sources and plenty of time. AP gives you 6-7 and 15 minutes. The skill is the SAME -- source perspective evaluation -- but the speed is different. What did you skip? What did you prioritize?" (2 min)
- **Passages:** SYN-07 (Free Speech on Campuses), SYN-04 (Space Exploration)
- **Content Files:** Rapid Synthesis Matrix template (streamlined for 15-min use), Source selection justification guide
- **Notes:** Two full timed reps on real source sets. The key shift from A2: students must triage sources, not read them all equally. M6 preview continues -- noting whose perspective each source represents helps with strategic selection.

### Lesson 14 | Strategic Source Selection + Source Weaving
- **Time:** ~30 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.11 (Strategic Source Selection)
- **Mental Model:** M4, M6 (preview)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP Synthesis requires using at least 3 sources, but strategic writers use 4-5. Selection criteria: (1) Does this source support my thesis? (2) Does it provide a counterargument I can address? (3) Is the evidence specific enough to quote or paraphrase? (4) Does it add a perspective the other sources don't? Apply M4: your sources should BUILD your argument hierarchy, not just list support. (2 min)
  - Model: Using the SYN-07 matrix from L13, select 4 sources for a specific thesis. Show: "Source A supports my claim directly, Source C provides the counterargument I'll concede and rebut, Source E gives me the data point, and Source F adds the broader context for Row C." Then write one source-weaving paragraph integrating 3 of these sources. (5 min)
  - Supported: Source Weaving Drill #1 -- using SYN-07 sources, write a paragraph integrating 3 sources. Sources must SERVE the argument, not be listed serially. Use attribution patterns from A2. (10 min)
  - Supported: Source Weaving Drill #2 -- same source set, different paragraph focus. Integrate 2-3 different sources. (8 min)
  - Independent: Self-assess both paragraphs: (1) Did sources serve the argument or did I write serial summaries? (2) Did I use attribution fluently? (3) Did any source weaving feel forced? (3 min)
  - Transfer: "Compare your source weaving to A2 L11. The AP bar is the same skill -- sources serve the argument -- but with more sources and less time. Which attribution patterns felt most natural?" (2 min)
- **Passages:** SYN-07 (Free Speech on Campuses) sources
- **Content Files:** Source Selection Strategy guide, Attribution pattern reference (from A2, adapted)
- **Notes:** Source weaving is where A2 synthesis skills transfer most directly. The 2-rep drill format mirrors A2 L11. Students who mastered A2 synthesis should find the SKILL familiar; the SPEED is the new challenge.

### Lesson 15 | Attribution Fluency + Full Synthesis Planning
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.11 (deepened), C.AP.L.12 (preview)
- **Mental Model:** M4
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP Synthesis attribution must be fluent, not mechanical. Review the 10+ attribution patterns from A2. New AP-level patterns: (1) Embedding source credentials ("According to a 2019 Pew study..."), (2) Attributing while disagreeing ("While Source B claims..., this overlooks..."), (3) Synthesizing across sources ("Both Source A and Source C acknowledge..., yet they diverge on..."). (2 min)
  - Supported: Attribution Fluency Drill -- using SYN-04 (Space Exploration) sources, write 5 sentences using 5 different attribution patterns. Each sentence must integrate a source smoothly into an argument. (8 min)
  - Supported: Full Synthesis Planning -- given the SYN-06 prompt (Environment & Economic Growth), spend 15 minutes: read sources (using rapid analysis from L13), build matrix, select sources, write thesis, outline 3 body paragraphs with source assignments. This is the full planning sequence for tomorrow's essay. (13 min)
  - Transfer: "You just did the full 15-minute planning sequence. Tomorrow you'll do it for real and then write the essay. What part of the planning felt slowest? That's where you need to speed up." (2 min)
- **Passages:** SYN-04 (Space Exploration) for drill, SYN-06 (Environment & Economic Growth) for planning
- **Content Files:** AP Attribution Patterns reference (expanded from A2), Synthesis Essay Planner
- **Notes:** This lesson serves double duty: deepening attribution fluency AND providing a scaffolded planning session before the timed essay in L16. Students walk into L16 having already seen the source set and built a plan -- this is deliberate scaffolding that will be removed in the gate assessment.

### Lesson 16 | Full AP Synthesis Essay
- **Time:** ~65 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.12 (Write AP Synthesis Essay) -- GATEWAY KC
- **Mental Model:** M4, M5
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP Synthesis essay timing: (1) Read prompt (1 min), (2) Rapid source analysis + matrix (14 min), (3) Thesis + outline with source assignments (5 min), (4) Write introduction + thesis (5 min), (5) Write 3 body paragraphs with source integration (22 min), (6) Conclusion (5 min), (7) Review (3 min). Total: 55 min. Note: You planned this source set yesterday. Today you may revise your plan or start fresh -- the AP exam won't give you a preview, so practice BOTH. (2 min)
  - Model: Show annotated model AP synthesis essay with Row A, B, C labels. Highlight: thesis (Row A), source integration + analysis (Row B), sophistication move (Row C). (3 min)
  - Planning: Students may use their L15 plan or start fresh with SYN-06 (Environment & Economic Growth). Finalize plan. (5 min)
  - Independent: Write the full synthesis essay. 40 minutes timed. (40 min)
  - Self-Score: Score on AP rubric (Rows A, B, C). Count sources used. Identify weakest row. (5 min)
  - Gateway check: Row A = 1 and Row B >= 2. Must use at least 3 sources. (2 min)
- **Passages:** SYN-06 (Environment & Economic Growth)
- **Content Files:** AP Synthesis Rubric, Synthesis Essay Planner, Model annotated synthesis essay
- **Notes:** Third full timed AP essay and the most demanding (55 min total with reading). The L15 planning scaffold means students have SEEN the sources -- on the gate assessment they won't have this advantage. The gateway threshold (Row A = 1, Row B >= 2, 3+ sources) ensures minimum synthesis competence before calibration.

## Unit 4: AP Calibration & Revision (~1 hr, 2 lessons)

Students learn to score essays on the AP rubric and use that scoring to drive targeted revision. This is the self-assessment progression from A2's 3-point rubric to the full AP 6-point rubric (Rows A, B, C).

### Lesson 17 | AP Rubric Calibration
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.13 (Self-Assess Against AP Rubric)
- **Mental Model:** None (calibration)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The AP rubric has 3 rows: Row A (Thesis, 0-1), Row B (Evidence & Commentary, 0-4), Row C (Sophistication, 0-1). Total: 0-6 per essay. You've been self-scoring after each essay. Now: calibrate against official AP scoring. The question: when YOU say "Row B = 3," does the AP exam agree? (2 min)
  - Model: Score a sample AP rhetorical analysis essay on all 3 rows. Show reasoning: "Row A: the thesis names strategies and connects them to purpose -- that's a 1. Row B: two paragraphs reach S-F-E analysis, one stops at feature-spotting -- I'll say 3. Row C: no sophistication move -- that's a 0. Total: 4." Reveal official score. Discuss calibration accuracy. (5 min)
  - Supported: Score a sample AP argument essay on Rows A, B, C. Compare to official score. Track calibration accuracy: were you within 1 point on each row? (7 min)
  - Supported: Score a sample AP synthesis essay on Rows A, B, C. Compare to official score. (7 min)
  - Independent: Return to your OWN essays (L07, L11, L16). Re-score each using what you've learned from calibration. Has your scoring changed? Where were you too generous or too harsh? (4 min)
- **Passages:** 3 pre-scored sample AP essays (rhetorical analysis, argument, synthesis)
- **Content Files:** AP Rubric scoring guide (all 3 FRQ types), 3 sample scored essays with official Row A/B/C scores, Calibration tracking sheet
- **Notes:** Three calibration reps (one per FRQ type) mirrors the A2 calibration structure. The key insight: most students over-score Row B (they think feature-spotting earns a 3) and under-score Row C (they miss sophistication moves). Re-scoring their own essays with calibrated eyes is the payoff.

### Lesson 18 | Weakest Row Revision
- **Time:** ~30 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.14 (Revise Essay Toward Higher Score)
- **Mental Model:** M2
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Targeted revision strategy: (1) Identify your weakest row across all 3 essays, (2) Apply the specific fix for that row. Row A fix: rewrite thesis to include line of reasoning. Row B fix: convert feature-spotting sentences to S-F-E analysis. Row C fix: add a sophistication move (broader context, tension, alternative interpretation, or style). You don't revise the WHOLE essay -- you revise the WEAKEST ROW. (3 min)
  - Model: Take a sample essay scored at Row B = 2. Identify the problem: two paragraphs feature-spot without reaching Effect. Revise one paragraph: add the S-F-E chain. Re-score: Row B is now 3. "One paragraph revision raised the essay from 4 to 5." (4 min)
  - Supported: Choose your weakest essay (L07, L11, or L16). Identify the weakest row. Apply the targeted fix. Revise 1-2 paragraphs or the thesis. (12 min)
  - Independent: Choose a DIFFERENT essay. Identify its weakest row. Revise. This time, also check: did the revision improve one row without weakening another? (8 min)
  - Transfer: "You've now revised 2 of your 3 AP essays. Before the gate assessment, re-read all 3 revised essays. Your weakest row PATTERN tells you what to focus on during the exam." (3 min)
- **Passages:** Student's own essays from L07, L11, L16
- **Content Files:** Weakest Row Fix guide (Row A, B, C specific strategies), Revision checklist
- **Notes:** Two revision reps on the student's own work. The "weakest row pattern" insight is critical: most students have a consistent weakness (usually Row B or Row C) that shows up across all 3 essay types. Identifying this pattern before the gate assessment is the most efficient use of revision time.

## Gate: AP Practice Exam (~2.25 hrs, 2 lessons)

All 3 FRQs under AP timing conditions. Synthesis (55 min) + Rhetorical Analysis (40 min) + Argument (40 min) = 2 hrs 15 min. Split across 2 sessions.

### Lesson 19 | AP Practice Exam -- Synthesis
- **Time:** ~65 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.12 (assessed), C.AP.L.15 (AP Timing Strategy)
- **Mental Model:** All (M1-M5, M6 preview)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: AP timing strategy for the full exam: Synthesis first (55 min) because it requires the most reading time. Rhetorical Analysis second (40 min). Argument last (40 min) because you generate your own evidence and can write even if time is short. Recovery strategy: if you run out of time, write a thesis + 2 body paragraphs. A short essay with analysis beats a long essay with summary. (3 min)
  - Independent: Full AP Synthesis Essay. Source set: SYN-08 (Universal Basic Income). 15 minutes reading + 40 minutes writing. Full AP timing. No scaffolds, no templates. (55 min)
  - Self-Score: Score on Rows A, B, C. Note sources used. (5 min)
  - Transition: "Rest. Review your timing. Did you use all 55 minutes? Did the 15-minute reading feel rushed? Tomorrow: Rhetorical Analysis + Argument back-to-back." (2 min)
- **Passages:** SYN-08 (Universal Basic Income) -- RESERVED gate set, NOT used in instruction
- **Content Files:** AP Synthesis Rubric, AP Timing Strategy reference
- **Notes:** SYN-08 is reserved exclusively for the gate. Students have never seen these sources. This is the authentic AP experience: cold source set, full timing, no scaffolding. The self-score provides immediate feedback before the second session.

### Lesson 20 | AP Practice Exam -- Rhetorical Analysis + Argument
- **Time:** ~90 min
- **KCs:** C.AP.L.06 (assessed), C.AP.L.09 (assessed), C.AP.L.15 (assessed)
- **Mental Model:** All (M1-M5, M6 preview)
- **Phases:**
  - Warm-up: Review timing strategy. Rhetorical Analysis: 5 min reading + planning, 30 min writing, 5 min review. Argument: 5 min planning, 30 min writing, 5 min review. (2 min)
  - Independent: Full AP Rhetorical Analysis Essay. Passage: SP-034 (Anna Julia Cooper, "Womanhood: A Vital Element" excerpt). 40 minutes timed. (40 min)
  - Break: 3 minutes. (3 min)
  - Independent: Full AP Argument Essay. Original AP-style argument prompt (reserved for gate). 40 minutes timed. (40 min)
  - Self-Score: Score both essays on Rows A, B, C. (5 min)
  - Gate check: Combined gate assessment across all 3 essays (L19 synthesis + L20 rhetorical analysis + L20 argument). Pass = 4+ on at least 2 of 3 essays. Identify weakest essay and weakest row pattern. (5 min)
- **Passages:** SP-034 (Anna Julia Cooper) -- RESERVED, Original AP-style argument prompt -- RESERVED
- **Content Files:** AP Rhetorical Analysis Rubric, AP Argument Rubric, Gate Assessment scoring sheet
- **Notes:** Back-to-back timed essays simulate the AP exam's endurance demand. Both passages are reserved -- students have never analyzed Cooper or seen this argument prompt. The gate threshold (4+ on at least 2 of 3) allows one weaker essay while ensuring overall competence. Students who pass are ready for B2's sophistication focus.

## QA Standards

All B1L HTML lessons must follow the same 8 QA rules established for A1 and A2:

1. **Check-question format:** All comprehension checks use `<ol type="A">` for answer choices
2. **Self-check for reflections:** Reflection prompts use `self-check` class, not `check-question`
3. **Passage attribution:** All passages include author, title, date, and source note
4. **Rubric-box format:** All rubric displays use `rubric-box` class with `<ul>` inside
5. **Phase gating:** Lessons gate progress phase-by-phase; students cannot skip ahead
6. **Coping model false starts:** All model phases include a false start with correction (no line-through styling)
7. **Scoring integration:** Gateway lessons (L07, L11, L16) include self-scoring with AP rubric rows
8. **Nav links:** Every lesson links to previous and next lesson with correct filenames
