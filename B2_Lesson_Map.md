# Course B2: G12 AP Mastery & Sophistication -- Lesson Map (v1)

## Course Summary
- **Track:** B (AP)
- **Grade:** 12
- **Calibration:** AP Rubric (Rows A, B, C) -- target 5+
- **Hours:** ~8-10
- **Prerequisites:** B1 Gate passed (4+ on at least 2 of 3 essays) or diagnostic entry
- **Mental Model Introduced:** M6 (Who Benefits?)
- **Mental Models Continued:** M1-M5 (all operating simultaneously and automatically)
- **Gate:** Full AP Practice Exam -- Synthesis (55 min) + Rhetorical Analysis (40 min) + Argument (40 min), pass = 5+ on at least 2 of 3 essays, no essay below 4
- **Completion Credential:** AP exam-ready writer (target score 5+)

## Design Rationale

B1L completers can: write rhetorical analysis, argument, and synthesis essays at AP level (score 4+), self-assess against AP rubric (Rows A, B, C), identify weakest row and revise, manage AP timing across all 3 FRQs.

**What they cannot yet do:**
1. Consistently earn Row C (Sophistication) -- they previewed it in B1L L12 but cannot reliably produce sophistication moves
2. Apply M6 (Who Benefits?) as an analytical lens -- they saw a preview, not a full tool
3. Sustain quality under pressure -- final paragraphs often drop in quality
4. Self-diagnose with 80%+ calibration accuracy
5. Distinguish surface editing from substantive revision
6. Write with a distinctive analytical voice rather than generic academic prose
7. Execute multi-pass revision that transforms IDEAS, not just fixes errors

**B2 sequence:**
1. Unit 1: Who Benefits? & Framing Analysis (~1.5 hrs, 3 lessons) -- M6 full introduction, framing analysis, sophistication move identification
2. Unit 2: Writing Sophistication (~2 hrs, 4 lessons) -- broader context drill, alternative reading drill, tension/complexity drill, sophistication in full essay
3. Unit 3: Timed Performance (~2.5 hrs, 6 lessons) -- 3 speed runs, 2 plan-to-essay bridges, 1 recovery drill
4. Unit 4: Self-Diagnosis & Revision (~1.5 hrs, 4 lessons) -- expert calibration, self-diagnosis, surface vs. substantive, multi-pass revision
5. Unit 5: Voice Development (~1 hr, 3 lessons) -- voice analysis, voice experiment, voice under pressure
6. Gate: AP Practice Exam (~2.25 hrs, 2 lessons) -- all 3 FRQs, target 5+

**Key continuity from B1L:**
- C.AP.L.13 (AP Rubric Calibration) -> C.HS.05 (Self-Diagnose at 80%+ accuracy)
- C.AP.L.14 (Weakest Row Revision) -> C.HS.06 (Multi-Pass Revision -- 4 passes, not just weakest row)
- B1L L12 (Row C Preview) -> Units 1-2 (Row C mastery via M6)
- C.AP.L.15 (AP Timing Strategy) -> C.HS.04 (Sustained Quality -- no drop-off in final paragraphs)

## Unit 1: Who Benefits? & Framing Analysis (~1.5 hrs, 3 lessons)

M6 is the final Mental Model. It transforms every previous model by adding a perspective layer: whose interests are served by this choice, this framing, this argument? M6 is what earns Row C.

### Lesson 01 | Who Benefits? (M6 Introduction)
- **Time:** ~30 min
- **KCs:** M6 introduced, C.HS.01 (preview)
- **Mental Model:** M6 introduced
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: M6 -- Who Benefits? For any argument or rhetorical move, ask: Who benefits from the audience accepting this claim? Whose interests are served by framing the issue this way? What is made visible -- and what is obscured? This is NOT cynicism. Every writer has a perspective. Recognizing perspective is an analytical move, not an accusation. Show the M6 table: how M6 sharpens M1-M5. (4 min)
  - Model: M6 analysis on Frederick Douglass's "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" (SP-001). For 3 rhetorical moves: (1) Who benefits from this framing? (2) What does it make visible? (3) What does it obscure? (4) What alternative framing exists? Coping model false start: "Douglass benefits because he's arguing for abolition." Correction: "That's too simple -- M6 isn't about the writer's personal gain. It's about what the FRAMING reveals and hides. Douglass's framing of the Fourth of July as hypocrisy makes visible the gap between American ideals and practice -- but it obscures the possibility that some audience members already felt that tension. The benefit is to the abolition movement's argument, not to Douglass personally." (6 min)
  - Supported: M6 analysis on Patrick Henry's "Give Me Liberty or Give Me Death" (SP-008). For 2 key moves, complete the M6 protocol: visible/obscured/who benefits/alternative framing. Template visible. (8 min)
  - Independent: M6 analysis on a contemporary editorial excerpt about technology and education. No template. Full M6 protocol for 2 key moves. (8 min)
  - Transfer: "Compare M6 to your B1L analysis. In B1L, you analyzed HOW strategies work. M6 asks WHY those strategies -- and not others -- were chosen. M6 is the lens that asks: what does this analysis NOT see? That question is the sophistication point." (4 min)
- **Passages:** SP-001 (Douglass), SP-008 (Patrick Henry), contemporary editorial excerpt (embedded)
- **Content Files:** M6 protocol template (visible/obscured/who benefits/alternative framing), M6 sharpening table (M1-M5 with and without M6)
- **Notes:** M6 was previewed in B1L L12 and L13. Here it gets full treatment. The coping model's false start ("Douglass benefits") addresses the most common misunderstanding: M6 is about framing effects, not personal motivation.

### Lesson 02 | Framing Analysis
- **Time:** ~30 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.01 (Framing Analysis)
- **Mental Model:** M6
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Framing Analysis is the M6 protocol applied systematically. Every passage FRAMES its subject: what's included, what's excluded, what language is chosen, what audience is centered. The 4-question protocol: (1) What does this framing make visible? (2) What does it obscure? (3) Who benefits from this framing? (4) What alternative framing exists? The alternative framing question is the key to sophistication -- it shows you can think beyond the text's own terms. (3 min)
  - Model: Full framing analysis on Sojourner Truth's "Ain't I a Woman?" (SP-002). Show: Truth frames women's rights through physical labor and motherhood (visible) rather than legal rights or education (obscured). This framing benefits working-class and Black women's claims. Alternative framing: a legal argument would center property rights but exclude lived experience. (5 min)
  - Supported: Framing analysis on Susan B. Anthony's "On Women's Right to Vote" (SP-012). Complete M6 protocol. Then: write 2 sentences that could appear in an essay, using the framing analysis as sophistication. (10 min)
  - Independent: Framing analysis on a passage about economic policy (embedded excerpt). Full M6 protocol + 2 sophistication sentences. No template. (9 min)
  - Transfer: "You now have a systematic way to generate sophistication. The M6 protocol isn't something you do AFTER writing -- it's something you do DURING planning. When you map the rhetorical situation, add one M6 question: 'What is this framing NOT showing me?' That answer becomes your Row C move." (3 min)
- **Passages:** SP-002 (Sojourner Truth), SP-012 (Susan B. Anthony), economic policy excerpt (embedded)
- **Content Files:** Framing Analysis template (4-question protocol), Sophistication sentence starters
- **Notes:** The shift from M6 as a reading tool (L01) to M6 as a writing tool (generating sophistication sentences) is the key pedagogical move. Students must see that analysis generates writing material.

### Lesson 03 | Sophistication Move Identification
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.02 (Identify Sophistication Moves in Published Analysis)
- **Mental Model:** M6
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The AP rubric rewards 4 types of sophistication: (1) Broader context -- connecting to larger historical, cultural, or social forces, (2) Alternative interpretation -- acknowledging a different reading and explaining why yours is stronger, (3) Tension/complexity -- identifying something that resists simple resolution, (4) Vivid style -- consistently clear, precise, and engaging prose. You need ONE of these for Row C. Most students never attempt any. Today: learn to SPOT them before writing them. (3 min)
  - Model: Read 2 essay excerpts side by side -- one scored 5, one scored 6 on the same prompt. Identify the specific sophistication move(s) in the 6 that the 5 lacks. Name the type. (4 min)
  - Supported: Sophistication Move Sort -- 5 essay excerpts. For each: (a) Does it contain a sophistication move? (b) If yes, which type? (c) If no, what type could be added? (10 min)
  - Independent: Read a complete short essay (~300 words) scored 4. Identify where a sophistication move COULD go. Write 2-3 sentences adding a sophistication move of the type you think fits best. (6 min)
  - Transfer: "You can now see sophistication. Next lesson: you'll write it. The key insight: sophistication isn't decorative. It's analytical depth -- the moment where your essay acknowledges that the world is more complex than your thesis alone suggests." (2 min)
- **Passages:** Essay excerpts (5 scored at various levels), complete short essay scored 4
- **Content Files:** Sophistication Move Identification items (5), 4-type reference card, Scored essay pair (5 vs. 6)
- **Notes:** Discrimination before production, exactly as in A1 and B1L. Students identify sophistication moves in others' writing before attempting their own. The 5 vs. 6 comparison is particularly powerful -- it shows exactly what the sophistication point adds.

## Unit 2: Writing Sophistication (~2 hrs, 4 lessons)

Students move from identifying sophistication to producing it. Each lesson targets one of the 4 move types, with the final lesson integrating into a full essay plan.

### Lesson 04 | Broader Context Drill
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.03 (Write Sophistication Moves -- Broader Context)
- **Mental Model:** M6
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The Broader Context move: "Situated within the broader context of [movement/period/debate], [author]'s choice takes on added significance because..." This move connects your analysis to something LARGER than the passage itself. It must feel organic, not tacked on. The test: does the broader context deepen your analysis, or is it just a fact you added? (2 min)
  - Model: Take an analysis paragraph about Ida B. Wells (SP-013). Version A: analysis without broader context. Version B: same analysis + broader context sentence connecting Wells's rhetorical strategy to the post-Reconstruction era's systematic dismantling of Black political power. Show: B doesn't just add a fact -- it explains WHY Wells chose this strategy at THIS moment. (4 min)
  - Supported: Given 2 analysis paragraphs (on different passages), add a broader context sentence to each. Self-check: "Does my context explain WHY, or did I just add historical trivia?" (10 min)
  - Independent: Write a body paragraph about Emma Goldman's "The Tragedy of Woman's Emancipation" (SP-017) that includes an organic broader context move. The context should explain why Goldman's specific rhetorical choices make sense given the early 20th-century suffrage movement's internal divisions. (7 min)
  - Transfer: "Broader context is the most common sophistication move on AP exams. It's also the easiest to do badly -- 'tacked on' context is worse than none. The test: if you removed the context sentence, would your analysis lose explanatory power? If yes, it's organic. If no, cut it." (2 min)
- **Passages:** SP-013 (Ida B. Wells), SP-017 (Emma Goldman), 2 analysis paragraphs (provided)
- **Content Files:** Broader Context sentence frames, Before/After examples (organic vs. tacked on)
- **Notes:** Two supported reps + one independent rep. The "tacked on" vs. "organic" distinction is critical -- many students add historical facts without connecting them to the analysis. The self-check question ("Does my context explain WHY?") is the filter.

### Lesson 05 | Alternative Reading Drill
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.03 (Write Sophistication Moves -- Alternative Reading)
- **Mental Model:** M6
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The Alternative Reading move: "An alternative reading might suggest that... However, [your reading] is more persuasive because..." This move shows you can hold two interpretations simultaneously and argue for yours. It's NOT the same as a counterargument (which is about positions). Alternative reading is about INTERPRETATIONS of the same text. (2 min)
  - Model: Analysis of Frances Harper's "We Are All Bound Up Together" (SP-024). Your reading: Harper uses personal testimony to establish authority. Alternative reading: Harper's personal testimony risks reducing a systemic argument to individual experience. Why your reading is stronger: the personal testimony functions as evidence of systemic patterns, not as an exception. (4 min)
  - Supported: Given 2 analysis claims, write an alternative reading for each + explain why the original is stronger. (10 min)
  - Independent: Write a body paragraph about William Apess's "An Indian's Looking-Glass for the White Man" (ES-029) that includes an alternative reading move. (7 min)
  - Transfer: "Alternative reading shows the AP reader that you thought PAST your first interpretation. Most students write their first reading and stop. The sophistication point rewards the second thought -- the moment where you ask, 'But what if someone read this differently?' and then argue back." (2 min)
- **Passages:** SP-024 (Frances Harper), ES-029 (William Apess), 2 analysis claims (provided)
- **Content Files:** Alternative Reading sentence frames, Model alternative reading examples
- **Notes:** The distinction between counterargument (positions) and alternative reading (interpretations) is important. Students confuse these. A counterargument says "others disagree with your CLAIM." An alternative reading says "the same EVIDENCE could support a different analysis."

### Lesson 06 | Tension/Complexity Drill
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.03 (Write Sophistication Moves -- Tension/Complexity)
- **Mental Model:** M6
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The Tension/Complexity move: "The tension between [X] and [Y] resists simple resolution, instead pointing to..." This is the hardest sophistication type. It means identifying something in the passage that your thesis CANNOT fully explain -- and sitting with that complexity instead of resolving it. This IS the sophistication. Not every passage has a productive tension, but most AP passages do. (2 min)
  - Model: Analysis of Douglass's "Fourth of July" (SP-001). Tension: Douglass addresses the audience as "fellow citizens" while arguing they are complicit in slavery. This tension between inclusion and accusation doesn't resolve -- it's the engine of the speech's power. A resolved version ("Douglass ultimately rejects his audience") loses the complexity. Show: 3 sentences exploring the tension WITHOUT resolving it. (5 min)
  - Supported: Given 2 passages, identify the central tension in each. Write 3 sentences exploring each tension without resolving it. (10 min)
  - Independent: Return to a passage you've analyzed before (SP-013, SP-017, or SP-024). Find a tension you missed in previous analysis. Write 3 sentences exploring it. (6 min)
  - Transfer: "Tension/complexity is the rarest sophistication move on AP exams -- and the most impressive. It shows you understand that great writing often holds contradictions in productive tension. If you can identify and explore one tension per essay, you're writing at the 6 level." (2 min)
- **Passages:** SP-001 (Douglass), 2 AP-level passages, student's choice from prior passages
- **Content Files:** Tension/Complexity sentence frames, Model tension explorations (3 examples)
- **Notes:** This is deliberately the hardest of the 3 drill lessons. Students who can explore tension without resolving it are thinking at the highest AP level. The "sit with complexity" instruction is counterintuitive for students trained to resolve everything.

### Lesson 07 | Sophistication in Full Essay Plan
- **Time:** ~30 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.03 (integrated), C.HS.04 (preview)
- **Mental Model:** M6, all models
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Sophistication isn't bolted on after writing. It's PLANNED from the start. During your 5-minute planning phase, add one step: "Where will my sophistication move go, and what type will it be?" Most effective placement: end of second body paragraph or beginning of third (where the argument deepens). The Sophistication Checklist: before writing, check that your plan includes at least one of the 4 types. (2 min)
  - Model: Full essay plan for a rhetorical analysis prompt. Show the planning process with M6 integrated: map rhetorical situation, select strategies, write thesis, outline body paragraphs, IDENTIFY sophistication move location and type. (4 min)
  - Supported: Write a full essay plan for a fresh rhetorical analysis prompt. Plan must include: thesis, 3 body paragraph claims, specific sophistication move with location and type. Peer self-check: "Is my sophistication organic or tacked on?" (12 min)
  - Independent: Write a full essay plan for an argument prompt. Include sophistication move. Then: write ONLY the paragraph that contains the sophistication move (7 min timed). (10 min)
  - Transfer: "You've now practiced all 4 sophistication types and integrated one into a full plan. From here forward, every essay plan includes a sophistication move. It's not optional -- it's part of your process." (2 min)
- **Passages:** Rhetorical analysis prompt (fresh), argument prompt (fresh)
- **Content Files:** Sophistication Checklist, Essay plan template with M6 integration, Sophistication placement guide
- **Notes:** Transfer bridge lesson. The shift from isolated drills (L04-L06) to integrated planning is the critical move. Writing only the sophistication paragraph (not the full essay) keeps the focus on Row C quality.

## Unit 3: Timed Performance (~2.5 hrs, 6 lessons)

Three full-speed essays, two plan-to-essay bridges, and a recovery drill. The goal: sustain Row C quality under AP timing with no quality drop-off in final paragraphs.

### Lesson 08 | Speed Run 1: Rhetorical Analysis
- **Time:** ~50 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.04 (Sustain Quality Under Time Pressure)
- **Mental Model:** All (M1-M6)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Speed runs are full AP essays at full AP timing. The difference from B1L: you MUST include a sophistication move. Your plan (5 min) must specify: thesis, body paragraph claims, sophistication move type and location. New focus: monitor your final paragraph. Does it drop in quality? If yes, your timing allocation needs adjustment. (2 min)
  - Independent: Full rhetorical analysis essay. Fresh passage: Wendell Phillips's "The Philosophy of the Abolition Movement" (SP-027). 40 minutes timed: 5 min reading/planning + 30 min writing + 5 min review. Plan must include sophistication move. (40 min)
  - Self-Score: Score on Rows A, B, C. Specific focus: (1) Did your sophistication move appear? (2) Did your final paragraph maintain the same quality as your first? (3) Where did quality drop? (5 min)
  - Debrief: "Mark the exact moment in your essay where quality started to drop. For most students, it's the third body paragraph or the conclusion. That's your timing target for Speed Run 2." (3 min)
- **Passages:** SP-027 (Wendell Phillips, "The Philosophy of the Abolition Movement")
- **Content Files:** Speed Run scoring sheet (Rows A, B, C + quality drop-off tracker), Sophistication Checklist
- **Notes:** First full timed essay in B2. SP-027 hasn't been used for a full essay before (only as independent practice in B1L L02). The quality drop-off tracker is new -- students mark WHERE their essay weakened, not just what score they got.

### Lesson 09 | Speed Run 2: Argument
- **Time:** ~50 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.04 (continued)
- **Mental Model:** All (M1-M6)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Argument speed run. Same rules: plan must include sophistication move. New focus: evidence specificity under pressure. When time is short, students default to vague evidence ("Throughout history..."). Counter-strategy: during your 4-minute brainstorm, write 3 SPECIFIC examples with at least one detail each. Specific beats numerous. (2 min)
  - Independent: Full argument essay. Fresh AP-style prompt (about the relationship between knowledge and action). 40 minutes timed: 5 min planning + 30 min writing + 5 min review. (40 min)
  - Self-Score: Rows A, B, C + quality drop-off tracker. Compare drop-off point to Speed Run 1. (5 min)
  - Debrief: "Did your drop-off point move later in the essay? That's progress. Did your evidence stay specific or go vague? Check your third body paragraph." (3 min)
- **Passages:** AP-style argument prompt (fresh, about knowledge and action)
- **Content Files:** Speed Run scoring sheet, Evidence specificity self-check
- **Notes:** The evidence specificity focus addresses a common B1L-level problem: students who can generate sophisticated arguments but default to vague support when time-pressured. The comparison between Speed Runs 1 and 2 makes progress visible.

### Lesson 10 | Speed Run 3: Synthesis
- **Time:** ~65 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.04 (continued)
- **Mental Model:** All (M1-M6)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Synthesis speed run. This is the hardest: 6+ sources in 15 minutes, then 40 minutes writing. Sophistication in synthesis often comes from M6: asking whose perspective the source set represents and whose is missing. This is NOT a criticism of the sources -- it's an analytical move that demonstrates you think beyond what's given. (2 min)
  - Independent: Full synthesis essay. Fresh source set: SYN-09 (Artificial Intelligence and Employment, 6 sources). 55 minutes timed: 15 min reading + 40 min writing. (55 min)
  - Self-Score: Rows A, B, C + quality drop-off tracker + source count. Compare drop-off pattern across all 3 speed runs. (5 min)
  - Debrief: "You've now written 3 full AP essays in 3 lessons. Look at your Rows A, B, C across all 3. Which ROW is consistently weakest? Which ESSAY TYPE is weakest? Those two answers define your practice plan for the rest of B2." (3 min)
- **Passages:** SYN-09 (Artificial Intelligence and Employment) -- 6 sources
- **Content Files:** Speed Run scoring sheet, Cross-essay comparison tracker
- **Notes:** The cross-essay comparison is the key diagnostic move. Students who score 4-4-4 need different intervention than students who score 5-3-5. The pattern across 3 essay types reveals whether the weakness is skill-based (always Row B) or format-based (always synthesis).

### Lesson 11 | Plan-to-Essay Bridge 1
- **Time:** ~45 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.04 (deepened)
- **Mental Model:** All
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Plan-to-Essay Bridge: 8 minutes planning, 32 minutes writing. The plan is your blueprint -- every minute saved in planning costs 2 minutes in writing (because you have to figure out your argument while writing). The 8-minute plan must include: thesis, 3 body claims, evidence for each, sophistication move type/location. If your plan is good, the essay writes itself. (2 min)
  - Model: Show a completed 8-minute plan. Then show the first paragraph of the essay that came from it. Highlight: the thesis came directly from the plan, the first body paragraph's claim was pre-written. "I didn't have to think about WHAT to argue while writing. I only had to think about HOW to argue it." (3 min)
  - Independent: Rhetorical analysis. 8 minutes planning (strict timer) + 32 minutes writing. Passage: Carrie Chapman Catt's "The Crisis" (SP-015). (40 min)
  - Self-Score + Plan Review: Score on Rows A, B, C. Then: compare your plan to your essay. Where did you follow the plan? Where did you deviate? Was the deviation an improvement or a loss of focus? (5 min)
- **Passages:** SP-015 (Carrie Chapman Catt, "The Crisis")
- **Content Files:** 8-minute plan template, Plan-to-essay alignment checklist
- **Notes:** SP-015 was used in B1L L06 for a body paragraph sprint, so students have some familiarity but have never written a full essay on it. The plan-to-essay comparison teaches students to evaluate their own planning effectiveness.

### Lesson 12 | Plan-to-Essay Bridge 2
- **Time:** ~45 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.04 (deepened)
- **Mental Model:** All
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Second plan-to-essay bridge. This time: argument essay. The challenge: your plan must include specific evidence from knowledge (not sources). During the 8-minute plan, brainstorm AND select evidence. Don't just list topics -- write the specific example with one detail. (1 min)
  - Independent: Argument essay. 8 minutes planning + 32 minutes writing. Fresh prompt (about whether tradition serves or hinders progress). (40 min)
  - Self-Score + Plan Review: Rows A, B, C. Plan comparison. Was your evidence as specific in the essay as in the plan? (4 min)
- **Passages:** AP-style argument prompt (about tradition and progress)
- **Content Files:** 8-minute plan template (argument version)
- **Notes:** Shorter lesson -- students don't need the teach/model scaffold again. The evidence specificity check connects to Speed Run 2's focus.

### Lesson 13 | Recovery Drill
- **Time:** ~45 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.04 (applied under extreme constraint)
- **Mental Model:** All
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Recovery scenario: you start an essay 10 minutes late. Maybe you spent too long on the previous FRQ. You have 30 minutes instead of 40. What's the minimum viable essay? (1) Thesis with line of reasoning (Row A): 2 min. (2) Two body paragraphs with S-F-E analysis (Row B): 22 min. (3) Brief conclusion: 3 min. (4) Quick review: 3 min. Total: 30 min. You sacrifice the third body paragraph and the sophistication move. Expected score: Row A = 1, Row B = 2-3, Row C = 0 = Total 3-4. That's passing. A short essay with analysis beats a long essay that runs out of time mid-sentence. (3 min)
  - Model: Show a minimum viable essay (~250 words, 2 body paragraphs). Score it: 4/6. "This isn't your best work, but it's a passing essay written in 30 minutes. Now: what if you can add ONE sophistication sentence in 30 seconds? That's 5/6." (3 min)
  - Independent: Recovery drill. You have 30 minutes (not 40). Rhetorical analysis on William Lloyd Garrison's "To the Public" (SP-028). Write the minimum viable essay. Then, if time remains: add a sophistication move. (30 min)
  - Self-Score: Rows A, B, C. Did you finish? Did you sacrifice the right things? (4 min)
  - Transfer: "Recovery isn't about writing a great essay. It's about writing a COMPLETE essay that earns the points available. The worst outcome on the AP exam isn't a mediocre essay -- it's an unfinished one." (2 min)
- **Passages:** SP-028 (William Lloyd Garrison, "To the Public")
- **Content Files:** Minimum Viable Essay template, Recovery timing guide
- **Notes:** This lesson teaches triage. SP-028 was used in B1L L05 for thesis practice only, so students know the passage somewhat but have never written a full essay on it. The "add sophistication in 30 seconds" move shows students that Row C doesn't require a full paragraph -- sometimes one well-placed sentence suffices.

## Unit 4: Self-Diagnosis & Revision (~1.5 hrs, 4 lessons)

Students develop expert-level self-assessment and learn to revise for meaning transformation, not just error correction.

### Lesson 14 | Expert Calibration
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.05 (Self-Diagnose Against AP Rubric)
- **Mental Model:** None (calibration)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Expert calibration = scoring accuracy of 80%+. In B1L you calibrated against the AP rubric. Now: can you score WITHIN 1 POINT of the official score on EVERY row? That's the expert standard. Calibration drift happens -- if you haven't scored sample essays recently, your standards shift. (2 min)
  - Supported: Score 4 sample AP essays (2 rhetorical analysis, 1 argument, 1 synthesis) on all 3 rows. Each is ~300 words. After scoring each, reveal official score. Track accuracy: within 1 point per row? (16 min)
  - Independent: Return to your Speed Run 1 essay (L08). Re-score it with calibrated eyes. Has your score changed from your original self-score? Where were you most inaccurate? (5 min)
  - Transfer: "Calibration accuracy predicts improvement. If you can't accurately diagnose your writing, you can't fix it. From this point, your self-scores should be within 1 point of expert scoring on every row." (2 min)
- **Passages:** 4 sample scored AP essays (~300 words each)
- **Content Files:** 4 calibration essays with official Row A/B/C scores, Calibration accuracy tracker
- **Notes:** Four calibration reps (up from 3 in B1L) plus re-scoring of own work. The 80%+ accuracy target is explicit. Students who score their own work 2+ points off from expert scoring need additional calibration before the gate.

### Lesson 15 | Diagnose Your Own Work
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.05 (applied)
- **Mental Model:** None (metacognition)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Self-diagnosis protocol: (1) Score your essay on Rows A, B, C. (2) Identify the weakest row. (3) Identify the SPECIFIC problem within that row (not "Row B is weak" but "my second paragraph feature-spots without reaching Effect"). (4) Write a 2-sentence diagnosis. (5) Prescribe: what ONE thing would you practice to fix this? This is what AP tutors do -- and now you can do it yourself. (3 min)
  - Supported: Apply the self-diagnosis protocol to your Speed Run 2 essay (L09). Write the 2-sentence diagnosis and prescription. Then: compare your diagnosis to your Speed Run 1 diagnosis. Is the same weakness appearing? (10 min)
  - Independent: Apply the protocol to your Speed Run 3 essay (L10). Write diagnosis and prescription. Then: look across ALL THREE speed runs. Write a 3-sentence "pattern diagnosis" identifying your consistent weakness across essay types. (10 min)
  - Transfer: "Your pattern diagnosis is your practice plan. If Row B is consistently weak, practice S-F-E analysis. If Row C never appears, practice sophistication moves. If quality drops in final paragraphs, practice timing. You now know what to practice -- and that knowledge is more valuable than any single essay." (2 min)
- **Passages:** Student's own Speed Run essays (L08, L09, L10)
- **Content Files:** Self-Diagnosis Protocol template, Pattern Diagnosis template
- **Notes:** This is the metacognitive capstone. Students who can accurately diagnose their own writing across multiple essays can continue improving independently. The "pattern diagnosis" across 3 essays is more reliable than any single essay's score.

### Lesson 16 | Surface vs. Substantive Revision
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** D.11.01 (Spot Surface Editing vs. Substantive Revision)
- **Mental Model:** M1
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Two types of revision: Surface editing = fixing errors (spelling, grammar, word choice, sentence fluency). Substantive revision = changing IDEAS (thesis, evidence selection, argument structure, analytical depth, sophistication). Most students think they're revising when they're just editing. Editing makes a 4 into a cleaner 4. Revision makes a 4 into a 5. (2 min)
  - Model: Show 2 versions of the same essay paragraph. Version A: surface edits (cleaner prose, fixed comma splice, better word choice). Version B: substantive revision (new evidence, deeper S-F-E, added sophistication). "Both are 'revised.' Only B changed the score." (3 min)
  - Supported: Surface vs. Substantive Sort -- classify 5 revision actions as surface editing or substantive revision. For each surface edit, describe what a substantive revision of the same paragraph would look like. (10 min)
  - Independent: Take your weakest Speed Run paragraph. First: make surface edits (2 min). Then: make a substantive revision (5 min). Compare: which version improved the score? (8 min)
  - Transfer: "From now on, when you 'revise,' ask: am I editing or revising? Editing is necessary but insufficient. Revision is what changes scores." (2 min)
- **Passages:** Essay paragraph versions (provided), student's own Speed Run paragraph
- **Content Files:** Surface vs. Substantive Sort items (5), Revision vs. Editing reference card
- **Notes:** Discrimination before production, again. The sort exercise ensures students can SEE the difference before they attempt substantive revision. The timed comparison (2 min editing vs. 5 min revision) shows the asymmetry: substantive revision takes longer but has more impact.

### Lesson 17 | Multi-Pass Revision Protocol
- **Time:** ~30 min
- **KCs:** C.HS.06 (Substantive Multi-Pass Revision), D.11.02 (Revise for Meaning Transformation)
- **Mental Model:** M6
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The 4-pass revision protocol: Pass 1 -- Thesis & Structure (Is my thesis defensible with a line of reasoning? Does each paragraph serve the thesis?). Pass 2 -- Evidence & Analysis (Does every evidence have S-F-E? Am I analyzing or summarizing?). Pass 3 -- Sophistication (Where can I add one M6 move? Is it organic?). Pass 4 -- Style & Concision (Can I cut 10%? Are sentences varied? Does my voice hold?). Each pass has ONE focus. Don't try to fix everything at once. (3 min)
  - Model: Demonstrate Pass 1 on a sample essay: identify a thesis that lacks a line of reasoning. Revise it. Show how the revised thesis reshapes the entire essay structure. Then demonstrate Pass 3: add a sophistication move. (4 min)
  - Supported: Apply the full 4-pass protocol to your strongest Speed Run essay. Time each pass: Pass 1 (3 min), Pass 2 (5 min), Pass 3 (3 min), Pass 4 (3 min). Write a before/after note for each pass. (14 min)
  - Independent: Apply the protocol to a different Speed Run essay. This time, no timing -- focus on quality. (7 min)
  - Transfer: "Score your revised essay. Compare to the original score. If your revision raised the score by 1+ point, the protocol worked. If not, check: did you actually change IDEAS, or did you just edit?" (2 min)
- **Passages:** Student's own Speed Run essays (L08-L10)
- **Content Files:** Multi-Pass Revision Protocol reference, Before/After scoring sheet
- **Notes:** Two revision reps on the student's own work. The timed first rep (14 min total) forces efficiency; the untimed second rep allows depth. The before/after scoring provides concrete evidence of improvement.

## Unit 5: Voice Development (~1 hr, 3 lessons)

Voice is the final skill. Students learn that analytical prose can have personality -- that clear, precise, and engaging writing is itself a form of sophistication (Row C, Type 4: vivid style).

### Lesson 18 | Voice Analysis in Published Criticism
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** D.9.03 (Develop Voice Through Syntactic Choices)
- **Mental Model:** M1, M6
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Voice = the distinctive way a writer sounds on the page. It comes from diction (word choice), syntax (sentence structure), register (formal/informal), and rhythm (sentence length variation). Apply M1: voice is a CHOICE, not an accident. Great analytical writers have voices as distinctive as great novelists. Today: reverse-engineer 3 different analytical voices. (3 min)
  - Model: Read Excerpt A (spare, direct style -- short sentences, active voice, plain diction). Read Excerpt B (elaborate, layered style -- complex sentences, embedded clauses, Latinate vocabulary). Identify the specific syntactic and diction choices that create each voice. "These writers are both analyzing texts. One uses 12-word sentences. The other uses 45-word sentences. Both are effective. Voice is not about 'good' writing -- it's about CHOSEN writing." (5 min)
  - Supported: Read Excerpt C (balanced, rhythmic style -- varies between short and long, uses parallel structure). Identify the voice choices. Then: compare all 3. Which do you find most persuasive? Why? The answer reveals YOUR preferred analytical voice. (8 min)
  - Independent: Read your own Speed Run 1 essay. Describe your current voice in 2 sentences. Is it chosen or default? Identify 2 specific syntactic choices you could make to strengthen your voice. (7 min)
  - Transfer: "Your voice is currently 'default academic.' That's fine for a 4. A 5+ has a voice that the reader notices -- clear, precise, and distinctive. You don't need to sound like anyone else. You need to sound like YOU, on purpose." (2 min)
- **Passages:** 3 published criticism excerpts (embedded, ~150 words each), student's own Speed Run essay
- **Content Files:** Voice Analysis framework (diction, syntax, register, rhythm), 3 published criticism excerpts with voice annotations
- **Notes:** Discrimination lesson for voice. Students identify voice in others' writing before developing their own. The M1 connection (voice as choice) reframes voice from a mysterious talent to a set of identifiable, learnable decisions.

### Lesson 19 | Voice Experiment
- **Time:** ~25 min
- **KCs:** D.9.03 (applied)
- **Mental Model:** M1
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: Voice Experiment: write the SAME analytical paragraph in 2 different voices. This proves that voice is a choice, not fixed. You'll write about the same passage, making the same analytical point, but with different diction, syntax, and rhythm. Then evaluate: which voice is more effective for THIS task? (2 min)
  - Model: Write the same body paragraph about Douglass (SP-001) in Voice A (spare/direct) and Voice B (elaborate/layered). Show: both make the same S-F-E point. Voice A is 60 words. Voice B is 120 words. "Voice A is more effective here because the irony hits harder in short sentences. But for a different passage -- one with complex, layered imagery -- Voice B might work better." (5 min)
  - Supported: Write the same analytical paragraph about Ida B. Wells (SP-013) in 2 voices (your choice of which 2). Evaluate: which is more effective for Wells's rhetorical strategies? Why? (10 min)
  - Independent: Write a body paragraph about a passage of your choice in YOUR chosen voice. Then: read it aloud (silently). Does it sound like you? If it sounds like a template, revise one sentence to sound like a human being. (6 min)
  - Transfer: "You've now proven that voice is controllable. For the gate assessment, write in the voice that's most effective for each passage. You might use a different voice for rhetorical analysis than for argument. That adaptability IS sophistication." (2 min)
- **Passages:** SP-001 (Douglass), SP-013 (Ida B. Wells), student's choice
- **Content Files:** Voice Experiment template, Voice effectiveness evaluation criteria
- **Notes:** The two-voice experiment is the proof that voice is a choice. Students who write the same point in two voices can never again claim that their voice is "just how I write." The "read aloud" test in the independent phase catches template language.

### Lesson 20 | Voice Under Pressure + Gate Prep
- **Time:** ~30 min
- **KCs:** D.9.03 (under pressure), C.HS.04 (integrated), all KCs reviewed
- **Mental Model:** All (M1-M6)
- **Phases:**
  - Teach: The final challenge: maintaining your voice under time pressure. When the clock is ticking, students revert to template prose. "However, this demonstrates..." appears in every stressed paragraph. Counter-strategy: write your first sentence in your OWN voice, not a template. Once the first sentence sounds like you, the rest follows. (2 min)
  - Independent: Timed body paragraph (7 minutes). Passage: Carrie Chapman Catt's "The Crisis" (SP-015). Write one body paragraph with S-F-E analysis, a sophistication move, and YOUR voice. (7 min)
  - Self-Check: Read your paragraph. Circle any template language. Revise ONE sentence to sound more like you. (3 min)
  - Gate Preview: Review the gate format: Synthesis (55 min) + Rhetorical Analysis (40 min) + Argument (40 min) = 2 hrs 15 min. Target: 5+ on at least 2 of 3 essays, no essay below 4. Review your pattern diagnosis (L15): what is your consistent weakness? Build a specific gate strategy: (1) Which essay type will you write first? (2) Where will your sophistication move go in each essay? (3) What is your recovery plan if you fall behind? (10 min)
  - Independent: Write your thesis + first body paragraph for a practice rhetorical analysis prompt (5 min planning + 10 min writing). This is a warm-up for the gate. Focus: voice, sophistication, and your pattern weakness. (15 min -- but only if time remains)
  - Transfer: "You've completed B2's skill development. Tomorrow: the gate. You have all 6 Mental Models. You can write sophistication moves. You can self-diagnose. You have a voice. Now: execute under pressure." (2 min)
- **Passages:** SP-015 (Carrie Chapman Catt), practice rhetorical analysis prompt (for warm-up)
- **Content Files:** Gate strategy planner, Template language checklist
- **Notes:** This lesson serves double duty: final voice practice AND gate preparation. The gate preview + strategy building ensures students enter the gate with a plan. The warm-up paragraph (if time allows) reduces cold-start anxiety.

## Note on Lesson Numbering

B2 has 22 lessons (L01-L22) rather than the standard 20. This reflects the course's ~8-10 hour time budget:
- Units 1-2 (L01-L07): 7 lessons at ~25-30 min = ~3.5 hrs
- Unit 3 (L08-L13): 6 lessons at ~45-65 min = ~5 hrs
- Unit 4 (L14-L17): 4 lessons at ~25-30 min = ~1.75 hrs
- Unit 5 (L18-L20): 3 lessons at ~25-30 min = ~1.3 hrs
- Gate (L21-L22): 2 lessons at ~65-90 min = ~2.6 hrs
- **Total: ~14.15 hrs** -- this exceeds the 8-10 hr budget.

**ADJUSTMENT:** To fit the 8-10 hr budget, compress as follows:
- Merge L11+L12 into one lesson (Plan-to-Essay Bridge, with both rhetoric and argument in one session)
- Merge L14+L15 into one lesson (Expert Calibration + Self-Diagnosis)
- Merge L18+L19 into one lesson (Voice Analysis + Voice Experiment)
- This yields **19 lessons** at ~10 hrs total, within budget.

However, for the HTML generation, we will generate all 22 lessons and let the instructor decide which to merge or skip. The content is modular -- each lesson is self-contained.

## QA Standards

All B2 HTML lessons must follow the same 8 QA rules established for A1, A2, and B1L:

1. **Check-question format:** All comprehension checks use `<ol type="A">` for answer choices
2. **Self-check for reflections:** Reflection prompts use `self-check` class, not `check-question`
3. **Passage attribution:** All passages include author, title, date, and source note
4. **Rubric-box format:** All rubric displays use `rubric-box` class with `<ul>` inside
5. **Phase gating:** Lessons gate progress phase-by-phase; students cannot skip ahead
6. **Coping model false starts:** All model phases include a false start with correction (no line-through styling)
7. **Scoring integration:** Gate lessons (L21, L22) include self-scoring with AP rubric rows
8. **Nav links:** Every lesson links to previous and next lesson with correct filenames
