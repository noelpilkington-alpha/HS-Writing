# Critical Analysis: *Writing for Success* (Saylor Foundation, CC-licensed)

**Purpose:** Determine whether this 613-page introductory college writing textbook offers valuable direction for G9–G10 lesson and assignment design in our A1/A2 courses.

**Bottom line:** The textbook is a competent procedural writing guide with useful **discrete skill components** we can extract and repackage — but it is fundamentally misaligned with our instructional approach. Its tell-then-do pedagogy, absence of text-based writing, and lack of strategic/metacognitive instruction mean it cannot serve as a model for *how* to teach. It is a parts bin, not a blueprint.

---

## 1. What the Textbook Does Well (Borrow These)

### 1a. Weak/Strong Pairs for Discrimination Items

The textbook's greatest asset for our purposes is its extensive collection of **before/after and weak/strong pairs** across multiple skills. These are directly convertible into discrimination-before-production items:

| Source | Skill | What's There |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| Ch 6, §6.2 | Topic sentences | 5 weak/strong pairs with 5 quality criteria (accurate scope, controlling idea, clarity, no embedded support, engaging vocabulary) |
| Ch 9, §9.1 | Thesis statements | 4 weak-thesis patterns (mere declaration, outrageous claim, obvious fact, too broad) with examples |
| Ch 8, §8.4 | Wordiness | 5 specific patterns (there is/are, unnecessary modifiers, deadwood phrases, passive voice, compressible constructions) with before/after |
| Ch 7, §7.3 | Parallelism | ~12 faulty/correct pairs across coordinating conjunctions, than/as, and correlative conjunctions |
| Ch 12, §12.1 | Paraphrase quality | Bad paraphrase ("thesaurus method") vs. genuine paraphrase — side by side |
| Ch 12, §12.1 | Over-quoting | Over-quoted paragraph revised into properly integrated paragraph |

**Recommendation for A1:** Convert the topic sentence and thesis weak/strong pairs into 15–20 discrimination items for early lessons (L01–L05). Students classify, then revise.

**Recommendation for A2:** Convert the paraphrase and over-quoting pairs into discrimination items for source integration KCs.

### 1b. The Thesis Revision Protocol (Ch 9, pp. 343–344)

Four concrete, sequenced moves for sharpening a thesis:
1. Replace vague words with specific ones
2. Ask narrowing questions
3. Swap linking verbs for action verbs
4. Cut unsupportable claims

This maps directly to our **Claim Hierarchy mental model (M4)** and is immediately usable as a Supported Practice activity in A1 L03–L04. Give students a weak thesis → walk through all four moves.

### 1c. Sentence Combining via Coordination & Subordination (Ch 7, §7.2)

The textbook teaches sentence combining through:
- **Coordination** (FANBOYS, semicolons) — joining equal ideas
- **Subordination** (after, although, because, since, while, etc.) — emphasizing one idea over another

This is evidence-based (sentence combining has strong effect sizes in writing research). The exercises are mechanical but adaptable. We should incorporate sentence-combining practice in A1 Units 5–6 (style) as quick drills.

### 1d. Three-Pass Revision Protocol (Ch 8, §8.4)

The book explicitly separates revision into three sweeps:
- **Pass 1:** Unity (does every sentence serve the main point?)
- **Pass 2:** Coherence (are transitions logical and complete?)
- **Pass 3:** Conciseness (eliminate wordiness patterns)

This maps cleanly to our SRSD self-regulation checklists. Formalize as a named strategy for A1 Independent Practice.

### 1e. Transition Tables by Rhetorical Function (Ch 8)

Transitions categorized by purpose: sequence, position, conclusion, continuation, change of thought, importance, examples. More useful than a flat alphabetical list. Adapt as a reference scaffold for A1 paragraph writing.

### 1f. Concession Language Frames (Ch 10, §10.9)

Specific phrases for acknowledging opposing views: "although," "granted that," "of course," "though," "yet." Plus rationale: "such humility can go a long way toward earning credibility." Directly usable in A2 argument sophistication and ACT Writing prep.

### 1g. Source Evaluation Taxonomy (Ch 11, §11.4)

Three-tier ranking: High-Quality / Varied-Quality / Questionable, with concrete examples (beef industry author, diet book foundation funding studies). Convert into discrimination items for A2 source evaluation KCs.

### 1h. Comparison/Contrast Organizational Diagram (Ch 10, §10.7)

Visual showing subject-by-subject vs. point-by-point structure. Useful as a planning scaffold when A1 students first encounter comparative analysis tasks.

## 2. What the Textbook Does Poorly (Avoid These)

### 2a. No Strategic or Metacognitive Instruction

The book never names a strategy, never provides a mnemonic, never teaches students *how to think* about writing decisions. It's entirely procedural: "do X, then Y." This is the fundamental gap between tell-then-do instruction and SRSD. Our mental models (Everything Is a Choice, S-F-E, Force Field, Claim Hierarchy, Reverse Engineering, Who Benefits?) fill this gap entirely.

### 2b. No Coping Modeling

The book shows finished products and occasionally shows revision (the "Mariah" case study in Ch 8), but never models the *thinking process*. No false starts. No "I considered X but rejected it because Y." No expert-as-struggling-learner. Every example is clean and polished, which gives students no model for how to handle difficulty.

### 2c. No Text-Based Writing — Anywhere

This is the most fundamental misalignment. Across all chapters analyzed (6–12, 15), **not a single exercise asks students to analyze, respond to, or synthesize from a text**. Every prompt writes from personal experience or general knowledge. Our A1 course pivots to text-based analytical writing in Unit 3, and A2 is entirely source-based. The textbook offers zero models for this.

### 2d. No Evidence Integration Mechanics

The book tells students to use evidence but never teaches *how to embed a quotation within a sentence*, how to use signal phrases, or how to sandwich evidence between introduction and analysis. Ch 12 mentions "never use a stand-alone quotation" but doesn't teach the alternative mechanics.

### 2e. Five-Paragraph Essay as Unquestioned Default

Chapters 8–9 anchor repeatedly to the five-paragraph format without discussing when to deviate. This creates rigidity that harms students when they encounter AP-level or college-level writing.

### 2f. Rhetorical Modes as Discrete, Mutually Exclusive Types (Ch 10)

The textbook presents narration, description, classification, comparison, cause-effect, and persuasion as separate essay types. In reality, good argumentative and analytical essays use multiple modes simultaneously. The modes-as-taxonomy approach is outdated and limiting.

### 2g. No Self-Assessment or Calibration

No rubrics for evaluating quality. No anchor papers. No scoring practice. The peer review form (Ch 8) is structured but there's no training on *how to evaluate*. Our discrimination-before-production approach and calibration progression (binary → 3-point → AP → self-diagnosis) is far superior.

### 2h. Sample Essays Set a Low Ceiling (Ch 15)

The nine model essays are uniformly mediocre student-level five-paragraph essays. None cite sources. None would score well on AP or ACT assessments. The online alternatives listed (King's "Letter from Birmingham Jail," Cisneros, Tan) are far more valuable — but as source texts, not as models of student writing.

## 4. Gaps in Our Current A1/A2 Design Highlighted by the Textbook

The textbook, despite its weaknesses, does surface a few areas where our current design could be more explicit:

### 4a. Paragraph Mechanics in A1 Unit 1

The textbook's Ch 6 spends significant time on topic sentence placement, supporting sentence types, and concluding sentences. Our A1 Unit 1 (argumentative foundations) assumes students can write competent paragraphs. Given that G9 students are coming from G8 expository writing, we should verify that our bridge KCs (C.9.B01–B05) include **explicit paragraph structure** — not just argument structure. If L01 asks students to write an argument paragraph, they need to know what makes a paragraph a paragraph.

**Action:** Review A1 L01–L02 to ensure paragraph mechanics are scaffolded, not assumed.

### 4b. Transition Instruction

Our A1 lesson map doesn't explicitly address transitions until later units. The textbook's categorized transition tables suggest this is a discrete, teachable skill that benefits from early introduction. Consider adding a mini-lesson on transitions in A1 Unit 2 (bridge lessons).

**Action:** Add transition scaffolds to L06–L08 bridge lessons.

### 4c. Revision as Named, Separable Passes

Our SRSD self-regulation checklists handle revision, but we haven't explicitly named the passes. The textbook's three-pass model (unity, coherence, conciseness) is clean and memorable. Consider formalizing this as a named revision strategy.

**Action:** Name and sequence revision passes in A1 Unit 4+ self-regulation checklists.

### 4d. Sentence-Level Style Instruction

The textbook's Ch 7 (sentence variety, coordination/subordination, parallelism) covers skills that our A1 Unit 6 (style) addresses. The textbook's faulty/correct parallelism pairs are directly usable as discrimination items. We should ensure our style unit includes this level of specificity.

**Action:** Build parallelism and sentence-combining discrimination items for A1 L17–L19.

## 5. Summary Verdict

| Dimension | Textbook | Our Approach | Gap |
|-----------|----------|-------------|-----|
| **Discrete skills** (thesis, topic sentences, transitions, parallelism) | Good — concrete, paired examples | Must build items from scratch | **Borrow pairs, convert to discrimination items** |
| **Writing process** (prewriting, drafting, revising) | Adequate — procedural checklists | SRSD with strategic modeling | No gap — ours is superior |
| **Argument instruction** | Thin — one section in Ch 10 | Deep — bridge KCs, claim hierarchy, counterargument | No gap — ours is superior |
| **Text-based/analytical writing** | Absent | Core of A1 Units 3–6 and all of A2 | No gap — textbook irrelevant here |
| **Source integration** | Partial — Ch 11–12 | Must build for A2 synthesis | **Borrow paraphrase/over-quoting pairs** |
| **Metacognition/self-regulation** | Absent | SRSD core | No gap — textbook irrelevant here |
| **Coping modeling** | Absent | Required in every lesson | No gap — textbook irrelevant here |
| **Calibration/self-assessment** | Absent | Binary → 3-point → AP progression | No gap — textbook irrelevant here |
| **Sentence-level style** | Good — Ch 7 | A1 Unit 6 | **Borrow parallelism pairs, sentence-combining drills** |

**Final recommendation:** Mine the textbook for ~30–40 discrete discrimination items (weak/strong pairs across thesis, topic sentences, parallelism, paraphrase, wordiness). Discard its instructional approach entirely. Our SRSD framework, mental models, and discrimination-before-production sequencing are categorically more effective than the textbook's tell-then-do pedagogy.
